- Empirical finding: Skip-gram embeddings trained on lemmatized BNC encode robust POS information, enabling high-accuracy POS prediction with a simple classifier.
- Error analysis contribution: Systematic misclassifications reveal graded POS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, adjective/adverb confusions).
- Distributional finding: POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized to a few.

- Clear experimental design: Pipeline (lemmatization, UD mapping, Skip-gram, logistic regression) and multi-setting evaluation (in-domain, frequency-held-out, UD Treebank) support robustness of claims.
- Linguistic insight: Misclassification study yields meaningful findings about category membership (e.g., participles, nominal modifiers) and corpus annotation errors.
- Comparative diagnostic value: Contrasting classifier confusions with a standard POS tagger shows complementary boundary cases surfaced by embeddings.
- Well-written and accessible presentation, with thoughtful methodological analysis (ANOVA ranking and incremental feature sets).

- Limited methodological novelty: Uses standard embeddings and a linear classifier with no new algorithms or modeling contributions.
- Straightforward experiments: Core contribution is analytical rather than advancing models or training regimes, reducing impact for method-focused venues.
- Narrow literature coverage: Fails to sufficiently cite/discuss prior embedding-based POS tagging/induction work (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016).
- Venue fit concern: Stronger fit for empirical/quantitative linguistics than for conferences prioritizing novel NLP methods.

- Generalizability: How well do you expect the approach to generalize to other languages or tagsets, and are there plans for multilingual validation?
