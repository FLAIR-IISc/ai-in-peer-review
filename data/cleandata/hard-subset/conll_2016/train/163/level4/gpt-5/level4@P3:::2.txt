Summary
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings correlate with part-of-speech (PoS) classes. Using the BNC (lemmatized and remapped to Universal PoS tags), the authors train a multinomial classifier on word vectors to predict PoS. High in-domain and cross-corpus performance indicates that embeddings encode PoS information. The error analysis highlights “outlier” words whose distributional behavior diverges from their annotated tags, revealing graded PoS affiliations and potential annotation inconsistencies (notably in BNC). The authors further show that PoS-relevant information is distributed across many embedding dimensions and present a preliminary PoS predictor trained only on untagged embeddings and a small set of “prototypical” words.

Contributions (as I see them)
- A careful, data-driven error analysis showing how embedding-based PoS predictions uncover graded category membership and corpus annotation inconsistencies; this is the paper’s primary contribution.
- Empirical evidence, across BNC and UD, that embeddings robustly encode PoS information, with information distributed over many dimensions rather than localized in a few.
- A simple, low-resource PoS predictor trained on untagged embeddings and a small prototypical lexicon, demonstrating reasonable accuracy without contextual features.

Strengths
- Thorough and insightful error analysis: The investigation of misclassified cases (e.g., passive participles, noun–adjective alternations, numerals vs. nouns, time expressions) is linguistically compelling and actionable for corpus maintenance. The identification of systematic BNC inconsistencies (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs) is especially valuable.
- Robustness across datasets: The method performs well both on held-out BNC words and on UD lemmas, supporting generalizability beyond the training corpus and tagger.
- Clear evidence of graded PoS affiliations: The analysis supports “soft” category boundaries (e.g., participles, nominal modifiers), aligning with long-standing descriptive claims and showing how distributional behavior reflects this.
- Component-level analysis: The ANOVA-based feature ranking and accuracy curves convincingly show that PoS cues are distributed across many embedding dimensions, countering simplistic “one-dimension-per-feature” interpretations.
- Useful diagnostic tool: Comparing confusion patterns against a mainstream tagger suggests the embedding-based approach surfaces different classes of issues, making it a complementary tool for corpus auditing.

Weaknesses
- Positioning and novelty relative to PoS induction literature: The claim that embeddings contain PoS information is not new. A substantial body of work on unsupervised/semi-supervised PoS induction and clustering (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al., 2010 survey; Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012) has long reported high many-to-one and purity scores indicative of strong PoS signals in distributional representations. The paper should acknowledge and position itself against this literature, clarifying what is new (the corpus-auditing perspective and detailed error analysis seem to be the key novelty).
- Missing baselines and comparisons: There is no comparison to classic distributional baselines such as Brown clusters (which often align strongly with PoS), or to simple context-count features. Including these would strengthen claims about what modern dense embeddings add, and would contextualize Section 5’s predictor against established PoS induction methods.
- Limited extrinsic evaluation: Since major corpora are PoS-tagged primarily to support downstream tasks (notably parsing), an extrinsic comparison would be informative. For example, do parser accuracies improve or degrade if one substitutes the standard tags with the embedding-informed predictions, “soft” labels, or corrected annotations?
- Tagset choice and granularity: Collapsing BNC tags to UPOS is practical but loses fine-grained distinctions that are central to the paper’s theme (graded boundaries). The authors should justify this choice and, ideally, report complementary analyses on the original tagset to see whether the same patterns hold (e.g., proper vs. common nouns; participle subtypes).
- Methodological clarity: The note that naive clustering “inevitably failed” lacks detail (algorithm, K, initialization, metrics, and failure criteria). Given the relevance to PoS induction, this needs to be specified. Similarly, frequency-weighting of training instances is reasonable but could bias toward frequent-function-word phenomena; please report class-wise scores and ablations (e.g., without frequency weighting; with/without lemmatization) and examine low-frequency “tail” behavior, where distributional representations are weakest.

Questions to Authors
- Clustering attempts: Which algorithms (k-means, GMMs, spectral), number of clusters, initializations, and evaluation metrics (V-measure, NMI, many-to-one) did you try? How did they “fail,” and how do these outcomes compare to Brown clusters on the same data?
- Baselines: How would your classifier compare to (i) Brown cluster IDs as features, (ii) sparse context vectors, or (iii) PoS induction systems with similar resource assumptions? Could you add a Brown-cluster baseline for Section 5?
- UPOS vs. original BNC tags: Why was UPOS preferred? Can you replicate key analyses with the original tags to assess whether your findings persist at finer granularity?
- Tail analysis: What are the error patterns for low-frequency words? Could you quantify performance as a function of frequency and consider subword/morphological features to improve the tail?
- Extrinsic impact: Can you perform a small parsing experiment comparing standard tags, your predicted tags, and “corrected” tags (where you believe BNC is inconsistent), to test whether the distributionally motivated categories are more useful for downstream tasks?

Additional comments
- The statement that relations in embedding space are “mostly semantic” is too strong; prior work shows substantial syntactic regularities (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013; Collobert et al., 2011).
- Consider citing and discussing PoS induction literature: Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom & Cohn (2011), Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others.
- Please clarify the status of “footnote 3” and provide experimental details for the clustering claim.
- It would strengthen the corpus-auditing claim to provide a small manual validation of a sample of detected inconsistencies, reporting precision of error detection.
- Minor: Use \newcite{} for sentential citations; clean up references (years, authors, and duplicates); ensure consistent capitalization (e.g., “UPOS”), and fix typos (“is is,” “concatination,” “fullfledged,” etc.).

Overall, this is a well-executed and useful study of how embeddings reflect PoS structure, with compelling error analysis and practical insights for corpus auditing. To reach the bar for novelty, the paper should engage more deeply with PoS induction work, add strong baselines, justify tagset choices, and, if possible, demonstrate extrinsic benefits.
