This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) categories and word embeddings, showing that vector representations correlate strongly with PoS and providing a detailed error analysis of misclassified words. The outlier analysis is a clear strength and offers useful insights into where distributional methods succeed and fail.

That said, the paper misses a key motivation for why corpora like the BNC were PoS-tagged in the first place: to support downstream NLP tasks, especially parsing. The central question should not be whether embeddings encode PoS information per se, but whether the distinctions captured by embeddings are more useful for downstream tasks than traditional tagsets (or UPOS). A reframing along these lines would significantly strengthen the contribution: for example, by comparing parsing performance when using gold tags, UPOS, and embedding-driven PoS predictions or clusters.

Related work is also undercited, particularly on distributional PoS induction. A good starting point is Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), which reviews much of the earlier literature. Please also discuss non-neural approaches such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. Against this background, the findings in Section 5 are not especially novel; several prior systems with comparable or fewer external resources achieve similar clustering purity or many-to-one mapping performance. Positioning your work with respect to these results—and clarifying what is new—would help.

Specific comments
- Abstract claim: Stating that “distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation” is not new for English. Numerous PoS induction studies over the past 15 years report strong many-to-one and cluster-purity scores that imply the same conclusion. Calibrate this claim or emphasize what your analysis adds beyond prior evidence (e.g., finer-grained diagnostics, cross-corpus robustness, or insights from error typology).
- Lines 79–80: The assertion that relations between vectors are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate or, at best, incomplete. Work by Mikolov et al. and Collobert et al., and subsequent studies, shows substantial syntactic regularities in embeddings. You appear to acknowledge this later (lines 107–108). Please reconcile these statements and cite the relevant literature.
- Choice of tagset: The move to UPOS is not fully justified. For the questions you pose, the fine-grained distinctions in the original tagset may be more revealing. If you retain UPOS, motivate the choice clearly (e.g., cross-lingual comparability, smoothing sparsity) and, ideally, provide parallel analyses with the original tags to show what is gained or lost.
- Footnote 3: This is unclear. Were the reported failed attempts yours or prior work? By what criteria did they fail? Please be precise about settings (embedding types, dimensionality, training data, supervision) and outcomes. Also consider including Brown clusters as a baseline; they are known to align closely with PoS (often even better with UPOS) and would be a strong reference point.
- Proper vs. common nouns: The observation that proper nouns are not particularly similar to common nouns (lines 331–332) seems expected. Distributional context—especially frequent function words like “the”—is likely sufficient to drive this separation. Consider controlling for determiners or capitalization and reporting whether the effect persists.
- Head vs. tail behavior: Focusing on the most frequent word/tag pairs is practical, but the tail is crucial. Please analyze low-frequency words: how do their vectors behave, what errors does the classifier make, and what patterns emerge? This could motivate incorporating features beyond pure distributional signals (e.g., subword morphology, character n-grams, affixes, capitalization) to improve generalization for rare words.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Reframe the central question around utility: Do embedding-induced distinctions improve downstream tasks (e.g., tagging or parsing), compared to original tags or UPOS? A simple experiment feeding predicted tags or clusters into a parser, or ablations that mix gold vs. embedding-based cues, would be compelling.
- Expand related work and metrics: Situate your results against PoS induction literature using standard evaluations (many-to-one, V-measure, cluster purity) and report directly comparable numbers where possible. Discuss the supervision and resources used to ensure a fair comparison.
- Baselines and controls: Include Brown clusters, context window ablations, and different embedding types (e.g., skip-gram, CBOW, GloVe). Where feasible, consider static vs. subword-informed embeddings and assess robustness to lemmatization.
- Clarify novelty: Make explicit what is new—e.g., the depth of error analysis, quantitative evidence about specific tag confusions, or systematic diagnostics across correlation structures—not just that embeddings encode PoS.
- Interpretability: When highlighting outliers, connect them to linguistic phenomena (polysemy, named entities, derivational morphology, MWEs) and quantify their prevalence. This will make the analysis more actionable.

Minor issues
- Replace sentential references to \newcite{} with standard citations, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed …”.
- Ensure consistent spelling of author names (e.g., Collobert, not “Colobert”).
- Provide full citations for Croft (1991), Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom and Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012).
- Define all abbreviations at first use (e.g., BNC, UPOS) and state preprocessing choices clearly (tokenization, lemmatization, casing).

Overall assessment
- Strengths: Clear empirical setup, informative outlier/error analysis, and an intuitively appealing link between embeddings and PoS.
- Weaknesses: Limited novelty relative to prior PoS induction work; missing related literature; unclear motivation for UPOS; and lack of evaluation that ties findings to downstream utility.

With an expanded related-work section, clearer positioning of contributions, stronger baselines (especially Brown clusters), and an evaluation that connects embedding-based distinctions to parsing or tagging performance, this paper could offer a valuable empirical perspective on the interaction between distributional representations and PoS categories.
