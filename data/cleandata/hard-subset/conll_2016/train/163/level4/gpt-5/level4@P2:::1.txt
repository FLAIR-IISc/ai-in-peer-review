Summary and contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on a lemmatized, UD-tagged version of the BNC encode part-of-speech (POS) information. The authors train skip-gram embeddings and a multinomial logistic classifier to predict POS from word vectors, evaluate on BNC and UD English, analyze systematic misclassifications to probe “soft” POS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies, and examine which embedding dimensions correlate with POS.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration—across BNC and UD data—that standard word embeddings contain robust, broadly distributed signals predictive of POS, enabling a simple classifier to achieve high accuracy without sentence-level context.
2) A detailed error/outlier analysis showing graded category membership and surfacing plausible annotation inconsistencies (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, adverbs vs. adjectives, PROPN vs. NOUN), which is useful for corpus QA and linguistic discussion of POS boundaries.
3) Evidence that POS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (rather than concentrated in a few), based on feature ranking and ablation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation with thorough experiments. The setup (BNC preprocessing, embedding training, classifier design) is described sufficiently for replication, and results are reported on both in-domain and out-of-domain (UD) data, lending credibility to the claims.
- Strong and insightful error analysis. The confusion analyses are carefully interpreted. The outlier cases (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally, currency/percent forms aligning with numerals, decades/years aligning with nouns) compellingly illustrate graded POS behavior and hint at annotation issues—exactly the kind of evidence needed for discussions about POS as soft categories.
- Practical utility for corpus diagnostics. The work shows that simple distributional predictors can flag systematic tagging inconsistencies (e.g., adverbs vs. adjectives for forms like “plain/clear/sharp”), which can guide corpus cleanup and guideline refinement.
- Robustness despite minimal modeling. Achieving high F-scores with a basic logistic classifier and small context windows, and still performing reasonably without any sentence context, indicates the POS signal is strong in embeddings and not an artifact of elaborate modeling.
- Transparency about where information resides. The feature ablation suggests POS cues are spread over many dimensions, aligning with the intuition of distributed representations and offering a concrete empirical characterization.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core techniques (skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression) and the idea that embeddings capture POS cues are well known. The contributions are primarily empirical and analytical rather than algorithmic.
- Incomplete engagement with related work. Prior research on POS tagging/induction with embeddings and feature representations should be discussed more extensively (e.g., Lin et al., 2015; Ling et al., 2015; Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg, 2016), including how this work differs (e.g., focus on error-driven corpus diagnostics, graded category analysis) and where it corroborates or extends earlier findings.
- Potential confounds in the main setup. The principal classifier is trained on embeddings of lemma+POS concatenations, which might bias the representation toward the gold tag distribution and blur the boundary between “discovering POS from embeddings” and “memorizing tag-conditioned contexts.” Section 5 mitigates this, but clearer separation and emphasis on the tag-free setting would strengthen the claims.
- Outlier analysis is partly anecdotal. While the qualitative discussion is persuasive, the paper would benefit from more systematic quantification (e.g., prevalence of each error category across frequencies, confidence calibration, significance tests) and controlled checks for frequency and OOV effects (e.g., rare proper names).
- Comparisons and baselines could be fairer. The Stanford tagger comparison uses token-level inputs without sentence context, which is an unusual and harsh evaluation for a contextual tagger; the interpretability of that confusion matrix is therefore limited. Including standard POS tagging baselines in their typical contextual setting, or clearly framing this as a corpus-wide lexical probe, would avoid confusion.

Questions to the authors
- Could you quantify the impact of frequency on misclassifications (e.g., stratifying by frequency deciles) to separate representation sparsity effects from genuine category ambiguity?
- How sensitive are the results to embedding hyperparameters (window size, negative samples, downsampling) and to lemmatization/casing? For instance, would preserving capitalization improve PROPN vs. NOUN discrimination?
- In Section 5’s tag-free setting, how does performance change if you expand the “prototypical” training lexicon or vary the threshold for ambiguity? Any ablation on training size vs. accuracy?
- Can you provide more systematic evidence for the “distributed across many components” claim (e.g., cumulative variance explained, mutual information per feature, or regularized models to measure sparsity)?
- Will you release the BNC-to-UD mapping, trained models, and code to facilitate replication and corpus diagnostics by others?

Overall assessment
The paper is clear, careful, and empirically valuable. While it does not introduce new modeling techniques, it offers a solid, well-argued analysis that substantiates the presence and distribution of POS information in embeddings and demonstrates a useful methodology for surfacing graded categories and annotation inconsistencies. This makes it a strong fit for venues interested in quantitative/empirical linguistics and corpus methodology; for a primarily NLP/ML audience, the main novelty lies in the analysis rather than the methods and should be positioned accordingly. The related work section should be expanded to situate the contributions more precisely.
