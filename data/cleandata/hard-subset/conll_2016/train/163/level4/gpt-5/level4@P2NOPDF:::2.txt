General comments:
This paper examines the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. Using distributional vectors, the authors draw reasonable—if somewhat unsurprising—conclusions about the consistency of PoS labels and their strong correspondence to embedding space. The detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a notable strength.

However, the paper overlooks a key motivation for resources like the BNC being PoS-tagged in the first place: they were designed to support downstream NLP, especially parsing, rather than to pursue a purely linguistic investigation of morphosyntactic categories (which are often framed within prototype-based semantics; see Croft, 1991). The discussion would be much stronger if reframed around whether the distinctions emergent in distributional vectors produce more useful signals for parsers than the original tagset or UPOS.

The paper also omits substantial related work on distributional PoS induction. I suggest starting with the survey by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), and including more recent non–neural network approaches such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this literature, the results in Section 5 offer limited novelty; there exist systems that, with fewer external resources, achieve comparable performance.

Specific issues:
- The abstract claims that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” For English, this is not new: virtually all distributional PoS induction work over the past 15 years that reports many-to-one or cluster purity scores demonstrates this point.
- The statement in lines 79–80 that relationships between word vectors are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate. Prior work (e.g., Collobert et al.; Mikolov et al. and successors) shows that embeddings capture substantial syntactic regularities. This also appears inconsistent with your own discussion at the start of Section 2 (lines 107–108).
- Why switch to UPOS? The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more informative and could reveal more nuanced structure in the embeddings.
- Footnote 3 is unclear. Were the failed attempts yours or drawn from prior work? What criteria defined “failure”? Also consider Brown clusters: historically they align closely with UPOS and would be a strong baseline or point of comparison.
- The observation that proper nouns are not very similar to common nouns (lines 331–332) seems unsurprising. The distributional behavior of “the” (the most frequent function word) alone could largely drive this difference.
- While focusing on the most frequent word/tag pairs is practical, please also analyze the tail. What happens to rare words and low-frequency tags, both in the embedding space and in classification errors? This could motivate features beyond purely distributional ones (and beyond lemmatization-based morphology) to improve generalization for low-frequency items.
- To connect more directly to the intended utility of PoS tags, consider evaluating whether your induced or embedding-informed distinctions improve parsing (e.g., UAS/LAS) relative to the original tagset and UPOS.

Minor issues:
- Use \newcite{} for sentential citations, e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed ...”
