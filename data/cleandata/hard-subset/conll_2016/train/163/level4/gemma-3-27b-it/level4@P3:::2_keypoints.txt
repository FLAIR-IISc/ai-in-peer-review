- Paper shows word embeddings encode PoS information via a predictive classification task on a large corpus.
- Detailed error analysis of misclassified "outlier" words reveals inconsistencies and ambiguities in the BNC PoS annotations.
- PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized to a few components.
- Use of the British National Corpus (BNC) provides a solid, standard empirical basis for experiments.
- Experimental setup is clearly described, including embedding training, classification, and class-imbalance weighting.
- High predictive performance (F1 0.979 train, 0.91 unseen) demonstrates effective PoS prediction from distributional vectors without context.
- Analysis of how many vector components are needed gives insight into how grammatical information is encoded.
- Paper fails to frame findings in terms of downstream NLP task performance (e.g., whether revised tags improve parsing).
- Substantial related work on distributional PoS induction is omitted and should be discussed to position novelty.
- Claim of novelty that embeddings contain PoS information is overstated given prior literature.
- Converting to UPOS is not convincingly justified; fine-grained BNC distinctions may be informative.
- Analysis focuses on frequent words; low-frequency word behavior and additional features are underexplored.
- Ask whether revising BNC tags based on distributional evidence improves downstream tasks like parsing.
- Request clarification on "failed attempts" mentioned in the paper (methods tried and reasons for failure).
- Ask for comparison to existing distributional PoS induction methods, especially neural approaches.
- Ask what non-frequency characteristics (morphological/semantic) explain outlier misclassifications.
- Suggest replacing citation phrasing with proper LaTeX citation command and investigating function-word influence on noun dissimilarity.
- Recommend testing generalizability on a held-out set from a different corpus (e.g., Penn Treebank).
