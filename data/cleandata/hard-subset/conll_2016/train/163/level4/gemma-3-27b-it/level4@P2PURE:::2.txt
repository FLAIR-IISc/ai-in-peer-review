This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors demonstrate, through detailed error analysis – a notable strength of the paper – some expected connections between distributional vector representations and PoS. However, the paper overlooks a key consideration: the original motivation for PoS tagging in corpora like the BNC was to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, primarily parsing. Consequently, the discussion would be strengthened by framing the work as an evaluation of whether distinctions captured by distributional vectors are more beneficial for parsing than the original tags, or even Universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend including Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) as a foundational reference, and expanding the survey to encompass more recent non-DNN approaches, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Given this existing body of work, the results presented in Section 5 appear incremental, as comparable results have been achieved by systems with more limited external knowledge.

Specifically, the abstract’s claim that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation” is not novel, particularly for English. This finding has been consistently demonstrated by distributional PoS induction systems over the past 15 years through metrics like “many-to-one” mapping and cluster purity. 

The assertion on lines 79-80 that “relations between… vectors… are mostly semantic” is inaccurate; work such as Mikolov et al. (or Colobert et al.) and subsequent research has established the presence of significant syntactic information within these vectors. This contradicts the authors’ own statement in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108), and is further challenged by the discussion of cluster purity scores. 

The shift to UPOS is also unclear, as the finer-grained distinctions of the original tagset are potentially more informative.

Footnote 3 requires clarification. Were the “failed attempts” referenced conducted by the authors or reported in other work? What criteria were used to define failure, and what about the performance of Brown cluster vectors, which demonstrate a near-perfect alignment with UPOS tags? 

While the observation regarding the dissimilarity between proper and common nouns (lines 331-332) is noted, its significance is questionable given the strong influence of function words like “the.”

Although analyzing frequent word/tag pairs is practical, an investigation into the tail of the distribution – for both vectors and classifier errors – would be valuable. This could suggest alternative features, beyond purely distributional and morphological ones, that might improve generalization to low-frequency words and address the observed errors.

Minor issues:

Please use \newcite{} for sentential references, for example, replacing “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed” with the appropriate \newcite{} command.
