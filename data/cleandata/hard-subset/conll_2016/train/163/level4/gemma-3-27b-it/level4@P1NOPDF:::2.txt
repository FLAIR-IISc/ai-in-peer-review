This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors demonstrate, through insightful – though perhaps not entirely surprising – analysis, a clear connection between word vector representations and PoS. A key strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis, specifically the investigation of misclassified instances.

However, the paper overlooks a crucial context: the original motivations behind PoS tagging corpora like the BNC. These corpora weren’t primarily created as a purely linguistic exercise exploring morphosyntactic categories (as informed by prototype theory, e.g., Croft, 1991); rather, they were built to facilitate downstream NLP tasks, predominantly parsing. Consequently, the discussion could be significantly strengthened by reframing the work around whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors offer advantages for parsing compared to the original tags – or even UPOS tags.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend beginning with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and expanding to include more recent non-DNN approaches, such as Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Given this existing body of work, the results presented in Section 5 are only marginally novel, as systems with more limited external knowledge have achieved comparable performance.

**Specific Issues**

The abstract states that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." This claim feels unremarkable, particularly for English. The past 15 years of distributional PoS induction research, which routinely reports “many-to-one” or “cluster purity” scores, has consistently demonstrated this finding.

The assertion in lines 79-80 that "relations between… vectors… are mostly semantic" is inaccurate. The work of Mikolov et al. (or Colobert et al.) and subsequent research shows that these vectors encode significant syntactic information, a point reinforced by the aforementioned cluster purity scores. Indeed, the authors appear to retract this statement early in Section 2 (lines 107-108).

I question the move to UPOS. The finer-grained distinctions within the original tagset seem more potentially interesting. 

Footnote 3 is unclear. Were the referenced failed attempts conducted by the authors, or by other researchers? What were the criteria for failure? Additionally, the near-perfect alignment of Brown cluster vectors with UPOS tags is not acknowledged.

Is the observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331-332) particularly insightful? The frequent occurrence of the definite article “the” likely explains much of this difference.

While I appreciate the practical rationale for analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs, exploring the tail of the distribution – for both the vectors themselves and the types of errors the classifier makes – would be valuable. This analysis could inspire consideration of alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological, given the lemmatization applied) features, potentially leading to better generalization for low-frequency words.
