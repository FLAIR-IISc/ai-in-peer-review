- Demonstrates a connection between PoS tags and word embeddings, but the finding is relatively intuitive.  
- Strength: detailed error analysis that identifies outlier classifications.  
- Lacks core motivation: PoS tagging of corpora like the BNC is primarily to support downstream tasks (e.g., parsing); paper should evaluate benefits for parsing or against UPOS.  
- Insufficient engagement with prior work in distributional PoS induction; include Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and non-DNN methods like Blunsom & Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012).  
- Results (Section 5) appear incremental given prior systems with less external knowledge reached comparable performance.  
- Claim that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation” is not novel, especially for English.  
- The assertion that vector relations are “mostly semantic” is inaccurate and contradicted elsewhere in the paper; prior work (e.g., Mikolov et al., Colobert et al.) shows substantial syntactic information.  
- Questionable decision to transition to UPOS tags—original, more fine-grained tagset may be more informative.  
- Footnote 3 is unclear: state whether the failed attempts were conducted by the authors or reported elsewhere and define failure criteria.  
- The near-perfect alignment of Brown cluster vectors with UPOS tags should be discussed.  
- The noted dissimilarity between proper and common nouns is unsurprising (likely driven by contexts like “the”) and not a novel insight.  
- Analysis focuses on frequent word/tag pairs; the paper should examine low-frequency words and their classifier errors.  
- Suggest developing or exploring alternative features beyond purely distributional and morphological cues to improve generalization for low-frequency words.  
- Revise sentential citations to use the \newcite{} command (e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”).
