This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors demonstrate, with detailed error analysis as a particular strength, a connection between distributional vector representations and PoS. While the conclusions reached are generally sensible, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the motivations behind the original PoS tagging of corpora like the Brown Corpus and the British National Corpus (BNC). These corpora were primarily tagged to support downstream NLP tasks, particularly parsing, and the discussion could be reframed to evaluate whether distinctions captured by distributional vectors offer advantages for parsing compared to traditional tags or Universal PoS (UPOS) tags. 

A significant omission is a comprehensive review of related work in distributional PoS induction. The paper should engage with foundational work such as Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), as well as subsequent non-deep learning approaches including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this existing body of research, the results presented in Section 5 appear incremental, as systems utilizing more limited external knowledge have already achieved comparable performance.

Specifically, the claim in the abstract that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation” is not particularly novel, especially for English. This finding has been consistently demonstrated by prior work in distributional PoS induction that reports metrics like cluster purity. The assertion in lines 79-80, suggesting that relationships between word vectors are “mostly semantic,” is inaccurate. Prior research, including the foundational work of Mikolov et al. and Colobert, has established the presence of substantial syntactic information within these vectors. This statement also contradicts the authors’ own acknowledgement in lines 107-108 at the beginning of Section 2.

The decision to transition to UPOS tags is also unclear; the finer-grained distinctions of the original tagset likely offer more nuanced insights. 

Further clarification is needed regarding Footnote 3. It is unclear whether the “failed attempts” referenced were conducted by the authors or reported in other work, and the criteria for failure remain unspecified. The near-perfect alignment of Brown clusters with UPOS tags should also be addressed.

The observation regarding the dissimilarity between proper and common nouns (lines 331-332) feels unremarkable, potentially largely explained by the high frequency of the definite article “the.” 

While the analysis of frequent word/tag pairs is practical, expanding the investigation to the long tail – considering both the vector representations and the errors made by the classifier – would be valuable. This could stimulate consideration of alternative feature combinations, beyond purely distributional and morphological features (given the lemmatization process), to improve generalization to low-frequency words.

Finally, a minor point: please consistently use `\newcite{}` for sentential references (e.g., replace "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" with the appropriate `\newcite{}` command).
