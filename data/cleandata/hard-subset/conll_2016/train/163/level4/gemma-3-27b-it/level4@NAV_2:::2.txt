This paper presents an exploration of the connection between part-of-speech tags and word embeddings. Specifically the authors use word embeddings to draw some interesting (if not somewhat straightforward) conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the clear connection of word vector representations to PoS. The detailed error analysis (outliers of classification) is definitely a strong point of this paper. However, the paper seems to lack a critical framing element: the original motivation for PoS tagging corpora. Unlike a purely linguistic exploration of morphosyntactic categories, these corpora were created and tagged to facilitate further NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion could be reframed as whether the distinctions made by the distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing compared to the original tags (or UPOS). Also, this paper overlooks significant related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend citing Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-DNN work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In this context, the results of section 5 are not particularly novel, as systems with more constraints and less external knowledge achieve comparable results.

The paper’s main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) a demonstration, through error analysis of a PoS classifier trained on word embeddings, of specific inconsistencies and potential errors within the BNC’s PoS annotation; (2) an empirical confirmation that distributional semantic models encode information related to PoS, albeit distributed across many vector components, rather than concentrated in a few; and (3) a re-examination of the boundaries between PoS classes, revealing instances where distributional patterns suggest alternative tagging choices. 

**Strengths:**

1.  **Detailed Error Analysis:** The meticulous analysis of misclassified words is a significant strength. Identifying patterns in the errors – such as the misclassification of proper nouns, currency amounts, and ambiguous adjective/verb cases – provides valuable insights into the nuances and potential inconsistencies of the BNC tagging scheme. This goes beyond simply reporting accuracy scores and delves into *why* the model makes mistakes, yielding concrete observations about linguistic ambiguity.
2.  **Confirmation of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The consistent performance of the PoS classifier, achieving high accuracy even without contextual information, strongly suggests that distributional semantics captures meaningful PoS-related information. This reinforces the idea that syntactic and semantic information are intertwined, supporting the distributional hypothesis. The observation that this information isn’t localized to a few vector dimensions is also interesting.
3.  **Identification of Boundary Cases:** The paper successfully identifies words that lie on the boundaries between PoS classes (e.g., participles, nouns functioning as adjectives). This highlights the inherent fuzziness of PoS categorization and prompts a re-evaluation of annotation guidelines. The examples given are clear and illustrate the challenges of assigning unambiguous tags.
4.  **Robustness Demonstrated Across Datasets:** Testing the classifier on both the UD Treebank and the initial BNC test set, and achieving high F-scores, demonstrates the generalizability of the findings and reduces concerns about overfitting to the BNC corpus.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Framing within NLP Tasks:** The paper primarily focuses on linguistic observations without explicitly connecting them to their potential impact on downstream NLP tasks, such as parsing. Framing the analysis in terms of parsing performance, or comparing the utility of distributional features to traditional PoS tags for parsing, would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.
2.  **Insufficient Related Work:** The paper overlooks a substantial body of work on distributional PoS induction. The omission of key papers like Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom and Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012) diminishes the novelty of the findings and fails to position the work within the broader research landscape. 
3.  **Triviality of Some Findings:** The statement that distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation, and that proper nouns and common nouns are distinct, is hardly novel. These results are well-established in the field of distributional semantics and PoS induction. The paper should either contextualize these findings more effectively or focus on the more nuanced observations made through error analysis.
4.  **Choice of UPOS:** The justification for converting to the UPOS tagset isn't compelling. The finer-grained distinctions in the original BNC tagset might be more informative for identifying subtle tagging inconsistencies.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific parsing performance improvements (or lack thereof) that might result from using the insights gained from the distributional model to refine PoS tags?
2.  How does the performance of your classifier compare to existing distributional PoS induction methods, particularly those utilizing techniques beyond simple logistic regression?
3.  What characteristics of the misclassified words in the ‘tail’ (low-frequency words) contribute to their incorrect classification, and what alternative features might improve their tagging accuracy?
4.  The footnote mentions failed attempts at classification. Could you provide more details about these attempts and the criteria used to determine failure?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please replace the `\cite{}` commands with `\newcite{}` for more appropriate citation style in the final version.
*   Consider analyzing the performance of the classifier on different subsets of the data (e.g., based on word frequency or morphological complexity) to identify potential biases and limitations.
*   Exploring the use of more sophisticated machine learning models, beyond logistic regression, could potentially improve the accuracy and robustness of the PoS classifier.
