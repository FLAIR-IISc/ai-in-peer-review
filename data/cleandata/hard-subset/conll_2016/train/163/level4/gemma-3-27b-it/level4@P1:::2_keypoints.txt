- Paper studies connection between PoS tags and word embeddings and shows vectors reflect PoS information.
- Detailed error analysis of classification outliers is a major strength.
- Missing discussion of why corpora like the BNC were PoS-tagged (mainly for downstream NLP/parsing), which should reframe the contribution.
- Should evaluate whether distributional distinctions improve parsing compared to original tags or UPOS.
- Lacks coverage of key related work on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012).
- Given prior work, results in Section 5 appear of limited novelty; comparable systems exist with stricter external knowledge.
- Claim in abstract that “distributional vectors contain PoS information” is not novel for English.
- Assertion that embedding relations are “mostly semantic” is incorrect and inconsistent with cited work showing syntactic information in vectors.
- Unclear motivation for switching to UPOS instead of analyzing the more informative fine-grained original tagset.
- Footnote 3 is ambiguous: unclear who attempted the failed methods, what criteria defined failure, and omission of Brown cluster results.
- Observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns is unsurprising and likely driven by function-word contexts (e.g., “the”).
- Analysis focuses on frequent word/tag pairs; paper should examine tail/low-frequency words and classifier errors there.
- Recommend exploring alternatives to purely distributional (and morphological/lemmatized) features to better generalize PoS for rare words.
- Minor formatting: use \newcite{} for sentential references (e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”).
