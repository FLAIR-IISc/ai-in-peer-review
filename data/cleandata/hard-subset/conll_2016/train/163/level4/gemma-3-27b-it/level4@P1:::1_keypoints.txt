- Paper shows word embeddings trained on a lemmatized UD-annotated BNC encode POS information and uses them to train a logistic regression POS classifier.
- Results are clearly presented and analyzed, with cross-validation on BNC and evaluation on other corpora.
- Paper is clear and well-written.
- Main limitation: limited novelty — straightforward application of existing NLP/ML methods rather than new algorithms or theory.
- Suggestion: work may be more suitable for a quantitative/empirical linguistics venue than an NLP/ML conference.
- Literature review is insufficient; key related work (Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016) is missing.
- Contribution 1: robust empirical validation that embeddings encode POS distinctions using a standard corpus and modern techniques.
- Contribution 2: insightful error analysis highlighting annotation inconsistencies and POS boundary cases with suggestions for corpus improvement.
- Strength: strong empirical results — high accuracy (F1 0.979 on training, 0.91 on unseen test).
- Strength: detailed error analysis revealing annotation errors and linguistic nuances (e.g., currency vs. numerals).
- Strength: demonstrated robustness across datasets (BNC and UD English Treebank), supporting generalizability.
- Strength: useful visualizations (e.g., prototypical conjunction vectors) showing vector components correlate with POS.
- Weakness: lack of methodological novelty — standard techniques used without new methods.
- Weakness: limited innovation scope — identifies annotation issues but does not propose solutions.
- Weakness: inadequate citation of prior work on POS induction with embeddings.
- Weakness: dependence on gold-standard POS tags; a fully unsupervised or low-resource demonstration would be more compelling.
- Question: why was logistic regression chosen; were other classifiers considered?
- Question: please elaborate on how this approach would apply concretely to resource-poor languages and anticipated challenges.
- Question: any hypotheses for why POS information is distributed across many vector dimensions rather than concentrated?
- Additional comments: maintain consistency in “PoS” vs “POS” usage.
- Additional comments: anonymization note in the review appears unnecessary.
