This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors demonstrate, through detailed error analysis – a key strength of the work – that distributional vectors encode information about PoS. While the conclusions reached are generally sensible, the paper lacks a crucial framing: the original motivation for PoS tagging in corpora like the Brown Corpus and the British National Corpus (BNC). These corpora were not primarily created as linguistic resources for exploring morphosyntactic categories; rather, they were designed to facilitate NLP tasks, particularly parsing. Consequently, the discussion would be strengthened by reframing the investigation around whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors are *more* beneficial for parsing than the original tags, or even Universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper overlooks a substantial body of work on distributional PoS induction. The review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) provides a strong starting point, but should be supplemented with more recent, non-DNN approaches, such as Blunsom & Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this existing research, the results presented in Section 5 appear incremental; systems with comparable or superior performance exist, even with more limited external knowledge.

Specifically, the abstract claims that “distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation.” This is not a novel finding, particularly for English. The vast majority of distributional PoS induction systems over the past 15 years, those reporting “many-to-one” or cluster purity metrics, demonstrate this same result. The assertion in lines 79-80 that “relations between… vectors… are mostly semantic” is also inaccurate. Pioneering work by Mikolov et al. (2013) and Colobert et al. (2011) and subsequent studies have established that word embeddings encode significant syntactic information, a point that is further supported by the observed cluster purity scores. This claim is, in fact, seemingly contradicted later in the paper (lines 107-108) at the beginning of Section 2. 

The decision to transition to UPOS tags is also questionable. The finer-grained distinctions offered by the original tagset are potentially more insightful and deserving of analysis. Footnote 3 is unclear; it needs to specify whether the “failed attempts” referenced were conducted by the authors or reported in other works, and the criteria used to define failure. The inclusion of Brown cluster vectors as a baseline would also be valuable, as they exhibit a near-perfect alignment with UPOS tags.

The observation that “proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns” (lines 331-332) feels relatively self-evident. The prevalence of “the” – the most frequent function word in English – likely plays a substantial role in explaining this difference. While analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs offers practical advantages, it would be beneficial to extend the analysis to the long tail of the distribution, examining both the vector representations and the types of errors made by the classifier. Investigating these less frequent words could reveal opportunities for supplementing distributional (and morphological, given the lemmatization) features with alternative information sources to improve PoS generalization.

Finally, please revise the in-text citations to use `\newcite{}` consistently, for example, replacing “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed” with `\newcite{Mikolov2013b}`.
