- Paper studies how PoS tags relate to word embeddings by training classifiers to predict PoS and analyzing misclassifications to reveal tagset inconsistencies.
- Strength: Detailed error analysis of misclassified “outlier” words illuminates annotation ambiguities with concrete examples (currency, years, mis-tagged adjectives/adverbs).
- Strength: Empirical evidence shows embeddings encode PoS information, with consistent F-scores across BNC and UD datasets strengthening the claim.
- Strength: PoS information is distributed across many embedding components rather than concentrated in a few features.
- Weakness: Insufficient engagement with prior work on distributional PoS induction (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012), weakening novelty claims.
- Weakness: Framing is narrow—should consider whether embedding-derived distinctions improve downstream NLP tasks (e.g., parsing) to increase practical impact.
- Weakness: Some findings (embeddings contain PoS info; proper vs. common noun differences) are predictable and lack deeper novelty without additional analysis.
- Weakness: Converting BNC tags to UPOS is not well justified; fine-grained original tags may offer more analytical value.
- Question: How does the distributional PoS predictor compare to state-of-the-art taggers and affect parsing when used as preprocessing?
- Question: What exactly were the failed attempts mentioned in footnote 3—methods tried and failure modes?
- Question: Do the identified outlier words show similar distributional patterns across other corpora and languages?
- Question: How sensitive are results to embedding hyperparameters (window size, dimensionality)?
- Additional: Use \newcite{} for in-text citations to improve referencing.
- Additional: Further analysis of long-tail vocabulary errors could yield more insights.
- Additional: Incorporating morphological features alongside distributional vectors might improve PoS prediction robustness.
