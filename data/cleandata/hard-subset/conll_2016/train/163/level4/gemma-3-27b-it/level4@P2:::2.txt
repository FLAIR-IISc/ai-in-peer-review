This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors encode information about PoS affiliation and revealing inconsistencies in existing tagsets. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze classification errors to identify words with atypical distributional patterns. A key contribution of this work is a detailed analysis of these ‘outlier’ words, highlighting potential ambiguities and inconsistencies within the British National Corpus (BNC) annotation scheme. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components, rather than being localized to a few key features. Finally, the work provides a baseline for PoS prediction using only distributional information, offering insights into the robustness of such an approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is the detailed analysis of misclassified words. Identifying and categorizing these ‘outliers’ sheds light on the inherent difficulties in PoS tagging and reveals potential areas for improvement in existing annotation guidelines. The examples provided – currency amounts, years, adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs – are concrete and illustrative, providing compelling evidence for the paper’s claims.
2. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper successfully demonstrates that word embeddings capture information relevant to PoS. While not entirely novel (as discussed below), the empirical evidence presented through the classifier’s performance and the visualization of prototypical vectors effectively reinforces this point. The consistent F-scores across different datasets (BNC test set, Universal Dependencies Treebank) add to the robustness of this finding.
3. **Distribution of PoS Information:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components is interesting and challenges the assumption that PoS is encoded in only a few readily identifiable features. This nuanced understanding of how embeddings represent grammatical information is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Contextualization within Existing Literature:** The paper insufficiently acknowledges and engages with the substantial body of work on distributional PoS induction. Omitting key references, such as Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010), Blunsom and Cohn (2011), and Yatbaz et al. (2012), diminishes the novelty of the presented results. Specifically, the performance of the PoS predictor (Section 5) is not positioned effectively against prior work in the field.
2. **Limited Framing of the Core Research Question:** The paper frames the exploration of PoS and embeddings largely as a linguistic exercise. However, a more compelling framing would acknowledge that corpora are *created* for NLP tasks like parsing. The research question should then consider whether the distinctions revealed by distributional vectors offer improvements over existing tags for downstream NLP applications. Reframing the work this way would increase its practical impact and relevance.
3. **Triviality of Some Findings:** The claim in the abstract that distributional vectors “contain information about PoS affiliation” is a restatement of a well-established finding in the field. Similarly, the observation that proper nouns differ distributionally from common nouns is not particularly surprising and lacks significant insight without further analysis. The paper would benefit from more critical self-assessment of the novelty of its findings.
4. **Questionable Tagset Conversion:** The decision to convert to UPOS tags is not adequately justified. The fine-grained distinctions present in the original BNC tagset likely contain more valuable information for analysis. The rationale for simplification should be more thoroughly explained.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of your distributional PoS predictor compare to state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., those leveraging features beyond distributional information) when used as a pre-processing step for parsing?
2.  Could you elaborate on the failed attempts mentioned in footnote 3? What approaches were tried, and what specific issues led to their failure?
3.  Have you investigated whether the ‘outlier’ words identified in your analysis exhibit similar patterns across different corpora and languages?
4.  How do the results change if different embedding parameters (e.g., window size, vector dimensionality) are used?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider using `\newcite{}` for in-text citations.
*   Further exploration of the errors in the ‘long tail’ of the vocabulary could yield more interesting insights.
*   Investigating the incorporation of morphological features alongside distributional information might improve the robustness and accuracy of the PoS predictor.
