- Investigates how word embeddings trained on the BNC relate to part-of-speech (PoS) boundaries by training classifiers to predict PoS from embeddings.  
- Main contribution: strong empirical demonstration that distributional vectors encode PoS information and can reveal “outlier” words with atypical distributional patterns.  
- Secondary contribution: proposes using this approach to identify annotation inconsistencies and support notions of “soft” PoS affiliations.  
- Finds PoS information is distributed across many embedding components rather than concentrated in a few dimensions.  
- Strength: robust experiments on a large corpus with strong classifier performance and successful transfer to the UD Treebank (high F-scores reported).  
- Strength: detailed error analysis that uncovers blurred PoS boundaries and potential annotation issues (e.g., proper vs common nouns, nouns vs adjectives, numerals vs nouns).  
- Strength: demonstrates distributed nature of syntactic information in embeddings and provides useful visualizations of prototypical vectors.  
- Strength: clear, systematic presentation with reproducible methodology and use of the Universal Dependencies tagset.  
- Weakness: limited methodological novelty—uses existing embedding/classifier techniques without new algorithms.  
- Weakness: incomplete engagement with prior literature; several relevant papers (e.g., Lin et al. 2015; Ling et al. 2015; Plank et al. 2016) are missing.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on BNC annotation quality; the impact of annotation errors on results needs deeper exploration.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (vector size, window, negative sampling) and how choices affect findings.  
- Question: request for more details on experiments with different hyperparameter settings and their effects.  
- Question: ask how sensitive results are to the choice of training corpus and whether findings generalize across corpora.  
- Question: request elaboration on how BNC annotation inconsistencies were verified and their impact assessed.  
- Question: ask whether the results are expected to generalize to languages with different morphological and syntactic properties.  
- Additional: recommend stating the rationale for choosing the Universal Dependencies tagset.  
- Additional: suggest adding a table summarizing related work and clarifying how this paper differs.  
- Additional: figures (Figures 1 & 2) need clearer labels and descriptions.
