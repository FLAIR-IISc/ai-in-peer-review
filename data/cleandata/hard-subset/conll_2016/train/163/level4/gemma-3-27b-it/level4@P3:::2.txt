## Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional representations encode information about PoS and revealing inconsistencies within existing tagsets. The detailed error analysis, focusing on misclassified words, is a notable strength of the work. However, the paper lacks a crucial framing: the original motivation behind PoS tagging in corpora—facilitating downstream NLP tasks like parsing. Reframing the discussion around whether distributional distinctions improve parsing performance compared to existing tags (or Universal Dependencies, UPOS) would strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, the paper omits substantial related work in distributional PoS induction.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Word Embeddings:** The paper empirically validates that word embeddings, trained on a large corpus, contain information relevant to PoS, through a predictive classification task. While not entirely novel, this provides a concrete demonstration with a specific corpus and embedding setup.
2. **Error Analysis Revealing Tagset Inconsistencies:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words, termed “outliers,” identifies potential inconsistencies or areas of ambiguity in the BNC’s PoS annotation scheme. This is a valuable contribution, highlighting specific cases where distributional information conflicts with existing tags.
3. **Exploration of Distributed PoS Representation:** The finding that PoS information isn't localized to a few vector dimensions but is distributed across many components offers insight into how grammatical information is encoded within these models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The core strength of this paper lies in the thorough error analysis. By focusing on misclassifications, the authors uncover interesting cases where distributional semantics clashes with traditional PoS assignments. Examples like currency amounts being classified as numerals, and the misclassification of adverbs due to overlap with adjective contexts, point to genuinely interesting linguistic phenomena and potential tagging issues. This provides tangible evidence for refining annotation guidelines.
2. **Empirical Validation with a Standard Corpus:** The use of the British National Corpus (BNC) provides a solid foundation for the experiments. It's a well-established, widely-used resource, allowing for easier comparison with other work.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup:** The methodology is clearly explained, including details about the embedding model, training process, and classification approach. The weighting scheme to address class imbalance is a thoughtful addition.
4. **Demonstrated Predictive Power:** Achieving an F-score of 0.979 on the training set and 0.91 on unseen data demonstrates the effectiveness of using distributional vectors for PoS prediction, even without contextual information.
5. **Investigation of Feature Distribution:** The experiment analyzing the number of vector components required for accurate prediction provides valuable insight into how PoS information is represented within the embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Framing within NLP Task Performance:** The paper’s analysis is largely self-contained. It doesn’t connect the observed discrepancies to the ultimate goal of PoS tagging: improving performance on downstream NLP tasks like parsing. The question of whether distributional distinctions *improve* parsing compared to the original tags remains unanswered.
2. **Insufficient Related Work:** The paper overlooks a significant body of work on distributional PoS induction. The review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and subsequent work (Blunsom & Cohn, 2011; Yatbaz et al., 2012, and more recent work) should be discussed. This context is crucial for positioning the novelty of the findings, particularly those in Section 5.
3. **Understated Novelty:** The claim that distributional vectors “contain information about PoS affiliation” (Abstract) is a well-established result in the field. Similarly, the performance achieved in Section 5 is not significantly better than previous work, especially considering the restrictions applied. The paper needs to more carefully position its contributions in light of existing literature.
4. **Questionable Move to UPOS:** The justification for converting to the Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset isn’t entirely compelling. The fine-grained distinctions of the original BNC tagset might reveal more nuanced patterns. The benefits of UPOS for this specific analysis are not clearly articulated.
5. **Limited Exploration of Low-Frequency Words:** The analysis focuses primarily on frequent words. Examining the errors made on low-frequency words, and considering features beyond distributional and morphological information, could reveal further insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered evaluating whether using the distributional information to *revise* the BNC tags (for a subset of ambiguous words) improves performance on a downstream NLP task like parsing?
2.  Could you elaborate on the "failed attempts" mentioned in footnote 3? What methods were used, and why did they not perform well?
3.  How does the performance of your system compare to existing distributional PoS induction methods, particularly those employing more sophisticated techniques (e.g., neural networks)?
4.  What characteristics of the “outlier” words (beyond frequency) contribute to their misclassification? Are there specific morphological or semantic features that distinguish them?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Replace all instances of "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed" with `\newcite{Mikolov2013b}`.
*   Investigate the potential influence of function words (like "the") on the observed dissimilarity between proper and common nouns.
*   Consider presenting results on a held-out set with a different corpus than the BNC (e.g., a subset of the Penn Treebank) to assess the generalizability of the findings.
