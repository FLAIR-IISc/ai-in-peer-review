This paper investigates the extent to which distributional information captured in word vector models reflects part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors utilize a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, training word embeddings on this corpus. These embeddings are then used to train a logistic regression classifier to predict POS tags. The methodology includes evaluations on the original corpus, employing cross-validation, and generalization to other datasets. The results are presented clearly, accompanied by thorough discussion and analysis.

The paper is well-written and easy to understand. However, the primary weakness lies in its lack of novelty within the fields of Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Machine Learning (ML). The work consists of a straightforward experimental evaluation rather than the introduction of new NLP or ML techniques or concepts. While the results *are* interesting, specifically offering empirical support for the connection between distributional semantics and POS categories, this contribution feels more aligned with quantitative or empirical linguistics than with pushing the boundaries of NLP/ML research. Therefore, publication in a linguistics-focused venue would be particularly suitable. 

A significant omission is a more comprehensive review of existing literature. The paper would benefit from a more extensive citation of prior work on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings. Relevant studies include, but are not limited to, Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015); Ling et al. (2015); and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016). Addressing this gap would better contextualize the contribution of this work within the broader research landscape.
