The aim of this paper is to demonstrate that distributional information stored in word vector models contains information about part-of-speech (POS) labels. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and then employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic regression classifier to predict word POS. Evaluations are performed on the same corpus (using cross-validation) and on other corpora. The results are clearly presented, discussed, and analyzed in detail.

The paper is clear and well-written. The primary limitation of this work is its lack of novelty in terms of Natural Language Processing (NLP) or Machine Learning (ML). The authors describe a set of straightforward experiments without introducing new NLP or ML ideas or methods. While the results are interesting, providing empirical support for the notion of POS distinctions, this work might be more appropriately published in a quantitative or empirical linguistics venue.

Additionally, the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings should be cited more comprehensively (e.g., Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin 2015; Ling et al. 2015 [EMNLP]; Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg 2016).

**Contributions:**

I identify two key contributions of this work. First, the authors provide a robust empirical validation, using a well-established corpus and modern distributional semantic techniques, that word embeddings *do* encode POS information. This is a valuable confirmation of theoretical linguistic principles. Second, their error analysis is insightful, highlighting potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation scheme and identifying interesting boundary cases between POS categories. This analysis offers concrete suggestions for improving existing corpora and annotation strategies. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The classifier achieves a high accuracy (F-score of 0.979 on the training set, 0.91 on an unseen test set) demonstrating the effectiveness of word embeddings in capturing POS information. This provides strong evidence supporting the central hypothesis.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The thorough examination of misclassified words reveals potential annotation errors in the BNC and identifies linguistic nuances at POS boundaries. The specific examples provided (e.g., currency amounts being misclassified as numerals) are compelling and contribute to a deeper understanding of the challenges in POS annotation.
3. **Robustness Demonstrated Across Datasets:**  The model's consistent performance on both the BNC test set and the Universal Dependencies English Treebank indicates its generalizability beyond the training corpus. This strengthens the claim that the observed POS-related information isn't specific to the BNC.
4. **Insightful Visualization:** The visualization of prototypical vectors for conjunctions provides a compelling illustration of how distributional semantics can capture grammatical features. It reinforces the idea that specific vector components correlate with POS categories.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The methodology employed is relatively standard. The paper does not introduce any novel algorithms, techniques, or theoretical frameworks. It’s an application of existing methods to a specific problem.
2. **Limited Scope of Innovation:** While the error analysis is insightful, it largely confirms previously known issues with POS tagging and corpus annotation. The paper doesn't propose a solution to these issues, only identifies them.
3. **Insufficient Literature Review:** The existing literature on POS induction and the use of distributional semantics for POS tagging is not adequately reviewed. Important related work (e.g., Lin et al. 2015, Ling et al. 2015, Plank et al. 2016) is missing, which diminishes the paper's contribution within the broader research landscape.
4. **Dependence on Pre-existing Tags:** While demonstrating that embeddings *contain* POS information, the initial model training relies on gold-standard POS tags. A more compelling demonstration would involve a fully unsupervised approach or a low-resource scenario.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for using a logistic regression classifier? Were other classification methods considered, and if so, why was logistic regression chosen?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for applying this approach to resource-poor languages. Could you provide a more concrete example of how this would work in practice, and what challenges might be encountered?
3.  You mention the distribution of POS information across many vector components. Do you have any hypotheses about *why* this information is distributed in this way, rather than being concentrated in a few key dimensions?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Throughout the paper, “PoS” and “POS” are used interchangeably. Maintaining consistency would improve readability.
*   The anonymization note seems unnecessary for a review.
