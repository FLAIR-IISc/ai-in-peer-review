This paper investigates the extent to which distributional information captured in word vector models encodes information about part-of-speech (POS) tags. The authors leverage a lemmatized version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags. They train word embeddings on this corpus and subsequently utilize these vectors to train a logistic classifier for POS prediction. The evaluation is comprehensive, employing cross-validation on the training corpus alongside testing on external corpora. The results are presented clearly, with a thorough discussion and analysis.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, the primary weakness lies in its lack of novelty within the fields of Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Machine Learning (ML). The work essentially describes a set of well-established experiments – training embeddings and applying a classifier – without introducing any new NLP or ML techniques or methodologies. While the results are interesting, confirming an empirical link between distributional semantics and POS categories, this confirmation does not represent a significant advancement in the field. The findings provide valuable empirical support for the intuitive connection between a word’s usage and its grammatical role, and in this respect, the paper would be a suitable contribution to a quantitative or empirical linguistics venue. 

A crucial omission is a more extensive review of related work. The paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough discussion of existing literature on POS tagging and POS induction utilizing word embeddings. For example, the work of Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015), Ling et al. (2015, EMNLP), and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) should be cited and discussed to properly situate this work within the broader research landscape. Addressing this gap would demonstrate a clearer understanding of the existing body of knowledge and highlight how this contribution, even if incremental, adds to it.

Specifically, it would be helpful to discuss *how* this work differs from existing approaches. Does the lemmatization step introduce a unique perspective? Does the choice of the BNC as a corpus offer advantages or disadvantages compared to others used in similar studies? Providing answers to these questions would strengthen the paper's contribution.

In summary, while technically sound and clearly presented, the paper’s lack of novelty limits its impact within the core NLP/ML community. Its value is primarily empirical and linguistic. Addressing the missing related work, and framing the contribution in relation to existing research, would significantly improve the paper's overall standing.
