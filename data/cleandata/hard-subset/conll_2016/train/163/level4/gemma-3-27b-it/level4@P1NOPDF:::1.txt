The aim of this paper is to demonstrate that distributional information captured in word vector models contains information about part-of-speech (POS) labels. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and then employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier for POS prediction. Evaluations are performed both on the original corpus – using cross-validation – and on additional corpora. The results are clearly presented and thoroughly discussed and analyzed.

The paper is clear and well-written. However, its primary weakness is a lack of novelty in terms of NLP or machine learning. It describes a straightforward set of experiments without introducing any new NLP or ML ideas or methods. The results are interesting, insofar as they provide empirical support for the established notion of POS. In this respect, the paper is certainly worthy of publication in a (quantitative/empirical) linguistics venue.

Additionally, the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings should be cited more extensively. For instance, the work of Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015); Ling et al. (2015, EMNLP); and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) should be considered.
