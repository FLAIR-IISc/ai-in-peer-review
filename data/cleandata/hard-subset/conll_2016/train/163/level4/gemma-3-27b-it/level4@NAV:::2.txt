This paper investigates the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, demonstrating a connection—though one that is relatively intuitive—between distributional vector representations and PoS categories. A key strength of the work lies in its detailed error analysis, identifying outlier classifications. 

However, the paper overlooks a fundamental motivation for the PoS tagging of corpora like the BNC: to support downstream NLP tasks, particularly parsing. Consequently, the discussion would benefit from being reframed to evaluate whether the distinctions captured by distributional vectors offer advantages for parsing compared to traditional tags, or even UPOS. Furthermore, the paper lacks sufficient engagement with related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend including a review of Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) as a starting point, alongside more recent non-DNN approaches like those by Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). Considering this existing research, the results presented in Section 5 appear incremental, as systems with more limited external knowledge have achieved comparable performance.

Specifically, the claim in the abstract that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation" is not novel, particularly for English, as it has been consistently demonstrated by distributional PoS induction systems for the past 15 years. The statement on lines 79-80 asserting that “relations between…vectors…are mostly semantic” is inaccurate; work by Mikolov et al. (or Colobert et al.) and subsequent studies demonstrate substantial syntactic information within these vectors, a point also supported by cluster purity scores. This assertion is even contradicted later in the paper, in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108). 

I question the decision to transition to UPOS tags, as the more nuanced distinctions offered by the original tagset are arguably more informative. The purpose of footnote 3 is unclear; it should specify whether the described failed attempts were conducted by the authors or reported in other works, and clarify the failure criteria. Additionally, the near-perfect alignment of Brown cluster vectors with UPOS tags warrants consideration. 

The observation regarding the dissimilarity between proper and common nouns (lines 331-332) seems unremarkable, likely explained by the prevalence of the definite article "the." While analyzing frequent word/tag pairs is practical, exploring the behavior of less frequent words—both in vector space and in terms of classifier errors—would be valuable. This analysis could inspire the development of alternative features, beyond purely distributional and morphological cues, to improve generalization to low-frequency words.

Finally, please revise all sentential citations to utilize the \newcite{} command (e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”).
