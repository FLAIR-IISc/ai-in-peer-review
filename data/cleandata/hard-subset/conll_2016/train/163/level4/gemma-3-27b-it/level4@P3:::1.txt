## Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) boundaries. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags from word embeddings, demonstrating that distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation. This allows them to identify word groups with distributional patterns differing from other words of the same PoS, potentially revealing inconsistencies in annotation or supporting the notion of ‘soft’ PoS affiliations. They also find that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components, not limited to a few key features.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is a comprehensive empirical demonstration of the extent to which distributional semantic models capture information relating to traditional linguistic categories – specifically, part of speech. While the idea that embeddings reflect syntactic information isn’t novel, the detailed analysis presented here, particularly the identification of “outlier” words and the examination of vector component distribution, provides a valuable and robust validation of this concept. A secondary contribution lies in the application of this approach to potentially identify inconsistencies in existing PoS annotation schemes, offering a data-driven method for refining linguistic conventions. Finally, the work provides a useful exploration of the distributed nature of syntactic information within word embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Robust Empirical Validation:** The paper presents a thoroughly executed set of experiments using a sizable and well-established corpus (BNC). The consistent performance of the classifier, both on the training data and when transferred to the UD Treebank, provides strong evidence supporting the claim that word embeddings encode PoS information. The accuracy figures (97.9% weighted F-score on training data, 91% F-score on unseen BNC data, and 99% F-score on UD Treebank) are compelling.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. By focusing on the “outliers,” the authors uncover potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation and highlight nuanced cases where PoS boundaries are blurry (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives, numerals vs. nouns). This analysis goes beyond simply reporting overall accuracy and offers valuable linguistic insights.
3. **Investigation of Feature Distribution:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components, rather than concentrated in a few key dimensions, is noteworthy. This supports the idea that distributional semantics captures subtle linguistic relationships and contributes to a more holistic representation of word meaning and function. The visualization of prototypical vectors (Figures 1 & 2) is a helpful illustration of this.
4. **Clear and Systematic Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and clearly explains the methodology and results. The authors provide sufficient detail to allow for replication of their experiments. The consistent use of the Universal Dependencies tagset also aids clarity and comparability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** The core methodology – training word embeddings and using them to classify PoS tags – is not novel. The paper does not introduce any new algorithms or techniques in NLP or machine learning. The primary contribution lies in the application of existing methods and the insightful analysis of the results.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Literature:** While the paper mentions related work, it doesn’t sufficiently engage with the existing literature on PoS tagging and induction using word embeddings. Several relevant papers (e.g., Lin et al. 2015, Ling et al. 2015, Plank et al. 2016) are missing from the related work section. A more comprehensive literature review is needed to properly contextualize the work.
3. **Dependence on BNC Annotation:** The analysis relies heavily on the quality and consistency of the BNC annotation. While the authors acknowledge the automatic tagging process (CLAWS4 and Template Tagger), the potential impact of annotation errors on the results isn't fully explored. The discovery of inconsistencies *within* the BNC is valuable, but it’s also important to consider whether those inconsistencies are driving the results more than genuine linguistic phenomena.
4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper describes the hyperparameters used for training the embeddings, but doesn't explore the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings. A brief discussion of how different window sizes, embedding dimensions, or negative sampling rates might affect the findings would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of how the choice of hyperparameters (vector size, window size, negative samples) influenced the results? Did you experiment with different configurations, and if so, what were the outcomes?
2.  How sensitive are your findings to the choice of the corpus used for training the embeddings? Would you expect similar results if you trained the model on a different corpus with potentially different annotation guidelines?
3.  You mention discovering inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Could you elaborate on the process you used to verify these inconsistencies and assess their potential impact on your results?
4.  The paper focuses primarily on English. Do you anticipate that these findings would generalize to other languages with different morphological and syntactic structures?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of the Universal Dependencies tagset is commendable, but it would be helpful to explicitly state the rationale for choosing this tagset over others.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key related work and highlighting how this paper builds upon or differs from those studies.
*   The figures (Figures 1 & 2) could benefit from clearer labels and descriptions.
