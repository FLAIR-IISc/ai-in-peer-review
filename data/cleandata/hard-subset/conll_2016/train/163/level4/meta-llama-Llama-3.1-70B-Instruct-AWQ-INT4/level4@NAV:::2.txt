General comments:
This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech tags and word embeddings, yielding some notable (albeit somewhat intuitive) insights into the consistency of PoS tags and the clear connection between word vector representations and PoS. A significant strength of this paper is its detailed error analysis, particularly in the classification outliers.

However, a crucial aspect appears to be missing: the original purpose of creating and tagging corpora such as the BNC. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories (grounded in semantic prototype theory, e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were designed to facilitate NLP tasks, primarily parsing. This discussion could be reframed to examine whether distributional vectors offer more benefits to parsing compared to the original tags or UPOS.

Additionally, this paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-DNN studies, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this body of work, the results in section 5 are not particularly novel, as systems with more restrictions on external knowledge have achieved comparable results.

Specific issues:
The abstract claims that "distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation" as a contributed result. However, this finding is not new, especially for English, as many distributionally-based PoS induction systems have demonstrated similar results over the past 15 years.

The assertion in lines 79-80 that "relations between vectors are mostly semantic" is incorrect, as the work of Mikolov or Collobert (and subsequent studies) has shown that these vectors contain significant syntactic information. This is also evident in cluster purity scores. In fact, this statement is later contradicted in the beginning of section 2 (lines 107-108).

It is unclear why the authors opted to use UPOS instead of the original tagset, which offers more fine-grained distinctions.

Footnote 3 is unclear: were the failed attempts mentioned performed by the authors or other researchers? What criteria were used to determine failure? Additionally, Brown cluster vectors almost perfectly align with UPOS tags.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly interesting, as the frequency of the word "the" could largely explain this difference.

While the practical reasons for analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs are understood, it would be interesting to examine the tail in terms of vector representations and classification errors. This could lead to exploring alternative features that allow for better generalization of PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues:
Sentential references should be changed to \newcite{}: e.g., "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed".
