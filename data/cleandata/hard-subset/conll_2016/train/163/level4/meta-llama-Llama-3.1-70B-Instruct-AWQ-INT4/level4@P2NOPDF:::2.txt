General comments:

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, yielding some interesting, albeit somewhat straightforward, conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and their clear connection to word vector representations. A strong point of this paper is the detailed error analysis of outliers in classification.

However, the paper seems to be missing a critical main point: the original purpose of PoS tagging corpora such as the British National Corpus (BNC). Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, these corpora were created and tagged to facilitate further NLP tasks, primarily parsing. The discussion could be reframed to examine whether the distinctions made by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing compared to the original tags or Universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Additionally, the paper lacks a thorough review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-DNN work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this body of work, the results in Section 5 are barely novel, as systems with more restrictions on external knowledge have achieved comparable results.

Specific issues:

The abstract states that one of the contributed results is that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." However, this result is not new, especially for English, as every distributionally-based PoS induction system in the past 15 years has shown similar results.

The assertion in lines 79-80 that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic" is incorrect. Research by Mikolov and Collobert (and subsequent work) has demonstrated that there is a significant amount of syntactic information in these vectors. This statement is also contradicted in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

The decision to move to UPOS is unclear, as the fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are more interesting.

Footnote 3 is unclear. It would be helpful to specify whether the failed attempts mentioned were performed by the authors or other works, and what criteria were used to determine failure. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a discussion on Brown cluster vectors, which almost perfectly align with UPOS tags.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly interesting, as the existence of "the" (the most frequent function word) could largely explain this difference.

While analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs is practical, it would be interesting to explore what happens in the tail, both in terms of the vectors and the types of errors the classifier makes. The authors could then consider alternative features that would allow better generalizations of PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues:

The paper should change sentential references to \newcite{} format, for example, "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed".
