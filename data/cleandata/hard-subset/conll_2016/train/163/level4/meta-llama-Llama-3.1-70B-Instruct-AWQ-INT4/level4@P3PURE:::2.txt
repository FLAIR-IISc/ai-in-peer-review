General comments:
This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech tags and word embeddings, using the latter to draw interesting conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and their clear connection to word vector representations. The detailed error analysis, particularly the examination of outliers in classification, is a strong point of the paper.

However, the paper appears to be missing a critical main point: the original purpose of creating and tagging corpora such as the British National Corpus (BNC). Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, which are often grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were primarily designed to facilitate further NLP tasks, such as parsing. Reframing the discussion in this context could provide valuable insights into whether the distinctions made by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing than the original tags or Universal Part-of-Speech (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-deep neural network (DNN) work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this body of work, the results presented in Section 5 are not particularly novel, as there are systems with more restrictions on external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

Specific issues:
The abstract claims that one of the contributed results is that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." However, this finding is not new, especially for English, as every distributionally-based PoS induction system in the past 15 years has reported similar results, often in the form of "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" numbers.

The assertion in lines 79-80, stating that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic," is incorrect. The work of Mikolov et al. (and subsequent research) has shown that there is a significant amount of syntactic information in these vectors. This point is also contradicted in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

The decision to switch to UPOS tags is not clearly justified. The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are arguably more interesting and worthy of exploration.

Footnote 3 is unclear. It would be helpful to specify whether the failed attempts mentioned were conducted by the authors or other researchers, and what criteria were used to evaluate their failure. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a discussion of Brown cluster vectors, which have been shown to align closely with UPOS tags.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly insightful, as the presence of function words like "the" (the most frequent function word) could largely explain this difference.

While the analysis of the most frequent word/tag pairs is understandable from a practical perspective, it would be interesting to examine the tail of the distribution, both in terms of the vectors and the types of errors made by the classifier. This could involve exploring alternative features that would allow for better generalization of PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues:
The paper would benefit from adopting a consistent citation style, using \newcite{} for sentential references (e.g., "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed").
