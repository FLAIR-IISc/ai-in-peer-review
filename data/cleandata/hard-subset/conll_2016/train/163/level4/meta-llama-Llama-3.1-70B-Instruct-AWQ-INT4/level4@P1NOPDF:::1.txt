The paper aims to demonstrate that word vector models contain distributional information relevant to part-of-speech (POS) labels. To achieve this, the authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and subsequently employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier for predicting word POS. The evaluations are conducted on the same corpus using cross-validation, as well as on other corpora. The results are clearly presented, discussed, and analyzed in detail.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, the primary concern is that it does not introduce any novel concepts or methods in the fields of natural language processing (NLP) or machine learning (ML). The authors describe a set of straightforward experiments that, although interesting, do not contribute any new NLP or ML ideas. The results are indeed intriguing, as they provide empirical evidence supporting the notion of POS. In this regard, the paper is worthy of publication in a quantitative or empirical linguistic venue.

One notable omission is the lack of extensive citations from the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings. For instance, relevant works such as Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015), Ling et al. (2015) [EMNLP], and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) should be referenced to provide a more comprehensive context for the research.
