General comments:

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, using word embeddings to draw some interesting conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and their clear connection to word vector representations. The detailed error analysis, particularly the examination of outliers in classification, is a strong point of this paper.

However, the paper appears to be missing a critical main point: the original purpose of PoS tagging in corpora such as the British National Corpus (BNC). Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, which are often grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were created and tagged primarily to facilitate further natural language processing (NLP) tasks, such as parsing. The discussion could be reframed to consider whether the distinctions made by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing than the original tags (or Universal PoS tags).

Additionally, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-deep neural network (DNN) work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this body of work, the results presented in Section 5 are not particularly novel, as there are systems with more restrictions in terms of external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

Specific issues:

The abstract claims that one of the contributed results is that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." However, this is not a new result, especially for English, as every distributionally-based PoS induction system in the past 15 years that reports "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" numbers has shown the same result.

The assertion in lines 79-80, stating that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic," is incorrect. The work by Mikolov et al. (and subsequent research) demonstrates that there is a significant amount of syntactic information in these vectors. This is also evident in the discussion of cluster purity scores. In fact, this statement is later contradicted in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

It is unclear why the authors chose to move to Universal PoS tags (UPOS). The fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are more interesting and could provide more insightful results.

Footnote 3 is unclear. Were the failed attempts mentioned in this footnote performed by the authors or other researchers? What criteria were used to determine failure? Additionally, Brown cluster vectors almost perfectly align with UPOS tags, which is not discussed.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly interesting, as the existence of the most frequent function word "the" could largely explain this difference.

While the practical reasons for analyzing the most frequent word/tag pairs are understood, it would be interesting to explore what happens in the tail, both in terms of the vectors and the types of errors the classifier makes. This could involve imagining alternatives to pure distributional (and morphological, since lemmatization is used) features that would allow better generalization of PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues:

Change sentential references to \newcite{} format, for example, "\newcite{Mikolov et al. (2013b)} showed".
