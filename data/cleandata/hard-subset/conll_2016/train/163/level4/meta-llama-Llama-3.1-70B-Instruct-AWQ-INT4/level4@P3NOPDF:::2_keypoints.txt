- Paper shows a clear connection between PoS tags and word embeddings, and the detailed error/outlier analysis is a strong point.  
- Conclusions are interesting but somewhat straightforward and not particularly novel.  
- Missing discussion of the original purpose of PoS-annotated corpora (e.g., BNC) — they were created mainly to support NLP tasks like parsing.  
- Should investigate whether distributional vector distinctions are more beneficial to parsing than original or UPOS tags.  
- Lacks a comprehensive related-work review on distributional PoS induction; must cite Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and other non-DNN work (Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012).  
- Results in Section 5 are not novel given prior systems that achieve comparable performance with stronger restrictions.  
- Claim that “distributional vectors contain information about PoS affiliation” is not new for English and has been shown repeatedly in prior work.  
- Assertion that vector relations are “mostly semantic” is incorrect; previous work shows substantial syntactic information in embeddings.  
- The paper contradicts itself on the semantic vs. syntactic content of vectors (lines 79–80 vs. 107–108).  
- Rationale for switching to UPOS tags is unclear; fine-grained original tags may be more informative.  
- Footnote 3 is unclear about who attempted and failed and what failure criteria were used; needs clarification.  
- Should consider discussing Brown cluster vectors, which may align well with UPOS tags.  
- The observation that proper nouns differ from common nouns may be trivial (e.g., driven by high-frequency function words like “the”).  
- Recommend analyzing the long tail: behavior of low-frequency words, their vectors, and classifier error types; explore alternatives to pure distributional/morphological features.  
- Minor formatting/ citation issues: change sentential references to \newcite{} format (e.g., “Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed”).
