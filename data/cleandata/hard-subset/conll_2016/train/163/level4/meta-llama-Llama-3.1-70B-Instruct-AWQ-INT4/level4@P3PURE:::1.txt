This paper aims to demonstrate that distributional information stored in word vector models contains information about part-of-speech (POS) labels. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and then use the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier to predict word POS. Evaluations are performed on the same corpus using cross-validation, as well as on other corpora. The results are clearly presented, discussed, and analyzed in depth.

The paper is well-written and clear. However, the main issue with this paper is that it does not introduce any novel concepts or methods in the fields of natural language processing (NLP) or machine learning (ML). It describes a set of straightforward experiments that lack innovative NLP or ML ideas. Nevertheless, the results are interesting in that they provide empirical evidence for the notion of POS. In this regard, the paper is certainly worthy of publication in a quantitative or empirical linguistic venue.

On a separate note, the authors should provide a more extensive review of the literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings. For instance, relevant studies such as Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) should be cited.
