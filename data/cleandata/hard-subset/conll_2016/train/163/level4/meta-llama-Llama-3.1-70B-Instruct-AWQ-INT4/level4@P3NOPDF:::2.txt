This paper presents an exploration of the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, yielding some interesting, albeit somewhat straightforward, conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and the clear connection between word vector representations and PoS. The detailed error analysis, specifically the examination of outliers in classification, is a strong point of this paper.

However, the paper appears to be missing a critical main point: the original purpose of creating and annotating corpora such as the British National Corpus (BNC) with PoS tags. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, which are often grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were primarily designed to facilitate further natural language processing (NLP) tasks, particularly parsing. The discussion could be reframed to investigate whether the distinctions made by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing compared to the original tags or Universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with the review by Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-deep neural network (DNN) work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012), among others. In light of this body of work, the results presented in Section 5 are not particularly novel, as there are systems with more restrictions in terms of external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

Regarding specific issues, the abstract claims that one of the contributed results is that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation." However, this finding is not new, especially for English, as every distributionally-based PoS induction system in the past 15 years has presented similar results, often in the form of "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" numbers.

The assertion in lines 79-80, stating that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic," is incorrect. Previous work, including the paper by Mikolov et al. (and subsequent research), has demonstrated that there is a significant amount of syntactic information in these vectors. This is also evident in the cluster purity scores. In fact, the authors later contradict this statement in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

The decision to move to UPOS tags is unclear, as the fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are more interesting and potentially more informative.

Footnote 3 is unclear and requires further explanation. It is unclear whether the failed attempts mentioned were performed by the authors or other researchers, and what criteria were used to determine failure. Additionally, the authors may want to consider discussing Brown cluster vectors, which appear to align well with UPOS tags.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly interesting, as the existence of the most frequent function word "the" could largely explain this difference.

While the analysis of the most frequent word/tag pairs is understandable from a practical perspective, it would be interesting to explore what happens in the tail, both in terms of the vectors and the types of errors the classifier makes. This could involve imagining alternatives to pure distributional (and morphological, given the use of lemmatization) features that would allow better generalizations of the PoS tags to low-frequency words.

Minor issues include changing sentential references to \newcite{} format, such as "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed."
