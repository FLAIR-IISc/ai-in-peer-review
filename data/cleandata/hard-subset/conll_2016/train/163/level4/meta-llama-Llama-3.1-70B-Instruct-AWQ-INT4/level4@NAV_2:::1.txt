The paper explores the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus and part-of-speech boundaries, demonstrating that distributional semantic models contain information about parts of speech. The authors train a classifier to predict part-of-speech tags based on word embeddings and achieve high accuracy, showing that the information is distributed among several vector components.

Main contributions:
1. The paper provides empirical evidence that word embeddings contain information about parts of speech, supporting the notion of 'soft' or 'graded' part-of-speech affiliations.
2. The authors develop a method for detecting 'outlying' words with distributional patterns different from other words of the same part of speech, which can reveal inconsistencies in annotation processes or guidelines.
3. The study demonstrates the robustness of the classifier in predicting part-of-speech tags across different corpora, including the Universal Dependencies English Treebank.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a clear and well-structured argument, with thorough explanations and evidence supporting the claims.
2. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are clearly presented and discussed.
3. The study provides new insights into the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech boundaries, contributing to the understanding of linguistic phenomena.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not introduce new NLP or ML ideas or methods, relying on straightforward experiments and existing techniques.
2. The literature review could be more extensive, particularly in the area of POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings.
3. The study's findings, although interesting, may not be surprising to researchers familiar with the field, as they provide empirical grounding for existing notions.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do the authors plan to extend their study to other languages, and what challenges do they anticipate in applying their method to languages with different typological characteristics?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the correspondence between particular embedding components and part-of-speech affiliation, and how they plan to investigate this issue further?
3. How do the authors envision the application of their method in real-world scenarios, such as preliminary tagging of large corpora or improving the performance of existing POS taggers?
