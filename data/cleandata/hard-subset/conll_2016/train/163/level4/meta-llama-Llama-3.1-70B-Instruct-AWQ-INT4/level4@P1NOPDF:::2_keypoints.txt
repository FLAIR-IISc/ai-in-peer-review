- Strength: strong detailed error analysis and examination of outliers in PoS classification.  
- Missing framing: paper neglects the original purpose of PoS tagging (e.g., for parsing) and should assess whether distributional distinctions help parsing.  
- Related work gap: lacks comprehensive review of distributional PoS induction literature (e.g., Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010; Blunsom & Cohn 2011; Yatbaz et al. 2012) and ignores comparable prior systems.  
- Novelty concern: claim that distributional vectors encode PoS affiliation is not new for English given prior many-to-one/cluster purity results.  
- Incorrect claim: statement that vector relations are “mostly semantic” is false—vectors contain substantial syntactic information, and the paper contradicts itself on this.  
- UPOS choice unclear: moving to Universal PoS tags is not justified; fine-grained original tags could yield more informative analyses.  
- Footnote 3 ambiguity: unclear who performed the failed attempts, what failure criteria were, and omission of the strong alignment between Brown clusters and UPOS.  
- Trivial observation: difference between proper and common nouns may be driven by high-frequency function words (e.g., “the”) and is not especially insightful.  
- Frequency/tail analysis missing: paper focuses on frequent word/tag pairs but should analyze low-frequency tail items and classifier errors there.  
- Model features limitation: suggest exploring alternatives to pure distributional/morphological features to better generalize PoS to rare words.  
- Section 5 novelty: results are not particularly novel given prior systems with stricter external-knowledge constraints achieving comparable performance.  
- Minor issue: citation formatting should follow \newcite{} style for sentential references.
