This paper explores the interaction between word embeddings and part-of-speech (POS) boundaries, demonstrating that distributional semantic models can be used to discover new insights about grammar and syntax. The authors' primary contribution lies in their empirical analysis, which reveals that word embeddings contain information about POS classes. They also show that this information is distributed among several components of the word embeddings, rather than being concentrated in one or two specific features.

The authors' main contributions can be summarized as follows:

1. They demonstrate that word embeddings can be used to predict POS tags with high accuracy, even when the classifier is trained on a limited set of words.
2. They show that the information about POS affiliation is distributed among several components of the word embeddings, rather than being concentrated in one or two specific features.
3. They provide empirical evidence for the notion of "soft" or "graded" POS affiliations, highlighting the flexibility of POS boundaries in natural languages.

The strengths of this paper include:

1. The authors provide a clear and well-written presentation of their experiments and results, making it easy to follow their analysis.
2. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through evaluations on multiple corpora, including the British National Corpus and the Universal Dependencies Treebank.
3. Their analysis of the errors made by the classifier provides valuable insights into the nature of POS boundaries and the limitations of their approach.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

1. The authors do not present any new NLP or ML ideas or methods, relying on established techniques such as logistic regression and word embeddings.
2. The literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings is not cited extensively, which may limit the paper's impact and relevance to the broader NLP community.
3. The authors could have explored more thoroughly the implications of their findings for linguistic theory and the annotation of POS tags in corpora.

Questions to the authors:

1. How do the authors plan to extend their analysis to other languages, and what challenges do they anticipate in doing so?
2. Can the authors provide more details on the specific components of the word embeddings that are most relevant to POS affiliation, and how these components interact with each other?
3. How do the authors think their approach could be used to improve the annotation of POS tags in corpora, and what are the potential benefits and limitations of this approach?
