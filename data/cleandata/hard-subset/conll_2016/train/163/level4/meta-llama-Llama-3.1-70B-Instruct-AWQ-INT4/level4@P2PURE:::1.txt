The paper's primary objective is to demonstrate that distributional information stored in word vector models contains information about part-of-speech (POS) labels. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and then employ the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier for predicting word POS. The evaluations are conducted on the same corpus using cross-validation, as well as on other corpora. The results are clearly presented, discussed, and analyzed in detail.

The paper is well-written and clear. However, the main issue with this submission is that it does not contribute any novel ideas or methods in the fields of natural language processing (NLP) or machine learning (ML). The paper describes a set of straightforward experiments that, although well-executed, do not introduce any new concepts or approaches in NLP or ML. Nevertheless, the results are interesting, as they provide empirical evidence supporting the notion of POS. In this regard, the paper is certainly worthy of publication in a quantitative or empirical linguistic venue.

One notable omission is the lack of extensive citations to the existing literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings. For instance, relevant works such as Lin, Ammar, Duer, and Levin (2015), Ling et al. (2015), and Plank, Søgaard, and Goldberg (2016) should be included in the discussion.
