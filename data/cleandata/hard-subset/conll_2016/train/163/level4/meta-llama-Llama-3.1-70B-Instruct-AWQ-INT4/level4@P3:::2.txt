**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to analyze the consistency of PoS tags and the relationship between word vector representations and PoS. They train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and evaluate its performance on a test set. The results show that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation, and the classifier can detect 'outliers' that do not conform to traditional PoS patterns.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Distributional models for PoS analysis**: The authors demonstrate that distributional models can be used to analyze PoS tags and detect inconsistencies in annotation.
2. **Word embeddings as a feature for PoS prediction**: The authors show that word embeddings can be used as a feature for PoS prediction, and the classifier can achieve high accuracy.
3. **Error analysis and outlier detection**: The authors perform a detailed error analysis and detect outliers that do not conform to traditional PoS patterns.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to PoS analysis**: The authors propose a novel approach to PoS analysis using distributional models and word embeddings.
2. **Detailed error analysis**: The authors perform a detailed error analysis and detect outliers that do not conform to traditional PoS patterns.
3. **High accuracy of the classifier**: The authors show that the classifier can achieve high accuracy in predicting PoS tags.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of clear motivation**: The paper lacks a clear motivation for why corpora such as the BNC were PoS tagged in the first place.
2. **Missing related work**: The paper is missing a lot of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction.
3. **Novelty of the results**: The results of section 5 are barely novel, and there are systems with more restrictions in terms of their external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more context on why corpora such as the BNC were PoS tagged in the first place?
2. How do you plan to extend this work to other languages typologically different from English?
3. Can you provide more details on the failed attempts to cluster word vectors into the number of clusters equal to the number of PoS tags?

**Additional Comments**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work in the context of distributional PoS induction.
* The authors should consider adding more recent non-DNN work to the related work section.
* The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" is not particularly interesting, and the existence of "the" (the most frequent function word) almost singlehandedly explains this difference.
