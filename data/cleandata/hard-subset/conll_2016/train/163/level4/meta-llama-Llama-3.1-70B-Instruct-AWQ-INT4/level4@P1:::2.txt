**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the relationship between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to analyze the consistency of PoS tags and the connection between word vector representations and PoS. They train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and analyze the errors to identify outliers and inconsistencies in the annotation process. The paper also investigates the distribution of PoS information in word embeddings and its potential application in PoS tagging.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Analysis of PoS tags and word embeddings**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the relationship between PoS tags and word embeddings, highlighting the inconsistencies and outliers in the annotation process.
2. **Distribution of PoS information in word embeddings**: The authors show that PoS information is distributed among multiple components of word embeddings, rather than being concentrated in a single feature.
3. **Application of word embeddings in PoS tagging**: The paper demonstrates the potential of word embeddings in PoS tagging, particularly in identifying prototypical examples of PoS classes and detecting errors in annotation.

**Strengths**

1. **Detailed error analysis**: The paper provides a thorough analysis of the errors made by the classifier, which helps to identify inconsistencies and outliers in the annotation process.
2. **Insights into PoS boundaries**: The paper offers insights into the boundaries between different PoS classes, highlighting the complexity and nuance of linguistic categories.
3. **Potential application in PoS tagging**: The paper demonstrates the potential of word embeddings in PoS tagging, which could be useful in improving the accuracy of PoS taggers.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in results**: Some of the results, such as the finding that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation, are not new and have been reported in previous studies.
2. **Limited context**: The paper focuses primarily on the British National Corpus (BNC) and the Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset (UPOS), which may limit the generalizability of the results to other corpora and tagsets.
3. **Methodological issues**: The paper uses a simple logistic regression classifier, which may not be the most effective method for PoS tagging, particularly for low-frequency words.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the results of the paper relate to previous studies on distributional PoS induction, and what new insights do they offer?
2. Can the authors provide more information about the failed attempts to cluster word vectors into PoS classes, and what criteria were used to evaluate their success?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of low-frequency words, which may not be well-represented in the word embeddings?
4. Can the authors provide more details about the annotation process and the guidelines used to create the UPOS tagset?
5. How do the authors plan to extend the study to other languages and corpora, and what challenges do they anticipate in doing so?
