**Summary of the Paper**

The paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings. The authors use word embeddings to analyze the consistency of PoS tags and the relationship between word vector representations and PoS. They train a classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings and evaluate its performance on the British National Corpus (BNC) and the Universal Dependencies English Treebank.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper demonstrates that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation, which can be used to predict PoS tags with high accuracy.
2. The authors provide a detailed error analysis of the classifier's performance, highlighting cases where the classifier disagrees with the gold standard PoS tags.
3. The paper shows that distributional models can be used to detect inconsistencies and errors in PoS-tagged corpora.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a thorough analysis of the relationship between word embeddings and PoS tags, including a detailed error analysis.
2. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their results, making it easy to follow their arguments.
3. The paper highlights the potential of distributional models for detecting errors and inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks a clear motivation for why the connection between word embeddings and PoS tags is important for NLP tasks.
2. The authors do not provide a thorough review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction.
3. Some of the results, such as the observation that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation, are not novel and have been reported in previous work.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more context on why the connection between word embeddings and PoS tags is important for NLP tasks, and how this work contributes to the field?
2. How do you plan to extend this work to other languages and corpora?
3. Can you provide more details on the failed attempts to cluster word vectors into PoS tags, and how this relates to the current work?

**Additional Comments**

1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction.
2. The authors should consider analyzing the performance of the classifier on low-frequency words and exploring alternative features that could improve generalization to these words.
3. The paper could benefit from more visualizations and plots to illustrate the results, particularly in the error analysis section.
