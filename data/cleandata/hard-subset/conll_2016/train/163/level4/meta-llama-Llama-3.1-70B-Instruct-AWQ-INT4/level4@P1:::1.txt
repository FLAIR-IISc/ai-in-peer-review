The aim of this paper is to demonstrate that distributional information stored in word vector models contains information about part-of-speech (POS) labels. The authors utilize a version of the British National Corpus (BNC) annotated with Universal Dependencies (UD) POS tags, where words have been replaced by their lemmas. They train word embeddings on this corpus and then use the resulting vectors to train a logistic classifier to predict the word's POS.

The evaluations are performed on the same corpus (using cross-validation) as well as on other corpora. The results are clearly presented and discussed at length. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors show that distributional information in word vector models can be used to predict POS labels with high accuracy.
2. They demonstrate that the proposed approach can be used to identify words that do not conform to traditional POS categories, revealing inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines.
3. The authors also show that the proposed approach can be used to identify systematic errors in POS tagging.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a clear and well-written description of the experiments and results.
2. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, discussing the implications of their findings for our understanding of POS categories.
3. The paper demonstrates the potential of distributional semantics for linguistic analysis, highlighting the importance of considering the distributional properties of words in linguistic theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not present any new NLP or ML ideas or methods. The experiments described are straightforward and do not contribute to the development of new NLP or ML techniques.
2. The literature on POS tagging and POS induction using word embeddings is not cited extensively. The authors should consider including more references to relevant work in this area.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to extend this work to other languages, particularly those with different grammatical structures or linguistic properties?
2. Can the authors provide more information on the specific features of the word embeddings that are most relevant to POS prediction?
3. How do the authors plan to address the issue of inconsistencies in POS annotation, particularly in cases where the distributional model disagrees with the gold standard annotation?
