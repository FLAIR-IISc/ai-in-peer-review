General comments:
This paper explores the connection between part-of-speech (PoS) tags and word embeddings, using word embeddings to draw interesting conclusions about the consistency of PoS tags and their clear connection to word vector representations. A notable strength of the paper is its detailed error analysis, which provides valuable insights into the outliers of classification.

However, the paper appears to be missing a critical main point: the primary purpose of creating and tagging corpora such as the British National Corpus (BNC) was to facilitate further natural language processing (NLP) tasks, particularly parsing. Unlike purely linguistic explorations of morphosyntactic categories, which are often grounded in semantic prototype theory (e.g., Croft, 1991), these corpora were designed to support downstream NLP applications. The discussion could be reframed to focus on whether the distinctions made by distributional vectors are more beneficial to parsing than the original tags or Universal PoS (UPOS) tags.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in the context of distributional PoS induction. I recommend starting with Christodoulopoulos et al. (2010) and incorporating more recent non-deep neural network (DNN) work, including Blunsom and Cohn (2011) and Yatbaz et al. (2012). In light of this body of work, the results presented in Section 5 are not particularly novel, as there are systems with more restrictive external knowledge that achieve comparable results.

Specific issues:
The abstract claims that "distributional vectors do contain information about PoS affiliation" as one of the contributed results. However, this finding is not new, especially for English, as many distributionally-based PoS induction systems have reported similar results over the past 15 years, often in the form of "many-to-one" or "cluster purity" numbers.

The assertion in lines 79-80 that "relations between... vectors... are mostly semantic" is incorrect. Previous work, including the papers by Mikolov and Collobert, has shown that there is a significant amount of syntactic information in these vectors. This is also evident in the cluster purity scores reported in previous studies. In fact, the authors seem to contradict this statement in the beginning of Section 2 (lines 107-108).

It is unclear why the authors chose to move to UPOS tags, as the fine-grained distinctions of the original tagset are more interesting and potentially more informative.

Footnote 3 is unclear: were the failed attempts mentioned in the footnote performed by the authors or other researchers? What criteria were used to determine failure? Additionally, the authors do not discuss Brown cluster vectors, which have been shown to align well with UPOS tags.

The observation that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns" (lines 331-332) may not be particularly interesting, as the existence of high-frequency function words like "the" may largely explain this difference.

While the authors' decision to analyze the most frequent word/tag pairs is practical, it would be interesting to explore what happens in the tail of the distribution, both in terms of the vectors and the types of errors the classifier makes. This could involve imagining alternative features that would allow for better generalization of PoS tags to low-frequency words, moving beyond purely distributional and morphological features.

Minor issues:
The authors should change sentential references to \newcite{} format, as in "Mikolov et al. (2013b) showed".
