- Contribution: Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (at least ~100/300), not localized to a few features.  
- Contribution: Proposes a data-driven method to detect systematic PoS annotation inconsistencies in corpora.  
- Contribution: A classifier using only distributional features can predict PoS with ~84% accuracy.  

- Strength: Theoretical insight that PoS is encoded distributively in embeddings, with some dimensions more class-specific (e.g., one for conjunctions).  
- Strength: Practical corpus-quality application—analysis uncovers real annotation errors (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs).  
- Strength: Methodology validated across multiple setups (10-fold CV, unseen BNC, UD Treebank) and compared to a standard tagger.  

- Weakness: Novelty is overstated—similar results reported in prior work (Tsvetkov et al. 2015, Liu et al. 2016) and distinctions are not made clear.  
- Weakness: Methodological circularity—training on embeddings labeled with PoS (word_POS) risks learning tags from the training data rather than revealing inherent embedding information.  
- Weakness: Practical contribution limited—84% accuracy is far below supervised taggers (>97%), so utility and claims about minimal manual tagging are questionable.  

- Question: How do you address the circularity of using PoS-tagged inputs when claiming embeddings "contain" PoS information?  
- Question: Given lower performance than standard taggers, what practical advantages does this approach offer?  
- Question: How will this method generalize to morphologically rich languages with less clear PoS boundaries?  

- Overall: The paper offers an interesting theoretical and corpus-analysis contribution but is weakened by overstated novelty, circular methodology, and limited practical impact.
