

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates whether word embeddings contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze misclassifications to uncover potential annotation inconsistencies. The main contributions are:

1) Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components (at least 100 out of 300 dimensions) rather than concentrated in specific features, challenging the notion of discrete linguistic features mapping to specific vector dimensions.

2) Providing a data-driven method to identify systematic annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora, such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs or currency amounts misclassified as nouns.

3) Showing that a classifier trained solely on distributional features (without prior PoS information) can predict PoS tags with 84% accuracy, demonstrating the robustness of PoS information in embeddings.

## Strengths

1) **Theoretical contribution on distributed representation of PoS**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components (not concentrated in 1-2 features), which aligns with the fundamental principle of embedding models. The finding that component 94 is highly specific to conjunctions while other PoS classes require multiple dimensions provides concrete evidence supporting this claim. This insight has implications for understanding how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models.

2) **Practical application for corpus annotation**: The analysis of misclassifications reveals genuine annotation issues that would be difficult to detect through manual inspection alone. For instance, the paper identifies that 600+ instances of "plain" are incorrectly tagged as adverbs instead of adjectives. This demonstrates how distributional models can serve as a quality control tool for PoS-tagged corpora, potentially improving annotation consistency across different corpora.

3) **Robust methodology with multiple validation approaches**: The authors employ multiple validation strategies (10-fold cross-validation, testing on unseen BNC data, evaluation on UD Treebank), and compare with a standard PoS tagger (Stanford). The consistent performance across different datasets (97.9% F1 on BNC, 99% on UD Treebank) strengthens the validity of their findings.

## Weaknesses

1) **Overstated novelty**: The core finding that distributional models contain PoS information is not novel. Tsvetkov et al. (2015) and Liu et al. (2016) have already demonstrated similar results. The paper fails to adequately distinguish its contribution from these prior works, making the paper seem like a replication rather than a significant advance. The claim that "this approach allowed us to discover word groups with distributional patterns different from other words of the same part of speech" is largely a restatement of what others have shown.

2) **Methodological circularity in classifier training**: The paper trains a classifier using word embeddings where each word is represented with its PoS tag (e.g., "love_VERB"), then uses this to predict PoS tags. This creates a circular validation approach where the classifier is essentially learning to predict the tag used to create the training data. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address how it impacts their conclusions about "information about PoS being contained in the embeddings."

3) **Underwhelming practical contribution**: The PoS predictor achieving 84% accuracy on UD Treebank is significantly below state-of-the-art taggers (typically >97% accuracy). The paper doesn't adequately address why this is the case or how it compares to other unsupervised methods, making the practical utility of the approach questionable. The claim that "only a handful of non-ambiguous words need be manually PoS-tagged" is overstated given the accuracy gap with standard approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the methodological circularity in your classifier training (using PoS-tagged words as input) when claiming that the model "contains" PoS information, rather than simply learning to predict the tag used in training?

2. Given that the classifier's performance is substantially lower than standard supervised PoS taggers, what specific advantages does your approach offer over traditional taggers for practical NLP applications?

3. How do you plan to extend your work to languages with more complex morphological systems, where PoS boundaries might be even less clear-cut than in English?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to understanding how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models, particularly regarding the distributed nature of PoS information. The methodology is sound and the findings about annotation inconsistencies are valuable for corpus linguistics. However, the novelty is overstated, the practical contribution is limited by the classifier's performance, and the methodological circularity weakens the core claim. The paper would be strengthened by more substantial comparison with prior work, addressing the circularity issue, and providing a clearer path to practical application. While the findings are interesting, the contribution does not represent a significant advance beyond existing literature.
