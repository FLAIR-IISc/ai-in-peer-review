

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates whether word embeddings contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classification. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags based on embeddings from a Continuous Skipgram model trained on the British National Corpus. They analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" - words whose distributional patterns differ from their assigned PoS tags. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), not concentrated in a few features.
2. Using distributional models to identify systematic annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora, revealing inconsistencies in the BNC and Universal Dependencies Treebank.
3. Showing that a PoS classifier can be built with minimal manual annotation (only 1564 unambiguous words), achieving 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank test set.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a novel and valuable method for detecting systematic annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora. The authors demonstrate that their distributional classifier reveals different error patterns than traditional PoS taggers (like Stanford tagger), identifying specific inconsistencies such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs in BNC (e.g., "plain," "clear"). This is a significant contribution to corpus linguistics and NLP, as it offers a data-driven approach to improving PoS annotation guidelines.

2. The paper makes a strong empirical case for the distributed nature of PoS information in word embeddings. The authors systematically analyze how accuracy increases with the number of vector components used (Figure 3), showing a smooth progression from 0.38 accuracy with 2 components to nearly perfect performance with 100+ components. This directly challenges the assumption that linguistic features are concentrated in a few dimensions, providing a more nuanced understanding of how distributional models encode grammatical information.

3. The practical utility of the approach is well-demonstrated. The authors show that a PoS classifier can be trained with only 1564 unambiguous words from the UD Treebank (with minimal manual tagging), achieving 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank test set. This is particularly valuable for resource-poor languages where manual PoS tagging is expensive, as it suggests a pathway for preliminary tagging of large corpora with minimal human effort.

4. The methodology is well-justified and carefully executed. The authors provide clear details about their preprocessing steps, corpus choices, and evaluation methodology. The comparison with the Stanford PoS tagger provides strong evidence for the value of their approach, showing that different error patterns emerge when using distributional features versus traditional tagging methods.

5. The paper offers concrete examples of annotation inconsistencies that could lead to improvements in PoS tagging guidelines. For instance, the authors identify cases where "currency amounts and percentages" (e.g., "£70," "33%") are incorrectly tagged as nouns rather than numerals, which the UD Treebank handles more consistently by splitting them into separate tokens (NUM and SYM).

## Weaknesses

1. The paper's primary contribution (demonstrating distributed PoS information) is somewhat incremental. Previous work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already shown that dimensions of distributional models are correlated with linguistic features. The authors' main contribution is demonstrating this for PoS specifically, but this is not a substantial advancement over existing research.

2. The paper lacks comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers on the UD Treebank dataset. The claim that "the classifier showed an accuracy of 0.84" is not meaningful without comparison to current methods. The authors should provide this comparison to properly assess the practical significance of their approach.

3. The claim that "information about PoS is distributed among dozens of vector components" is somewhat overstated. The most significant jump in accuracy occurs between 2 and 3 components (from 0.38 to 0.51), suggesting that a few components might be sufficient for reasonable performance. The paper does not sufficiently address why the accuracy increases so gradually with more components.

4. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the distributional model's predictions are more accurate than the original annotations. The authors identify cases they claim are annotation errors, but they don't verify that these are indeed errors rather than cases where the distributional model is making mistakes. This weakens their argument that their method reveals "true" linguistic boundaries.

5. The evaluation is limited to English. While the authors mention future work with other languages, the current results don't demonstrate cross-lingual applicability, which would be a significant contribution. The lack of evaluation on multiple languages limits the generalizability of their claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you account for the significant jump in accuracy between 2 and 3 vector components (from 0.38 to 0.51)? This seems to contradict your claim that PoS information is distributed across dozens of components.

2. How does your classifier's 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank compare to state-of-the-art PoS taggers on this dataset? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of your results.

3. How did you verify that the cases you identified as annotation errors (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs) are indeed errors rather than cases where the distributional model is making mistakes?

4. The paper claims that "proper nouns are not much similar to common nouns, with cosine similarity between them only 0.67," yet the authors show in Figures 1 and 2 that conjunctions have a strong feature at component 94. What specific vector components distinguish proper nouns from common nouns?

5. The paper states that "distributional models trained in a nondeterministic and stochastic way on large amounts of word contexts learn knowledge about part of speech clusters." Can you elaborate on how the stochastic nature of training might affect the consistency of these PoS-related patterns across different runs of the model?
