

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train classifiers to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings from the British National Corpus (BNC), analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" that reveal inconsistencies in PoS annotation, and demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components rather than concentrated in specific features.

The main contributions, ranked by strength, are:

1. **Discovery that PoS information is distributed across many vector components**: The authors demonstrate through systematic analysis that PoS affiliation is not captured by a few key features but distributed across at least 100 components of the 300-dimensional word embeddings. This challenges assumptions about how linguistic features might be encoded in vector spaces.

2. **Method for identifying annotation inconsistencies**: The paper presents a novel approach to discovering inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora by training classifiers on distributional vectors and analyzing misclassifications. This reveals cases where words behave distributionally differently from their assigned PoS tags, suggesting possible annotation errors or "soft" boundaries between classes.

3. **Demonstration of PoS prediction without pre-existing tags**: The authors show that a PoS tagger can be built using only word embeddings and a small set of manually tagged words (1564 unambiguous lemmas), achieving 84% accuracy on the Universal Dependencies test set.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application to linguistic theory**: The paper makes a significant contribution to computational linguistics by demonstrating how distributional semantic models can provide empirical insights into the nature of part-of-speech boundaries. The findings directly address long-standing linguistic debates about whether PoS categories have "sharp" or "soft" boundaries (e.g., the observation that proper nouns are more similar to common nouns than to other proper nouns, but still distinguishable).

2. **Insights into annotation inconsistencies**: The analysis of misclassifications reveals concrete examples where the BNC annotation appears inconsistent or ambiguous (e.g., "white-collar" tagged as adjective but functioning as a noun, or "1804" tagged as numeral but functioning as a noun). These findings provide valuable evidence for revising annotation guidelines, particularly for cases where distributional evidence suggests a different tagging decision than the original annotation.

3. **Robust demonstration of distributed representation**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (not concentrated in one or two features), which aligns with theoretical expectations about how embedding models work. The experiments showing accuracy improves smoothly with more features (Figure 3) effectively demonstrate this point.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited linguistic interpretation of findings**: While the paper identifies many misclassification cases, it doesn't sufficiently engage with the linguistic literature on these phenomena. For example, the paper notes that "white-collar" is tagged as adjective but functions as a noun, but doesn't discuss how this relates to established linguistic theories of adjectival nouns or compound nouns in English. The paper would be stronger if it connected its findings to existing linguistic research.

2. **Insufficient comparison with established methods**: The paper claims their approach "reveals different error cases" than standard PoS taggers, but doesn't compare the magnitude of these differences or demonstrate how their method could be integrated with existing tagging pipelines. The 84% accuracy of their PoS predictor is not compared to state-of-the-art taggers (which typically exceed 97% accuracy), making it difficult to assess the practical utility of the approach.

3. **Lack of analysis of language-specific limitations**: The paper focuses exclusively on English (BNC and Universal Dependencies English Treebank). It doesn't address how their findings might generalize to languages with different grammatical structures, particularly those with richer morphology or different syntactic patterns. Without this analysis, the broader implications of the work are limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that "rare names" like "luftwaffe" and "stonehenge" are frequently misclassified. Could you elaborate on how this limitation might be addressed in practice, particularly for applications requiring robust PoS tagging of proper names?

2. The authors note that 27% of word types in the Universal Dependencies Treebank were missing from their distributional model. How would this limitation affect the practical implementation of their approach for low-resource languages where training data is scarce?

3. The paper states that "distributional models can actually detect outright errors in PoS-tagged corpora," but the examples given (like "plain" tagged as adverb) could be interpreted as annotation inconsistencies rather than clear errors. Could you clarify the distinction between annotation inconsistencies and actual tagging errors in your methodology?

4. The authors mention they plan to extend the work to "languages typologically different from English." What specific languages do you plan to target, and what preliminary evidence do you have that the approach would generalize to them?
