

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories, moving beyond their typical semantic applications. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components (at least 100 out of 300) rather than concentrated in specific features, (2) revealing annotation inconsistencies in the British National Corpus through outlier analysis, and (3) showing that a simple classifier trained on embeddings can predict PoS with high accuracy (F1=0.979), enabling systematic exploration of PoS boundaries.

Strengths: First, the paper provides compelling evidence that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings, challenging the common assumption that linguistic features are encoded in specific vector components. Second, the outlier analysis successfully identifies meaningful annotation inconsistencies (e.g., proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, adjectives erroneously tagged as adverbs), demonstrating the approach's utility for corpus quality assessment. Third, the cross-corpus validation on Universal Dependencies Treebank confirms the robustness of the findings beyond the BNC.

Weaknesses: The paper's approach to identifying annotation errors may be circular since the classifier is trained on the same corpus it's analyzing, potentially reinforcing existing biases rather than revealing novel insights. The PoS prediction task, while demonstrating distributional information exists, uses a simple logistic regression classifier that is not competitive with modern taggers (F1=0.84 vs. state-of-the-art systems achieving >97%), making the practical contribution less compelling. The paper fails to adequately compare its error analysis method with standard approaches (e.g., confusion matrices from traditional taggers), limiting the assessment of its novelty.

Questions to Authors: How do you distinguish between genuine linguistic phenomena and artifacts of the embedding training process? What specific annotation guidelines would you recommend changing based on your findings, and how would you implement these changes in large-scale corpus creation?
