

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) categories by training classifiers to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings from the British National Corpus (BNC). The authors demonstrate that word embeddings contain significant information about PoS affiliation, allowing for accurate prediction (97.9% F-score). Their main contributions are:

1. **Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions** (at least 100 out of 300) rather than concentrated in a few features, challenging assumptions about how linguistic information is encoded in embeddings.

2. **Providing a practical method for identifying potential inconsistencies in PoS annotation** by analyzing classifier "outliers" (words misclassified by the distributional model), revealing cases where words exhibit distributional patterns more consistent with another PoS category than their assigned tag.

3. **Supporting the notion of 'soft' or 'graded' PoS boundaries** by showing that many words have distributional patterns overlapping with multiple PoS categories, rather than fitting neatly into one category.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical demonstration of distributed PoS information**: The paper provides compelling evidence that PoS information is not concentrated in a few vector dimensions but distributed across many. Their analysis of classifier accuracy as a function of the number of vector components used (Figure 3) clearly shows this pattern. This finding has important theoretical implications for how we understand the relationship between semantic and grammatical information in distributional models, and it aligns with the fundamental principles of distributional semantics.

2. **Practical contribution to corpus linguistics**: The authors demonstrate how distributional models can identify systematic errors in PoS annotation (e.g., adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs), which has direct utility for corpus linguists. Their analysis of specific error cases (like "white-collar_ADJ" being classified as a noun) provides concrete examples of annotation inconsistencies that could be addressed in future corpus development.

3. **Robust evaluation across multiple datasets**: The paper tests the approach on both the BNC and Universal Dependencies Treebank, showing consistent results. The classifier achieves 91% F-score on unseen BNC data and 99% on UD Treebank, demonstrating the approach's generalizability. This multi-dataset validation strengthens the paper's claims significantly.

## Weaknesses

1. **Circular methodology in identifying annotation errors**: The paper trains a classifier on word vectors concatenated with PoS tags, creating a circular dependency. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address how it affects their interpretation of "outliers" as evidence of annotation inconsistencies. For example, if a word is incorrectly tagged in the BNC, the classifier might learn to associate the wrong tag with the word's distributional pattern, potentially misidentifying annotation errors as natural variation. This undermines the paper's strongest claim about identifying true annotation errors.

2. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The paper claims its approach is useful for PoS analysis but fails to compare directly with modern PoS taggers that achieve >97% accuracy (e.g., neural models). The classifier's 84% accuracy on UD Treebank (without contextual information) is presented as "impressive" but doesn't contextualize how it compares to existing methods. This weakens the paper's contribution to the field of PoS tagging.

3. **Overstated novelty**: The paper builds on prior work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015) that has already established connections between distributional models and linguistic features. The main contribution is an application of existing techniques to PoS analysis rather than a novel methodological insight. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly situated its work within this existing literature and articulated precisely what new insights it provides.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the circularity in your methodology when interpreting "outliers" as evidence of annotation inconsistencies? Would a classifier trained on word vectors alone (without PoS tags) produce similar error patterns, or would this fundamentally change your findings?

2. The paper claims "information about PoS is distributed among dozens of vector components." However, Figure 3 shows that accuracy increases smoothly with the number of components used, with no clear plateau. How would you define "dozens" in this context, and what evidence supports the claim that PoS information is distributed across "dozens" rather than "many"?

3. The paper notes that proper nouns are often misclassified as common nouns, but 88% of proper nouns were correctly classified. How would you explain this apparent contradiction, and what does it say about the distributional properties of proper nouns versus common nouns?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between semantic and grammatical information in distributional models, particularly through its demonstration of distributed PoS information. While the methodology has limitations, the empirical findings are compelling and have practical value for corpus linguistics. With clarification of the circularity issue and better comparison to existing PoS tagging approaches, this work could make a stronger contribution to the field.
