## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The core idea is that distributional semantic models implicitly encode information about PoS, allowing for the identification of words with atypical distributional patterns within their assigned PoS class – potentially revealing annotation inconsistencies or highlighting the ‘soft’ boundaries between categories. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze the errors to uncover these linguistic nuances.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the demonstration and detailed analysis of inconsistencies revealed *within* a well-established, automatically tagged corpus (BNC) using distributional semantic models. While the idea of a relationship between word embeddings and PoS is not novel, the systematic error analysis, revealing specific cases like mis-tagged adjectives functioning as adverbs and currency amounts erroneously labeled as nouns, is significant. A secondary contribution is the finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components, challenging the notion of a few key features defining each tag. Finally, the confirmation that even without contextual information, a reasonably accurate PoS classifier can be trained solely from distributional vectors is a valuable result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Significance of Error Analysis:** The paper doesn’t simply *show* that embeddings contain PoS information, but actively uses them to pinpoint potential annotation errors in a widely used corpus. This is a concrete application with implications for corpus linguistics and NLP resource creation. The detailed examples provided are compelling and illustrate the method's utility.
2. **Robustness & External Validation:** The classifier’s strong performance on both the training data and a separate test set (including the UD Treebank) demonstrates the robustness of the approach. Testing on the UD Treebank provides valuable validation beyond the initial BNC corpus.
3. **Detailed Investigation of Vector Representation:** The analysis showing that PoS information is not concentrated in a few vector dimensions is interesting and challenges simplistic interpretations of word embeddings. This contributes to a deeper understanding of how grammatical information is represented in these models.
4. **Clear and Reproducible Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the data processing steps, model training parameters, and evaluation metrics, making the research readily reproducible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** The central premise – that word embeddings encode syntactic information – is well-established in the literature (referenced in the paper). The novelty lies primarily in the application and detailed analysis, rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Focus on English Specifics:** The findings are heavily tied to the specific characteristics of the English language and the BNC corpus. The generalizability to other languages with different grammatical structures and annotation conventions remains unclear.
3. **Superficial Discussion of Annotation Guidelines:** While the paper identifies potential annotation errors, it doesn't delve deeply into the rationale behind the original tagging decisions, or offer a robust argument for revising the annotation guidelines. It would be strengthened by a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs inherent in PoS tagging.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the manual mapping process between BNC tags and the Universal Tagset? Were any systematic inconsistencies encountered during this process?
2.  How sensitive is the error analysis to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling)? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis to assess this?
3.  Given the observation that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, do you see potential for using dimensionality reduction techniques to improve the interpretability of the embeddings and potentially enhance classification performance?
