This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, investigating whether distributional semantic models can reveal insights into PoS boundaries and inconsistencies in annotation. The core idea is to train a PoS predictor based on word embeddings and analyze its errors to identify words with atypical distributional patterns for their assigned PoS.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Word Embeddings:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that word embeddings trained on a large corpus (BNC) encode information about PoS affiliation. The high accuracy achieved by the PoS predictor (97.9% F-score on training data, 91% on unseen data) supports this claim. This isn't a fundamentally novel finding given prior work (acknowledged in the related work section), but the robust empirical validation strengthens the argument.
2. **Error Analysis for Linguistic Insight:** The most significant contribution lies in the systematic error analysis. By identifying words misclassified by the predictor, the authors uncover potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation guidelines, particularly regarding the boundary between nouns/adjectives, numerals/nouns, and adverbs/nouns. This suggests a methodology for refining annotation schemes based on distributional evidence.
3. **Distribution of PoS Information across Vector Dimensions:** The finding that PoS information isn’t localized to a few vector components but is spread across many (at least 100) is interesting. This reinforces the idea that distributional semantics captures nuanced relationships that aren't easily represented by a limited set of features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation using a standard corpus (BNC) and a well-established tagset (Universal POS). The use of 10-fold cross-validation and evaluation on both the training corpus and a separate test set (UD Treebank) lends credibility to the results.
2. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation, and the methodology is described in detail. The error analysis is particularly well-presented, with specific examples and insightful interpretations.
3. **Practical Implications:** The methodology presented—using distributional models to identify potential annotation errors—has practical implications for corpus linguistics and NLP tasks that rely on accurate PoS tagging.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags were released online, increasing the potential for reproducibility. This is a commendable practice.
5. **Exploration of Vector Space:** The investigation into the distribution of PoS information across the vector space adds a valuable layer to the analysis, moving beyond simply demonstrating the presence of PoS information to understanding *how* it’s represented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the empirical validation is strong, the core idea of using distributional semantics to analyze PoS boundaries isn't entirely novel. Prior work (cited in the paper) already established a link between word embeddings and PoS tags. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new technique.
2. **Reliance on BNC Annotation:** The error analysis heavily relies on the assumption that the UD Treebank provides a more accurate “gold standard” for comparison. While UD Treebank is generally considered high-quality, it's still a human-annotated resource with potential biases. A stronger argument could be made by comparing the results against multiple annotated corpora.
3. **Superficial Discussion of Error Types:** While the error analysis identifies several interesting cases, the discussion remains somewhat superficial. For example, the explanation for misclassifications of numerals as nouns (currency amounts) could be expanded to discuss the semantic ambiguity of these terms and the challenges they pose for PoS tagging. The observation that errors often related to annotation inconsistencies would benefit from deeper analysis of the original BNC annotation guidelines.
4. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameters:** The paper briefly mentions the potential for exploring different hyperparameters for training the distributional models but doesn’t present any results from such experiments. Investigating the impact of vector size, window size, and training algorithm could provide further insights.
5. **Minor - Presentation of Tables:** Table 2 is slightly difficult to interpret. Presenting the errors as percentages of *all* words, rather than just the words of a specific PoS, would make it more intuitive.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Did you consider using more recent and larger pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe, Word2Vec trained on larger corpora) instead of training your own model on the BNC? How might this have affected the results?
2. Could you provide more detail about the manual mapping process between BNC tags and Universal tags? What criteria were used to resolve discrepancies?
3. The paper highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Did you investigate whether these inconsistencies are specific to the BNC or are also present in other corpora?
4.  You mention that the classifier "knows absolutely nothing about a word’s context in the current sentence."  Would incorporating contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo) significantly improve the performance of the PoS predictor, even without considering the surrounding words in a traditional tagging pipeline?
5. Were you surprised by the extent to which PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components, or did this align with your prior expectations based on the theoretical properties of distributional models?
