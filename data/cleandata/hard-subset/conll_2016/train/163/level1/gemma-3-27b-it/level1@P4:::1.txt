This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, investigating whether distributional semantics can reveal insights into PoS boundaries and inconsistencies in annotation. The core idea is to train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings and then analyze errors to identify words with atypical distributional patterns. The authors claim contributions in demonstrating that word embeddings contain PoS information, identifying annotation inconsistencies, and suggesting a distributed representation of PoS features.

**Contributions:**

1. **Demonstration of PoS information in word embeddings:** The authors successfully trained a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings with high accuracy, establishing that distributional semantic models capture information related to grammatical categories. This is the strongest contribution, providing empirical evidence supporting the link between semantics and syntax.
2. **Identification of potential annotation inconsistencies:** Analysis of classifier errors revealed cases where distributional patterns suggest a different PoS tag than the one assigned in the BNC corpus, hinting at potential annotation errors or ambiguities. This offers a novel approach to corpus evaluation and refinement.
3. **Evidence for a distributed representation of PoS features:** The finding that PoS information is spread across numerous vector components, rather than concentrated in a few, supports the idea that PoS is not simply a discrete feature but is encoded in a complex, distributed manner within embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The high accuracy of the PoS classifier (F-score of 0.979 on the training set and 0.91 on unseen data) strongly supports the central claim that word embeddings encode PoS information. This is a clear and quantifiable result.
2. **Novel Error Analysis Approach:** The method of using classifier errors to identify potential annotation issues is a clever and insightful application of distributional semantics. This provides a practical way to improve corpus quality.
3. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors conducted experiments with both the BNC and the UD Treebank, demonstrating the robustness and generalizability of their findings. This lends credibility to the conclusions.
4. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a logical progression of ideas and clear explanations of the methodology.
5. **Quantified Insights:** The analysis extends beyond simply identifying errors; the authors quantify the coverage of different error types and analyze patterns in misclassifications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The notion that distributional semantics can capture syntactic information is not entirely new. Previous work (cited in the paper) has already established this link. The novelty lies in the specific application to error analysis and the detailed investigation of distributed representations. However, the core idea is evolutionary rather than revolutionary.
2. **Dependence on Existing Annotations:** The error analysis is inherently dependent on the quality of the BNC annotations. While the authors acknowledge the precision of the BNC tagger, it is still possible that the identified "errors" are genuine ambiguities or stylistic choices, rather than actual mistakes.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Tagging:** The authors briefly mention that their results don’t match modern taggers, but don’t provide a direct comparison in terms of performance metrics. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art PoS taggers would strengthen the evaluation and put the findings into context.
4. **Limited Exploration of Vector Components:** While the authors demonstrate that PoS information is distributed, the analysis of specific vector components feels somewhat superficial. A more in-depth investigation of which dimensions correlate with specific PoS features would be valuable.
5. **Unclear Implications for Annotation Guidelines:** The paper identifies potential inconsistencies, but doesn't offer specific recommendations for revising annotation guidelines. A more concrete discussion of how the findings could inform best practices would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the selection criteria for the "unambiguous" lemmas used to train the second classifier? How sensitive are the results to this selection process?
2.  The analysis of vector components suggests a distributed representation of PoS. Can you provide more details on the specific features captured by the identified components (e.g., 31, 51, and 11)?
3.  What steps were taken to mitigate the impact of potential errors in the BNC annotations during the error analysis? Did you manually review a subset of the identified errors to verify their validity?
4. How do the results change when different embedding parameters are used (e.g., different vector sizes, window sizes, or negative sampling rates)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the references section is inconsistent.
*   The anonymity of the corpus source is understandable but somewhat hinders reproducibility. Consider providing details sufficient for others to obtain similar data.
*   The phrase "\Q2008\E" appearing throughout the reference section seems to be an artifact of the formatting.
