This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors demonstrate that word embeddings capture information about PoS, allowing for reasonably accurate PoS prediction and, more interestingly, the identification of words with distributional patterns atypical for their assigned PoS tag. The primary contribution lies in the analysis of these “outliers” as potential indicators of annotation inconsistencies or nuanced linguistic phenomena related to flexible PoS boundaries. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the demonstration that PoS information isn’t localized to a few vector dimensions, but is distributed across many.

**Strengths:**

1. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is the strongest aspect of the paper. Identifying currency amounts tagged as nouns and adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs (specifically 'plain', 'clear', 'sharp') reveals potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation and highlights the inherent ambiguity in certain linguistic constructions. This type of investigation is valuable for corpus linguistics and could inform improvements to tagging guidelines. The authors thoughtfully connect these errors back to broader linguistic observations.
2. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper convincingly shows that word embeddings aren't merely semantic representations; they also encode syntactic information about PoS. Achieving a 97.9% F-score on a PoS prediction task using embeddings is a strong indicator. This supports the idea that distributional semantics can be a useful tool for grammatical analysis. Furthermore, the robustness demonstrated via testing on the UD Treebank strengthens this claim.
3. **Distributed Representation Finding:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector dimensions is interesting and counters any simplistic notion that specific dimensions neatly correspond to specific grammatical features. This reinforces the complex and holistic nature of distributional representations. While not revolutionary, it’s a useful empirical observation.
4. **Practical Application Potential:** The authors hint at the potential for using this approach for low-resource languages, where manually tagged data is scarce. The ability to train a reasonable PoS tagger with a relatively small number of manually annotated examples is a promising avenue for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Finding:** The observation that word embeddings encode PoS information is not entirely novel. Prior work (cited by the authors) has already established this connection. The paper doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art in embedding methodology or PoS prediction; its strength lies in the *analysis* of the resulting predictions, not the prediction itself.  The claim of "redefining" PoS classes is overstated.
2. **Classifier Setup and Baselines:** The choice of a logistic regression classifier, while straightforward, feels somewhat rudimentary given the wealth of more sophisticated classification methods available. While the authors demonstrate improvement over a majority-class baseline and a single-feature baseline, comparisons to more competitive PoS tagging models (e.g., BiLSTMs, transformers) would provide a better sense of the model's performance and limitations. The justification for weighting training instances by corpus frequency is sound, but a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of this weighting on the model's generalizability would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Quantitative Analysis of Annotation Inconsistencies:** While the authors identify potential annotation errors, they don’t provide a quantitative assessment of their prevalence or systematicity. A more rigorous analysis, perhaps involving manual validation of a sample of misclassified words, would strengthen the claims about annotation quality. The observation that the UD treebank has fewer of these errors is interesting but not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Limited Exploration of Vector Components:** The authors briefly touch upon the analysis of vector components, but this aspect feels underdeveloped. While Figure 3 shows a diminishing return in accuracy with increasing dimensions, a deeper exploration of *which* dimensions contribute most to PoS discrimination for specific tags (beyond just component 94 for conjunctions) would be valuable. The mention of ANOVA F-value is promising, but this analysis requires more detail.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific criteria used to identify "unambiguous" lemmas from the UD Treebank for training the PoS predictor? Were any filters applied beyond the frequency threshold and the dominant tag rule?
2. You mention potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Did you consider performing a manual validation of a random sample of misclassified words to quantify the frequency of genuine annotation errors versus legitimate linguistic ambiguity?
3. Regarding the distributed representation of PoS information: Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the dimensions that were most significantly correlated with specific PoS tags, and what linguistic features might be captured by those dimensions?
4. How sensitive are your results to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling)? Did you perform any ablation studies to assess the impact of these parameters on PoS prediction accuracy and the identification of outliers?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from clearer visualisations of the prototypical vectors (beyond Figures 1 & 2). Perhaps a PCA plot showing the distribution of vectors for different PoS tags.
*  The discussion of related work could be slightly more comprehensive, particularly regarding recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) and their impact on PoS tagging.
*  The formatting of the references is inconsistent.
