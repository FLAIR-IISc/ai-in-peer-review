This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags, aiming to refine PoS class definitions and detect inconsistencies in annotation. The primary contribution is a demonstration that distributional semantic models, specifically word embeddings, *do* encode information about PoS affiliation. A secondary contribution lies in the identification of lexical outliers – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned PoS – revealing potential annotation errors or nuanced linguistic behavior. Finally, the authors present a PoS predictor based solely on word embeddings, showcasing the feasibility of such an approach without relying on contextual information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Validation of a Hypothesis:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that word embeddings capture PoS information. The logistic regression classifier achieving high accuracy (97.9% on training data, 91% on unseen test data) provides strong evidence for this claim. This is significant as it supports the idea that distributional semantics can offer insights into syntactic categories, bridging the gap between semantic and syntactic representations. The use of both a training and separate test set strengthens this argument.
2. **Error Analysis & Identification of Annotation Issues:** The detailed error analysis is a standout feature. By focusing on misclassified words, the authors uncover potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation, particularly regarding numerals, adjectives/adverbs, and proper/common nouns. This is valuable for corpus linguistics, as it highlights areas requiring further scrutiny and potential correction, and provides supporting evidence for claims of ‘soft’ or ‘graded’ PoS affiliations.  The comparison to the Universal Dependencies Treebank further validates these findings.
3. **Feasibility of Embedding-Based PoS Prediction:** The successful construction of a PoS predictor *without* relying on context is noteworthy. Achieving 84% accuracy solely based on distributional vectors is a testament to the richness of information contained within the embeddings.  The demonstration of reasonably good performance with limited labeled data (prototypical words from UD Treebank) offers a promising direction for resource-poor languages. The analysis regarding the number of components needed for reasonable performance is insightful.
4. **Robustness and Validation:** Testing the classifier on a different dataset (UD Treebank) demonstrates the robustness of the approach and suggests generalizability beyond the BNC corpus. This makes the findings more convincing.
5. **Clear Presentation and Logical Structure:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively motivate their work, present their methods, and discuss their results in a logical and coherent manner. The reference to historical work (Houston, 1985) grounds the investigation in existing linguistic discussions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the findings are valuable, the core method – training word embeddings and using them for classification – is not novel in itself. The application to PoS analysis, and particularly the error analysis revealing annotation issues, is where the paper’s contribution lies, but the underlying technique is well-established.  This isn't a critical flaw, but diminishes the perceived impact.
2. **Classifier Simplification:** The use of logistic regression as the classifier is a pragmatic choice for initial exploration, but it's a relatively simple model. Exploring more sophisticated classification algorithms (e.g., neural networks) could potentially yield higher accuracy and uncover more subtle relationships between embeddings and PoS. The rationale for not exploring these more complex models could be strengthened.
3. **Focus on English:** The study is limited to the English language. While the findings are interesting, it is unclear to what extent they generalize to other languages with different morphological and syntactic structures. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but further discussion on potential cross-linguistic differences would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Quantitative Error Comparison:** The authors compare errors to the UD Treebank, which is good. However, a more direct quantitative comparison of the types and frequency of errors made by the embedding-based classifier *versus* a standard PoS tagger (e.g., Stanford tagger, even beyond the initial mentions) would strengthen the analysis. Simply stating the Stanford Tagger finds different errors is not a sufficient comparison.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing a vector size of 300 for the word embeddings? Did you experiment with different vector sizes, and if so, how did they affect the results?
2.  You mention the importance of weighting training instances by corpus frequency. Could you provide more details on the impact of this weighting scheme? How did the classifier's performance change when using unweighted instances?
3.  The error analysis highlighted inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you considered contacting the BNC maintainers to report these findings?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with training the distributional model and the classifier? This is particularly relevant given the potential application to resource-poor languages.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are helpful and well-presented.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The discussion of related work is adequate, but could benefit from including more recent advances in distributional semantics and PoS tagging.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed study. The findings are interesting and contribute to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and syntactic categories. While the core method is not novel, the insightful error analysis and the demonstration of a viable embedding-based PoS predictor make this paper a worthwhile contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
