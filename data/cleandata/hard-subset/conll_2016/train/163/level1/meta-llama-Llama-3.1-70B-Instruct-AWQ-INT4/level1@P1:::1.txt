**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings and evaluate its performance on the British National Corpus (BNC) and the Universal Dependencies English Treebank. They find that the classifier can accurately predict PoS tags, even for words that are not in the training set. The authors also analyze the errors made by the classifier and identify patterns that reveal inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines. They conclude that distributional models can be used to discover new information about grammar and syntax, particularly PoS classes.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Demonstration of the effectiveness of word embeddings in predicting PoS tags**: The authors show that word embeddings contain information that can be used to accurately predict PoS tags, even for words that are not in the training set.
2. **Identification of patterns in errors made by the classifier**: The authors analyze the errors made by the classifier and identify patterns that reveal inconsistencies in the annotation process or guidelines.
3. **Insights into the distribution of PoS-related information in word embeddings**: The authors find that PoS-related information is distributed among multiple components of the word embeddings, rather than being concentrated in one or two specific features.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to PoS tagging**: The authors propose a novel approach to PoS tagging that uses word embeddings and a classifier, which is different from traditional approaches that rely on hand-crafted features and rules.
2. **Large-scale evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on a large corpus (BNC) and a treebank (Universal Dependencies English Treebank), which provides a comprehensive understanding of the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Insights into the nature of PoS classes**: The authors provide insights into the nature of PoS classes and how they are represented in word embeddings, which can inform future research in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited analysis of the classifier's performance**: The authors primarily focus on the accuracy of the classifier and do not provide a detailed analysis of its performance, such as precision, recall, and F1-score.
2. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The authors do not compare their approach to state-of-the-art PoS taggers, which makes it difficult to evaluate the effectiveness of their approach in relation to existing methods.
3. **Limited discussion of the implications of the findings**: The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of the implications of their findings, such as how their approach can be used to improve PoS tagging in low-resource languages.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the classifier's performance, including precision, recall, and F1-score?
2. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art PoS taggers, and what are the advantages and disadvantages of your approach?
3. Can you discuss the implications of your findings, such as how your approach can be used to improve PoS tagging in low-resource languages?
