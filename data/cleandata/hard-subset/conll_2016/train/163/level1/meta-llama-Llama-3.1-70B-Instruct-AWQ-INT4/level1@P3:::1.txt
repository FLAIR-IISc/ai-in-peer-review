**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper explores the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a distributional semantic model on the British National Corpus and use the resulting word embeddings to predict PoS tags for words. They show that the model can accurately predict PoS tags and identify words that do not conform to traditional PoS boundaries. The main contributions of this paper are:

1. **Demonstration of the effectiveness of distributional semantic models in predicting PoS tags**: The authors show that word embeddings contain information about PoS affiliation and can be used to predict PoS tags with high accuracy.
2. **Identification of words that do not conform to traditional PoS boundaries**: The authors use the model to identify words that are misclassified by traditional PoS taggers and show that these words often exhibit distributional patterns that are different from other words in the same PoS class.
3. **Analysis of the distributional patterns of words and their relationship to PoS affiliation**: The authors analyze the distributional patterns of words and show that certain features, such as vector component 94, are strongly associated with specific PoS classes.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel approach to PoS tagging**: The authors propose a novel approach to PoS tagging that uses distributional semantic models to predict PoS tags. This approach is different from traditional approaches that rely on hand-crafted features and rules.
2. **High accuracy of the model**: The authors show that the model can accurately predict PoS tags with high accuracy, even on unseen data.
3. **Insights into the distributional patterns of words**: The authors provide insights into the distributional patterns of words and their relationship to PoS affiliation, which can be useful for improving PoS taggers and understanding the nature of language.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate the model on a single dataset (the British National Corpus) and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance on different datasets and languages.
2. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The authors do not compare the performance of their model to state-of-the-art PoS taggers, which makes it difficult to assess the model's performance in relation to existing approaches.
3. **Limited analysis of the model's errors**: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the model's errors and do not discuss the limitations of the model in terms of its ability to handle certain types of words or contexts.
