**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates how syntactic information, specifically part-of-speech (PoS) categories, is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). By analyzing the classifier's errors, they identify words whose distributional patterns conflict with their annotated tags. This process uncovers linguistically interesting boundary cases (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives) and potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation scheme. The key contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel methodology for corpus analysis that uses embedding-based classification errors to audit PoS tagsets and identify words with atypical distributional behavior. This provides a data-driven approach to exploring the "graded" nature of grammatical categories.
2.  A detailed qualitative analysis of these "outlier" words, which successfully highlights both known linguistic ambiguities and previously unnoticed, systematic annotation inconsistencies within a widely used corpus.

**Strengths**

1.  The primary strength is the innovative use of a standard machine learning setup (classification on embeddings) for a descriptive linguistic purpose. Rather than aiming for state-of-the-art PoS tagging, the authors cleverly repurpose the model's failures as a tool for linguistic discovery. The error analysis in Section 4 is particularly compelling, offering concrete and well-reasoned examples that support the paper's thesis.
2.  The analysis is thorough and well-supported. The authors wisely compare their findings against an analysis of errors from a conventional PoS tagger (the Stanford Tagger), demonstrating that their distributional approach reveals different and arguably more nuanced issues. Testing on multiple datasets (BNC and UD Treebank) further strengthens the validity of the conclusions.
3.  The paper is clearly written and the experiments are well-described, which should facilitate reproducibility. The methodological choices are justified, and the connection between the experimental results and the linguistic claims is articulated effectively.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The novelty of the core finding—that word embeddings encode PoS information—is somewhat limited, as this has been established by prior work cited by the authors (e.g., Tsuboi, 2014; Tsvetkov et al., 2015). The paper's contribution lies in the *application* of this principle for corpus analysis, but the framing occasionally overstates the discovery of the link itself.
2.  The main experiment in Section 3 trains embeddings on tokens where the lemma is concatenated with its PoS tag (e.g., `love_VERB`). This setup risks "baking in" the PoS distinction into the input representations, potentially making the classification task artificially simple and the resulting clusters less reflective of natural language ambiguity. While a second experiment in Section 5 uses untagged lemmas, the primary qualitative analysis is based on this potentially confounding setup.
3.  The study is limited to English. While the findings are insightful, claims about redefining PoS classes and auditing annotations would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the method's utility on a language with more complex morphology or different syntactic properties. This is noted as future work but remains a limitation of the current submission.
