- Trains skip-gram on BNC with lemma_POS tokens and uses logistic regression to predict PoS, using classifier errors to identify boundary/outlier words.  
- Builds a context-free tagger on lemma embeddings achieving ~84% accuracy, arguing PoS is an intrinsic distributional property.  
- Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (~100) rather than localized.  
- Contribution: Presents a methodology that uses embedding classification errors for linguistic analysis and corpus-annotation diagnostics.  
- Contribution: Quantifies how PoS is encoded in embeddings and offers a context-free tagger as a proof-of-concept.  
- Strength: Deep, insightful qualitative analysis of misclassifications revealing linguistically interesting cases and annotation issues.  
- Strength: Novel framing—using classifier confusion to explore fuzzy PoS boundaries and comparing results to the Stanford tagger.  
- Strength: Sound, well-explained experimental design (lemma_POS training, frequency weighting, complementary lemma-only experiment).  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—core claim that embeddings encode PoS is largely known; paper hinges on the quality of analysis.  
- Weakness: Main setup may be circular because embeddings are trained on lemma_POS tokens, conflating tag information with distributional signatures.  
- Weakness: Practical utility of the context-free tagger is unclear for low-resource scenarios compared to HMM/CRF taggers.  
- Question: Which clustering algorithms were tried and why did unsupervised clustering fail where supervised classification succeeded?  
- Question: Have you trained embeddings on raw lemmas and tested ambiguous lemmas (e.g., 'love') against PoS prototypes for a cleaner test?  
- Question: Have you interpreted the most discriminative embedding dimensions (highest F-values) to link them to specific linguistic properties?
