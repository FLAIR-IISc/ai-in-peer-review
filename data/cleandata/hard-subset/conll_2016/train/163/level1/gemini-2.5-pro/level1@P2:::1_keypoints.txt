- Paper probes how much PoS information is encoded in word embeddings by training a logistic regression to predict PoS and analyzing systematic misclassifications as diagnostic "outliers."
- Primary contribution: repurposes classifier errors on embeddings as a novel method for linguistic and corpus analysis to reveal fuzzy PoS boundaries and annotation issues.
- Contribution: provides qualitative analyses of outlier categories (e.g., passive participles, numerals-as-nouns) linking model errors to linguistic ambiguities and corpus inconsistencies.
- Contribution: shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, with classifier performance scaling smoothly with feature count.
- Strength: creative analytical framework that uses model errors for linguistic insight rather than optimizing metrics.
- Strength: high-quality, convincing error analysis connecting mistakes to real linguistic phenomena and annotation problems.
- Strength: sound experimental comparison showing distributional method uncovers different boundary cases than a sequence-based Stanford PoS tagger.
- Weakness: experimental setup may be confounded by training embeddings on lemma_POS tokens, making the task circular and needing stronger justification.
- Weakness: limited novelty since embeddings’ encoding of syntactic/PoS information is already known; lacks a concrete, scalable procedure for corpus correction.
- Weakness: unfair comparison to Stanford tagger by evaluating it on isolated tokens, handicapping a context-aware model and possibly misleading conclusions.
- Question: why were embeddings trained on lemma_POS tokens, and have the authors tried training on raw lemmas to compare outlier analyses?
- Question: how does the 0.84 accuracy of the context-free tagger compare to a unigram baseline (most-frequent-tag per word) to quantify gains over frequency statistics?
