**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings and part-of-speech (PoS) categories. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag using only its pre-trained word embedding, derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). The primary goal is not to create a state-of-the-art tagger, but to use the classifier's misclassifications as a diagnostic tool. The analysis of these "outliers" reveals words with non-prototypical distributional properties, which the authors argue can highlight graded PoS affiliations and inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation scheme. The paper also demonstrates that PoS-related information is distributed across numerous dimensions of the word vectors rather than being concentrated in a few. Finally, a context-independent PoS predictor is built using embeddings from untagged text, showing reasonable performance based solely on distributional information.

**Main contributions**

1.  A methodology for linguistic analysis and corpus error detection that uses the misclassifications of a word-embedding-based PoS classifier. This approach systematically identifies words whose distributional behavior deviates from the prototype of their assigned PoS class, thereby uncovering linguistic boundary cases and potential annotation inconsistencies.
2.  An empirical analysis showing that word embeddings robustly encode PoS information. The work demonstrates that this information is distributed across a large number of vector dimensions and can be used to build a context-agnostic PoS predictor that achieves a respectable level of accuracy with a small, curated training set.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the proposed methodology.** The core idea of using a classifier's errors as a tool for linguistic inquiry is a notable strength. Rather than treating misclassifications as failures, the paper reframes them as a source of insight into the structure of grammatical categories and the quality of corpus annotations. This provides a creative and effective method for data-driven linguistic analysis.
2.  **Insightful qualitative analysis.** The analysis of classification errors in Section 4 is thorough and compelling. The authors present concrete examples of words and word classes that are misclassified, such as passive participles behaving like adjectives, currency amounts acting like numerals, and years behaving like nouns. These examples effectively support the claims about the graded nature of PoS membership and expose plausible inconsistencies and systematic errors in the BNC annotation.
3.  **Clear and reproducible experimental setup.** The paper's methodology is described with sufficient clarity to be understood and replicated. The data sources (BNC, UD Treebank), model choices (Skip-gram, logistic regression), and evaluation procedures are well-documented. The presentation is logical, and the use of tables to summarize misclassification patterns is effective.
4.  **Demonstration of context-independent PoS prediction.** The experiment in Section 5, which trains a PoS predictor on embeddings from untagged text and a small set of unambiguous words, is an interesting proof of concept. Achieving 84% accuracy on a test set without any sentence-level context demonstrates the strength of the PoS signal contained within the word embeddings themselves.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The novelty is in application, not technical methods.** The paper relies on standard machine learning components (Skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression). The finding that embeddings capture syntactic information is also previously established in the literature. The contribution is therefore limited to the specific application and the resulting analysis, rather than any new modeling technique.
2.  **The title may overstate the contribution.** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" suggests a more foundational contribution than is delivered. The paper provides evidence that challenges the strict boundaries of existing PoS schemes and identifies issues in a specific corpus, but it does not propose a new, formal PoS framework or a systematic redefinition of classes. The work is more of a critique and analysis.
3.  **The initial experimental setup is unconventional and could be a source of confusion.** In Section 3, the classifier is trained on embeddings for `lemma_POS` strings (e.g., 'love_VERB'). This design choice is critical to the paper's goal of identifying outliers within pre-defined classes, but it is not a standard classification task. The motivation and implications of this setup could be explained more clearly upfront. Furthermore, the comparison with the Stanford Tagger is not entirely parallel, as the tagger is deprived of the contextual information it is designed to use, potentially limiting the conclusions that can be drawn from the comparison.
4.  **The findings are dependent on a single model configuration.** All analyses are based on one set of embeddings trained with specific hyperparameters (Skip-gram, 300 dimensions, window size of 2) on a single corpus. The robustness of the specific findings (e.g., which words are identified as outliers, the number of informative dimensions) to variations in the embedding model, its parameters, or the training data is not explored.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The primary analysis in Sections 3 and 4 is based on a classifier trained on embeddings of `lemma_POS` types. Could you elaborate on why this method was chosen over the more direct approach of training a classifier to predict the PoS tag for a given lemma's embedding (where the embedding is trained on a corpus of lemmas only)? Does the `lemma_POS` approach capture nuances that would otherwise be lost?
2.  The analysis of PoS information distribution in Section 5 shows that accuracy plateaus around 100 features. Have you investigated whether the top-ranked features correlate with specific, interpretable linguistic or syntactic properties beyond broad PoS categories? For instance, does component 94, which is prominent for conjunctions, capture a more general grammatical function that is simply most salient in conjunctions?
