**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors' primary methodology involves training a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its embedding. The key insight is to use the classifier's errors not as a flaw, but as a diagnostic tool. By analyzing words that are systematically misclassified ("outliers"), the authors identify cases where a word's distributional behavior is at odds with its gold PoS tag in the British National Corpus (BNC). This analysis reveals linguistically interesting phenomena, such as the adjective-like nature of passive participles and the noun-like behavior of numerals representing years. The authors argue this approach supports a "graded" view of PoS membership and can be used to find inconsistencies or errors in annotated corpora. A secondary experiment demonstrates that a reasonable context-free PoS tagger can be built using only word embeddings, and a final analysis shows that PoS information is distributed across a large number of embedding dimensions rather than being localized to a few.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a novel application of word embeddings and classification as a method for linguistic and corpus analysis. Instead of aiming for state-of-the-art performance, the work uses a classifier's prediction errors as a probe to identify words with atypical distributional properties, thereby highlighting fuzzy PoS boundaries and potential annotation inconsistencies.
2.  The paper provides a compelling qualitative analysis of several "outlier" categories (e.g., nouns vs. adjectives, verbs vs. adjectives, nouns vs. numerals), successfully linking distributional patterns learned by the model to well-known linguistic ambiguities and potential corpus annotation problems.
3.  The work provides empirical evidence that PoS information is highly distributed across embedding dimensions, showing that classifier performance scales smoothly with the number of features used, rather than relying on a small subset of "PoS-specific" dimensions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Analytical Framework:** The paper's core strength is its creative repurposing of a standard machine learning pipeline. Using a classifier's errors for qualitative linguistic analysis is an insightful idea. It shifts the focus from simply improving a metric to using models to gain a deeper understanding of language and the corpora we use to study it. This approach is well-aligned with the goals of computational linguistics.
2.  **High-Quality Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is thorough, well-reasoned, and presents convincing examples. The authors successfully connect the model's "mistakes" to genuine linguistic ambiguities (e.g., passive participles like 'overgrown' being verb/adjective hybrids) and logical inconsistencies in annotation (e.g., '£70' being a NOUN while 'seventy' is a NUM). This section forms the heart of the paper and makes a strong case for the utility of the proposed method.
3.  **Sound Experimental Comparison:** The comparison against the errors made by the Stanford PoS Tagger is a valuable addition. It demonstrates that the distributional method uncovers a different, and arguably more semantically grounded, set of boundary cases than a traditional sequence-based tagger. For instance, the distributional model highlights the adverb-like nature of some conjunctions, a subtle point not captured in the top errors of the Stanford tagger. This strengthens the claim that this method offers a unique analytical lens.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Potentially Confounding Experimental Setup:** The main analysis (Sections 3 & 4) is performed on embeddings trained on `lemma_POS` concatenated strings (e.g., 'love_VERB'). A classifier is then trained to predict the PoS from the embedding. This setup feels somewhat circular, as the model is essentially learning to predict a suffix that was part of the input during embedding training. It is unclear how this influences the vector space and the subsequent analysis. A cleaner design might have been to train embeddings on raw lemmas and then train the classifier to predict the gold PoS tags, which would more directly test whether a lemma's general distributional profile matches its assigned tag. This methodological choice needs stronger justification.
2.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Premise:** While the application is novel, the underlying finding that word embeddings capture syntactic and PoS information is well-established in the literature (as noted by the authors). The paper could do a better job of situating its contribution. Is this primarily a demonstration of a known property, or is it a proposal for a new, systematic methodology for corpus refinement? The latter is implied but not fully developed; the analysis remains qualitative and does not lead to a concrete, scalable procedure for corpus correction.
3.  **Unfair Comparison to a Context-Aware Tagger:** In Section 4, the Stanford PoS Tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens to "mimic our workflow with the distributional predictor." This is an unfair comparison that severely handicaps the Stanford tagger, which is designed to leverage sentence context. While the goal was to compare token-level classifiers, the conclusions drawn might be misleading. The types of errors a context-aware tagger makes on full sentences could be different, and a more nuanced comparison would be needed to truly contrast the insights from the two approaches.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the main experimental setup: Could you please elaborate on the rationale for training embeddings on `lemma_POS` tokens? Have you experimented with training on lemmas alone and then performing the same outlier analysis? It seems this alternate approach would provide a more direct test of the hypothesis that a lemma's distributional context aligns (or not) with its annotated PoS tag, and I am curious if it would yield similar or different insights.
2.  The accuracy of the context-free tagger in Section 5 (0.84) is presented as impressive for a model with no access to local context. How does this compare to a simple unigram baseline (i.e., assigning each word its most frequent tag from the training corpus)? This comparison would help contextualize the performance and better quantify the contribution of the distributional information over simple frequency statistics.
