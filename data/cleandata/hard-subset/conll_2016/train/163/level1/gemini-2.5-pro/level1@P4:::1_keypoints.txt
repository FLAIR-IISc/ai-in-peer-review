- Summary: Uses logistic regression on BNC-trained word embeddings to predict PoS, analyzes classifier misclassifications to reveal graded PoS membership and annotation inconsistencies, shows PoS info is distributed across many vector dimensions, and builds a context-independent PoS predictor.

- Contribution 1: Presents a methodology that uses embedding-classifier misclassifications to identify linguistic boundary cases and corpus annotation errors.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows embeddings robustly encode PoS information across many dimensions and enables a context-agnostic PoS predictor with a small curated training set.

- Strength 1: Novel methodological reframing of classifier errors as a tool for linguistic inquiry rather than mere failures.

- Strength 2: Thorough, convincing qualitative analysis with concrete misclassification examples supporting graded PoS claims and BNC inconsistencies.

- Strength 3: Clear, reproducible experimental setup with well-documented data sources, models, and evaluations.

- Strength 4: Demonstrates a context-independent PoS predictor achieving ~84% accuracy, highlighting strong PoS signal in embeddings.

- Weakness 1: Technical novelty is limited—uses standard embeddings and logistic regression and reiterates known findings that embeddings capture syntactic info.

- Weakness 2: Title overstates impact by implying a formal redefinition of PoS classes rather than presenting critique/analysis of a corpus.

- Weakness 3: Unconventional training on `lemma_POS` types is nonstandard and potentially confusing; comparison with a context-dependent tagger is not fully parallel.

- Weakness 4: Results depend on a single embedding configuration and corpus; robustness to model, hyperparameter, and data variation is not assessed.

- Question 1: Why choose the `lemma_POS`-type training setup instead of training on lemma-only embeddings—what nuances does `lemma_POS` capture?

- Question 2: Have the top-ranked embedding features been linked to interpretable linguistic/syntactic properties (e.g., does component 94 reflect a general grammatical function beyond conjunctions)?
