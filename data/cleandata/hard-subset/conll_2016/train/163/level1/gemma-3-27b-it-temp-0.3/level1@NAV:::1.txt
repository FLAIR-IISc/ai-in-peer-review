## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

This paper investigates the relationship between word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) and part-of-speech (PoS) tags. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings and analyze misclassifications to identify inconsistencies in annotation or support the notion of “soft” PoS boundaries. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that distributional semantic models *do* encode information about PoS, and that analyzing errors made by a classifier trained on these embeddings can reveal linguistic insights – specifically, potential annotation errors and areas where traditional PoS boundaries are blurry. A secondary contribution is the empirical finding that PoS information is distributed across numerous vector components, rather than being localized to a few key features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Established Techniques:** The paper effectively combines well-established techniques – word embeddings and classification – to address a fundamental linguistic question: how well do distributional semantics align with traditional grammatical categories? This is a valuable approach, offering a data-driven perspective on PoS.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a strong point. Identifying specific cases where the model challenges BNC annotations (e.g., currency amounts as numerals, adverbs tagged as adjectives) provides concrete examples supporting the idea of flexible PoS boundaries and potential annotation inconsistencies. This is a significant contribution to understanding the limitations of existing corpora.
3. **Empirical Support for Distributed Representation:** The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector components reinforces the core principle of distributional semantics – that meaning (and, by extension, grammatical function) is encoded in a holistic, rather than localized, manner. This provides further evidence for the validity of these models.
4. **Robustness Demonstrated Through Multiple Datasets:** Testing the classifier on both the UD Treebank and the BNC test set demonstrates the generalizability of the findings and strengthens the claims about the robustness of the PoS information encoded in the embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** While the application is interesting, the core methodology (training embeddings, training a classifier) is fairly standard. The paper doesn't introduce any novel algorithmic advancements.
2. **Superficial Discussion of Annotation Errors:** While the paper identifies potential annotation errors, it lacks a deeper investigation into *why* these errors occurred. A more thorough analysis of the annotation guidelines and the context of the misclassified words would strengthen this argument.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art PoS Taggers:** The paper mentions that the classifier's performance isn't comparable to modern taggers, but doesn't fully contextualize this. A direct comparison (even a brief one) with a strong baseline would provide a clearer understanding of the value added by this distributional semantic approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific annotation guidelines used for the BNC and how the identified errors relate to potential ambiguities or inconsistencies within those guidelines?
2.  You mention the potential for using this approach in resource-poor languages. Could you provide a more detailed outline of how this would work in practice, including the amount of manually tagged data required?
3.  Were any attempts made to incorporate contextual information (beyond the word embeddings) into the classifier to improve performance? If so, what were the results?
