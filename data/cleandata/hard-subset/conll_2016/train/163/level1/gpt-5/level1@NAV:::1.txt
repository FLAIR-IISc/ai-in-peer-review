Summary and Contributions
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings trained on the BNC encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. A multinomial classifier trained on embeddings (first with lemma+PoS tokens, then with lemma-only) achieves high accuracy and is used to surface “outlier” words, which often correspond to annotation inconsistencies or graded PoS boundaries. The authors also probe how PoS information is distributed across embedding dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them), in order:
1) A systematic error/outlier analysis showing that embedding-based PoS predictions reveal boundary phenomena and corpus annotation issues (notably in BNC), offering a practical tool for corpus auditing.
2) An empirical characterization that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (∼100+), rather than localized to a few features.
3) A lightweight, contextless PoS predictor trained from a small set of prototypical lemmas that attains reasonable accuracy (∼0.84) on UD.

Strengths
- Novel diagnostic utility: The outlier analysis is careful and yields actionable insights (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally, numerals vs. nouns for years/currencies, SCONJ/ADV confusions), with concrete examples and cross-checks against UD vs. BNC guidelines.
- Cross-corpus robustness checks: Results transfer reasonably to UD (high F-scores when in-vocabulary), supporting that the observed phenomena are not BNC-specific artifacts.
- Interpretability and probing: The feature ranking and “prototypical vectors” provide interpretable evidence that PoS cues are diffuse across dimensions; identifying a conjunction-specific component is intriguing.
- Practicality for low-resource settings: The prototype classifier trained on ~1k unambiguous lemmas and lemma-only embeddings suggests a viable pre-tagging/auditing strategy when full taggers are unavailable.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in CV: The 10-fold CV on instance-replicated data likely splits duplicate vectors of the same word across folds, inflating F-scores. Word-type-level splits are needed. Clarify whether folds were grouped by word type.
- Label leakage in main setup: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens and then predicting PoS from those vectors conflates the representation with gold labels; reported metrics overstate predictive difficulty, though the setup is still useful for outlier discovery.
- Baseline comparison is unfair: Running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens (no context) disadvantages it and weakens claims about unique insights vs. mainstream taggers. A fairer comparison would use sentence context and analyze confusion matrices post hoc.
- Limited generality/ablations: Only BNC is used; hyperparameter and preprocessing effects (window, subsampling, casing, subwords) are not explored; the dimensionality finding is based on training accuracy and lacks stability analysis across random seeds/models.

Questions to Authors
- Did your cross-validation ensure fold-level grouping by word type (lemma or lemma+PoS) to avoid duplicate-vector leakage?
- How were capitalization and morphology handled for PROPN vs. NOUN in lemma-based models? Could casing cues explain the separation?
- Can you report stability of “important dimensions” across multiple embedding runs and corpora, and provide ablations on window size/subword modeling?
