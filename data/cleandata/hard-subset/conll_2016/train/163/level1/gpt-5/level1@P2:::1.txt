Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings trained on the British National Corpus (BNC) encode part-of-speech (PoS) information, and whether this signal can be used to (i) detect words with atypical PoS behavior and annotation inconsistencies, and (ii) build a simple PoS predictor without sentence context. The authors train skip-gram embeddings (300d) and a multinomial logistic regression classifier over word-type embeddings to predict Universal PoS tags, analyze misclassifications as “outliers,” and study which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) A diagnostic framework that uses embedding-based PoS prediction to surface graded PoS behavior and to identify likely corpus annotation inconsistencies, with concrete analyzed cases (e.g., participial verbs vs adjectives; NUM vs NOUN; ADJ incorrectly tagged as ADV in BNC).
2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is strongly encoded in embeddings and distributed across many dimensions, supported by classification performance, prototypical vectors, and ANOVA-based feature ablation.
3) A simple, purely type-based PoS predictor trained on a small set of “prototypical” lemmas that achieves reasonable accuracy (≈0.84) without any sentence context.

Strengths
1) Clear, linguistically relevant insights from error analysis. The paper convincingly shows that misclassifications are not random noise but align with known graded category boundaries (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally; nominal modifiers; years and times acting nominally). The identification of systematic BNC tagging inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as ADV) is particularly compelling and shows practical utility for corpus quality control. The coverage-based analysis adds quantitative support beyond anecdotal examples.

2) Strong empirical signal that embeddings encode PoS. The classifier substantially outperforms baselines on disjoint vocabulary (F≈0.91 on words with freq 100–500), and generalizes well to UD types (F≈0.99 after OOV filtering). The prototypical vectors and inter-PoS cosine similarities are consistent with linguistic intuition (e.g., closeness of ADP to SCONJ; DET/PRON similarity; PROPN distinct from NOUN). This helps substantiate claims about graded categories with distributional evidence.

3) Methodological simplicity and robustness. The approach uses standard skip-gram and logistic regression, is easy to reproduce, and yields interpretable outputs (confusion patterns, feature rankings). The frequency weighting addresses the type/token imbalance between open and closed classes, a sensible design choice that improves learning for closed-class tags.

4) Evidence that PoS cues are distributed, not localized to a single dimension. The ANOVA-based feature ranking and incremental training show that many dimensions (roughly 100+) contribute meaningfully. This is an important clarification relative to earlier anecdotal “one-dimension-per-feature” narratives and aligns with current understanding of distributed representations.

5) Practical potential for low-resource settings. The demonstration that a small lexicon of “prototypical” lemmas can train a reasonable type-based tagger without sentence context suggests a feasible bootstrap path for preliminary tagging in low-resource languages.

Weaknesses
1) Potential circularity/label leakage in the main experiment. The primary embedding space is trained on tokens concatenated with their PoS tags (lemma_POS). This injects PoS supervision into the contexts: neighboring tokens carry explicit PoS labels that shape the embedding geometry. Predicting a word’s PoS from such a representation partly measures how well the model encodes distributional patterns over labeled contexts, not how well unlabeled distribution captures PoS. The authors acknowledge this is tailored to outlier detection, but the resulting high F-scores (including 0.99 on UD types) might overstate how “semantic-only” embeddings reflect PoS. The second experiment (lemma-only embeddings) partially addresses this, but the outlier findings should be validated in an unlabeled-embedding setting.

2) Validation of “annotation errors” is anecdotal and lacks systematic human evaluation. While the presented cases are plausible and illustrative, claims of systematic BNC errors would be stronger with a blinded human study quantifying precision/recall of detected errors, or at least a larger random sample with adjudication and error taxonomy.

3) Evaluation methodology issues reduce interpretability of some numbers. The 10-fold cross-validation on the 10k frequent words reuses identical word-type vectors across folds (instances are repeated proportional to frequency), inflating CV performance because the same types appear in both train and validation folds. The disjoint-frequency test is more informative (F≈0.91), but the paper should downplay the CV number and emphasize type-level splits. Similarly, the comparison to the Stanford tagger is not fully fair: running a sentence-based tagger on isolated tokens removes its main advantage (context) and changes its error profile; the resulting confusion matrix comparison is thus apples-to-oranges.

4) Limited scope and ablations. Results are only for English BNC with a single architecture and hyperparameter setting (window=2, no subsampling). There is no comparison to alternative distributional spaces (e.g., dependency-based embeddings), alternative classifiers, or hyperparameter sweeps. Stability across random seeds is not explored, so claims about specific dimensions (e.g., “dimension 94”) are not substantiated for invariance.

5) Reproducibility and clarity gaps. The BNC-to-UD mapping is “anonymized” and not assessable in the submission; details on lowercasing, lemma conventions for PROPN/NUM, and the precise evaluation protocol for the UD-type experiment are sparse. Some reference formatting errors also detract from clarity.

Questions to authors
- Can you replicate the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on lemmas only (no PoS in tokens), and report how many of the previously found “annotation issues” reappear? This would address the label-leakage concern.
- How stable are the ANOVA-ranked dimensions and the observed “conjunction dimension” across random seeds and hyperparameters? Please report rank correlations or overlap statistics.
- For the UD-type 0.99 F-score, what proportion of types/tokens remained after OOV removal, and was the evaluation type-weighted or token-weighted? Can you provide a per-class breakdown?
- Could you provide a small-scale human validation (e.g., 100–200 randomly sampled flagged cases) estimating precision of detected annotation errors?
- How do results change with dependency-based embeddings, larger windows, subsampling, or CBOW? Any ablation highlights?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a useful, well-motivated diagnostic use of embeddings to probe graded PoS categories and corpus consistency, with convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence. However, the reliance on PoS-labeled tokens in the main embedding space raises circularity concerns, and some evaluations and validations need tightening. With clarifications and an unlabeled-embedding replication of the outlier analysis, this would be a strong contribution. I lean toward weak accept.
