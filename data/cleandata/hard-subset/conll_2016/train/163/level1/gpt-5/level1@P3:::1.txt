Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings interact with part-of-speech (PoS) categories. Using BNC-based embeddings (trained both with PoS-augmented tokens and, later, with plain lemmas) and a multinomial logistic classifier, the authors show that:
- embeddings can predict PoS with high accuracy;
- misclassifications reveal (i) graded class membership and boundary phenomena (e.g., adjectives vs nouns, participles) and (ii) systematic annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adverbs mis-tagged in BNC);
- PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Empirical error analysis: A careful, linguistically informed analysis of classifier confusions exposes boundary cases and corpus annotation issues in BNC (and contrasts with UD), offering a practical diagnostic tool for corpus quality and for reconsidering category definitions (graded PoS membership).
2) Methodological probe: A simple, interpretable probing framework demonstrating that PoS information is recoverable from embeddings and is distributed across many dimensions, with preliminary evidence via ANOVA feature ranking.
3) Context-free PoS prediction: A lemma-only, no-context classifier trained on a small set of unambiguous UD lemmas achieves reasonable accuracy, suggesting utility for bootstrapping low-resource tagging.

Strengths
- Novel diagnostic of PoS boundaries and annotation quality: The paper uses misclassifications not merely as errors but as signal. Findings about numerals vs nouns (years, currencies), ADJ/NOUN alternations, and participles substantiate well-known boundary phenomena and surface inconsistencies in BNC (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as RB). This is practically useful for corpus cleanup and tagset design discussions (graded categories).
- Robust empirical results across datasets: High performance on an unseen BNC frequency band (F≈0.91) and very high performance on UD lemmas (F≈0.99) suggest that the probe captures stable distributional cues (despite the caveats below).
- Clear, simple probing method with interpretable outcomes: Logistic regression over embedding dimensions is transparent; the frequency-weighting choice to handle closed-class imbalance is reasonable; the presentation of prototypical class vectors and inter-class cosine similarities aids linguistic interpretation (e.g., PROPN vs NOUN similarity unexpectedly lower than adverbs).
- Useful negative comparison to a standard tagger: The “token-only” Stanford tagger experiment, although non-standard, shows that the proposed approach exposes different confusions than a sequence tagger—highlighting complementary error signatures.
- Preliminary feature-distribution finding: The ANOVA-based k-best feature curves indicate that PoS signal is spread across many dimensions (≈100+), aligning with the intuition that embeddings encode syntactic compatibility broadly rather than in a small set of axes.

Weaknesses
- Circularity and leakage in the main experiment: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens bakes gold PoS labels into the identity of each token and also into every context. The classifier then predicts the tag of a vector whose identity already includes the tag. While evaluation is per word type and on unseen types, the representation itself is learned in a tag-labeled universe, making the result unsurprising as a test of whether “unsupervised” embeddings encode PoS. Section 5 addresses this with plain-lemma embeddings; that should be foregrounded and emphasized as the main evidence for the core claim.
- Inflated cross-validation due to instance-level folds: For the 10,000-word training set, instances are replicated by frequency. If cross-validation splits by instances rather than by word type, the same word type appears in both train and test folds, inflating the 0.979 F-score. Type-level splits should be used for all CV and reported.
- Missing strong baselines: The lemma-only tagger (Sec. 5) should be compared to (i) a majority-tag-per-lemma lexicon baseline built from the same UD training data; (ii) simple bag-of-contexts co-occurrence features without embedding; (iii) character n-gram or affix features (for ADJ/ADV/VERB/NOUN); and (iv) Brown clusters or SVD on co-occurrence. These would clarify how much the embeddings add beyond very simple cues.
- Rotation invariance and component interpretability: Claims about specific dimensions (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions) are fragile under arbitrary linear rotations of embedding space. The ANOVA+k-best experiment is useful, but the conclusion that PoS is spread across “at least a hundred” dimensions should be supported with rotation-invariant analyses (e.g., random orthogonal rotations, sparse probes, L1-regularized logistic regression, or CCA to tag indicators).
- Stanford tagger comparison is unfair: Feeding isolated tokens to a contextual tagger is not a meaningful baseline; conversion across tagsets further complicates interpretation. The conclusion that the distributional probe reveals different errors may hold, but a fairer comparison would use a lexicon-only or prior-based model, or a proper contextual tagging setup with standard evaluation.
- Limited related work on PoS induction: The paper should position itself with respect to classic unsupervised/supervised PoS induction and distributional clustering literature (e.g., Schütze 1993; Clark 2003; Goldwater & Griffiths 2007; Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010), and more recent probing work, to clarify novelty.

Questions to authors
- Can you redo the main BNC experiment with embeddings trained on plain tokens (no tag in the token identity) and show comparable clustering/confusion results? This would remove leakage concerns and strengthen the central claim.
- For all CV results, did you split by word type (ensuring no type overlap between train and test)? If not, please report type-level CV and updated scores.
- What is the performance of a majority-tag-per-lemma baseline on the UD token evaluation in Sec. 5? How does your 0.84 compare?
- Are lemmas lowercased? Could capitalization drive PROPN vs NOUN performance? Please control for case and report effects.
- How sensitive are findings to hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, dimensionality)? Any ablations (e.g., larger window should increase syntactic signal)?
- Can you test rotation robustness of the “distributed signal” claim (e.g., repeat k-best feature curves after random orthogonal rotations)?

Additional comments
- The diagnostic utility is a valuable contribution; consider releasing code and ranked “suspect” items per tag for BNC/UD to facilitate corpus repair.
- Extending to typologically diverse languages and richer morphology would significantly strengthen the generality of the graded PoS findings.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The linguistic error analysis and the lemma-only probe are valuable and likely to spur follow-up work on corpus diagnostics and graded categories. However, the main experiment’s reliance on PoS-augmented tokens, potential CV leakage, and missing baselines weaken the methodological claims. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper.
