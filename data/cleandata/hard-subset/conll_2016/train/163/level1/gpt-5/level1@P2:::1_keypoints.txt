- Paper studies whether BNC-trained word embeddings encode PoS information and whether this can detect atypical PoS behavior/annotation errors and build a context-free PoS predictor.
- Method: skip-gram (300d) embeddings and multinomial logistic regression on type embeddings to predict Universal PoS tags; analyze misclassifications as outliers and correlate dimensions with PoS.

Contributions:
- Diagnostic framework using embedding-based PoS prediction to surface graded PoS behavior and corpus annotation inconsistencies with concrete case analyses.
- Empirical evidence that PoS information is strongly encoded and distributed across many embedding dimensions (classification performance, prototypical vectors, ANOVA ablation).
- A simple type-based PoS predictor trained on a small set of prototypical lemmas achieving reasonable accuracy (≈0.84) without sentence context.

Strengths:
- Error analysis yields clear linguistically relevant insights; misclassifications align with graded category boundaries and reveal systematic BNC tagging inconsistencies.
- Strong empirical signal: classifier outperforms baselines on disjoint vocab (F≈0.91) and generalizes well to UD types after OOV filtering (reported ≈0.99).
- Methodologically simple, reproducible, and interpretable (skip-gram + logistic regression; sensible frequency weighting).
- Evidence that PoS cues are distributed across many dimensions (roughly 100+ contribute), countering single-dimension narratives.
- Practical potential for low-resource settings: small prototypical lexicon can bootstrap preliminary tagging.

Weaknesses:
- Circularity/label leakage: main embeddings trained on lemma_POS tokens inject PoS into contexts, potentially inflating PoS signal and outlier findings.
- Validation of detected annotation errors is anecdotal and lacks systematic human adjudication or precision/recall estimates.
- Evaluation methodology issues: 10-fold CV reuses identical type vectors across folds inflating performance; comparison to sentence-based tagger on isolated tokens is unfair.
- Limited scope and ablations: single corpus, single architecture/hyperparameters, no dependency-based embeddings or seed stability checks.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps: anonymized BNC-to-UD mapping, missing preprocessing/lemma conventions, sparse UD evaluation protocol details.

Questions for authors:
- Can you replicate outlier analysis using embeddings trained on lemmas only (no PoS) and report overlap with previously found annotation issues?
- How stable are ANOVA-ranked dimensions and the identified “conjunction dimension” across random seeds and hyperparameters (rank correlations/overlap)?
- For the UD-type 0.99 F-score, what proportion remained after OOV removal, was evaluation type- or token-weighted, and can you provide a per-class breakdown?
- Can you provide a small-scale human validation (e.g., 100–200 sampled flagged cases) estimating precision of detected annotation errors?
- How do results change with dependency-based embeddings, larger windows, subsampling, CBOW, or other ablations?

Overall assessment:
- A useful and well-motivated diagnostic contribution with convincing evidence, but weakened by PoS-labeled training circularity and evaluation/validation gaps; reviewer leans toward weak accept.
