Summary
The paper investigates how word embeddings trained on the BNC relate to part-of-speech (PoS) categories. A multinomial logistic classifier is trained to predict PoS from word vectors, first using embeddings trained on lemma+PoS tokens (to identify outliers and boundary cases) and then using embeddings trained on lemmas only (to build a context-free PoS predictor). The authors analyze systematic misclassifications to expose PoS boundary phenomena and corpus annotation inconsistencies, and show that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A systematic error analysis showing that distributional representations can identify borderline PoS phenomena and surfacing annotation inconsistencies/errors (e.g., adjectives mislabeled as adverbs in BNC; numerals vs nouns for years and currencies).
2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is broadly distributed in embeddings (not concentrated in a few dimensions), via a top-k feature study.
3) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained from a small set of “prototypical” lexemes that achieves reasonable accuracy, suggesting utility for bootstrapping in low-resource settings.

Strengths
- Insightful, data-driven analysis of PoS boundaries and annotation issues. The paper goes beyond “embeddings encode PoS” to diagnose concrete, linguistically interpretable patterns (e.g., passive participles trending toward adjectives; nominal modifiers like “hypertext” trending toward ADJ; years behaving nominally; “7pm” and similar tokens mis-tagged as ADV). The finding of systematic BNC errors (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as ADV) makes a compelling case that this approach can assist corpus auditing.
- Cross-resource evaluation reduces overfitting concerns. After training on BNC-based embeddings, applying the classifier to UD English lemmas yields high F-scores, and repeating the error analysis on UD shows broadly similar tendencies. This supports the claim that the approach captures general distributional regularities rather than corpus idiosyncrasies.
- Methodological simplicity and transparency. Using skip-gram embeddings and logistic regression keeps the setup interpretable. The frequency-weighting rationale for balancing closed vs open classes is reasonable and practically motivated.
- Useful negative/contrastive baseline using a mainstream tagger. Although imperfect (see weaknesses), the confusion-matrix comparison suggests that the proposed method highlights different phenomena from a standard tagger, supporting its complementary auditing value.
- The feature-importance study is informative. Ranking dimensions by ANOVA F-value and showing that performance grows steadily with more features substantiates the claim that PoS signals are distributed and not reducible to a couple of axes, with a plausible “plateau” around ~100 dimensions.

Weaknesses
- Methodological circularity and data leakage in the main experiment. The primary outlier-detection setup trains embeddings on tokens concatenated with gold PoS tags (lemma_POS), and then predicts PoS from those embeddings. Because all contexts also carry gold PoS suffixes, the embeddings encode explicit PoS labels of neighbors, making the task easier and potentially inflating separability. Moreover, the reported 10-fold cross-validation on the 10k-word training set is performed over repeated instances of the same word vector (weighted by frequency), so the exact same vector type appears in both train and test folds. This undermines the 0.979 CV F-score as evidence. The authors partly mitigate with a disjoint test set (F=0.91), but the confound remains. A leave-lexeme-out CV and embeddings trained without PoS in the corpus should be used for the main analysis.
- Unclear protocol for the UD evaluation in Section 3. The embeddings were trained on lemma_POS tokens, yet the classifier is “applied to all lemmas from the UD Treebank.” Are UD inputs provided as lemma_POS (using UD’s gold PoS) or as bare lemmas? If the former, the evaluation is not informative; if the latter, most lemmas would be OOV since the model’s vocabulary consists of lemma_POS tokens. This requires clarification.
- Incomparable baseline with the Stanford tagger. Running a supervised sequence tagger on isolated tokens (no sentence context) is a severe handicap and not a fair comparison to a model that aggregates large corpus contexts via distributional training. The resulting confusion patterns are therefore hard to interpret as evidence that the proposed method reveals “different” phenomena; the baseline is not an apples-to-apples audit tool.
- Limited validation of the outlier lists. The analysis is largely anecdotal, with illustrative examples and coarse counts. There is no human evaluation to confirm that flagged cases are true boundary phenomena vs noise, nor quantitative measures of precision/recall of error detection against a manually verified sample. This weakens the strength of claims about systematic annotation problems.
- Narrow scope and limited ablations. All core results are on English BNC/UD with one embedding configuration (win=2, no subsampling, neg=10). The conclusion mentions plans to vary hyperparameters and languages; without at least some ablations (e.g., window size, subsampling, model type) and a non-English language, generality remains speculative. The top-k feature study reports training accuracy; evaluating generalization vs k would be more convincing.

Questions to Authors
- Can you redo the main outlier analysis with embeddings trained on lemmas only (no PoS concatenation) and show that key findings (e.g., passive participles; numerals vs nouns; BNC ADV/ADJ confusions) still emerge? This would remove the most serious circularity concern.
- For the UD evaluation in Section 3, what exact form did inputs take when querying the classifier trained on lemma_POS embeddings? If inputs were lemmas, how were they embedded given the lemma_POS vocabulary? If inputs were lemma_POS, how were the UD gold tags used?
- Can you report leave-lexeme-out cross-validation (word types partitioned across folds) rather than instance-based CV to avoid identical vector leakage, and provide the resulting performance?
- For the feature selection study (Figure 3), can you show dev/test performance as a function of k (not only training accuracy), and report whether ranking features by ANOVA on training vs on all data materially changes the curve?
- Would you consider a small-scale human validation of a sampled set of flagged outliers to quantify precision of error/boundary detection?

Additional comments
- Please fix citation/bibliography formatting and ensure figures referenced (e.g., component 94 in conjunction prototypes) are clearly interpretable in the final version.
- Releasing the mapping from BNC tags to Universal tags, the trained models, and the misclassified word lists would materially improve reproducibility and impact.
