Summary: Evaluates how BNC-trained word embeddings encode PoS, uses a multinomial classifier to surface outliers and probes distribution of PoS info across dimensions.

Contribution 1: Embedding-based PoS predictions reveal annotation inconsistencies and graded PoS boundaries, providing a practical corpus-auditing tool.

Contribution 2: Empirically shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (~100+), not localized to a few.

Contribution 3: Presents a lightweight, contextless PoS predictor trained on ~1k prototypical lemmas achieving ~0.84 accuracy on UD.

Strength 1: Outlier analysis is novel and diagnostic, yielding actionable insights (e.g., participles, numerals vs. nouns, SCONJ/ADV confusions) with concrete examples.

Strength 2: Cross-corpus checks show reasonable transfer to UD, suggesting findings are not BNC-specific.

Strength 3: Interpretability probes and prototypical vectors indicate PoS cues are diffuse and identify an intriguing conjunction-specific component.

Strength 4: Prototype classifier is practical for low-resource settings as a pre-tagger/auditing strategy.

Weakness 1: 10-fold CV likely leaks duplicate word vectors across folds, inflating F-scores; word-type-level splits are needed.

Weakness 2: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens leaks labels into representations, overstating predictive performance in main setup.

Weakness 3: Baseline comparison is unfair because the Stanford tagger is run without context, weakening claims of unique insights.

Weakness 4: Limited generality and ablations — only BNC used; no hyperparameter, preprocessing, seed stability, or dimensionality stability analyses.

Question 1: Did cross-validation group folds by word type to avoid duplicate-vector leakage?

Question 2: How were capitalization and morphology handled for PROPN vs. NOUN in lemma-based models; could casing explain separation?

Question 3: Can you report stability of important dimensions across runs/corpora and provide ablations on window size and subword modeling?
