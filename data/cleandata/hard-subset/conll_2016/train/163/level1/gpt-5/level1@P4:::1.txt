Summary
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings relate to part-of-speech (PoS) categories. Using the British National Corpus (BNC), the authors first train embeddings over lemma+PoS tokens and fit a multinomial logistic regression classifier to predict PoS from embeddings; they then analyze misclassifications to reveal “outlier” words that exhibit distributional behavior divergent from their annotated PoS, suggesting graded PoS boundaries and pointing to annotation inconsistencies. A second experiment trains embeddings on plain lemmas (no PoS), learns a PoS predictor from a small set of “prototypical” UD lemmas, and evaluates contextless PoS prediction on UD test data. Finally, the authors analyze the distribution of PoS information across embedding dimensions using ANOVA-based feature ranking.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence and analysis showing that distributional embeddings encode PoS-related information strongly enough to (a) recover PoS for most words and (b) identify systematic “boundary” cases and likely corpus annotation inconsistencies.
2) A simple, contextless PoS predictor trained from a small set of “prototypical” lemmas achieving reasonable accuracy on UD test tokens, suggesting a lightweight approach for preliminary tagging in low-resource settings.
3) An observation, supported by feature-ranking experiments, that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than localized in a few.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven outlier analysis with actionable linguistic insights. The error analysis surfaces interpretable boundary phenomena (e.g., passive participles behaving adjectivally; numerals vs. years/amounts; prepositions used as modifiers) and points to plausible BNC inconsistencies (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs such as “plain/clear/sharp,” with counts suggesting systematic issues). This is a useful diagnostic application beyond mere accuracy reporting.
- Robustness across datasets and settings. The classifier trained on BNC shows strong results not only in cross-validation but also on a separate BNC test-set (F≈0.91) and when applied to UD lemmas (F≈0.99 on covered items), indicating that the phenomenon is not dataset-specific and that embeddings encode reliable PoS correlates.
- Simple, reproducible methodology yielding competitive contextless tagging. The lemma-only embedding plus logistic regression trained on ~1.5k “unambiguous” UD lemmas reaches 0.84 token accuracy on UD test tokens without any sentence context. Given zero use of local context and minimal supervision, this is technically sound and practically relevant as a low-cost bootstrap approach.
- Feature-distribution finding aligns with theoretical expectations. The gradual accuracy gains with more top-ranked dimensions (plateau around ~100 dimensions) support the claim that PoS cues are distributed, not single-feature artifacts; the observed strong conjunction-specific dimension also provides an interpretable case.
- Comparative analysis to a mainstream tagger surfaces complementary error profiles. Although the Stanford tagger comparison is limited (see weaknesses), the paper shows the distributional predictor highlights different confusions than a conventional tagger, arguing for complementary diagnostic value.

Weaknesses
- Primary experiment trains embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens, potentially inflating separability. Because each token’s identity encodes its gold tag (e.g., love_VERB vs. love_NOUN), embeddings are conditioned on the tag-specific contexts. This design makes PoS recovery less surprising and complicates claims about discovering PoS from “semantics.” While the second lemma-only experiment mitigates this, the central outlier analysis relies on the tagged-token embeddings; it would be stronger to repeat the outlier analysis with lemma-only embeddings.
- Cross-validation protocol may overestimate performance due to instance repetition. Training instances are repeated proportionally to frequency. If folds are created at the instance level rather than by type, the same word vector can appear in both train and test folds, leading to overly optimistic F-scores (0.979). Although the separate BNC test-set result (F≈0.91) alleviates this concern, the paper should clarify fold construction and ideally report type-level CV.
- Comparison to Stanford tagger is methodologically unbalanced. The tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens, a use case it is not designed for, and with a tagset mapping. While the intent is to compare error profiles without context, the conclusions drawn from this comparison are limited. A fairer baseline would be a classifier trained on surface or character features without context, or a contextual tagger evaluated with its intended inputs.
- Novelty is modest relative to prior work. Prior studies have shown that embeddings cluster by PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties. The main added value here is the systematic use of misclassifications to detect annotation issues and graded boundaries, and a lightweight contextless predictor; these are useful but incremental.
- Limited validation of claimed annotation errors and feature analysis. The paper reports compelling cases (e.g., adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs) but lacks manual validation or inter-annotator checks to confirm errors vs. genuine ambiguity. The dimension-importance analysis uses training accuracy; test-set curves would better support the claim about distributed information and reduce concerns about overfitting.

Questions to Authors
- Can you replicate the outlier/error analyses using embeddings trained on lemmas only (no PoS in token identity)? Do you recover similar boundary cases and BNC inconsistencies?
- How were cross-validation folds constructed for the frequency-weighted training set? Were folds created by word type or by repeated instances? Please report type-level CV results.
- For the feature-ranking experiment (varying top-k dimensions), can you report test-set accuracy (not just training) to substantiate the distributed-information claim?
- How sensitive are your findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, dimension, negative samples) and preprocessing (case normalization, multiword handling)? Any notable shifts in the outlier patterns?
- For the lemma-only predictor, what is the rationale for the 1/2 dominance threshold to define “unambiguous” lemmas? How does accuracy change with stricter thresholds?
- For the BNC inconsistency claims (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as adverbs), could you provide a small-scale manual validation or external evidence to confirm systematic tagging errors?

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically sound in its core experiments, and offers useful analytical insights. The main limitations concern potential inflation introduced by training embeddings on PoS-tagged tokens, limited validation of discovered inconsistencies, and modest novelty. Addressing the methodological clarifications and adding lemma-only outlier analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution.
