- Word embeddings contain robust information about PoS classes (F-score 0.979 on BNC).  
- Analysis of misclassified words reveals linguistic boundary phenomena between PoS classes.  
- Distributional models can detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora.  
- PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (~100), not concentrated in few.  

- Detailed error analysis provides evidence for fluid PoS boundaries and identifies specific ambiguous cases and annotation mistakes.  
- Results are validated across multiple corpora (BNC and UD Treebank) with consistent error patterns and high performance.  
- Method offers practical utility for corpus linguistics by identifying systematic tagging errors.  
- The finding that PoS signals spread over ~100 dimensions offers theoretical insight into how embeddings encode linguistic information.  

- Core method (classifier on embeddings to predict PoS) lacks strong novelty and builds on prior work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al. 2015).  
- No concrete comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers is provided, weakening claims about robustness.  
- The approach is similar to traditional PoS error analysis and the paper does not clearly distinguish its advantages over standard methods.  
- Theoretical claims that PoS boundaries are "non-categorical" are overstated; findings are consistent with borderline cases rather than wholesale non-categorical boundaries.  

- How does the claim of PoS information being spread over ~100 components reconcile with prior work showing specific dimensions correlate with linguistic features?  
- What minimum corpus frequency is required for reliable representation and correct classification of rare proper nouns?  
- How can annotation errors be reliably distinguished from distributional model mistakes?  

- Overall: valuable contribution through analysis of how PoS information is encoded in embeddings and practical corpus-quality applications, but methodological novelty and comparisons to strong baselines are lacking.
