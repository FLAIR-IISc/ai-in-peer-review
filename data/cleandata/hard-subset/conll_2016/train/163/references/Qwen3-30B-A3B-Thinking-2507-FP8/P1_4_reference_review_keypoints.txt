- Paper shows PoS information is robustly encoded in word embeddings; a classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979) predicting PoS from embeddings.
- Proposes using classifier misclassifications to systematically identify PoS boundary cases and annotation errors in corpora.
- Finds PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (claimed at least ~100 of 300).
- Novel use of distributional models to analyze linguistic PoS boundaries, with theoretical and practical implications.
- Methodology is rigorous with strong evaluation across datasets (BNC, UD Treebank) and meaningful error analysis.
- Provides concrete linguistic findings and documented annotation inconsistencies (e.g., adjectives mislabeled as adverbs in BNC; currency amounts mis-tagged).
- Demonstrates practical value for low-resource languages by building a PoS predictor with minimal manual tagging (≈1564 unambiguous words).
- Title/claims overstate impact: paper identifies boundary cases and errors but does not truly "redefine" PoS classes.
- Insufficient discussion of why performance differs between UD Treebank (F=0.99) and BNC (F=0.91), limiting assessment of generalizability.
- Lacks meaningful comparison with existing error-detection methods (e.g., standard PoS taggers), weakening claims of unique usefulness.
- Claim that PoS information is distributed among ~100 components is under-justified statistically; necessity of 100 components not firmly shown.
- Question: Explain the BNC vs. UD performance gap and its implications for generalizability across corpora.
- Question: Provide stronger statistical evidence that ~100 components are necessary for encoding PoS information.
- Question: How would this approach integrate into PoS tagging pipelines for morphologically rich languages and what additional tagging is required?
- Overall recommendation: Strong, valuable paper with sound methodology and insights; accept with minor revisions to temper claims and address comparisons/limitations.
