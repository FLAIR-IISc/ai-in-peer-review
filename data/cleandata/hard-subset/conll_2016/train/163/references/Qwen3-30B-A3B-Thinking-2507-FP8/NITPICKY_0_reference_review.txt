

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) can reveal meaningful boundaries between parts-of-speech (PoS) classes by training classifiers to predict PoS tags from embeddings. The authors claim that misclassifications highlight "outliers" at PoS boundaries, potentially revealing annotation inconsistencies in existing corpora. They also attempt to build a PoS tagger using only word embeddings without pre-existing tags.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Misclassification analysis** revealing fuzzy PoS boundaries (most significant contribution)
2. **Detection of annotation inconsistencies** in PoS-tagged corpora (secondary contribution)
3. **Demonstration of PoS information distribution** across embedding dimensions (tertiary contribution)

## Strengths
1. **Insightful outlier analysis**: The paper successfully identifies meaningful patterns at PoS boundaries (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, currency terms as numerals), providing linguistically interpretable insights that could inform corpus annotation practices.

2. **Robust methodology for PoS boundary analysis**: The approach of using distributional models to detect "outliers" from PoS classifiers offers a novel way to systematically examine boundary cases, complementing traditional manual analysis.

3. **Demonstration of embedding utility**: The paper convincingly shows that embedding space contains meaningful PoS information, with the classifier achieving high accuracy (F-score 0.91 on unseen data), suggesting practical utility for PoS-related tasks.

## Weaknesses
1. **Fundamental methodological confusion**: The paper's core classifier (Section 3) uses word+tag concatenation as input ("love_VERB"), making it a "word+tag" classifier rather than a true PoS tagger. This undermines the claim that the model predicts PoS without pre-existing tags, as the input already contains the PoS information. This is a critical flaw that misrepresents the contribution.

2. **Inconsistent corpus handling**: The paper claims to train on BNC but then uses UD Treebank as the source of "unambiguous" training words (Section 5), creating a circular evaluation where the classifier is trained on UD data but evaluated on UD data. This invalidates the claimed "without pre-existing tags" approach.

3. **Misunderstanding of Universal Dependencies tagset**: The paper incorrectly states that UD separates "1$" into "NUM" and "SYM" tokens, when in reality UD would tokenize "1$" as two separate tokens ("1" and "$") rather than treating "1$" as a single token to be split. This demonstrates a fundamental misunderstanding of the tagset they claim to use.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation reporting**: The paper states they used "17,000 vectors" for testing but then mentions "30,710 instances" (weighted by frequency), creating confusion about the actual test set size. The 0.99 F-score on UD Treebank is also unexplained given the 27% word type exclusion rate.

5. **Critical citation error**: The paper misattributes Biber et al. (1999) as "The handbook of English linguistics," when in fact the work is "Longman Grammar of Spoken and Written English." This is a basic scholarly error that undermines credibility.

## Questions to Authors
1. How exactly does the "word+tag" classifier (Section 3) differ from a standard PoS tagger, given that the input already contains the PoS information? Please clarify the distinction between your classifier and traditional PoS tagging.

2. In Section 5, you claim to train a classifier "without pre-existing tags," but your training data comes from UD Treebank. How is this different from using a standard PoS tagger with UD as training data?

3. The paper states "the classifier showed an F-Score equal to 0.99" on UD Treebank, but 27% of word types were excluded due to absence in the model. Please clarify whether the F-score is reported on the full test set (including excluded words) or only on the included words.

## Final Recommendation
While the paper presents interesting insights about PoS boundaries, the fundamental methodological confusion between "word+tag" classification and true PoS tagging, combined with the other critical errors (tagset misunderstanding, inconsistent corpus handling, and citation error), significantly undermine the paper's validity. The core contribution is misrepresented, and the evaluation is compromised by these methodological flaws. A paper making such basic errors in fundamental linguistic and computational concepts should not be accepted at a top-tier conference like CoNLL.

**Recommendation: Reject**
