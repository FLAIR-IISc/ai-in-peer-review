

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, beyond their semantic content. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings (from a Continuous Skipgram model trained on the British National Corpus) to predict PoS tags using the Universal PoS Tagset. They achieve high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on unseen test data), then analyze misclassified words ("outliers") to identify cases where PoS boundaries are fuzzy or where annotation errors might exist. The authors further demonstrate that a classifier trained on prototypical examples can predict PoS tags for words without pre-existing tags (accuracy 0.84 on UD Treebank test set), showing that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. **Demonstrating robust PoS information in distributional models**: The paper provides strong evidence that word embeddings contain reliable information about PoS classes, with high classification accuracy that generalizes to unseen data.
2. **Linguistic insights from "outlier" analysis**: The authors identify specific cases where PoS boundaries are fuzzy (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns) and reveal potential annotation inconsistencies in existing corpora.
3. **Distribution of PoS information across embedding dimensions**: The paper shows that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of word embeddings (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), rather than concentrated in a few specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of distributional models to PoS boundary analysis**: The paper makes a compelling case that distributional models can reveal linguistic insights about PoS boundaries that traditional methods might miss. By analyzing misclassified words, the authors identify specific cases where PoS categories blur (e.g., "white-collar" being classified as an adjective when tagged as a noun in BNC), which aligns with linguistic intuition about these fuzzy boundaries. This approach offers a data-driven perspective on long-standing linguistic debates about PoS boundaries.

2. **Practical utility for corpus annotation**: The paper demonstrates that distributional models can detect annotation errors that might otherwise go unnoticed. For example, the authors identify cases where adjectives like "plain" or "clear" are incorrectly tagged as adverbs in the BNC. This kind of insight could significantly improve the quality of PoS-tagged corpora, which are foundational for many NLP tasks. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger (Table 3-4) further highlights how distributional models reveal different error patterns than traditional taggers.

3. **Important insight about information distribution in embeddings**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (rather than concentrated in a few specific features) is a significant contribution to understanding the linguistic content of word embeddings. The authors provide compelling evidence for this through their experiments showing smooth accuracy improvement as more dimensions are used (Figure 3), which aligns with the theoretical foundation of embedding models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the outlier analysis is interesting, the core approach of using word embeddings for PoS classification is not novel. Previous work (Tsuboi, 2014; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already established that distributional models contain PoS information. The paper's main contribution is the analysis of misclassified words, but this is not sufficiently novel compared to what has been done in corpus linguistics. The authors could have strengthened their contribution by providing a more systematic comparison with existing linguistic analyses of PoS boundaries.

2. **Lack of meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The paper claims their classifier achieves "an accuracy of 0.84" on the UD Treebank test set but fails to compare this to modern state-of-the-art PoS taggers, which typically achieve F-scores of 0.95-0.98 on English. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed method is actually competitive with current approaches. The paper acknowledges that these numbers "may not seem very impressive," but doesn't provide concrete benchmarks for comparison.

3. **Superficial analysis of "outliers"**: While the paper identifies interesting misclassification cases, the analysis of these outliers is somewhat superficial. For example, the authors note that "proper nouns predicted as common nouns" is a frequent error but don't provide a deep linguistic analysis of why these words are misclassified. They also don't explore how these "outliers" might be reclassified based on the distributional evidence or how this might inform linguistic theory. The analysis feels more like a catalog of misclassifications rather than a systematic exploration of PoS boundaries.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the classifier achieves "an accuracy of 0.84" on the UD Treebank test set. Could you provide a comparison with the performance of state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., the Stanford PoS Tagger or modern neural taggers) on the same dataset? How does your approach compare to these systems in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and the ability to handle ambiguous cases?

2. The paper states that "PoS information is distributed among at least one hundred components of the word embeddings." How does this finding compare to other work on the linguistic interpretability of word embeddings? For example, Tsvetkov et al. (2015) found that specific dimensions of embeddings correspond to specific linguistic features. How do you reconcile your finding that PoS information is distributed with their finding that specific dimensions correspond to specific features?

3. The authors note that "the model has trained to distinguish proper names by contexts like 'My name is'." Could you elaborate on how the model might be trained to distinguish proper names from common nouns in contexts where "My name is" is not present (e.g., in the middle of a sentence)?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that distributional models contain robust information about PoS classes and using this to identify cases of fuzzy PoS boundaries. The outlier analysis provides interesting linguistic insights that could inform corpus annotation practices. However, the novelty of the core approach is limited, and the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers and a deeper linguistic analysis of the identified outliers. With these improvements, this work could make a significant contribution to both computational linguistics and linguistic theory.
