

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, and whether this information can be leveraged to identify boundary cases between PoS categories and detect annotation errors in existing corpora. The authors propose a novel approach where they train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings, then analyze the "outliers" (words misclassified by the model) to identify cases where traditional PoS boundaries appear ambiguous or inconsistent.

The main contributions of this work are:

1. Demonstrating that PoS information is robustly encoded in distributional semantic models, with a simple classifier achieving high accuracy (F-score 0.979) in predicting PoS tags from embeddings.
2. Providing a systematic method to identify boundary cases between PoS categories and potential annotation errors in existing corpora through error analysis of the classifier.
3. Revealing that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings (at least 100 components in their 300-dimensional model), rather than concentrated in a few specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of distributional models for linguistic analysis**: The paper makes a significant contribution by using distributional models not just for semantic tasks but to analyze the fundamental linguistic concept of part-of-speech boundaries. The authors go beyond the standard "does this information exist?" question to show how this information can be used to identify linguistic boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies, which has clear theoretical and practical implications for linguistics and NLP.

2. **Rigorous methodology and robust evaluation**: The authors employ a well-designed methodology with multiple evaluation metrics across different datasets (BNC test set, UD Treebank). The classifier achieves impressive results (F-score 0.91 on unseen BNC data and 0.99 on UD Treebank), and the error analysis reveals meaningful linguistic insights rather than just technical mistakes. The systematic analysis of misclassifications by coverage (rather than just frequency) is particularly strong.

3. **Insightful linguistic findings**: The paper provides concrete, well-documented examples of annotation inconsistencies that would be difficult to identify through traditional linguistic analysis alone. For instance, the discovery that adjectives like "plain" are systematically mislabeled as adverbs in BNC (appearing 600+ times) is a clear example of an annotation error that the distributional approach can detect. Similarly, the identification of currency amounts being misclassified as nouns (when they should be numerals) demonstrates how the method can reveal inconsistencies in annotation strategies.

4. **Practical implications for low-resource languages**: The demonstration that a simple PoS predictor can be built with minimal manual tagging (using only 1564 unambiguous words) is particularly valuable for low-resource language processing, where manual tagging is prohibitively expensive. This represents a significant practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and some sections claim the paper "redefines" PoS classes, but the work actually identifies boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies rather than proposing a fundamentally new classification system. The paper demonstrates that existing PoS boundaries are fuzzy, but doesn't provide a new framework for classifying words that would replace the current system. This misalignment between claim and contribution weakens the paper's impact.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The authors don't adequately address why their classifier performs better on UD Treebank (F-score 0.99) than on BNC (F-score 0.91), despite both being English corpora. The UD Treebank uses a different annotation scheme (Universal Dependencies vs. BNC's original tags), which likely explains the performance difference but isn't properly addressed. This omission makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of their approach.

3. **Lack of comparison with existing methods**: The paper doesn't compare its error analysis approach with other methods for detecting annotation errors (e.g., using a standard PoS tagger like Stanford's). The authors mention they did this briefly but don't provide a meaningful comparison showing how their approach reveals different or additional insights. This weakens the claim that their method is "useful" for identifying errors.

4. **Insufficient justification for the "distributed" PoS information claim**: While the authors show accuracy increases with more features, they don't provide strong statistical evidence that 100 components are necessary. The accuracy plateaus around k=100 without reaching 100% accuracy, and they don't demonstrate that the 100 components are truly necessary for good performance. This claim is somewhat speculative without stronger evidence.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the performance difference between BNC (F-score 0.91) and UD Treebank (F-score 0.99)? Is this difference primarily due to annotation differences, and how might this affect the generalizability of your approach to other corpora?

2. The paper claims that PoS information is "distributed among at least one hundred components," but the evidence shows accuracy plateaus around k=100 without reaching ideal performance. Can you provide stronger statistical evidence for why 100 components are necessary, rather than a more conservative estimate?

3. How do you envision your approach being integrated into existing PoS tagging pipelines, especially for languages with more complex morphology than English? Would your method require additional manual tagging for each language?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding how distributional semantic models encode linguistic information. The methodology is sound, the results are robust, and the linguistic insights are significant. The paper makes a compelling case for using distributional models to analyze PoS boundaries and detect annotation errors. However, the overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes and the lack of comparison with alternative methods for error detection weaken the paper's impact. With some revisions to address these issues, this would be an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
