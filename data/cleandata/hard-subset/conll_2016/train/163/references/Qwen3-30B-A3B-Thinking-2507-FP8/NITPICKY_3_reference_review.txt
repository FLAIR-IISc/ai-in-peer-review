

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes and proposes using such models to identify "outliers" - words that behave differently from their traditional PoS classification. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, analyze misclassified words to reveal potential annotation errors and linguistic ambiguities, and demonstrate a simple PoS predictor that works without pre-existing tags.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Methodological approach to identify PoS outliers using distributional models (least novel, as similar techniques exist)
2. Analysis of specific error cases that reveal potential annotation inconsistencies in BNC (moderately novel)
3. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (least significant)

## Strengths
1. The paper provides an interesting analysis of specific linguistic cases where PoS boundaries appear ambiguous (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, verbs vs. adjectives). The examples like "white-collar" and "1804" are well-chosen and demonstrate genuine linguistic insights.

2. The comparison between their distributional approach and standard PoS taggers (Stanford) reveals different error patterns, suggesting distributional models can uncover different types of annotation issues. This methodological insight has value for corpus linguistics.

3. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (not concentrated in a few features) is a useful observation that aligns with theoretical expectations about distributional semantics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Flaw**: The paper trains word embeddings on "lemmas concatenated with PoS tags" (e.g., "love_VERB"), which is not standard practice and fundamentally misrepresents how word embeddings work. This invalidates the core premise that the embeddings contain "unbiased" semantic information. The authors claim to "produce a version of BNC where all words were replaced with their lemmas and PoS tags converted into Universal tags," but then treat these as separate word types. This means the embedding model is learning to distinguish between "love_VERB" and "love_NOUN" as distinct words, not learning semantic features that could predict PoS. This is a fundamental methodological error that undermines the entire approach.

2. **Missing Figures**: The paper refers to "Figures 1 and 2" showing prototypical embeddings for conjunctions, but no figures are provided. Figure 3 is also mentioned but not included. This is a severe formatting inconsistency that prevents evaluation of their key visual evidence.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology and Data Handling**: The paper switches between "word types" and "tokens" without clear distinction. They claim to work with "10,000 most frequent words" (word types), but then state they "trained on 177,343 instances" by weighting by frequency. This inconsistency makes it impossible to understand whether they're using word types or tokens as the basic unit of analysis. Similarly, when discussing "17,000 vectors for words with BNC frequencies between 100 and 500," it's unclear whether these are word types or tokens.

4. **Inadequate Baseline Comparisons**: The paper compares to a "majority class baseline" (classify everything as nouns) and a "one-feature baseline," but these are not meaningful baselines for PoS tagging. The one-feature baseline is poorly explained (what does "one vector dimension with maximum F-value" mean?), and the paper fails to compare against standard PoS taggers or established distributional methods.

5. **Formatting and Citation Issues**: The reference list contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., "Manning.,? \Q2011\E, Manning.,"). There are inconsistent citation styles (some with parentheses, some with brackets), and the paper contains an anomalous "Anonymized" note. The paper also references Tables 1 and 2 that are not present in the submission.

## Questions to Authors
1. How exactly did you train the word embeddings on "lemmas concatenated with PoS tags"? Did you treat "love_VERB" as a single token in the training corpus, and if so, how does this not fundamentally bias the embeddings toward PoS information?

2. Can you provide the URL for the BNC-to-Universal tag mappings you created manually, as stated in the paper?

3. Why did you choose the specific frequency range of 100-500 for your test set, and how does this relate to your training set (10,000 most frequent words)?

## Overall Recommendation
Reject

The paper presents an interesting linguistic analysis of PoS outliers, but its core methodology is fatally flawed due to the inappropriate concatenation of PoS tags with lemmas during embedding training. This invalidates the entire approach, as the embeddings would inherently encode PoS information rather than learning it from semantic context. The missing figures, inconsistent terminology, and poor baseline comparisons further undermine the work. While the insights about specific linguistic cases are valuable, they cannot compensate for these fundamental methodological errors. The paper would require complete reworking of its methodology to be considered for publication. The minor flaws identified here are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they undermine the validity of the paper's core claims.
