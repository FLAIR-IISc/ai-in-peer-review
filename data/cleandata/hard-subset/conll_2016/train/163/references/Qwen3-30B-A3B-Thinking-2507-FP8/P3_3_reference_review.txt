

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, beyond just semantic information. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings, analyze misclassifications to identify boundary cases between PoS categories, and demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust PoS information, with a classifier achieving 0.979 F-score on a balanced test set
2. Identifying genuine linguistic boundary cases through misclassifications (e.g., currency amounts classified as numerals, "white-collar" as noun)
3. Showing that PoS information is distributed across at least 100 vector dimensions, not concentrated in specific features

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence for PoS information in embeddings**: The paper provides convincing evidence that word embeddings contain meaningful PoS-related information. The high classifier performance (0.979 F-score) on a balanced test set is compelling, especially given the classifier was trained on embeddings derived from PoS-tagged data. The robustness of the model is further demonstrated by its performance on the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99).

2. **Insightful analysis of boundary cases**: The paper's most significant contribution is the careful analysis of misclassifications, which reveals genuine linguistic phenomena rather than just model errors. Cases like currency amounts ("£70") being correctly classified as numerals (not nouns), and the observation that 90% of nouns misclassified as numerals are actually currency amounts, provide concrete evidence of annotation inconsistencies in the BNC. This is particularly valuable for corpus linguists.

3. **Demonstration of distributed PoS information**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300) is a significant insight about how linguistic information is encoded in embeddings. This challenges the common assumption that specific linguistic features might be localized to particular vector dimensions, and aligns with the theoretical underpinnings of distributional semantics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Circular dependency in methodology**: The paper creates a circular dependency by training embeddings on data with PoS tags concatenated to words ("love_VERB" and "love_NOUN"), then using these embeddings to predict PoS tags. This makes the high classifier performance somewhat expected and doesn't demonstrate that embeddings contain PoS information independently of the tagging process. A more convincing experiment would train embeddings on untagged data first.

2. **Overstated contribution**: The title and claims of "redefining" PoS classes are overstated. The paper identifies ambiguous cases and potential annotation errors but doesn't propose concrete new PoS categories or a revised classification system. The findings are more about identifying boundary cases than redefining categories.

3. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: While the paper mentions that the classifier's accuracy (0.84) is lower than modern taggers, it doesn't provide a meaningful comparison that would contextualize this result. For example, it doesn't compare against a baseline that uses only the word form (without embeddings) or against a simple rule-based system.

4. **Insufficient linguistic justification for misclassifications**: The paper often treats the model's classification as linguistically correct (e.g., "white-collar" should be a noun), without providing sufficient linguistic evidence. The claim that "white-collar" is a noun rather than an adjective is debatable and requires stronger linguistic justification beyond the model's prediction.

5. **Limited discussion of practical implications**: The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss how these findings would impact practical PoS tagging systems or linguistic theory. The potential use case of "preliminary tagging of large corpora of resource-poor languages" is mentioned but not developed with concrete examples.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the results change if the embeddings were trained on untagged data (without PoS tags concatenated to words), rather than on PoS-tagged data as used in the current experiment?

2. Can you provide more linguistic evidence to support your claim that "white-collar" should be classified as a noun rather than an adjective, rather than relying solely on the model's classification?

3. How do you envision these findings being incorporated into practical PoS tagging systems or linguistic theory, beyond identifying potential annotation errors?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of how linguistic information is encoded in word embeddings. The identification of genuine boundary cases through misclassifications is particularly insightful and has potential utility for corpus linguistics. However, the circular methodology and overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes are significant weaknesses that need addressing. With revisions to address these methodological concerns and provide clearer linguistic justification for the misclassification analysis, this paper could make a strong contribution to the field.

The paper's most significant contribution is not the classifier itself, but the demonstration that distributional models can reveal genuine linguistic phenomena at the boundaries of PoS categories. This insight is valuable for both linguistic theory and corpus annotation practices, though the paper would be stronger with a more rigorous methodology and clearer articulation of its contribution to the field.
