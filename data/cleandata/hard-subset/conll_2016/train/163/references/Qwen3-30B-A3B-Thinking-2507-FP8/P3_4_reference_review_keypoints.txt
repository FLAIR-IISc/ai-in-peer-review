- Paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode meaningful part-of-speech (PoS) information using BNC-trained embeddings and logistic regression.
- Authors achieve high PoS prediction performance (F-score 0.979 on BNC; 0.99 on UD Treebank) showing strong PoS signal in embeddings.
- Analysis of misclassifications identifies linguistic “outliers” and potential PoS annotation errors in corpora.
- Main contribution: demonstrates PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (at least ~100), not localized.
- Main contribution: presents a method to systematically detect potential PoS annotation issues via distributional misclassifications.
- Main contribution: proposes a low-resource, context-free PoS tagging approach achieving ~84% accuracy from a small unambiguous-word training set.
- Strength: compelling evidence for distributed PoS encoding via prototypical vector and feature-importance analyses.
- Strength: insightful error analysis revealing genuine linguistic patterns and corpus annotation issues (e.g., proper noun vs. common noun confusions).
- Strength: cross-corpus validation on UD Treebank supports generalizability beyond the BNC.
- Weakness: title/claims overstate impact—paper identifies outliers but does not actually redefine PoS categories or propose a new system.
- Weakness: classifier training on word+PoS tokens (e.g., "love_VERB") is artificial and departs from practical PoS tagging setups.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art/contextual taggers (modern taggers reach ≈97%+), so practical advantage is unclear.
- Weakness: claims of annotation "errors" lack sufficient linguistic justification and alternative grammatical analyses are not ruled out.
- Weakness: component-level analysis (e.g., dimension 94 for conjunctions) is underdeveloped and lacks linguistic interpretation.
- Question: how would these findings be translated into a practical redefinition of PoS categories or annotation guidelines?
- Question: how would incorporating contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) affect performance and trade-offs compared to the current method?
- Question: what linguistic evidence supports labeling identified misclassifications as annotation errors rather than valid alternative analyses?
- Overall: solid, interesting contribution on how PoS is encoded in embeddings, but claims overreach and methodology/comparisons need strengthening.
