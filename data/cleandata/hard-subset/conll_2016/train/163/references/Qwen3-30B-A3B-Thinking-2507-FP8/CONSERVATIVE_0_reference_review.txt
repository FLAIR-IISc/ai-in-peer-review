

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Claimed Contributions
The paper claims that distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes that can be used to identify "outlier" words on the boundaries between traditional PoS categories, detect possible annotation errors in corpora, and build a PoS tagger. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, achieve high accuracy (F-score 0.979), and analyze misclassifications to identify cases where traditional PoS tagging might be inconsistent.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstrating that word embeddings contain PoS information (not novel)
2. Identifying some questionable cases in BNC's PoS annotations (limited scope)
3. Showing PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (minor insight)

## Strengths
The paper uses well-established methods (word embeddings, logistic regression) on a standard corpus (BNC), with proper documentation of their experimental setup. The analysis of misclassifications does reveal some interesting edge cases where traditional PoS tagging might be questionable. The methodology is sound and the results are properly evaluated with cross-validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core claim**: The paper's fundamental premise—that distributional models contain PoS information—has been well-established for years. Mikolov et al. (2013b) demonstrated regular relations between words from different classes, Liu et al. (2016) showed clustering of words by PoS, and Tsvetkov et al. (2015) proved vector dimensions correlate with linguistic features. The paper merely replicates and slightly extends this known finding without providing sufficient evidence that this approach offers anything fundamentally new. The authors claim to "redefine" PoS classes, but their analysis merely identifies edge cases that linguists have long recognized.

2. **Insufficient comparison to established baselines**: The paper compares its classifier to a "majority class baseline" (F-score 0.07) and a "one-feature baseline" (F-score 0.22), but fails to compare against standard PoS taggers. For context, state-of-the-art neural PoS taggers (e.g., those using BERT) typically achieve F-scores above 0.99 on standard benchmarks. The paper's classifier (F-score 0.938 on training, 0.84 on test) is significantly worse than existing methods, yet the authors present it as a "full-fledged PoS tagger" without acknowledging this substantial performance gap.

3. **Overstated significance of findings**: The paper claims that their approach "reveals sub-classes of words lying on the verge between different parts of speech" and "allows for quickly discovering systematic errors in PoS-tagged corpora." However, the identified "errors" are largely consistent with known linguistic phenomena (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns), and the paper provides no evidence that these insights would lead to meaningful improvements in PoS tagging or linguistic theory. The "systematic errors" identified (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs) are already well-documented in linguistic literature.

4. **Inadequate validation of practical utility**: The paper claims their approach could be useful for "preliminary tagging of large corpora of resource-poor languages," but provides no evidence to support this. The classifier's performance (84% accuracy) is significantly worse than established methods, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how this would be practically useful compared to existing techniques. The authors also don't address how their approach would handle the complexities of morphologically rich languages.

5. **Misleading presentation of results**: The paper emphasizes the "distribution" of PoS information across vector dimensions (Figure 3), but the main insight—that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions—has been known since the early days of word embeddings. The authors present this as a significant finding, when it's actually a basic property of distributed representations. The "strongly expressed component 94" for conjunctions is presented as a major discovery, but this appears to be a case of overfitting to a specific corpus.

## Questions for Authors
1. Given that state-of-the-art PoS taggers achieve F-scores above 0.99, what specific advantage does your approach offer over existing methods? How would you integrate your method into a production PoS tagging pipeline?
2. The paper claims to identify "systematic errors" in BNC's PoS tagging. How would you propose resolving these inconsistencies? Have you consulted with linguists about your findings?
3. The classifier's performance (84% accuracy) is substantially worse than established methods. What evidence do you have that your approach would be practically useful for low-resource language settings, as claimed?

## Conclusion
This paper presents a methodologically sound but unoriginal study that replicates known findings about the relationship between distributional models and PoS information without providing sufficient evidence of novelty or practical utility. While the analysis of misclassifications reveals some interesting edge cases, the paper overstates the significance of these findings and fails to demonstrate how the approach would improve upon existing PoS tagging methods. The authors should substantially revise their claims to reflect the well-established nature of their core findings and provide a more rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods before considering this work for publication.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Removing all claims of "redefining" PoS classes
2. Providing a thorough comparison to established PoS taggers
3. Demonstrating clear practical utility beyond the academic exercise of identifying edge cases
4. Acknowledging the well-established literature on distributional models and PoS

Without these revisions, the paper's contribution is marginal and does not meet the high standards expected for CoNLL.
