- Paper investigates whether distributional semantic models encode PoS categories and uses classifier misclassifications to identify PoS boundary cases.
- Main contribution: shows PoS information is robustly embedded and distributed across many embedding dimensions (≈100/300).
- Main contribution: presents a method to detect PoS boundary cases via classifier misclassifications.
- Main contribution: uncovers potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC corpus.
- Strength: provides a novel, data-driven method that exposes systematic corpus annotation errors (e.g., currency tagged as NOUN vs NUM).
- Strength: empirically demonstrates PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions, not concentrated in few.
- Strength: methodological contribution by analyzing misclassifications to study fuzzy PoS boundaries and contextual similarities between tags.
- Weakness: methodological circularity—embeddings trained on lemma+PoS may force the classifier to learn the tags used to create embeddings.
- Weakness: overstates the significance of “boundary cases,” conflating inherent PoS ambiguity with true category boundaries.
- Weakness: limited and unfair comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers that use richer contextual information.
- Weakness: practical claims about low-resource tagging are overstated—1564 manually identified unambiguous lemmas is not “a handful.”
- Question: how would results change if embeddings were trained on plain lemmas without PoS tags?
- Question: how do findings engage with theoretical work on grammatical continua (e.g., grammaticalization literature)?
- Question: how scalable is the unambiguous-lemmas approach to languages with more complex PoS systems or less consistent resources?
- Overall assessment: useful for identifying annotation inconsistencies but limited by circular methodology, overstated claims, and insufficient comparisons; further work needed to strengthen theoretical and empirical claims.
