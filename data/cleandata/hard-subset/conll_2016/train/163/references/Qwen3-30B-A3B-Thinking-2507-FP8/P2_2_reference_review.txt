

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, achieving high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on test data). They then analyze misclassifications ("outliers") to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and potential annotation errors in the British National Corpus (BNC). The paper also demonstrates a context-free PoS tagger trained solely on word embeddings, achieving 84% accuracy on the Universal Dependencies Treebank.

The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength):
1. Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings (at least 100 components in a 300-dimensional model), not concentrated in specific features
2. Providing a novel method to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and systematic annotation errors through distributional analysis
3. Developing a context-free PoS tagger based solely on word embeddings (without contextual information)

## Strengths

1. **Novel insight about distributed PoS information**: The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features. The analysis in Section 5 (Figure 3) demonstrates that accuracy improves steadily as more features are added, reaching near-perfect performance only when using around 100 features. This challenges the common assumption that linguistic features would be localized in specific embedding dimensions and provides a more nuanced understanding of how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models.

2. **Practical tool for corpus annotation**: The authors provide a valuable method for identifying potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora. Their analysis reveals systematic errors in the BNC (e.g., adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs), which traditional tagger confusion matrices would miss. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger (Table 3-4) demonstrates that distributional analysis reveals different error patterns, suggesting it's a complementary tool for corpus linguists to identify annotation inconsistencies.

3. **Robust empirical validation**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence through multiple validation steps: 10-fold cross-validation on BNC, testing on Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99), and analysis of both training and test sets. The authors also demonstrate the method's robustness across different corpora, showing that their findings aren't specific to the BNC.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated contribution regarding "redefining" PoS classes**: The paper's title and claims overstate its contribution. While the authors identify boundary cases between PoS classes, they don't actually propose a new classification system or redefine PoS categories. They merely observe that certain words behave differently from their assigned categories. The paper would have been stronger if it had proposed a refined classification system based on their findings rather than simply documenting boundary cases.

2. **Limited practical significance of the context-free tagger**: The paper's context-free PoS tagger (Section 5) achieves only 84% accuracy, which is substantially lower than state-of-the-art taggers (typically >97% accuracy). The paper fails to convincingly argue why this method has practical value compared to existing techniques. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address why their approach would be useful in real-world applications.

3. **Insufficient differentiation from prior work**: The paper doesn't clearly distinguish its contributions from related work. Tsuboi (2014) already used embeddings to improve PoS tagging, and Liu et al. (2016) demonstrated PoS clustering in distributional models. The paper should more explicitly explain how its approach differs from these prior methods and why it advances the field beyond them.

4. **Lack of theoretical contribution**: While the paper provides interesting empirical observations about PoS boundaries, it doesn't advance linguistic theory about why these boundaries exist or how they should be conceptualized. The paper remains descriptive rather than explanatory, failing to contribute to the theoretical understanding of part-of-speech classification.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper identifies systematic annotation errors (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs), but doesn't discuss how these errors might be corrected in practice. How would you propose to revise the BNC annotation guidelines based on your findings?

2. Your context-free tagger achieves 84% accuracy, but you don't compare it to other context-free approaches. How does your method compare to simpler baseline approaches like using word frequency or morphological features for PoS prediction?

3. The paper claims PoS information is distributed across "at least one hundred components," but Figure 3 shows accuracy continues to improve beyond k=100. What theoretical implications does this have for understanding how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable empirical contribution by demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings and by providing a novel method to identify boundary cases between PoS classes. The findings about annotation errors in the BNC are particularly insightful and useful for corpus linguists. However, the paper overstates its contribution regarding "redefining" PoS classes and doesn't sufficiently address the practical limitations of its approach. The context-free tagger's performance is not competitive with existing methods, and the paper fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work on using embeddings for PoS tagging.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a clearer theoretical contribution about PoS boundaries, a more compelling argument for the practical utility of their approach, and a more precise differentiation from prior work. With these improvements, it would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid empirical study with some important insights but limited theoretical and practical significance.
