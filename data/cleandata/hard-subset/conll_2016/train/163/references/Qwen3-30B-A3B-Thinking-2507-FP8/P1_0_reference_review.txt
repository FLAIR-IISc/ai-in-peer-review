

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about parts of speech (PoS), and whether this information can be used to refine PoS classes and identify boundary cases between word classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassified words to identify linguistic phenomena at PoS boundaries. Their main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust information about PoS classes (achieving F-score 0.979 on the BNC corpus)
2. Providing insightful linguistic analysis of "outlier" words that reveal boundary phenomena between PoS classes
3. Showing that distributional models can detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora
4. Demonstrating that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of the embedding space

## Strengths

1. **Insightful error analysis of PoS boundaries**: The paper's strongest contribution is its detailed analysis of misclassified words, revealing meaningful linguistic phenomena at the boundaries between PoS classes. The authors identify specific cases where the distributional model detects genuine linguistic ambiguity (e.g., "white-collar" being classified as an adjective despite being tagged as an adjective in BNC), contextual similarities between seemingly distinct classes (proper nouns vs. common nouns), and annotation errors (adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs). This analysis provides concrete evidence for the fluidity of PoS boundaries, which is a significant theoretical contribution to linguistic theory.

2. **Validation across multiple corpora**: The authors demonstrate robustness of their approach by testing the classifier on independent datasets (BNC test set, Universal Dependencies Treebank). The high performance on the UD Treebank (F-score 0.99) and the consistency of error patterns across datasets provide strong evidence that their findings are not specific to the BNC corpus.

3. **Practical utility for corpus linguistics**: The paper demonstrates that distributional models can detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora, which has direct practical implications for corpus linguists. For example, the authors identify a systematic error in BNC where adjectives like "plain" are frequently mislabeled as adverbs, which distributional models can detect. This provides a valuable tool for corpus quality assessment and refinement.

4. **Theoretical insight on feature distribution**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across approximately 100 dimensions of the embedding space (rather than concentrated in a few features) provides important theoretical insight into how linguistic information is encoded in word embeddings. This aligns with the underlying principles of distributional semantics and helps explain why simple classifiers can achieve high accuracy.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core method**: While the paper's analysis is insightful, the core method (training a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS) builds directly on existing work by Tsvetkov et al. (2015) and others who have shown that distributional models contain linguistic information. The paper does not present a significantly novel methodological contribution, which weakens its claim to be a "redefining" approach.

2. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The authors acknowledge that their classifier's performance "may not seem very impressive in comparison with the performance of modern state-of-the-art PoS taggers," but they do not provide a concrete comparison. This omission weakens their argument about the robustness of the information contained in word embeddings. A direct comparison to a strong baseline PoS tagger (e.g., Stanford POS Tagger) would have strengthened their case.

3. **Lack of clear distinction from traditional error analysis**: The paper's approach to error analysis (training a classifier and examining misclassifications) is similar to standard approaches used in PoS tagger evaluation. The authors do not clearly articulate how their distributional approach differs from and improves upon traditional error analysis methods. For example, they mention comparing their results to the Stanford POS Tagger's confusion matrix but fail to show how their approach yields different or more useful insights.

4. **Ambiguous theoretical claim about PoS boundaries**: The paper claims that PoS boundaries are "not strict" and "non-categorical," but this interpretation is somewhat overstated. The findings actually support a more nuanced view: while some words exhibit ambiguous behavior, this does not necessarily mean that PoS boundaries are non-categorical. Many linguistic theories maintain that PoS boundaries are categorical, but with some words falling into borderline cases. The paper's findings are consistent with both categorical and non-categorical views of PoS.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the knowledge about PoS affiliation is distributed among at least one hundred components" of the embeddings. How does this finding relate to the work of Tsvetkov et al. (2015), who showed that specific dimensions correlate with linguistic features? Could these findings be reconciled?

2. The authors mention that "the model was just not presented with enough contexts for these words to learn meaningful representations" for rare proper nouns. What would be the minimum corpus frequency needed for the model to correctly classify a proper noun as such?

3. The paper claims that "distributional models can actually detect outright errors in PoS-tagged corpora." How would the authors distinguish between genuine annotation errors and cases where the corpus annotation is correct but the distributional model is incorrect?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how linguistic information is encoded in distributional semantic models, particularly regarding PoS classes. The error analysis provides insightful evidence about the fluidity of PoS boundaries in English. While the core method is not novel, the thoughtful analysis of boundary cases and the practical application to corpus annotation quality assessment make this a strong contribution to the field. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above (particularly adding a comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers), this paper would be a significant contribution to the literature on distributional semantics and linguistic theory.
