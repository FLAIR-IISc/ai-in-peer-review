

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper explores whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, demonstrating high accuracy (0.979 F-score on training data, 0.91 on unseen BNC test data). They analyze misclassified "outlier" words to identify potential annotation errors and boundary cases between PoS classes. The authors also propose a PoS tagger that doesn't require pre-existing PoS tags, achieving 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Demonstration that word embeddings contain PoS information (with high classification accuracy)
2. Identification of potential annotation inconsistencies through outlier analysis
3. A simple PoS tagger that operates without pre-existing PoS tags (though with limited performance)

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear demonstration that word embeddings do contain PoS-related information, as evidenced by the high classification accuracy. This aligns with prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) and represents a solid, replicable finding.

2. The outlier analysis reveals some interesting patterns, particularly regarding the boundary between nouns and adjectives, and the classification of currency amounts as numerals. The comparison with Stanford PoS tagger results (Table 3-4) shows that different error patterns emerge from different approaches, suggesting potential value in using distributional models as complementary tools for error detection.

3. The paper provides a useful visualization of prototypical vectors for different PoS classes (Figures 1-2), which helps illustrate the distributional patterns associated with different grammatical categories.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The authors present a simple logistic regression classifier achieving 84% accuracy, but fail to compare this to established PoS taggers. Modern neural PoS taggers routinely achieve 97-98% accuracy on standard benchmarks. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed approach offers any practical advantage. The authors dismiss this by stating "these numbers may not seem very impressive," but this is precisely the point that needs justification.

2. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The paper's title and claims suggest a fundamental rethinking of PoS categories, but the findings largely confirm what linguists have long known about flexible boundaries between categories. The "outliers" identified (e.g., proper nouns mistaken for common nouns, nouns that behave adjectivally) are well-documented phenomena in linguistic literature, not new discoveries. The paper fails to demonstrate how their approach leads to genuinely novel linguistic insights.

3. **Methodological limitations in the "distributional tagger"**: The authors claim their tagger "can be employed... in preliminary tagging of large corpora of resource-poor languages," but provide no evidence for this. The training requires a corpus with PoS tags (BNC) to create the initial classifier, which defeats the purpose for resource-poor languages. The approach would require manual tagging of a small number of unambiguous words, but the authors don't demonstrate how this would scale or outperform existing zero-shot or transfer learning approaches for low-resource languages.

4. **Insufficient evidence for the "distributed" nature of PoS information**: While the authors claim PoS information is distributed across many vector components (Figure 3), they don't demonstrate that this distribution is meaningful or useful for any practical application. The 100-component threshold (k=100) for good performance is arbitrary, and the paper doesn't show how this knowledge could be leveraged in a way that's superior to existing methods.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your proposed "distributional tagger" compare to established methods for low-resource languages? Specifically, how does it outperform transfer learning approaches from high-resource languages?

2. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but the identified outliers largely confirm existing linguistic knowledge. What specific, novel linguistic insights does this approach provide that couldn't be obtained through traditional corpus analysis?

3. The classifier achieves 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank, while state-of-the-art methods achieve 97-98%. What specific advantages does your approach offer that would justify using a less accurate method in practice?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper demonstrates a well-executed but largely unsurprising finding: that word embeddings contain PoS information. While the outlier analysis provides some interesting observations, the paper fails to demonstrate meaningful novelty or practical advantages over established methods. The claims about "redefining" PoS classes are overstated, and the proposed tagger lacks sufficient evidence of utility. I recommend rejection as the contribution is incremental and lacks sufficient evidence of practical value compared to existing approaches.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers, (2) demonstration of how the approach leads to genuinely new linguistic insights rather than confirming known phenomena, and (3) concrete evidence supporting the claim about utility for low-resource languages. Without these elements, the paper fails to make a compelling case for its contribution to the field.
