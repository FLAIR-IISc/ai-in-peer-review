- Paper tests whether word embeddings encode PoS information by training skip-gram on BNC and a logistic classifier to predict Universal PoS tags, analyzing misclassifications and building a tagger without prior PoS tags.  
- Main contribution: systematic outlier analysis revealing fuzzy PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies.  
- Main contribution: shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than a few features.  
- Main contribution: proposes a PoS tagger using distributional vectors without annotated data (potentially useful for low-resource languages).  
- Strength: provides concrete, data-driven linguistic insights and corpus annotation corrections (e.g., currency tokens, mis-tagged "plain").  
- Strength: demonstrates a novel practical application of distributional models for annotation quality control.  
- Strength: empirical evidence that PoS signal is spread across many vector components (accuracy improves smoothly with more features).  
- Weakness: core methodology lacks novelty and insufficiently distinguishes from prior work (Tsvetkov et al., Liu et al.).  
- Weakness: distributional-only PoS tagger attains only ~84% accuracy, far below state-of-the-art, with limited practical justification for low-resource use.  
- Weakness: linguistic analysis of outliers is superficial and lacks a systematic theoretical framework for redefining PoS categories.  
- Weakness: inadequate comparison with existing PoS taggers and insufficient analysis of differing error patterns.  
- Question: how should linguistic theory or PoS tagsets be revised based on these findings—what concrete redefinitions are proposed?  
- Question: why is PoS information distributed across many vector dimensions and how does this relate to semantic vs. syntactic category theories?  
- Question: how could incorporating context improve the context-free tagger, and how would that affect the linguistic insights?  
- Overall: valuable outlier analysis and annotation-quality contribution, but paper is incremental due to limited novelty, weak tagger performance, and underdeveloped linguistic implications; emphasize outlier analysis as primary contribution.
