

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about parts of speech (PoS) by training classifiers on word embeddings to predict PoS tags. The authors analyze misclassifications to identify "boundary cases" between PoS categories and claim these reveal inconsistencies in existing PoS annotations. They also demonstrate that a distributional model can predict PoS tags without prior tagging.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Identification of specific ambiguous PoS cases through distributional analysis
2. Demonstration that distributional models can reveal annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora
3. Exploration of how PoS information is distributed across vector dimensions

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a novel approach to identifying ambiguous PoS cases that could inform corpus annotation quality control. The analysis of misclassifications reveals specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives) that are not well-documented elsewhere.

2. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100) rather than concentrated in a few features is a valuable insight consistent with embedding theory.

3. The comparison with a standard POS tagger (Stanford) reveals different error patterns, suggesting complementary value in using distributional models for PoS analysis.

## Weaknesses
1. **Misrepresentation of contribution**: The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes but fails to actually propose any new classification system. The paper identifies boundary cases but doesn't redefine the classes, which is a fundamental misalignment between the title and the content. This is a significant methodological and conceptual flaw.

2. **Critical reproducibility issues**: The paper states "mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags were created manually by us and released online" but provides no link or details about these mappings. This is unacceptable for a computational linguistics paper. Additionally, the paper contains "Anonymized" in the middle of the text, indicating an incomplete manuscript.

3. **Methodological inconsistencies**: The paper claims to use the Universal POS Tagset but lists 16 tags (excluding PUNCT but including X), while the Universal Tagset has 17 tags. The paper states they excluded "PUNCT" but then includes "SYM" and "X" - this inconsistency undermines the methodological rigor.

4. **Unsupported claims**: The paper states "it seems that one needs as few as a thousand example words to train a decent classifier" but earlier used 1564 words for training, making this claim unsupported. The paper also claims "the classifier was based only on knowledge of what words occurred in the BNC near other words within a symmetric window of 2 words" - this is inaccurate as the embedding model was trained on the full corpus, not just a window of 2 words.

5. **Lack of generalizability**: The paper only uses English (BNC and UD Treebank) without any evidence of applicability to other languages. The claim of "redefining PoS classes" is limited to English and lacks cross-linguistic validation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please provide the exact mappings between BNC tags and Universal POS tags, along with a link to where they are publicly available.
2. How does the paper reconcile the claim of "redefining PoS classes" with the absence of any proposed new classification system?
3. Why does the paper list 16 Universal tags (excluding PUNCT but including X) when the Universal Tagset includes 17 tags (with PUNCT as a separate category)?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper identifies some interesting boundary cases between PoS categories, the fundamental misrepresentation of its contribution (claiming to "redefine" PoS classes when it only identifies boundary cases) combined with critical reproducibility issues and methodological inconsistencies renders the paper unsuitable for publication. The title and abstract overstate the contribution, and the paper fails to deliver on its promise. The minor technical flaws and inconsistencies identified above collectively undermine the paper's credibility and scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from significant revision to properly address these issues, but as written, it does not meet the standards required for publication at CONLL.

The paper's claim that it "redefines" PoS classes is particularly problematic, as it misleads readers about the paper's actual contribution. A paper that merely identifies boundary cases between existing classes cannot be said to be redefining those classes. This conceptual error, combined with the reproducibility issues and methodological inconsistencies, makes it impossible to recommend acceptance.
