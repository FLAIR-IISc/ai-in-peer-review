

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, using the British National Corpus (BNC) to train word embeddings and a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags. The authors demonstrate that embeddings contain robust PoS information (F-score 0.979), analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic "outliers," and test the classifier on the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99). They argue that these outliers reveal both linguistic phenomena (e.g., borderline cases between word classes) and potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Demonstrating distributed PoS information in embeddings**: The paper compellingly shows that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100), not concentrated in specific features, with evidence from prototypical vector analysis and feature importance experiments.

2. **Systematic identification of annotation issues**: The authors provide a novel method for detecting potential PoS annotation errors in corpora by analyzing misclassifications from a distributional model, identifying specific cases like adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs.

3. **Low-resource PoS tagging approach**: The paper demonstrates that a simple distributional classifier (trained on 1,564 unambiguous words) can achieve 84% accuracy for PoS tagging without contextual information, suggesting potential for low-resource language applications.

## Strengths

1. **Strong evidence for distributed PoS information**: The analysis of prototypical vectors (Figures 1-2) and the smooth performance increase with more features (Figure 3) provide compelling evidence that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions. This challenges the common assumption that linguistic categories might be captured by a few key dimensions.

2. **Insightful error analysis**: The paper identifies genuine linguistic patterns that are both interesting and potentially useful for corpus linguistics. For example, the identification of proper nouns like "Luftwaffe" that are misclassified as common nouns reveals how the model learned features specific to personal names but not place names, which has implications for corpus annotation strategies.

3. **Cross-corpus validation**: The robust performance on the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99) demonstrates that the findings are not specific to the BNC or its annotation scheme, significantly strengthening the paper's claims about the generalizability of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and paper overstate the contribution. The paper identifies "outliers" but does not actually propose a new PoS system or redefine classes. The authors identify words that behave differently from traditional classifications but don't provide a framework for how these insights should change PoS theory or annotation practices.

2. **Artificial classifier training approach**: The classifier in Section 3 is trained on word+PoS tags (e.g., "love_VERB"), which is not how PoS tagging works in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how this affects their interpretation of "outliers" or the practical applicability of their method.

3. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims 84% accuracy is "impressive" for a model without context, but modern neural PoS taggers achieve 97%+ accuracy using contextual information. The paper lacks comparison to even basic taggers (like Stanford Tagger) and doesn't explain why their approach would be preferable to existing methods.

4. **Lack of linguistic justification for "errors"**: The paper identifies cases where BNC might have annotation errors (e.g., "plain" tagged as adverb), but doesn't provide sufficient linguistic evidence that these are indeed errors rather than alternative grammatical analyses. The UD Treebank's different tagging of "50 pounds" as NUM+SYM doesn't necessarily prove BNC is wrong.

5. **Underdeveloped vector component analysis**: The paper identifies component 94 as "idiosyncratic for conjunctions" but provides no linguistic interpretation of what this dimension represents. This limits the paper's contribution to linguistic theory.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but doesn't propose a new classification system. How would you integrate these findings into a practical redefinition of PoS categories, beyond simply identifying outliers?

2. The classifier in Section 5 achieves 84% accuracy, significantly below modern taggers (97%+). How would incorporating contextual information (e.g., using BERT embeddings) improve your method, and what would be the trade-offs?

3. The paper identifies "systematic errors" like "plain" tagged as adverb. What linguistic evidence supports your claim that these are errors rather than alternative grammatical analyses?

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding how PoS information is encoded in word embeddings. The evidence for distributed PoS information is strong, and the error analysis reveals genuine insights about corpus annotation. However, the paper's claims are overstated, particularly regarding "redefining" PoS classes, and the methodology has limitations that need addressing. With more careful framing of contributions and stronger comparisons to existing work, this would be an excellent paper. As it stands, the core findings are interesting but the paper overreaches in its claims.
