

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a skip-gram model on the British National Corpus (BNC), then use logistic regression to predict PoS tags from word vectors. They claim their model can identify "outliers" (words whose distributional patterns differ from their traditional PoS classification) and even build a PoS tagger without context.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Analysis of misclassifications in a distributional PoS classifier reveals boundary cases between PoS categories
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (not concentrated in a single feature)
3. A modestly performing PoS tagger based on distributional vectors

## Strengths

1. **Methodologically sound approach**: The paper uses established techniques (word embeddings, logistic regression) applied to a well-defined problem (PoS classification). The experimental design is largely sound, with proper evaluation metrics (F-score), cross-validation, and testing on independent datasets (UD Treebank).

2. **Linguistic insights on boundary cases**: The analysis of misclassifications provides interesting linguistic observations about fuzzy boundaries between PoS categories (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns). These findings align with established linguistic theories about PoS boundaries being continuous rather than categorical.

3. **Validation against multiple datasets**: The model's performance is evaluated on both the training corpus (BNC) and an independent test set (UD Treebank), which strengthens the claim that the observed patterns are robust. The paper also compares their approach to a standard PoS tagger (Stanford Tagger), showing different error patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core claim**: The paper's main claim that "distributional models contain information about PoS classes" is not novel. The authors themselves cite multiple previous studies (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) that have already established this relationship. The paper fails to provide evidence that would significantly advance our understanding beyond what is already known in the literature.

2. **Misleading claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The paper's title suggests a fundamental redefinition of PoS classes, but the analysis of "outliers" merely confirms what linguists have long recognized about fuzzy boundaries between categories (e.g., participles behaving as both verbs and adjectives). The paper does not propose a new PoS system or provide evidence that would justify redefining existing categories.

3. **Questionable practical utility of proposed tagger**: The PoS tagger achieves only 84% accuracy on the test set, which is substantially lower than state-of-the-art taggers (typically 97-99% accuracy). The paper fails to convincingly argue why this approach would be useful given its performance limitations, especially when compared to established methods that use contextual information.

4. **Flawed experimental design for the "PoS predictor"**: The paper's main claim about building a "full-fledged PoS tagger" is undermined by the methodology. The classifier is trained on word+PoS tags (e.g., "love_VERB"), which is artificial and not applicable to real-world tagging where PoS tags are unknown. This design flaw makes the claim of a "proper PoS predictor" misleading and unconvincing.

5. **Overstated claims about annotation error detection**: The paper claims to "detect outright errors" in PoS-tagged corpora, but most of the cases they present as errors are debatable linguistic judgments rather than clear-cut errors. For example, the paper identifies words like "white-collar_ADJ" as misclassified when they're actually debatable cases that reflect different linguistic perspectives.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach to identifying "outliers" differ from previous work that has analyzed confusion matrices from standard PoS taggers? What unique insights does your distributional approach provide that the standard approach cannot?

2. Given that your PoS tagger achieves only 84% accuracy compared to state-of-the-art taggers (97%+), what specific use cases would justify adopting your approach over established methods?

3. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but the analysis of boundary cases merely confirms existing linguistic observations. What concrete new PoS categories or classification system do you propose based on your findings?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a methodologically sound analysis of PoS information in distributional models, but fails to deliver on its ambitious claims. While the linguistic insights about boundary cases are interesting, they don't constitute a significant advance over existing knowledge. The paper's main contribution is the demonstration of a relationship that has already been established multiple times in the literature, with no compelling evidence that this relationship leads to a new understanding of PoS categories or a practical improvement in PoS tagging.

The paper would be significantly strengthened if it focused on the linguistic insights (which are genuinely valuable) rather than making exaggerated claims about "redefining" PoS classes or presenting an underperforming tagger as a practical solution. As it stands, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its main claims, and its novelty is questionable given the existing literature.

The authors have done a good job with the technical aspects of the experiment, but the paper's contribution is marginal at best. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form, as it does not provide sufficient evidence to support its main claims of novelty and significance.
