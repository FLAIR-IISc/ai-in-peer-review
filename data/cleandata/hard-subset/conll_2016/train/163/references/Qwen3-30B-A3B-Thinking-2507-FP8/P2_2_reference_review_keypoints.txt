- Paper investigates whether distributional semantic models encode meaningful PoS information and trains a logistic classifier on embeddings to predict PoS, achieving high training/test scores and an 84% context-free tagger accuracy on UD data.  
- Main contribution 1: Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (≈100+), not localized to specific features.  
- Main contribution 2: Presents a distributional method to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and systematic annotation errors in corpora (e.g., BNC).  
- Main contribution 3: Builds a context-free PoS tagger using only word embeddings.  

- Strength 1: Novel empirical insight that PoS cues are spread over many embedding components, supported by feature-ablation analysis.  
- Strength 2: Practical tool for corpus annotation—distributional analysis uncovers annotation inconsistencies complementary to traditional tagger confusion matrices.  
- Strength 3: Robust empirical validation using cross-validation on BNC and testing on UD, showing results generalize across corpora.  

- Weakness 1: Title/claims overstate contribution—paper documents boundary cases but does not propose a redefinition or new PoS taxonomy.  
- Weakness 2: Context-free tagger has limited practical value (84% vs. >97% for SOTA) and lacks convincing real-world utility.  
- Weakness 3: Insufficient differentiation from prior work using embeddings for PoS tagging and clustering (e.g., Tsuboi 2014, Liu et al. 2016).  
- Weakness 4: Lacks theoretical explanation for why PoS boundaries arise in distributional space; remains descriptive rather than explanatory.  

- Question 1: How should identified annotation errors be corrected in practice and how would BNC guidelines be revised accordingly?  
- Question 2: How does the context-free tagger compare to other context-free baselines (e.g., frequency- or morphology-based methods)?  
- Question 3: If accuracy improves beyond k=100 features, what theoretical implications follow for how linguistic information is encoded in embeddings?  

- Overall: Solid empirical study with useful insights into PoS signals and corpus annotation, but overstated claims, limited practical impact of the tagger, and weak theoretical/novelty positioning relative to prior work.
