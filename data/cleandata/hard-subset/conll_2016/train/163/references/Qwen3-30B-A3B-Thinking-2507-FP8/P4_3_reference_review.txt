

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, analyze classification errors to identify "outliers" (words that behave differently from their assigned PoS), and demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions.

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. Demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust, distributed information about PoS classes, enabling high-accuracy classification (F-score 0.979).
2. Using classifier errors to identify potential annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora, providing concrete examples of where traditional PoS tagging may be inconsistent.
3. Showing that PoS affiliation is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), not concentrated in a few specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Technical soundness of core methodology**: The paper presents a well-structured experimental pipeline using standard NLP techniques (continuous skipgram, logistic regression) with appropriate evaluation metrics (10-fold cross-validation, independent test on Universal Dependencies Treebank). The authors carefully address potential biases by weighting training instances by word frequency, which is a significant methodological improvement over simple classification approaches. The ablation study (Figure 3) provides strong evidence about how PoS information is distributed across vector dimensions.

2. **Practical insights into PoS annotation inconsistencies**: The paper's most valuable contribution is its analysis of classifier errors to reveal potential annotation issues in the BNC. The authors provide specific, linguistically meaningful examples (e.g., currency amounts classified as numerals rather than nouns, adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs), which demonstrate how distributional models can identify inconsistencies that might be missed in traditional corpus annotation. This provides concrete value for corpus linguists and NLP practitioners.

3. **Insight about distribution of linguistic information in embeddings**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (not concentrated in a few features) is a significant technical contribution that aligns with theoretical expectations about embedding models. The ablation study (Figure 3) provides clear empirical evidence for this, showing that accuracy increases steadily with more features, reaching near-perfect performance with ~100 features.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core finding**: The paper's main finding—that word embeddings contain PoS information—is not novel. Previous work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already established that distributional models contain linguistic information, including PoS. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its contribution from these prior works or demonstrate how its specific approach advances the field beyond what's already known.

2. **Questionable practical significance of the classifier**: While the classifier achieves 84% accuracy on the UD Treebank without contextual information, this is substantially lower than state-of-the-art PoS taggers (typically >97% accuracy). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this approach would be practically useful compared to existing methods, despite the authors' claim about potential utility for resource-poor languages. The limited evidence provided for this claim weakens the practical contribution.

3. **Overstated significance of "outlier" analysis**: Many of the "outliers" identified (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns) are expected based on linguistic knowledge rather than revealing new insights. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the distributional model reveals genuinely novel linguistic phenomena rather than confirming existing linguistic observations. The examples of potential annotation errors are interesting but require more rigorous validation to establish they are true errors rather than different linguistic analyses.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of this distributional classifier compare to other unsupervised or weakly supervised approaches for PoS tagging, and what specific advantages does this method offer over existing techniques?

2. For the cases where the distributional model suggests potential annotation errors (e.g., adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs), how would you validate these as true errors rather than reflecting different linguistic analyses or annotation conventions?

3. Given that the classifier achieves high accuracy on the BNC but lower accuracy on the UD Treebank (84% vs. 99% on the BNC test set), what factors might explain this performance gap, and how might this inform the design of future distributional PoS models?

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by demonstrating how distributional models can reveal PoS annotation inconsistencies, providing valuable insights for corpus linguistics. However, the core finding about embeddings containing PoS information lacks sufficient novelty, and the practical significance of the approach is not convincingly established. The paper would benefit from more clearly differentiating its contribution from prior work and providing stronger evidence for the practical utility of the method. While the analysis of error patterns is insightful, the paper overstates the novelty of these findings in relation to existing linguistic knowledge.
