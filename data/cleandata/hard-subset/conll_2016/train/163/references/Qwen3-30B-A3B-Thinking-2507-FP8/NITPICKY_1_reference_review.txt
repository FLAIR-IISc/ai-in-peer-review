

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models can reveal meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassified words to identify boundary cases between PoS classes or potential annotation errors. Their main findings include: (1) word embeddings contain robust PoS information, (2) misclassifications reveal interesting linguistic phenomena at PoS boundaries, and (3) a classifier trained on embeddings achieves high accuracy on PoS prediction without contextual information.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Identification of linguistic boundary cases through analysis of misclassified words in distributional models
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features
3. Evidence that distributional models can detect potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora

## Strengths
1. The paper's analysis of misclassified words reveals genuine linguistic insights about PoS boundaries. For example, the identification of proper nouns like "luftwaffe" that are misclassified due to insufficient training data, or the detection of systematic annotation errors where adjectives like "plain" are incorrectly tagged as adverbs, provides valuable linguistic evidence.

2. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model) is a significant finding that aligns with theoretical expectations about embedding models and provides a more nuanced understanding of how semantic information relates to grammatical categories.

3. The paper's approach of using distributional models to detect annotation errors is innovative and has practical implications for corpus linguistics. The comparison between BNC and UD Treebank annotations reveals real inconsistencies in PoS tagging that could inform future corpus development.

## Weaknesses
1. **Unrealistic performance claims**: The paper claims "an F-Score equal to 0.99" on the UD Treebank test set. This is implausibly high for a model trained on BNC data and applied to UD Treebank without any adaptation. State-of-the-art PoS taggers typically achieve around 97% accuracy on English, and this model lacks contextual information. The authors should verify this claim and provide detailed error analysis to explain the discrepancy.

2. **Critical omission of reproducibility information**: The paper states "The mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags were created manually by us and released online" but fails to provide the URL. This is a fundamental omission for reproducibility in computational linguistics. Without this information, other researchers cannot replicate the exact preprocessing steps.

3. **Inconsistent formatting and citation errors**: The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies:
   - Inconsistent use of "PoS" vs. "POS" throughout the text
   - Inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "Houston, 1985" vs. "Manning, 2011")
   - Formatting errors like "Universal Part-of-Speech Tagset (Petrov et al., 2012)1" and "Anonymized" in reference to a URL
   - The phrase "We worked with the following 16 Universal tags" is inaccurate as the Universal POS tagset has 17 tags (including PUNCT, which the paper excludes)

4. **Lack of explanation for critical preprocessing step**: The paper states "We produced a version of BNC where all the words were replaced with their lemmas" but provides no details about the lemmatization process. This is a crucial step that could significantly impact results, and the paper should specify which lemmatizer was used and how it handled ambiguous cases.

5. **Misleading description of classifier training**: The paper claims "the classifier was trained on 177,343 (sometimes repeating) instances, instead of the original 10,000" but fails to explain how this number was calculated. The phrasing "sometimes repeating" is misleading - they are weighting instances by frequency, not repeating them. This needs clarification.

## Questions to Authors
1. The claimed F-score of 0.99 on UD Treebank appears unrealistically high for a model trained on BNC data without adaptation. Please provide detailed error analysis to explain this result.

2. Please provide the URL where the BNC-to-Universal POS tag mappings were released, as this is essential for reproducibility.

3. Please clarify the lemmatization process used for the BNC corpus. Which lemmatizer was employed, and how were ambiguous cases handled?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents interesting insights about the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech classes. However, the serious methodological and formatting issues, particularly the unrealistic performance claim and the omission of critical reproducibility information, undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer who focuses on meticulous attention to detail, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these substantial flaws, even though the core idea has potential value. The paper would benefit from thorough revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.
