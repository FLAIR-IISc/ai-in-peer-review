

# Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) can reveal meaningful insights about part-of-speech (PoS) classes and boundaries. The authors train a PoS classifier using word embeddings from the British National Corpus (BNC) and analyze misclassifications to identify words that straddle PoS boundaries or reveal inconsistencies in existing PoS tagging. They demonstrate that word embeddings contain robust PoS information (achieving 97.9% F-score), and that error analysis reveals linguistic patterns suggesting fluid PoS boundaries. The paper also develops a PoS predictor that works without pre-existing tags, achieving 84% accuracy on the Universal Dependencies Treebank.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Empirical demonstration of PoS information in word embeddings**: The paper provides strong evidence that word embeddings contain significant PoS information, not just semantic information.
2. **Insightful error analysis revealing linguistic patterns**: The paper's most valuable contribution is the error analysis, which identifies systematic patterns of misclassification that reveal fluid boundaries between PoS categories.
3. **Practical tool for corpus annotation**: The approach offers a data-driven method for detecting and potentially fixing PoS tagging inconsistencies in existing corpora.

## Strengths

1. **Robust empirical demonstration of PoS information in embeddings**: The paper provides compelling evidence that word embeddings contain significant PoS information. The classifier achieves 97.9% F-score on a 16-class PoS task, with results replicated on the UD Treebank (F-score 0.99). The analysis of prototypical vectors for each PoS class (Figures 1-2) visually demonstrates how different PoS classes cluster in the embedding space, with specific vector components (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions) showing strong associations with particular PoS categories. This is a significant contribution to understanding what linguistic information is encoded in word embeddings.

2. **Valuable linguistic insights from error analysis**: The paper's most important contribution is the systematic analysis of misclassified words, which reveals patterns that suggest PoS boundaries are more fluid than traditional grammars indicate. For example, the paper identifies that 22% of subordinate conjunctions were misclassified as adverbs, revealing a genuine boundary case rather than a tagging error. The analysis of proper nouns misclassified as common nouns (88% correctly classified) suggests the model has learned to distinguish proper names based on specific contexts, while errors occur with rare toponyms. The paper also identifies systematic errors in the BNC, such as adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs, which is a valuable contribution to corpus linguistics.

3. **Practical application for corpus quality control**: The paper demonstrates a useful tool for detecting inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora. As the authors note, "distributional models can actually detect outright errors in PoS-tagged corpora," which has significant implications for corpus linguistics and NLP. This approach could be particularly valuable for improving large-scale annotated corpora, which often contain systematic errors that are difficult to detect manually.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core methodology**: While the error analysis is insightful, the core approach of using word embeddings for PoS classification is not particularly novel. Previous work (Tsvetkov et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016) has already shown that word embeddings contain PoS information. The paper's contribution is primarily in the error analysis and revealing specific linguistic patterns, not in the core methodology. The authors could better position their work by explicitly comparing to prior work on this specific task.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The paper acknowledges that their 84% accuracy "may not seem very impressive in comparison with the performance of modern state-of-the-art PoS taggers" but does not provide a direct comparison. The absence of such a comparison weakens the claim about the practical value of their approach. For context, modern neural PoS taggers typically achieve >97% accuracy on standard benchmarks.

3. **Limited discussion of the "outlier" analysis methodology**: While the error analysis is insightful, the paper does not systematically analyze the linguistic properties of misclassified words. For example, the authors could have performed clustering on misclassified words to identify patterns (e.g., all misclassified proper nouns share certain morphological features) rather than just listing examples. A more systematic approach would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your distributional PoS predictor compare to state-of-the-art supervised PoS taggers (e.g., those using neural networks with contextual embeddings) on the UD Treebank? The paper mentions that 0.84 accuracy "may not seem very impressive," but doesn't provide a direct comparison.

2. Could you elaborate on why the model struggles with certain proper nouns (e.g., 'luftwaffe', 'stonehenge') but correctly identifies most proper nouns? Is this due to insufficient context, or are there specific linguistic features that distinguish these cases?

3. The paper states that "part of speech affiliation is distributed among several components (at least a hundred in our case of 300-dimensional model)." How does this finding relate to the broader literature on the interpretability of word embeddings, particularly regarding linguistic features?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating how distributional semantic models can reveal insights about PoS boundaries and identify tagging inconsistencies. The error analysis is particularly insightful and provides concrete evidence that PoS boundaries are more fluid than traditional grammars suggest. While the core methodology isn't novel, the paper's empirical demonstration of PoS information in embeddings and the linguistic insights from error analysis are significant contributions to the field. The paper would be strengthened by a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers and a more systematic analysis of the misclassified words.

The paper meets the reviewing criteria well, with strong empirical results and valuable linguistic insights. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers and the systematic analysis of misclassified words.
