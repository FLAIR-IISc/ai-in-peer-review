

# Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to studying part-of-speech (PoS) boundaries by leveraging distributional semantic models. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and analyze the misclassifications to identify linguistic boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **Conceptual innovation**: Using distributional semantic models to identify "boundary cases" between traditional PoS categories rather than merely using them for PoS tagging. This reframes how we study linguistic categories that are inherently fuzzy.

2. **Empirical insights into PoS annotation**: The paper identifies specific, concrete cases where traditional PoS annotation appears inconsistent or erroneous (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns, passive participles behaving like adjectives), providing actionable insights for corpus linguists.

3. **Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across vector dimensions**: The authors show that PoS affiliation isn't concentrated in specific vector features (contrary to common assumptions), but rather distributed across many dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model).

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual contribution**: This paper's core contribution is genuinely fresh - it doesn't just apply distributional models to PoS tagging (which has been done before), but uses PoS classification as a lens to study the nature of linguistic categories themselves. The paper challenges the traditional view of PoS as discrete categories and instead presents empirical evidence for the continuum model of linguistic categories. This conceptual shift is significant for both computational linguistics and linguistic theory.

2. **Practical value for corpus linguistics**: The paper demonstrates how distributional models can identify systematic annotation errors in existing corpora (like BNC), which has direct implications for corpus quality. For example, the paper identifies that 90% of "nouns" misclassified as numerals are actually currency amounts or percentages, suggesting a clear annotation improvement. This moves beyond theoretical discussion to provide concrete, actionable insights.

3. **Strong empirical validation of the core hypothesis**: The authors demonstrate robust PoS prediction performance (F-score 0.979) and then leverage the misclassifications to reveal meaningful linguistic patterns. The analysis of outliers is thorough and well-justified, with clear examples that illustrate the boundary cases between PoS categories.

4. **Theoretical alignment with linguistic continuity**: The paper aligns with the theoretical position that linguistic categories exist on a continuum (as cited from Houston, 1985) and provides empirical evidence supporting this view. This is a significant contribution to linguistic theory that computational linguistics rarely engages with.

## Weaknesses

1. **Circularity in the methodological approach**: The classifier is trained on word embeddings from a model that was trained on words concatenated with their PoS tags. This creates a circularity where the "outliers" might reflect the training process rather than genuine linguistic phenomena. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently address how to mitigate this concern.

2. **Limited scope of linguistic analysis**: The paper focuses exclusively on English and the Universal PoS tagset, without discussing how these findings might apply to languages with very different grammatical structures (e.g., agglutinative languages). While a reasonable starting point, this limits the generalizability of the approach.

3. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and some claims suggest the paper is redefining PoS classes, but the actual contribution is more about identifying boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies rather than proposing a new classification system. The paper doesn't present a new PoS tagset or systematic redefinition of categories.

4. **Lack of comparison with modern PoS taggers**: The paper doesn't compare its approach to state-of-the-art neural PoS taggers, which would have provided important context for their 0.84 accuracy on the UD Treebank test set. While they note this isn't impressive compared to modern systems, they don't explain how their conceptual approach differs from those systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach to identifying boundary cases differ for languages with very different grammatical structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish) compared to English?

2. Given the circularity in your training process (classifier trained on embeddings from a model trained on PoS-tagged words), how can you be confident that the "outliers" you identify represent genuine linguistic boundary cases rather than artifacts of your training procedure?

3. Could you elaborate on how your approach might be integrated with existing PoS tagging pipelines to improve annotation quality, rather than just identifying errors?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by reframing how we study PoS categories through the lens of distributional semantic models. While it has some methodological limitations, the conceptual innovation and practical insights for corpus linguistics are significant. The paper's core contribution is not just another PoS tagging method, but a new way to investigate the fuzzy boundaries of linguistic categories. Given the journal's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the circularity concern and clarify the scope of the contribution.

The paper successfully demonstrates that distributional models can reveal meaningful linguistic insights about PoS boundaries that traditional methods miss, which is a significant step toward understanding how linguistic categories actually function in natural language. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that CONLL should celebrate.
