

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) can reveal meaningful insights about parts of speech (PoS) boundaries and annotation inconsistencies. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" that reveal ambiguous cases between PoS classes. They demonstrate that these outliers often correspond to known linguistic ambiguities or potential annotation errors in existing corpora, and they show that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of the embedding space rather than concentrated in specific features.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel method for identifying PoS boundary cases**: The paper presents a methodology using distributional models to systematically discover words that fall ambiguously between PoS classes, revealing linguistic phenomena that traditional annotation schemes might miss.
2. **Practical application for corpus annotation**: The authors demonstrate how this approach can identify systematic errors in existing PoS-tagged corpora, providing a data-driven tool for corpus linguists to refine annotation guidelines.
3. **Insight into distribution of PoS information in embeddings**: The paper provides evidence that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of word embeddings, rather than concentrated in a few specific features.

## Strengths
1. **Linguistically meaningful analysis**: The paper's greatest strength is its insightful analysis of misclassified words, which reveals genuine linguistic ambiguities (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns, currency amounts vs. numerals). The authors provide well-justified examples that demonstrate how distributional patterns can highlight where traditional PoS boundaries are fuzzy or where corpus annotations may be inconsistent.

2. **Practical value for corpus linguistics**: The paper offers a valuable tool for corpus linguists to systematically identify and address potential annotation inconsistencies. The comparison between BNC and Universal Dependencies Treebank annotations demonstrates this utility effectively, showing how the distributional model can reveal annotation differences that might otherwise go unnoticed.

3. **Robustness across datasets**: The classifier's performance (F-score 0.91 on unseen BNC data, 0.99 on UD Treebank) demonstrates the robustness of the approach across different text corpora and annotation schemes, strengthening the argument that distributional patterns reliably capture PoS information.

## Weaknesses
1. **Overstated claim of "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and some claims suggest the paper proposes a new theoretical framework for PoS classes, but the actual contribution is more about identifying boundary cases rather than redefining the categories themselves. The paper does not present new PoS classes or a revised theoretical model, which weakens the main claim.

2. **Underwhelming PoS tagger performance**: The unsupervised PoS tagger built from distributional models achieves only 84% accuracy, which is significantly lower than state-of-the-art supervised taggers (often >97%). The paper doesn't adequately address why this gap exists or how this approach might be improved to be more competitive, making the practical application less compelling.

3. **Limited novelty of linguistic insights**: Many of the boundary cases identified (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns) are well-documented in linguistic literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish how its approach adds new insights beyond what linguists already know, which weakens the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do the boundary cases identified by your method compare to those documented in linguistic literature on PoS boundaries? Can you provide specific examples where your approach reveals novel insights not previously discussed?

2. The unsupervised PoS tagger achieves only 84% accuracy. Given that state-of-the-art supervised taggers achieve >97% accuracy, what are the key limitations of this approach, and how might they be overcome for practical applications?

3. The paper notes that vector component 94 is highly specific to conjunctions, while PoS information is otherwise distributed across many dimensions. Could you provide more linguistic analysis of this component and explain why it might be so specifically associated with conjunctions?

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-executed study that provides valuable insights into PoS boundaries using distributional semantic models. The paper's greatest strength is its practical application for corpus annotation, which is genuinely useful for linguists. However, the claim of "redefining" PoS classes is overstated, and the unsupervised tagger's performance is not compelling enough to support the paper's broader claims. The work would be significantly strengthened by more clearly distinguishing between what is novel in the linguistic insights versus what is already known, and by providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the unsupervised approach.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding how distributional models encode linguistic information, but it would benefit from a more modest framing of its contributions. With these refinements, the paper could be a strong addition to the literature on distributional semantics and PoS annotation. As it stands, it's a solid but not transformative contribution to the field. I recommend a borderline decision, leaning toward acceptance with substantial revisions to address the overstatement of contributions and provide a more balanced assessment of the unsupervised tagger's performance.
