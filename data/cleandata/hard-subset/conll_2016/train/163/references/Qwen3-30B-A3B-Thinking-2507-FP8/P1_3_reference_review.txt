

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about parts of speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings from the British National Corpus (BNC) to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassified "outlier" words to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and potential annotation inconsistencies. The classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on unseen test data), with errors revealing interesting linguistic phenomena such as the fluid boundary between nouns and adjectives, proper nouns vs. common nouns, and currency amounts being misclassified as numerals.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstrating that distributional semantic models contain robust information about PoS classes, with a classifier achieving high accuracy without using contextual information.
2. Providing a novel methodology for identifying boundary cases between PoS classes through error analysis of distributional classifiers, revealing both linguistic phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies in existing corpora.
3. Showing that these models can detect systematic annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora, which could inform future corpus annotation practices.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of distributional models to PoS boundaries**: The paper makes a significant contribution by using distributional models not just for classification, but for linguistic analysis of PoS boundaries. The authors go beyond typical classification results to identify specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., "materialist_NOUN" being classified as an adjective) that reveal the fluid nature of PoS categories. This approach provides concrete evidence for linguistic claims about PoS boundaries that have been discussed theoretically for decades.

2. **Insightful error analysis**: The paper excels in its detailed analysis of misclassified words, which reveals both linguistic phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies. For example, the discovery of currency amounts ("£70") being misclassified as numerals (while the Universal Dependencies Treebank correctly separates them) demonstrates how distributional models can identify inconsistencies in existing annotation practices. This is a valuable contribution to corpus linguistics.

3. **Demonstration of robustness**: The authors show their classifier performs well on unseen data (F-score 0.91 on BNC test set) and even on the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99), demonstrating the robustness of their approach. The finding that a classifier trained on BNC embeddings can correctly tag 99% of UD Treebank words is particularly impressive, especially since the UD Treebank uses a different tagging scheme.

4. **Practical implications for resource-poor languages**: The paper's demonstration that a classifier can be trained with as few as 1,564 unambiguous words (achieving 84% accuracy) has significant practical implications. This suggests a feasible approach for PoS tagging in resource-poor languages where manual tagging is expensive.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with existing methods**: The paper compares their approach to a Stanford PoS Tagger, but this comparison is superficial and doesn't fully demonstrate the unique value of their method. For example, they show that the distributional classifier reveals different error patterns (e.g., subordinating conjunctions misclassified as adverbs), but they don't demonstrate how this leads to better PoS tagging or linguistic insights than existing error analysis methods.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, it doesn't discuss how their method would perform on languages with different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages where PoS boundaries might be more fluid). The paper claims the information about PoS is distributed across many vector components, but doesn't provide evidence that this distribution is linguistically meaningful beyond the example of component 94 for conjunctions.

3. **Lack of clear theoretical contribution**: While the paper provides interesting empirical findings, it doesn't clearly position itself within the theoretical debate about whether PoS categories are categorical or gradient. The paper acknowledges that "PoS boundaries are not strict" (citing Manning, 2015), but doesn't advance this theoretical discussion beyond presenting empirical evidence.

4. **Overstatement of novelty**: The paper claims their approach "redefines" PoS classes, but the methodology doesn't actually propose new PoS categories or a revised PoS system. The paper identifies boundary cases but doesn't suggest how to incorporate these into a revised classification system.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach generalize to languages with different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative languages like Turkish or Finnish)? Would the boundary cases you identified in English also appear in these languages?

2. The paper demonstrates that the classifier can detect annotation inconsistencies, but how would you address the potential circularity of using a PoS-tagged corpus to train the embedding model and then using it to analyze the PoS tagging? What steps would you take to validate that the detected inconsistencies are genuine?

3. The paper shows that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, but doesn't provide evidence that this distribution is linguistically meaningful. How would you identify which specific components contribute to specific PoS distinctions beyond the example of component 94 for conjunctions?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS categories. The paper's strength lies in its insightful error analysis that reveals interesting linguistic phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies. While the theoretical contribution could be stronger and the comparison with existing methods needs more depth, the practical insights about PoS boundaries and corpus annotation are significant. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of limitations and clearer positioning within the existing theoretical literature on PoS categories.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding the theoretical contribution and comparison with existing methods.
