

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories, and whether this information can be used to refine PoS boundaries and identify annotation inconsistencies. The authors train a continuous skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC) with Universal PoS tags, then train a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags based on word embeddings. They achieve high accuracy (F-score 0.979), analyze misclassifications as "outliers" to identify cases where words behave differently from their traditional PoS classification, and demonstrate a method to build a PoS tagger using distributional vectors without prior PoS tags.

## Main Contributions
1. Systematic analysis of misclassifications to reveal linguistic insights about fuzzy PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies (most significant contribution)
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components rather than concentrated in specific features (important but not novel)
3. Development of a PoS tagger using distributional vectors without prior PoS tags, which could be useful for resource-poor languages (limited practical contribution)

## Strengths

1. **Valuable linguistic insights from outlier analysis**: The paper provides a compelling data-driven approach to examining PoS boundaries. The authors identify concrete examples where traditional PoS tagging appears inconsistent (e.g., currency amounts like "£70" being tagged as nouns instead of numerals, or adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs). These cases are not just interesting but have real implications for corpus annotation practices. The analysis reveals that the Universal Dependencies Treebank handles some of these cases more consistently than BNC, which is a valuable contribution to corpus linguistics.

2. **Novel application of distributional models for annotation quality control**: The paper successfully demonstrates how distributional models can identify systematic annotation errors, which is a useful tool for corpus linguists. For instance, the finding that 600+ instances of "plain" were incorrectly tagged as adverbs in BNC (when they should be adjectives) is a clear example of the model detecting annotation errors. This represents a practical application beyond just theoretical interest.

3. **Insight into distributional properties of PoS categories**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across many vector components (at least 100 out of 300 dimensions) rather than concentrated in a few features is an important insight that aligns with the theoretical underpinnings of distributional semantics. This is well-supported by the accuracy curve in Figure 3, which shows smooth improvement as more features are added.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core methodology**: The paper builds upon well-established work showing that distributional models contain PoS information (Tsvetkov et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016). The authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from this prior work. The core methodology of using embeddings for PoS prediction is not novel, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate what new insights their analysis provides over previous studies.

2. **Limited practical contribution of the PoS tagger**: The PoS tagger they develop achieves only 84% accuracy on the test set, which is significantly lower than state-of-the-art taggers (typically 97%+ accuracy). The authors don't adequately address why their method is inferior to existing approaches, nor do they provide a compelling argument for when their method would be preferable. The paper's claim that this could be useful for "resource-poor languages" is underdeveloped, as they don't demonstrate how their method would actually outperform existing approaches in such scenarios.

3. **Superficial analysis of linguistic implications**: While the paper identifies interesting cases of misclassification, it doesn't provide a systematic linguistic framework for understanding why these words are outliers or how they might inform a revised PoS system. For instance, the paper notes that "proper nouns" and "common nouns" are difficult to distinguish distributionally, but doesn't explore what linguistic features might differentiate them beyond what's already known.

4. **Inadequate comparison with existing PoS taggers**: The paper compares their distributional predictor to the Stanford PoS tagger, but doesn't provide a proper comparison of the error patterns. The key insight that their method reveals different error types (e.g., subordinating conjunctions mistaken for adverbs) is presented as a strength, but the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this difference in error patterns is more useful for linguistic analysis than the errors revealed by standard taggers.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that their approach "redefines part-of-speech classes," but doesn't provide a clear proposal for how these classes should be redefined. How do you envision the linguistic theory of PoS classes being revised based on your findings? What specific changes to PoS tagsets would you propose?

2. The paper demonstrates that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, but doesn't explain why this distributional pattern exists. How does this align with linguistic theories of part-of-speech categories, and what does it suggest about the relationship between semantic and syntactic categories?

3. The authors note that their PoS tagger achieves 84% accuracy without using context, which they consider impressive. However, the main limitation of this approach is that it doesn't consider sentence context. How might this limitation be addressed to improve performance, and how would this impact the linguistic insights gained from the distributional model?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a valuable analysis of PoS boundaries using distributional models, with significant insights into annotation inconsistencies in the BNC. However, the core methodology lacks novelty, and the practical contribution of the PoS tagger is limited. With stronger differentiation from prior work, more thorough discussion of linguistic implications, and a clearer justification for the practical utility of their approach, this paper could make a significant contribution. As it stands, it offers incremental progress in the field.

The paper would benefit from a more focused contribution statement that clearly articulates how this work advances beyond previous research. The outlier analysis is the strongest part of the paper, and this should be emphasized as the primary contribution rather than the PoS tagger development.
