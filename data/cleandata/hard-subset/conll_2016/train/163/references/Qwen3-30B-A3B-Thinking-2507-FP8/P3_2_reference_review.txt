

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary & Main Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, and whether analyzing errors in PoS prediction can reveal linguistic boundaries and annotation inconsistencies. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers," and demonstrate that their classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.91 on unseen data, 0.99 on Universal Dependencies Treebank). The primary contributions I identify are: (1) a novel method for identifying linguistic boundaries through error analysis of distributional PoS classifiers; (2) empirical evidence that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in specific features; and (3) a simple PoS classifier that operates without contextual information, potentially useful for low-resource languages.

## Strengths

1. **Insightful error analysis revealing linguistic boundaries**: The paper provides valuable linguistic insights by analyzing misclassifications. For example, the authors identify cases where currency amounts (e.g., "£70") are correctly classified as numerals rather than nouns, highlighting inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. This demonstrates how distributional models can reveal annotation issues that might not be apparent through manual review alone, offering practical value for corpus linguists.

2. **Demonstration of distributed PoS information**: The paper's finding that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300D model) is well-supported by the smooth accuracy curve in Figure 3. This challenges the idea that specific linguistic features might be encoded in specific dimensions and aligns with theoretical understanding of how word embeddings work, making it a significant contribution to understanding the nature of linguistic information in distributional models.

3. **Robustness across corpora**: The classifier's high accuracy (F-score 0.99) on the Universal Dependencies Treebank demonstrates the approach's robustness across different corpora and annotation schemes. This is particularly valuable as it suggests the method could be applied to other languages with minimal adaptation, enhancing its generalizability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of practical relevance**: The paper's classifier achieves only 0.84 accuracy on the UD Treebank, which is significantly lower than state-of-the-art PoS taggers (typically 97%+). While the authors claim this is "impressive" because it doesn't use contextual information, this is precisely the limitation that makes it impractical for real-world PoS tagging. The paper fails to demonstrate how this approach would be superior to existing methods in practical applications.

2. **Methodological issues with classifier training**: The initial classifier is trained on "word + PoS tag" combinations, which is not how PoS tagging works in practice. The authors acknowledge this as "artificial" but don't adequately address how this affects their analysis. The later section on "Distributional vectors as part-of-speech predictors" attempts to fix this, but the results remain modest compared to standard taggers, undermining the practical value of their approach.

3. **Insufficient exploration of why distributional models capture PoS information**: The paper acknowledges that PoS is primarily a syntactic category while distributional models capture semantic relationships. It doesn't adequately explain why distributional models should contain PoS information, which is a fundamental limitation. The authors could have explored the relationship between semantic and syntactic distributional patterns more deeply to strengthen their theoretical foundation.

4. **Limited linguistic implications**: While the error analysis provides interesting examples, the paper doesn't systematically explore how these boundary cases might inform linguistic theory. The authors identify cases where annotation might be inconsistent, but don't analyze whether these represent genuine linguistic phenomena or annotation errors, limiting the theoretical contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to existing methods for detecting annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora? Specifically, what advantages does distributional analysis offer over traditional manual review or statistical analysis of tag frequencies?

2. Given that your classifier achieves only 0.84 accuracy on the UD Treebank, how would you integrate this with existing state-of-the-art taggers to improve overall performance, rather than using it as a standalone tagger?

3. The paper notes that "distributional models trained in a nondeterministic and stochastic way on large amounts of word contexts learn knowledge about part of speech clusters." Could you elaborate on why this is the case, given that PoS is primarily a syntactic category while distributional models capture semantic relationships?

This paper makes an interesting contribution to understanding how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models, but the practical limitations of the approach and insufficient theoretical grounding prevent it from being a major contribution to the field. With stronger methodological justification and clearer demonstration of practical utility, this work could be significantly improved.
