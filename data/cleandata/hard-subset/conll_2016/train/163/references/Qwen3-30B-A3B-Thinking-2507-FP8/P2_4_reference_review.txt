

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, analyze misclassifications to identify "boundary cases" between PoS classes, and explore how PoS information is distributed across vector dimensions. They demonstrate that embeddings contain robust PoS information (97.9% F-score on training data), that misclassifications reveal interesting linguistic phenomena, and that PoS information is distributed across many vector components.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Insightful error analysis revealing boundary cases**: The paper's most significant contribution is its detailed analysis of misclassified words, which reveals genuine linguistic phenomena at the boundaries between PoS classes (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives, verbs vs. adjectives). This provides concrete evidence for the fluid nature of PoS boundaries.
2. **Demonstration of PoS information distribution**: The paper shows that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector components (at least 100 out of 300), not concentrated in a few specific features, which aligns with theoretical expectations of embedding models.
3. **Practical PoS tagging without contextual information**: The authors demonstrate that a simple classifier trained on distributional embeddings can achieve 84% accuracy for PoS tagging without any contextual information, suggesting potential applications for low-resource language processing.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling evidence for PoS boundaries**: The paper provides concrete, data-driven evidence for the fluid nature of PoS boundaries, which is a significant contribution to linguistic theory. The analysis of misclassifications (e.g., "white-collar" being classified as a noun rather than adjective, "1804" as a noun rather than numeral) reveals genuine linguistic phenomena that challenge traditional categorical PoS distinctions.

2. **Practical utility for corpus annotation**: The paper demonstrates that distributional models can detect potential annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora (e.g., "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverb). This has direct practical value for corpus linguists who maintain and revise PoS-tagged corpora, as it provides an automated way to identify questionable annotations.

3. **Rigorous evaluation across multiple datasets**: The authors validate their approach on multiple datasets (BNC, Universal Dependencies Treebank) and demonstrate consistent performance, showing that their findings are robust and not specific to one corpus or annotation scheme.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The core approach of using word embeddings for PoS classification is not novel. Prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already established that embeddings contain PoS information. The paper doesn't significantly advance the methodology or provide a new technical contribution to the field.

2. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and abstract claim to "redefine" PoS classes, but the paper doesn't actually propose new PoS categories or substantially revise existing ones. It identifies boundary cases and potential annotation inconsistencies, but doesn't develop a new theoretical framework for PoS classification.

3. **Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper claims that 84% accuracy is impressive for a system that doesn't use context, but fails to compare against modern contextualized approaches (e.g., BERT-based PoS taggers that achieve 97%+ accuracy). Without this comparison, the significance of the 84% figure is unclear.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address how the approach handles words with multiple PoS in different contexts (e.g., "run" as verb vs. noun). The current method would assign a single PoS tag to each word form, which is insufficient for many linguistic phenomena.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach handle words with multiple PoS tags in different contexts (e.g., "run" as verb vs. noun)? Would you need to modify your approach to account for this?

2. You claim that PoS information is distributed across at least 100 vector components, yet Figures 1 and 2 show a single component (94) that is highly specific to conjunctions. How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction?

3. You demonstrate that your classifier can detect potential annotation errors in BNC, but how would you propose to resolve these errors? What additional linguistic evidence would you use to support the new classification?

4. The paper states that "distributional models trained in a nondeterministic and stochastic way on large amounts of word contexts learn knowledge about part of speech clusters." How does this relate to the observation that PoS boundaries are not strict (Manning, 2015)? Is the stochasticity of training actually beneficial for capturing these fluid boundaries?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by providing empirical evidence for the fluid nature of PoS boundaries through systematic analysis of misclassifications. The error analysis is insightful and has clear practical applications for corpus annotation. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and the paper overstates its contribution to "redefining" PoS classes. The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art contextualized approaches weakens the significance of the reported accuracy. 

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more critical discussion of limitations, a comparison to modern contextualized models, and a clearer distinction between what the paper actually achieves (identifying boundary cases) versus the more ambitious claim of "redefining" PoS classes. 

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions (to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding methodology novelty and comparison to state-of-the-art).
