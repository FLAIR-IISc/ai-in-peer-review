

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, and if so, whether this information can be used to identify "boundary cases" where words behave ambiguously between PoS categories. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassifications to identify words that don't conform to their traditional PoS classification. They find that these "outliers" reveal inconsistencies in existing PoS annotations, and they demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of word embeddings rather than concentrated in specific features.

From my perspective, the paper's strongest contribution is **using distributional semantic models to systematically identify and analyze annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora**. The second contribution is **the finding that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions**, and the third is **a method for identifying prototypical examples of PoS classes that could aid in low-resource PoS tagging**.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to identifying PoS annotation inconsistencies**: The paper's most significant contribution is its method for detecting systematic errors in PoS-tagged corpora. By training a PoS classifier on distributional vectors and analyzing misclassifications, the authors identify specific cases where the BNC (and similar corpora) contain systematic errors (e.g., adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs). This is not just an incremental improvement to PoS tagging—it's a novel tool for corpus linguists to evaluate and improve the quality of their annotated data. For example, the paper shows that 600 instances of "plain" are incorrectly tagged as adverbs in BNC despite being adjectives, which is a concrete, actionable insight.

2. **Insight into the distributed nature of PoS information**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across at least 100 dimensions of word embeddings (rather than concentrated in a few features) is a subtle but important contribution. This aligns with the underlying principle of distributional semantic models and provides valuable insight into how linguistic information is encoded in these representations. The authors demonstrate this through careful feature analysis and accuracy curves showing performance improvement as more features are added.

3. **Practical application for low-resource languages**: The paper proposes a method for preliminary PoS tagging in resource-poor languages by identifying prototypical words for each PoS class and training a classifier. This is a practical, actionable contribution that could be immediately useful for linguists working with under-resourced languages, requiring only a small number of manually annotated examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of significant novelty in core methodology**: The paper builds directly on prior work by Liu et al. (2016) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015), who already demonstrated that distributional models contain PoS information. The authors don't sufficiently distinguish their contribution from these prior works, making the paper feel incremental rather than groundbreaking. The title's claim of "redefining" PoS classes is overstated—there's no actual redefinition of the classes themselves, only identification of boundary cases.

2. **Superficial comparison to existing PoS tagging approaches**: The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is limited to confusion matrices without demonstrating how the distributional approach offers advantages for practical PoS tagging. The paper shows that distributional models reveal different error patterns than traditional taggers, but doesn't demonstrate that these insights lead to better overall tagging performance.

3. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The paper's title and abstract suggest a fundamental rethinking of PoS classification, but the actual contribution is limited to identifying boundary cases and annotation errors. The authors don't propose any new PoS classes or fundamentally revise the existing classification system, which weakens the paper's impact and makes the title misleading.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you distinguish your approach to identifying PoS annotation errors from prior work that has used confusion matrices to identify tagging errors? What makes your distributional approach more insightful?

2. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but doesn't propose any actual redefinition. Could you clarify the specific contribution that constitutes this redefinition, and how it would change existing linguistic practice?

3. The analysis is limited to English. How would you expect these findings to generalize to languages with different morphological structures and PoS systems? What specific challenges might arise in other languages?

## Overall Recommendation

I am hesitant to recommend acceptance for this paper. While it contains valuable insights about PoS annotation inconsistencies and the distributed nature of linguistic information in embeddings, the core methodology builds directly on prior work without making a truly novel contribution. The paper doesn't "redefine" PoS classes as the title suggests—it identifies boundary cases and annotation errors, which is useful but not revolutionary.

The paper's main contribution is a method for detecting annotation errors, which is valuable for corpus linguists, but it's not sufficiently novel to meet the high bar for acceptance at CoNLL, particularly given the conference's emphasis on bold, novel approaches. The insights are interesting and useful, but they don't represent a significant new direction for the field.

I would recommend rejection, as the paper offers only incremental improvement over existing approaches to PoS analysis rather than a truly novel concept or direction. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the novelty claim or provide more compelling evidence of a fundamentally new approach to be considered for acceptance.
