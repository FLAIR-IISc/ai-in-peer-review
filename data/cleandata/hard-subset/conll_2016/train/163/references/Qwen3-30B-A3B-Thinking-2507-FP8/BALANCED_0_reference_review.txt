

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassifications ("outliers") to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and potential annotation inconsistencies. They demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of the embedding space and build a simple PoS predictor that works without contextual information.

## Main Contributions
1. Demonstrating that distributional semantic models contain robust information about PoS classes, evidenced by high classification accuracy (F-score 0.979).
2. Using classifier misclassifications to identify linguistic boundary cases and potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC corpus.
3. Showing that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of the embedding space (at least 100 components), rather than concentrated in specific features.

## Strengths
1. **Novel linguistic insights from outlier analysis**: The paper goes beyond simply demonstrating that embeddings contain PoS information to identify specific boundary cases between PoS classes (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, adjectives vs. nouns, passive participles). This provides concrete evidence of where traditional PoS boundaries are fuzzy, which is valuable for linguistic theory.

2. **Sound methodology and thorough evaluation**: The authors carefully document their data processing, model training, and evaluation procedures. Their use of multiple datasets (BNC, UD Treebank), multiple evaluation metrics (F-score, accuracy, coverage), and comparison with a Stanford PoS tagger's confusion matrix strengthens their claims.

3. **Important finding about distribution of PoS information**: The paper convincingly shows that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), which challenges the common assumption that specific linguistic features might be encoded in specific vector dimensions. This finding has implications for how we understand what information is stored in word embeddings.

4. **Practical potential for low-resource languages**: The demonstration that a PoS predictor can work with minimal contextual information (only distributional patterns from a corpus) has promising implications for low-resource language settings, where manual annotation is expensive.

5. **Comparative analysis with traditional taggers**: The comparison with the Stanford PoS tagger's confusion matrix is insightful, showing that the distributional approach reveals different error patterns than traditional taggers, suggesting complementary insights.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty claim**: The paper builds on well-established prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2016) without clearly differentiating its contribution. The core idea that embeddings contain syntactic information is not new, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how its specific approach advances the field beyond existing work.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper only compares its PoS predictor to simple baselines (majority class and one-feature classifiers), not to modern state-of-the-art PoS taggers. This makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of their approach. The claim that "these numbers may not seem very impressive" is accurate but doesn't address why their method would be preferable to existing taggers.

3. **Lack of concrete application for improvement**: While the paper identifies annotation inconsistencies, it doesn't provide a clear path for how these insights could be used to improve PoS tagging systems or corpus annotation strategies. The practical implications of their findings are underdeveloped.

4. **Insufficient addressing of polysemy**: The paper doesn't address how their approach would handle polysemous words with different PoS tags in different contexts, which is a major challenge in PoS tagging. This is a significant limitation for practical applications.

5. **Circular reasoning in conclusion**: The paper concludes that "part of speech boundaries are not strict" based on the model's ability to identify boundary cases, which essentially assumes what it's trying to prove rather than providing independent evidence.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would your approach handle polysemous words that have different PoS tags in different contexts? The paper doesn't address this central challenge in PoS tagging.

2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers, not just baselines, to better understand the relative strengths and weaknesses of your approach?

3. What specific improvements to the BNC annotation strategy would you recommend based on your outlier analysis? How would you implement these changes?

4. The paper mentions "the largest gap in performance is between k = 2 and k = 3" - can you elaborate on why this is the case, and what this implies about the distribution of PoS information in embeddings?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes an interesting contribution by demonstrating how distributional semantic models can reveal linguistic insights about PoS boundaries. The outlier analysis is particularly valuable, providing concrete examples of where traditional PoS boundaries are fuzzy. However, the paper's novelty claim is weak, and it doesn't sufficiently address how its approach compares to or improves upon existing PoS tagging systems.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the authors would need to substantially strengthen their novelty argument and provide a clearer path for how their approach could be practically applied to improve PoS tagging systems. The paper has significant potential but requires more careful positioning within the existing literature and clearer articulation of its practical contributions.

The authors should focus on clarifying how their approach differs from and improves upon existing work, provide a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers, and develop a more concrete plan for how their insights could be used to improve annotation or tagging systems. Addressing polysemy would also be a significant strength.
