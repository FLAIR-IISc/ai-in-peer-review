

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about parts of speech (PoS). The authors train a skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC), then use logistic regression to predict PoS tags from word vectors. They analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" that reveal fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes or potential annotation errors in the corpus. The paper also demonstrates that a classifier can predict PoS tags for words without prior PoS information, using only distributional features.

## Main Contributions
1. Empirical demonstration that PoS information is robustly embedded in distributional word vectors
2. Identification of linguistic patterns at PoS boundaries through error analysis
3. Methodological approach for detecting annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora

## Strengths

**1. Compelling empirical evidence of PoS information in embeddings**  
The paper provides strong evidence that distributional semantic models contain significant PoS information. The classifier achieves a high F-score (0.979) on the training set and 0.91 on unseen data, demonstrating that word embeddings contain meaningful PoS-related patterns. The authors effectively show that this information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300), rather than concentrated in specific features. This finding is well-supported by their analysis of prototypical vectors for different PoS classes.

**2. Insightful linguistic analysis of "outliers"**  
The error analysis reveals genuine linguistic phenomena that go beyond what traditional PoS taggers can identify. The authors demonstrate how distributional models can uncover:
- Ambiguous cases like "white-collar" (tagged as adjective but distributionally noun-like)
- Annotation inconsistencies (e.g., "plain" erroneously tagged as adverb)
- Contextual misclassifications (e.g., currency amounts like "£70" classified as numerals)
- Boundary cases between proper and common nouns

This analysis provides concrete evidence for linguistic theories about fuzzy PoS boundaries and offers a practical method for identifying annotation errors in corpora.

**3. Practical methodology for PoS boundary analysis**  
The paper presents a clear, reproducible methodology for using distributional models to investigate PoS boundaries. The approach of training a classifier on embeddings and analyzing misclassifications provides a systematic way to identify cases where traditional PoS tagging may be problematic. This method could be valuable for corpus linguists and NLP researchers working with PoS-tagged corpora.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited novelty of core approach**  
While the analysis of misclassifications is insightful, the core approach (using embeddings to predict PoS) builds directly on prior work by Tsuboi (2014), Liu et al. (2016), and Tsvetkov et al. (2015). The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new method but rather applies existing techniques to a new analytical purpose. The "redefining" claim in the title overstates the contribution, as the paper identifies cases of ambiguity rather than proposing new PoS categories.

**2. Not a competitive PoS tagger**  
The paper's main contribution is not a new tagger, as the classifier achieves only 0.84 accuracy on unseen data (compared to state-of-the-art taggers achieving >0.97). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently emphasize that their method is not intended as a practical tagger but rather as an analytical tool. The presentation blurs this distinction, potentially misleading readers about the paper's practical contribution.

**3. Limited scope of linguistic insights**  
The analysis focuses primarily on English, with no evidence that the approach would generalize to other languages with different grammatical structures. The paper mentions future work on other languages but provides no data to support this claim. The insights about PoS boundaries are largely consistent with established linguistic knowledge (e.g., "proper nouns vs. common nouns"), rather than revealing truly novel linguistic phenomena.

## Questions for Authors

1. How would you respond to the claim that the paper's "redefining" approach is misleading, as it doesn't propose new PoS categories but rather identifies cases of ambiguity in existing tagging?

2. The paper states that "the majority of words are classified correctly" but doesn't provide evidence that the classifier's accuracy is significantly better than simple heuristics (e.g., frequency-based PoS prediction). How would you demonstrate the added value of the distributional approach over simpler methods?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models. The analysis of misclassifications provides concrete evidence of PoS boundary phenomena that could inform linguistic theory and corpus annotation practices. However, the novelty is limited, and the paper overstates its contribution as a "redefinition" of PoS classes.

The main strength is the empirical demonstration of PoS information in embeddings and the insightful linguistic analysis of errors. The weaknesses are the limited novelty of the approach and the overstatement of the contribution. The paper would be stronger if it positioned itself more clearly as a methodology for linguistic analysis rather than as a redefinition of PoS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better clarify the paper's contribution and limit overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes. The linguistic insights and methodological approach are valuable, but the presentation should more accurately reflect the paper's actual contribution.
