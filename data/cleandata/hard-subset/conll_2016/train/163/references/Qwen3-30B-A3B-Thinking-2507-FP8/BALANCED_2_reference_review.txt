

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) can reveal insights about parts-of-speech (PoS) classes and boundaries. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" that may reveal fuzzy PoS boundaries or annotation errors. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust information about PoS classes (F-score 0.979), (2) using misclassifications to identify linguistic patterns at PoS boundaries (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, currency amounts as numerals), and (3) showing that distributional models can detect annotation inconsistencies in existing PoS-tagged corpora.

## Strengths
1. **Insightful analysis of PoS boundaries**: The paper's most significant contribution is its detailed examination of misclassified words, revealing concrete examples where PoS boundaries are linguistically fuzzy. For instance, the paper identifies that passive participles (e.g., "overgrown," "indented") behave like adjectives, and currency amounts (e.g., "£70," "33%") should be classified as numerals rather than nouns. These findings directly address the paper's central thesis that PoS boundaries are not strict.

2. **Demonstration of practical utility**: The paper convincingly shows that distributional models can detect annotation errors in existing corpora (like BNC) that might otherwise go unnoticed. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is particularly strong, as it demonstrates that the distributional approach reveals different error patterns (e.g., identifying adjectives mistakenly tagged as adverbs), suggesting it could complement traditional tagging approaches.

3. **Robust experimental design**: The authors employ careful methodology, including using a balanced training set weighted by word frequency, cross-validation, and testing on multiple corpora (BNC and UD Treebank). The classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training set, 0.91 on unseen BNC test set), providing strong evidence for their core hypothesis.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: While the application of distributional models to PoS analysis is interesting, the methodology itself (training a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings) is not novel. Previous work (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already shown that dimensions of distributional models correlate with linguistic features. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work, which weakens the perceived contribution.

2. **Superficial evaluation of practical utility**: The paper claims the method could be useful for "preliminary tagging of large corpora of resource-poor languages," but provides only minimal evaluation (84% accuracy on UD Treebank test set). Without comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers (which typically achieve 97-98% accuracy), it's difficult to assess the practical value of the approach. The paper doesn't address whether the method would be competitive with existing tools.

3. **Lack of deep linguistic analysis**: While the paper identifies interesting misclassification patterns, it doesn't provide sufficient linguistic explanation for why these patterns occur. For example, when discussing currency amounts misclassified as nouns, the paper doesn't delve into why these should be classified as numerals from a linguistic perspective, which would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the proposed method for identifying PoS boundary cases differ from traditional approaches that use confusion matrices from standard PoS taggers? What specific insights does the distributional approach provide that traditional taggers cannot?

2. The paper claims that "distributional models can detect outright errors in PoS-tagged corpora," but the evidence provided (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs) is somewhat anecdotal. Could the authors provide more systematic evidence of annotation errors detected by their method?

3. The paper notes that "part of speech affiliation is distributed among many components of the word embeddings." How does this finding relate to previous work on the semantic vs. grammatical information in word embeddings? What are the implications for future work in this area?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes an interesting contribution to understanding PoS boundaries and demonstrates a novel application of distributional semantic models for linguistic analysis. However, the lack of clear novelty in methodology and superficial evaluation of practical utility prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at CONLL. With substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above—particularly by comparing the method more thoroughly to prior work, providing deeper linguistic analysis, and offering a more robust evaluation of practical utility—this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. As currently written, it falls short of the high standards expected for CONLL.
