

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper explores whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, claiming to "redefine" PoS classes through this lens. The authors propose a method where they train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassified words ("outliers") to identify potential inconsistencies in PoS annotation. They further claim to build a PoS tagger using only distributional vectors without prior PoS tags.

The primary contributions claimed are: (1) demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust PoS information, (2) using this to identify potential annotation errors in existing corpora, and (3) building a PoS tagger based solely on distributional vectors.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a clear demonstration that word embeddings do contain PoS information, which is consistent with prior work (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016). The high classification accuracy (F-score 0.979) is a solid empirical result.

2. The outlier analysis offers some interesting insights into potential annotation inconsistencies, particularly regarding borderline cases like "proper nouns vs. common nouns" and "nouns vs. adjectives." This could be useful for corpus linguists.

3. The paper shows that their approach can detect annotation errors, which is a valuable contribution for corpus creation and maintenance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The core idea that word embeddings contain PoS information is not new. The paper cites prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) showing exactly this, yet the authors present their work as a novel contribution. The "redefining" claim is particularly overstated, as they're not proposing a new theoretical framework for PoS classes but rather analyzing existing annotations through a new lens. This is a standard application of existing knowledge rather than a significant methodological advance.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparisons**: The authors compare their method only to a "majority class baseline" and a "one-feature baseline," but fail to compare against established PoS taggers (e.g., Stanford Tagger, which they briefly mention but don't properly evaluate against). The paper claims their PoS tagger achieves "84% accuracy," but this is substantially lower than state-of-the-art taggers (which typically exceed 97% accuracy on standard benchmarks). Without proper comparison to established methods, the significance of this result is unclear.

3. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The paper's title and claims suggest a fundamental rethinking of PoS classes, but the analysis of outliers merely confirms what linguists have long known about fuzzy boundaries between word classes (e.g., participles being both verbs and adjectives). The examples they provide (e.g., "white-collar" being tagged as adjective but behaving as a noun) are well-documented in linguistic literature, not novel discoveries.

4. **Methodological concerns**: The paper trains a classifier on word vectors that were themselves trained on PoS-tagged data (by concatenating words with their PoS tags), which creates a circular dependency. This makes it difficult to claim that the model is learning PoS information independently of the annotation. The subsequent "unseen data" testing on the UD Treebank is promising but not sufficiently validated against standard PoS taggers.

5. **Lack of practical impact**: The paper's approach doesn't offer a practical improvement over existing PoS tagging methods. Modern taggers already incorporate distributional information, and the authors' simple logistic regression approach is substantially less accurate than established methods. The paper's claim that this could be useful for "preliminary tagging of large corpora of resource-poor languages" is speculative without evidence of its performance on such languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your PoS tagger's performance compare to established taggers (e.g., Stanford Tagger, UCCA, or spaCy) on standard English PoS tagging benchmarks? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the practical value of your approach.

2. Given that your training data already contains PoS tags (you concatenated words with their PoS tags), how can you claim your model is "discovering" PoS information rather than simply reproducing the existing annotation?

3. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but the examples you provide (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives) are well-known phenomena in linguistic literature. How is your analysis fundamentally different from what has already been established in the field?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to the literature. While the empirical results are correct, the claims of novelty and significance are overstated. The paper fails to demonstrate that their approach offers substantial advantages over established methods, and the "redefining" claims are not supported by the evidence presented. The work is more of an application of existing knowledge rather than a significant advance.

Given the high standards of CONLL, this paper does not meet the threshold for acceptance. The lack of methodological novelty and the failure to properly validate against established baselines are significant weaknesses that cannot be overlooked. The paper would benefit from a major revision that clarifies the novelty of their approach, provides proper comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and more carefully frames their contributions in relation to existing literature.
