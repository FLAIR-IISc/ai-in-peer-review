

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, then analyze misclassifications to identify "outliers" that lie on the boundaries between PoS classes. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that distributional models contain robust information about PoS classes, (2) providing a method to detect systematic errors in PoS-tagged corpora, and (3) showing that a classifier trained on distributional vectors can predict PoS tags with reasonable accuracy without prior annotations.

## Strengths

1. The paper presents a novel approach to analyzing PoS boundaries using distributional semantic models, which offers valuable insights into the fuzzy nature of parts of speech. The identification of "outliers" that behave differently from their traditional PoS classification (e.g., proper nouns mistaken for common nouns, currency amounts misclassified as nouns) provides concrete evidence for linguistic theories about PoS boundaries.

2. The method for detecting systematic errors in PoS-tagged corpora is particularly valuable. The authors demonstrate that distributional models can identify outright errors in corpora, such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs in the BNC, which is a significant contribution to corpus linguistics.

3. The paper shows that a distributional predictor can achieve reasonable PoS tagging accuracy (84%) without any prior PoS annotations, which could be useful for resource-poor languages where manual tagging is impractical. This is an important practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical reproducibility issues**: The paper lacks essential details for replication. The frequency cutoff for word inclusion is inconsistent (stated as "less than 5" but then using "more than 500" for the 10,000 most frequent words). The exact mapping between BNC tags and Universal tags is not provided, despite being claimed as "manually created" and "released online." The paper states "a symmetric window of 2 words" for the skip-gram model, which is unrealistically small (standard window sizes are 5-10) but offers no justification for this choice. These omissions make it impossible to reproduce the results.

2. **Misrepresentation of Universal Dependencies**: The paper claims "the Universal Dependencies Treebank is more consistent in this respect: it separates entities like '1$' into two tokens: a numeral (NUM) and a symbol (SYM)." This is inaccurate - UD guidelines specify that symbols like '$' should be tagged as SYM, but the tokenization of "1$" is typically handled by the tokenizer, not the tagger. The UD Treebank does not automatically split "1$" into two tokens.

3. **Methodological inconsistencies**: The paper states "the model was trained on 177,343 (sometimes repeating) instances, instead of the original 10,000" but doesn't clarify how these instances were counted or why this approach was necessary. The claim that "the classifier was in fact based only on knowledge of what words occurred in the BNC near other words within a symmetric window of 2 word to the left and to the right" is an oversimplification of how word embeddings work.

4. **Formatting and presentation errors**: The paper contains inconsistent capitalization of "PoS" vs. "PoS," references to figures that are not included, and inconsistent formatting in the reference list (e.g., "Biber et al., 1999" vs. "Biber et al.1999"). These minor issues detract from the professionalism of the submission.

5. **Unsupported claims**: The paper claims "it is obvious that distributionally these two classes of words are very similar" (referring to proper nouns vs. common nouns) without providing evidence for this assertion. The statement "the majority of proper nouns in the test set (88%) was correctly predicted as such" is presented as interesting without explaining why this percentage is noteworthy or how it compares to other PoS classes.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide the exact mapping between BNC tags and Universal tags that you used? The paper states this was created manually and released online, but no URL or details are provided.

2. The paper states "Words with corpus frequency less than 5 were ignored" but then uses "10,000 most frequent words" with "corpus frequency more than 500." Can you clarify this discrepancy and explain why the cutoff changed?

3. The paper mentions "a symmetric window of 2 words" for the skip-gram model. Can you justify this unusually small window size and explain how it affected the results compared to standard window sizes?

4. The paper claims "the Universal Dependencies Treebank is more consistent in this respect" regarding currency amounts. Can you provide specific examples from the UD Treebank that demonstrate this consistency?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to analyzing PoS boundaries using distributional semantic models. However, the methodological inconsistencies, lack of reproducibility details, and misrepresentations of the Universal Dependencies guidelines are serious issues. The paper's claims about the capabilities of distributional models are overstated due to these technical flaws. While the core idea has merit, the execution is hampered by these critical weaknesses that prevent the results from being properly evaluated or replicated.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the methodological shortcomings and lack of critical details prevent me from recommending acceptance. I would recommend rejection due to these fundamental reproducibility issues.
