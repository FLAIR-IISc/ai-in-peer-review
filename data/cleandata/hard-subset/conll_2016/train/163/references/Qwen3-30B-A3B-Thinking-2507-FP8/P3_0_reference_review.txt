

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a continuous skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC), then use logistic regression to predict PoS tags from word vectors. They identify "outliers" (words misclassified by their model) to explore potential inconsistencies in PoS annotation and boundaries between word classes. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) demonstrating robust PoS information in embeddings, (2) using misclassification analysis to identify annotation issues, and (3) showing PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions.

## Strengths

1. **Rigorous methodology and compelling evidence for PoS information in embeddings**: The authors systematically test their hypothesis through multiple experiments (classifier training, error analysis, cross-corpus validation). The high accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on unseen BNC data) and robust performance on Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99) provide strong evidence that word embeddings contain meaningful PoS information. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it moves beyond previous work that merely demonstrated the existence of some PoS-related information in embeddings.

2. **Insightful analysis of annotation inconsistencies**: The outlier analysis reveals concrete cases where traditional PoS boundaries may be problematic or where annotation errors exist. For example, the paper identifies that currency amounts like "£70" are correctly classified as numerals by the model (suggesting the BNC annotation may be inconsistent), and that proper nouns like "Luftwaffe" are often misclassified due to insufficient training examples. This practical application for corpus annotation is a valuable contribution with clear implications for linguistic practice.

3. **Demonstration of distributed representation of PoS information**: The paper provides compelling evidence that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), rather than concentrated in a few specific features. This aligns with theoretical expectations about how word embeddings work and offers important insights into the nature of semantic representations. The figure showing accuracy increasing with the number of top features used (Figure 3) is particularly convincing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The paper acknowledges their classifier's accuracy (0.84 on UD test set) is lower than modern taggers but fails to provide meaningful context for this comparison. Without benchmarking against established methods (e.g., neural taggers), it's difficult to assess whether their approach offers practical utility or represents a meaningful advance. For example, they don't state how much lower their accuracy is compared to SOTA, nor do they explain why their context-free approach should be useful given that modern taggers use rich contextual information.

2. **Overstated limitations of the model's contextual knowledge**: The authors claim their classifier "knows absolutely nothing about a word's context in the current sentence," but the distributional model is trained on context (window of 2 words left and right), so it does have some contextual information. This mischaracterization weakens their argument about the model's capabilities and creates confusion about what their approach actually achieves.

3. **Superficial analysis of linguistic implications**: While the paper identifies interesting patterns (e.g., currency amounts as numerals, proper nouns vs. common nouns), it doesn't provide deep linguistic analysis of what these patterns mean for linguistic theory. For instance, they note that proper nouns and common nouns have similar distributional patterns but don't explore how this challenges traditional linguistic views of these categories or what it means for the nature of "part-of-speech" as a linguistic concept.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to recent neural PoS taggers that use contextual information (e.g., BERT-based models)? What specific advantages might your context-free approach offer in resource-poor language settings?

2. The paper states that "part of speech boundaries are not strict" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical framework for how your findings challenge or refine existing linguistic theories about PoS categories. Could you elaborate on the theoretical implications of your findings for linguistic theory?

3. Your classifier achieved 0.91 F-score on BNC test data, but the authors note that "the vast majority of words are classified correctly." However, the 9% error rate (1558 misclassified words) represents a substantial number of linguistic cases worth deeper exploration. Could you elaborate on whether these errors cluster in particular linguistic phenomena that might inform a more nuanced PoS classification system?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating that distributional semantic models contain robust information about PoS classes and by providing a method to identify potential annotation inconsistencies. The methodology is sound and the findings are significant for both linguistic theory and corpus annotation practices. However, the paper would be strengthened by addressing the limitations in comparison to existing methods, clarifying the nature of the contextual information the model actually uses, and providing deeper linguistic analysis of the implications of their findings. With these improvements, this work could make a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics.
