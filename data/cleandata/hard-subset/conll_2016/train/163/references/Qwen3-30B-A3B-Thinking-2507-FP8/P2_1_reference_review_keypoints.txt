- Paper investigates whether word embeddings encode meaningful part-of-speech (PoS) information and trains a classifier to predict PoS from embeddings.  
- Classifier achieves high accuracy (F-score 0.979) and strong performance on Universal Dependencies (F-score 0.99).  
- Outlier/misclassification analysis reveals fuzzy PoS boundaries and potential annotation errors in the BNC.  
- Main contributions: empirical evidence of PoS information in embeddings, linguistic insights from misclassifications, and a method for detecting corpus annotation errors.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation with careful handling of class imbalance.  
- Strength: insightful linguistic findings (proper/common noun similarities, noun/adjective fuzziness, annotation inconsistencies, currency/numeral similarity).  
- Strength: practical utility for corpus quality control, complementary to standard PoS taggers.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—prior work has shown embeddings encode PoS information.  
- Weakness: title/claims overstate impact; analysis shows fuzzy boundaries rather than a new PoS framework.  
- Weakness: comparison to PoS taggers is misleading because classifiers lack contextual information and modern taggers reach 97%+ accuracy.  
- Question: how does this approach compare directly to prior embedding-based PoS methods (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015) and what unique value does the outlier analysis add?  
- Question: how should corpus creators incorporate this method to fix or improve annotations in practice?  
- Question: how does the distributed PoS signal vary across languages and what are the multilingual implications?  
- Overall: solid, useful contribution with strong results and practical insights but limited novelty and overstated claims; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify novelty and temper conclusions.
