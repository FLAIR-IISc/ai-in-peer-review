

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a Continuous Skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC), then train a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags based on word vectors. They find high classification accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on held-out test data), demonstrating that embeddings contain robust PoS-related information. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that word embeddings contain robust information about PoS classes, enabling accurate prediction of PoS tags from word vectors alone.
2. Using misclassified examples ("outliers") to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and potential inconsistencies in existing PoS annotation systems.
3. Showing that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300) rather than concentrated in specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical evidence of PoS information in embeddings**: The paper provides rigorous empirical validation of the relationship between word embeddings and PoS classes. The high classification accuracy (F-score 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on held-out test data) is compelling evidence that embeddings contain meaningful PoS-related information. The validation on the Universal Dependencies Treebank (F-score 0.99) further demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

2. **Insightful analysis of boundary cases**: The paper's most valuable contribution is the detailed analysis of misclassified words, which reveals concrete linguistic phenomena at PoS boundaries. The authors identify specific patterns (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives, verbs vs. adjectives) and even discover potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC (e.g., adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs). This provides data-driven evidence for linguistic theories about flexible PoS boundaries.

3. **Important finding about distribution of PoS information**: The paper demonstrates that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300), rather than concentrated in one or two specific features. This finding is significant for understanding how linguistic information is encoded in distributional models and contradicts the common assumption that specific linguistic features might be localized in particular dimensions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core approach**: The paper's main claim that word embeddings contain PoS information is not novel. Prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already established this relationship. The paper does not substantially advance beyond these findings, merely confirming what is already known with a different corpus and methodology. The claim to "redefine" PoS classes is overstated as the paper does not propose a new theoretical framework for PoS classes.

2. **Artificial training procedure for classifier**: The core classifier in Section 3 is trained on word vectors where each word is concatenated with its PoS tag (e.g., "love_VERB"). This means the model is trained on data that already contains the PoS information it's supposed to predict. This is not a fair comparison to a true PoS tagger that would predict PoS from raw words without prior tag information, undermining the paper's claim about "predicting" PoS from embeddings alone.

3. **Overstated claims about annotation errors**: The paper claims to "detect outright errors in PoS-tagged corpora," but the evidence is limited. The authors identify cases where the classifier disagrees with BNC annotations, but they don't provide systematic evidence that these are actually errors. In many cases, the disagreement could stem from different annotation conventions rather than actual errors. The paper doesn't compare their findings to other linguistic studies or annotation guidelines to establish that the BNC annotations are incorrect.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the classifier in Section 5 "predicts a PoS tag for a word without any pre-existing tags as hints." However, the training data (BNC lemmas without PoS tags) still implicitly contains PoS information through the context of the corpus. How does this differ from the training procedure in Section 3 (where words are concatenated with PoS tags), and why is the Section 5 approach considered more "real" for PoS prediction?

2. The paper identifies BNC annotation inconsistencies based on classifier disagreement. However, the BNC uses a different tagset than Universal Dependencies (UD), and the authors manually mapped BNC tags to UD tags. How did this mapping affect the identification of "errors," and how would the results change if they used a more direct comparison with BNC's own tagset?

3. The paper claims "PoS affiliation is distributed among at least one hundred components of the word embeddings." Figure 3 shows accuracy increases steadily with the number of features, but the plateau is unclear. What evidence supports the claim that at least 100 components are necessary, beyond the visual trend in Figure 3?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a solid empirical contribution by confirming and extending prior work on the relationship between word embeddings and PoS classes. Its most valuable aspect is the detailed analysis of boundary cases, which provides concrete linguistic insights. However, the core approach lacks novelty, and the claims about "redefining" PoS classes and detecting "outlier errors" are overstated. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly the artificial training procedure and the overstatement of annotation errors.
