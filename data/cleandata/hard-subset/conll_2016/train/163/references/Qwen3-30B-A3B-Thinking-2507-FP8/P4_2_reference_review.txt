

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors propose that by training a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, they can identify "outlier" words that behave differently from their assigned PoS class, revealing insights about PoS boundaries and potential annotation errors.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A methodology for identifying boundary cases between PoS classes by analyzing misclassifications of a distributional semantic model-based classifier, which reveals linguistic insights about PoS boundaries not apparent through traditional annotation.
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings (at least 100 out of 300 dimensions), rather than concentrated in specific features.
3. Proof-of-concept for using distributional models to detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora, showing different error patterns than traditional PoS taggers.

## Strengths

1. **Novel analytical approach to PoS boundaries**: The paper presents a fresh methodology for studying PoS boundaries by leveraging distributional semantic models to identify words that behave differently from their assigned PoS class. This approach reveals interesting boundary cases (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nominal vs. adjectival patterns) that align with linguistic intuition but are not easily captured by traditional methods. The analysis of "outliers" provides concrete evidence for the fluidity of PoS boundaries, supporting the theoretical foundation that "grammatical categories exist along a continuum."

2. **Rigorous technical evaluation**: The experimental design is methodologically sound, with proper cross-validation (10-fold) and multiple evaluation datasets (BNC, UD Treebank). The classifier achieves strong performance (F-score 0.91 on unseen data), providing robust evidence that embeddings contain PoS information. The analysis of vector components through ANOVA and accuracy curves (Figure 3) effectively demonstrates that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions, with accuracy increasing smoothly as more features are added.

3. **Practical utility for corpus linguistics**: The paper demonstrates that distributional models can detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora that traditional PoS taggers miss (e.g., adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs). This has direct practical implications for corpus linguistics, as the authors show their approach identifies systematic errors in BNC annotation that align with linguistic intuition. The comparison with Stanford PoS Tagger (Tables 3-4) effectively demonstrates the unique insights provided by the distributional approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core hypothesis**: While the methodology is novel, the basic hypothesis that word embeddings contain syntactic information is not new. Prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2016; Tsvetkov et al., 2015) has already established that distributional models capture syntactic features, including PoS. The paper's main contribution is in the analysis of boundary cases, but this represents an incremental extension of existing work rather than a fundamentally new approach.

2. **Overstated claims about "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and some claims suggest a more fundamental redefinition of PoS classes, but the analysis mostly confirms existing linguistic intuitions about PoS boundaries (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, participles behaving like adjectives). The paper doesn't propose a new PoS tagging scheme or fundamentally redefine PoS classes—instead, it identifies cases where current annotation might be inconsistent. This overstatement weakens the paper's contribution.

3. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper focuses exclusively on English, with no analysis of how the approach might generalize to languages with different grammatical structures. While the authors mention future work on other languages, the current evaluation is limited to English, reducing the generalizability of their findings. The analysis of vector components (Section 5) also lacks comparison to other embedding models or training parameters, making it difficult to assess the robustness of their findings.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the performance of the classifier change when using embeddings trained on different corpora (e.g., Wikipedia vs. BNC)? This would help assess whether the PoS information is corpus-dependent or more general.

2. The paper states that "the majority of proper nouns in the test set (88%) was correctly predicted as such." What specific features of proper nouns are captured by the model that distinguish them from common nouns, and how do these features compare to traditional linguistic features used to distinguish them?

3. The paper claims "the knowledge about PoS affiliation is distributed among at least one hundred components of the word embeddings." What is the evidence for this claim beyond the accuracy plateau at k=100? How would this finding impact the design of future word embedding models?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear methodology and appropriate evaluation. The insights about PoS boundaries are linguistically interesting and have practical implications for corpus annotation. However, the novelty claims could be more precisely framed to reflect the incremental nature of the contribution. The authors should clarify that they are not "redefining" PoS classes but rather providing a new analytical tool for studying existing boundary cases.
