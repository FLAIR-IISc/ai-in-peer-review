

# Review for CoNLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes, and whether this information can reveal the fuzzy boundaries between PoS categories. The authors propose a novel approach to studying PoS as a continuum rather than discrete categories, using distributional models to identify "outliers" - words that behave differently from their assigned PoS category.

The main contributions, as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **Novel linguistic perspective on PoS boundaries**: The paper provides a data-driven method to identify where PoS boundaries are fuzzy, demonstrating that many words exist on the "verge" between categories (e.g., nouns behaving like adjectives, verbs like adjectives). This challenges traditional categorical views of PoS and provides empirical evidence for the continuum nature of linguistic categories.

2. **Practical method for detecting PoS annotation errors**: The authors demonstrate how their approach can identify systematic errors in existing PoS-tagged corpora (like BNC), such as adjectives incorrectly tagged as adverbs, or "1804" being tagged as a noun rather than a numeral. This has direct utility for corpus linguists maintaining annotated corpora.

3. **Insight into how linguistic information is encoded in embeddings**: The paper reveals that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions (at least 100 in their 300-dimensional model), rather than concentrated in a few specific features. This provides a fundamental understanding of how linguistic information is represented in distributional models.

## Strengths

1. **Groundbreaking perspective on PoS as a continuum**: The paper's central insight - that PoS boundaries are fuzzy and can be studied empirically through distributional models - is profoundly novel. The authors don't just state this as a linguistic fact but provide a concrete method to identify exactly where boundaries are fuzzy. This represents a significant shift from traditional categorical approaches to PoS, and the data-driven approach to studying linguistic boundaries is exactly the kind of bold thinking that advances the field.

2. **Practical utility for corpus linguistics**: The paper demonstrates a genuinely useful application of distributional models beyond semantic tasks. By identifying systematic annotation errors (like adjectives tagged as adverbs), the method provides a valuable tool for corpus linguists to improve their annotated data. The authors show that their distributional predictor reveals different error patterns than standard taggers, offering a complementary perspective on corpus quality.

3. **Insight into linguistic information in embeddings**: The finding that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions (not concentrated in a few) is a significant contribution to our understanding of how linguistic knowledge is encoded in distributional models. This challenges assumptions about how linguistic features map to embedding dimensions and has implications for future work on embedding interpretability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological standardization**: While the approach is novel, the methodology itself (logistic regression on word embeddings) is quite standard. The paper doesn't introduce a novel algorithm or model architecture, which is a weakness for a conference like CoNLL that values methodological innovation. The authors should be more explicit about why this specific application is more valuable than the standard methods they're applying.

2. **Limited scope and generalizability**: The paper focuses exclusively on English, with no evidence that these findings hold across languages. The authors mention future work on other languages but provide no justification for why their approach would generalize. This limits the paper's broader impact and makes it difficult to assess the significance of their findings.

3. **Overstated claims in title**: The title promises to "redefine" PoS classes, but the paper doesn't actually propose a new PoS system. It identifies potential annotation issues but doesn't provide a principled framework for creating a new classification system. This is misleading and overstates the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the vast majority of words are classified correctly," but the classifier's accuracy (84% on the UD test set) is significantly lower than state-of-the-art PoS taggers (which typically exceed 97%). How do you justify that this is a "significant improvement" over baseline methods, given the substantial performance gap with existing taggers?

2. The paper identifies "systematic errors" in the BNC annotation, such as adjectives tagged as adverbs. However, the UD Treebank shows a different pattern of errors. How do you explain these discrepancies, and what does this say about the reliability of the distributional approach for identifying annotation errors?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold and novel perspective on part-of-speech categories that challenges traditional linguistic views. While the methodology is standard, the insight that PoS boundaries are fuzzy and can be studied empirically through distributional models is significant. The paper's practical utility for detecting annotation errors in corpora is particularly valuable for the NLP community.

Despite the methodological standardization and limited scope, the paper's novel linguistic perspective and practical applications outweigh these weaknesses. I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a data-driven approach to studying the continuum of PoS categories, which is exactly the kind of innovative thinking that CoNLL should prioritize.

**Recommendation: Accept**
