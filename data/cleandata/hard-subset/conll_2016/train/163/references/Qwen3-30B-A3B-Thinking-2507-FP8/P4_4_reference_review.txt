

# Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a skip-gram model on the British National Corpus (BNC), then use word vectors to train a classifier for PoS prediction. They analyze misclassifications to identify boundary cases between PoS classes and potential annotation errors in the BNC. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstration that word embeddings contain robust information about PoS classes, with a classifier achieving 97.9% F-score on a 10,000-word vocabulary.
2. Systematic analysis of misclassifications to identify boundary cases between PoS classes (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, nouns vs. adjectives) and potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC.
3. Evidence that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300), rather than concentrated in specific features.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to PoS boundary analysis**: The paper makes a significant contribution by using distributional models to systematically identify boundary cases between PoS classes. Previous work has shown embeddings contain some PoS information, but this paper goes further by analyzing misclassifications to reveal specific linguistic cases where traditional PoS boundaries appear fluid (e.g., nouns like "materialist_NOUN" exhibiting adjective-like distributions). This provides concrete evidence for the "continuum" nature of PoS categories.

2. **Rigorous experimental design and evaluation**: The authors employ multiple datasets (BNC, Universal Dependencies Treebank), use appropriate evaluation methods (10-fold cross-validation, held-out test sets), and provide thorough error analysis. The classifier achieves strong performance (91% F-score on unseen data), and the results are robust across different corpora. The paper also demonstrates the classifier's ability to detect annotation errors (e.g., adjectives like "plain" incorrectly tagged as adverbs in BNC), which is a significant practical contribution.

3. **Insight into distribution of PoS information in embeddings**: The paper provides novel evidence that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions rather than concentrated in a few specific features. The ANOVA analysis showing accuracy increases with the number of features (reaching near-perfect performance at ~100 features) challenges the notion that specific embedding dimensions correspond to linguistic features, offering important insights into how semantic models encode grammatical information.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated contribution regarding "redefining" PoS classes**: The title and some claims in the paper suggest the approach "redefines" PoS classes, but the paper doesn't actually propose a new PoS classification system or redefine traditional categories. It identifies boundary cases and potential annotation errors, but doesn't provide a new theoretical framework for PoS categories. This overstatement weakens the paper's contribution.

2. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art taggers**: While the paper notes that their classifier's performance (84% accuracy on UD Treebank) is lower than modern taggers, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison to show how their approach differs or complements existing methods. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's claims about the utility of their approach for linguistic analysis.

3. **Superficial analysis of vector components**: The paper identifies a specific component (94) as important for conjunctions but provides no linguistic explanation for why this component might be relevant. Similarly, the analysis of how PoS information is distributed across dimensions is limited to ANOVA F-values without deeper linguistic interpretation, which limits the paper's contribution to linguistic theory.

4. **Limited discussion of practical applications**: The paper briefly mentions potential use in resource-poor language settings but doesn't elaborate on how this approach would be implemented or what specific advantages it offers over existing methods. More concrete discussion of practical applications would strengthen the paper's impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "redefine" PoS classes, but doesn't propose a new tagging system. Could the authors clarify the specific theoretical contribution to PoS theory beyond identifying boundary cases?

2. How would the authors expect their approach to be integrated with existing PoS tagging pipelines? What specific benefits does it offer over current state-of-the-art taggers for linguistic analysis?

3. The paper states PoS information is distributed across "at least 100" components. How did the authors determine this threshold, and is this number consistent across different embedding models or training conditions?

4. The paper identifies annotation errors in BNC but doesn't discuss how these errors might affect downstream NLP applications. Could the authors comment on the potential impact of these errors on NLP tasks that rely on PoS tagging?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between distributional semantic models and PoS categories. The analysis of misclassifications reveals interesting boundary cases and potential annotation errors, providing concrete evidence for the fluid nature of PoS categories. The experimental design is sound, and the results are robust. However, the paper overstates its contribution by claiming to "redefine" PoS classes without providing a new theoretical framework. A more focused presentation of the boundary case analysis and annotation error detection would strengthen the paper's contribution. With minor revisions to address the overstatement of claims and provide more detailed comparisons to existing methods, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
