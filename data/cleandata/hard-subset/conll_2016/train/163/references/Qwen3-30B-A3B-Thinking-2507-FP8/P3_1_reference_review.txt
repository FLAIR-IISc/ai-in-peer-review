

# Peer Review: "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates whether distributional semantic models contain information about part-of-speech (PoS) categories that can reveal boundaries between traditional PoS classes. The authors train a classifier to predict PoS tags from word embeddings (trained on lemma+PoS combinations) and analyze misclassifications as "outliers" that reveal linguistic phenomena at PoS boundaries. Their main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that PoS information is robustly embedded in distributional models, distributed across multiple vector dimensions (at least 100 out of 300)
2. Providing a method to identify boundary cases between PoS categories through classifier misclassifications
3. Revealing potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC corpus

## Strengths

1. **Novel identification of corpus annotation inconsistencies**: The analysis of misclassifications reveals systematic annotation errors, such as currency amounts being tagged as nouns instead of numerals (e.g., "£70" classified as NOUN instead of NUM), which the Universal Dependencies Treebank correctly handles. This is a significant contribution to corpus linguistics, as it provides a data-driven method for detecting annotation issues that might otherwise go unnoticed.

2. **Empirical demonstration of distributed PoS representation**: The paper compellingly shows that PoS information is not concentrated in a few vector dimensions but distributed across many (Figure 3). This aligns with theoretical expectations about embedding representations and provides concrete evidence about how grammatical information is encoded in distributional models.

3. **Methodological contribution to PoS boundary analysis**: By systematically analyzing misclassifications rather than focusing solely on classification accuracy, the authors provide a new lens for studying the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes. Their approach reveals patterns like the contextual similarity between proper nouns and common nouns (88% correctly classified) and the adjectival behavior of certain nouns (e.g., "hypertext" classified as ADJ).

## Weaknesses

1. **Methodological circularity in classifier training**: The classifier is trained on embeddings derived from a corpus where words were concatenated with their PoS tags (lemma+PoS), creating a fundamental circularity. The classifier essentially learns the PoS categories used to create the embeddings, rather than discovering new linguistic insights. This undermines the paper's claim that the model reveals "real" PoS boundaries. A more valid approach would train embeddings on plain lemmas and then predict PoS tags.

2. **Overstated claims about "boundary cases"**: Many misclassifications (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns) reflect the inherent ambiguity of PoS categories rather than "boundary cases" between well-defined categories. The paper conflates cases where traditional PoS categories are ill-defined with cases where words genuinely straddle boundaries. This weakens the theoretical contribution of the work.

3. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers**: The comparison with the Stanford tagger is superficial and doesn't address the core question of whether the distributional approach provides more accurate PoS tags. The Stanford tagger uses rich contextual information that the distributional model lacks (only 2-word context window), making the comparison unfair. The paper claims their method reveals "different error cases," but doesn't demonstrate that these errors are linguistically meaningful or more accurate.

4. **Overstated practical implications**: The claim that "only a handful of non-ambiguous words need be manually PoS-tagged" is misleading. The authors actually used 1564 words to train their classifier (across 16 tags), which is not "a handful" but a significant manual effort. This undermines the paper's claim about low-resource applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the results change if you trained embeddings on plain lemmas (without PoS tags) and then predicted PoS tags? Would the distributional patterns still reveal the same boundary cases?

2. The paper claims that "grammatical categories exist along a continuum" but doesn't engage with relevant theoretical work on this topic (e.g., Hopper & Thompson, 1980 on grammaticalization). How do your findings specifically advance this theoretical discussion?

3. Your "unambiguous lemmas" approach required identifying 1564 words with consistent PoS tags. How does this scale to languages with more complex PoS systems or resources with less consistent tagging?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to identifying potential annotation inconsistencies in PoS-tagged corpora through a distributional approach. However, the methodological circularity and overstated theoretical claims significantly limit the paper's impact. The finding that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions is interesting but not novel in the context of distributional semantics research. The practical claims about low-resource PoS tagging are overstated due to the significant manual effort required to identify "unambiguous" words.

The paper would benefit from addressing the circularity issue, providing a clearer theoretical framework for understanding PoS boundaries, and conducting a more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers that use richer contextual information. With these improvements, the paper could make a stronger contribution to the field of computational linguistics.
