

# Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper investigates whether distributional semantic models (word embeddings) contain meaningful information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags, achieving high accuracy (F-score 0.979). They then analyze misclassified words to identify "outliers" that may indicate boundary cases between PoS classes or annotation inconsistencies. The authors also demonstrate that a classifier trained on prototypical examples can predict PoS without prior tags, and show that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1. Identification of boundary cases between PoS classes through analysis of classifier errors, revealing potential inconsistencies in standard PoS annotation
2. Demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings rather than concentrated in specific dimensions
3. Development of a method to automatically detect annotation errors in PoS-tagged corpora using distributional models

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a novel approach to identifying boundary cases between PoS classes through classifier error analysis. The authors systematically analyze misclassified words (e.g., proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, verbs as adjectives) to reveal where traditional PoS boundaries are fuzzy or inconsistent. This provides concrete linguistic insights that could inform future work on PoS tagsets and annotation guidelines.

2. The demonstration that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions of word embeddings (not concentrated in one or two dimensions) is significant and well-supported. The authors provide strong evidence through Figure 3, showing that accuracy increases smoothly with the number of dimensions used, eventually reaching near-perfect performance. This aligns with theoretical expectations about how distributional models encode information.

3. The paper demonstrates that a simple classifier trained on distributional vectors can achieve reasonable PoS tagging performance (84% accuracy) without access to contextual information, which is impressive given the simplicity of the approach. This suggests potential applications for PoS tagging in resource-poor languages where manual tagging is expensive.

4. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger reveals different error patterns, demonstrating that the distributional approach complements existing methods by highlighting different kinds of annotation inconsistencies. This provides evidence that the approach offers unique value beyond standard PoS tagging methods.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper does not sufficiently establish the novelty of its approach. While the paper acknowledges prior work on distributional models and PoS (e.g., Tsvetkov et al., 2015), it does not clearly distinguish its specific contribution from existing methods. The use of classifier errors to identify boundary cases is not sufficiently novelized in the context of prior research.

2. The paper lacks sufficient evidence that the identified inconsistencies reflect "real" linguistic phenomena rather than artifacts of the training data or model. For example, the misclassification of currency amounts as numerals may simply reflect the training data's distribution rather than a meaningful linguistic boundary. The paper would benefit from more thorough linguistic analysis of these cases.

3. The evaluation is limited to English, and the paper does not provide evidence that the approach generalizes to other languages. While the authors mention future work on other languages, this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged as a constraint of the current work.

4. The paper does not adequately address the performance gap between training (97.9% F-score) and test (91% F-score) data. This 7% drop in performance warrants more discussion about potential overfitting or data characteristics that could affect the generalizability of the approach.

5. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers. While it notes that the accuracy (84%) is lower than modern taggers, it does not provide specific quantitative comparisons with recent SOTA methods to contextualize this result.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the performance of the distributional model-based approach compare to a simple baseline that uses word form (e.g., suffixes) for PoS prediction? This would help establish whether the distributional approach provides a meaningful improvement over simpler methods.

2. The paper mentions that the classifier was trained on word vectors from a model trained on BNC with PoS tags. How would the results change if the model was trained on standard BNC without PoS tags?

3. The authors claim the approach can help "fix" annotation errors. What specific guidelines would they propose for modifying annotation schemes based on the identified inconsistencies?

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written and makes a methodological contribution to the field. The experimental design is sound, and the results are clearly presented. However, the claims about the linguistic significance of the identified errors could be strengthened with more thorough linguistic analysis. The authors should also more clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work on distributional models and PoS.
