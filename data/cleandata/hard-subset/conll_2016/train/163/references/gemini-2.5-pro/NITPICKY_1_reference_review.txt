**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors present two main experiments. First, they train a classifier on word embeddings derived from a corpus where lemmas are concatenated with their PoS tags (`lemma_TAG`). By analyzing the classifier's errors, they identify "outlier" words whose distributional behavior conflicts with their annotated PoS tag, thereby highlighting fuzzy linguistic boundaries and potential annotation inconsistencies in the British National Corpus (BNC). Second, they build a context-independent PoS tagger by training a classifier on standard word embeddings for a small set of "prototypical" words, demonstrating that a simple model can achieve reasonable accuracy using only lexical-semantic information.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel application of error analysis from an embedding-based classifier as a method for corpus-driven linguistic inquiry. The paper provides several concrete examples where this method uncovers interesting linguistic phenomena (e.g., years behaving as nouns) and potential systemic annotation errors.
2.  A demonstration and quantification of the PoS-related information inherent in word embeddings, independent of sentence context. The experiment in Section 5 provides a strong baseline for context-free PoS prediction and an interesting analysis of feature importance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clever Method for Linguistic Discovery:** The core idea of using a classifier's "confusion" about `lemma_TAG` embeddings to probe the fuzzy boundaries of PoS classes is compelling. It provides a principled, data-driven methodology to operationalize the notion of a "continuum" of grammatical categories and moves beyond simple clustering to a more targeted analysis of boundary cases.
2.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is the strongest part of the paper. The authors present a number of convincing examples where the classifier's errors point to genuine linguistic ambiguity or corpus artifacts. The discussion of nouns behaving like numerals (e.g., currency amounts) and numerals behaving like nouns (e.g., years) is particularly insightful and highlights legitimate inconsistencies in common annotation practices.
3.  **Effective Demonstration of Context-Free PoS Prediction:** The experiment in Section 5, while simple, effectively demonstrates the power of word embeddings alone for PoS disambiguation. Achieving 84% accuracy with a context-unaware classifier trained on only ~1500 prototypical examples is an impressive result that underscores how much grammatical information is implicitly captured during distributional model training.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its interesting premise, the paper suffers from a significant number of methodological and presentational flaws that undermine the credibility of its findings.

1.  **Questionable Methodological Choices and Lack of Clarity:**
    *   The instance weighting scheme described in Section 3 is highly unorthodox. The authors state that "each word’s vector was used several times, proportional to frequency of the word in the corpus." This amounts to manually duplicating data points, a computationally inefficient and crude method. Standard machine learning practice would involve using class or sample weights, which is a standard parameter in logistic regression classifiers. This choice raises questions about the authors' familiarity with standard classification techniques.
    *   The experimental setup for the comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger (Section 4) is deeply flawed. Feeding the tagger "separate tokens" cripples its performance by design, as its primary strength lies in using sequential context. Presenting this as a fair comparison to a context-independent model is misleading. The different error patterns observed could simply be an artifact of this hobbled setup, not a fundamental difference in what the models capture.
    *   The evaluation on the Universal Dependencies (UD) Treebank in Section 3 is insufficiently explained. The classifier was trained on `lemma_TAG` vectors from a BNC-trained model. It is entirely unclear how lemmas from the UD corpus were mapped to these input vectors to achieve the astonishingly high 0.99 F-score. This result is suspect without a detailed description of the evaluation protocol.

2.  **Inconsistent Analysis and Lack of Rigor:**
    *   The paper presents a direct contradiction regarding the distribution of PoS information in the embeddings. In Section 3, the authors emphasize the "very strongly expressed feature near the ‘100’ mark" (component 94) as an "idiosyncratic feature of conjunctions." However, in Section 5, they conclude that this "seems to be a solitary case" and that "knowledge about PoS affiliation is distributed among at least one hundred components." These two claims are at odds and this inconsistency is not addressed.
    *   The paper lacks any form of statistical significance testing. Are the results significantly better than the baselines? Are the error patterns identified in Table 1 and 2 statistically significant, or could they be due to chance? This lack of rigor weakens the presented conclusions.
    *   The figures are of poor quality. Figures 1 and 2 are missing a y-axis label and a clear description of the x-axis (presumably vector dimensions).

3.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The References section is completely garbled and unusable. It appears to be a corrupted BibTeX output, rendering it impossible for a reviewer to consult the cited literature. This level of carelessness is unacceptable in a formal submission. For instance, the Biber et al. (1999) entry is just a list of names, and the Aarts and McMahon (2008) entry is an unparsable string.
    *   Footnotes are used to convey critical methodological details that belong in the main text (e.g., Footnote 3, explaining the choice of classification over clustering).
    *   There are minor but frequent formatting errors (e.g., "concatination") and inconsistent citation formatting within the text itself.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Please provide a precise, step-by-step description of how your BNC-trained `lemma_TAG` classifier was evaluated on the UD Treebank lemmas in Section 3. How was an input vector constructed for a UD lemma, and how was a prediction evaluated against the gold tag to arrive at a 0.99 F-score?
2.  Could you provide a stronger justification for evaluating the Stanford PoS Tagger on isolated tokens? Given that this removes the contextual information central to modern taggers, how can this be considered a meaningful or fair comparison to your context-independent method?
3.  How do you reconcile the seemingly contradictory claims about PoS information being concentrated in a single "idiosyncratic" component (Section 3) versus being broadly distributed across "at least one hundred components" (Section 5)?
