### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which distributional word embeddings capture information about Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes. The authors propose using a classifier trained on word embeddings to identify "outlier" words whose distributional patterns are more similar to a different PoS class than their gold-standard annotation. The paper's primary goal is not to build a state-of-the-art PoS tagger, but rather to use this method as a tool for linguistic analysis to probe the fuzzy boundaries between PoS categories and identify potential inconsistencies in annotated corpora.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Method for Linguistic Analysis:** The primary contribution is the methodology itself: training a classifier on word embeddings specifically to analyze its misclassifications. This provides a systematic, data-driven way to identify words and word subclasses that lie at the boundaries of traditional PoS categories (e.g., participles that behave like adjectives, years that behave like nouns). The subsequent qualitative analysis of these outliers is the paper's core strength.
2.  **Identification of Corpus Artefacts:** A strong secondary contribution is demonstrating that this method can effectively highlight potential systematic errors or annotation inconsistencies in a well-established corpus like the BNC. The discovery that many words tagged as adverbs (e.g., 'plain', 'clear') cluster distributionally with adjectives is a compelling example.
3.  **Proof-of-Concept for a Simple PoS Tagger:** The paper also demonstrates that a simple, context-agnostic PoS tagger can be built using only word embeddings, achieving a respectable (though not state-of-the-art) accuracy. This serves as a strong proof-of-concept that PoS information is robustly encoded across many dimensions of the embeddings.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear and Compelling Research Question:** The paper is motivated by a well-established linguistic question: the continuous and often fuzzy nature of grammatical categories. The work provides a modern, computational approach to exploring this classic problem, making it a strong contribution to computational linguistics rather than just another engineering paper.
2.  **Insightful Qualitative Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is excellent. The authors go beyond simply reporting a confusion matrix and provide thoughtful linguistic interpretations for the major error categories. The discussion of nouns vs. numerals (e.g., '£70', '1804'), verbs vs. adjectives (passive participles), and nouns vs. adjectives ('hypertext') is insightful and convincingly demonstrates the value of their approach for uncovering interesting linguistic phenomena.
3.  **Novel Experimental Design for Outlier Detection:** The initial experiment's design—training embeddings on `lemma_PoS` strings—is a clever choice for the stated goal. It effectively creates distinct representations for homonyms with different parts of speech (e.g., `love_VERB` vs. `love_NOUN`), allowing the classifier to learn the "prototypical" distributional space for each class more cleanly. This makes the subsequent identification of outliers more meaningful.
4.  **Effective Comparison to a Standard Tagger:** The comparison of their method's confusion matrix with that of the Stanford PoS Tagger is a valuable addition. It successfully shows that their distributional approach uncovers different, and arguably more linguistically nuanced, types of ambiguities than a standard sequence-based tagger. This strengthens their claim that the method offers a novel analytical perspective.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potentially Overstated Framing:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" sets a very high bar that the paper does not quite meet. The work provides excellent evidence for the fuzziness of PoS boundaries and identifies interesting edge cases, but it stops short of proposing or formally motivating an actual *redefinition* or a new tagset. A title focused on "analyzing," "probing," or "exploring" PoS boundaries would more accurately reflect the paper's contribution.
2.  **Clarity on the Two-Experiment Structure:** The paper is structured around two main experiments (the outlier analysis in Sec 3-4 and the context-agnostic tagger in Sec 5), but the motivation for the shift in methodology is not fully explained. The first experiment uses non-standard `lemma_POS` embeddings, while the second uses standard lemma-only embeddings. It is unclear if the outlier analysis would yield similar results with standard embeddings. A more explicit discussion of why each methodology was chosen for its respective task would strengthen the paper's coherence.
3.  **Limited Technical Depth in Tagger Evaluation:** The PoS tagger experiment in Section 5 is interesting as a proof-of-concept but lacks depth. The analysis of feature importance (Figure 3) is somewhat cursory, concluding that "at least one hundred components" are important without deeper investigation. While SOTA performance is not the goal, the section feels less developed than the qualitative analysis in Section 4 and could be seen as a distraction from the paper's main contribution. The 0.84 accuracy, while impressive for the constraints, is difficult to contextualize without more sophisticated baselines (e.g., a unigram tagger trained on the same data).

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train embeddings on `lemma_POS` strings for the outlier analysis in Section 3? Have you experimented with using standard embeddings (trained on lemmas only) and simply providing the PoS tags as labels to the classifier? If so, how did the results of the outlier analysis differ? Does the `lemma_POS` approach perhaps amplify the separation between classes, making outliers more prominent?
2.  The paper's strongest contribution appears to be the qualitative analysis of boundary cases. Given this, could you comment further on the "Redefining" claim in the title? Do you view the identified outliers (e.g., years as nouns, certain adverbs as adjectives) as evidence that tagsets like the Universal Dependencies set should be formally modified, or do you see this more as a tool for annotators to handle ambiguity and inconsistency?
