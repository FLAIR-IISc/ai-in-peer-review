### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach for analyzing the boundaries of part-of-speech (PoS) classes using distributional semantic models. Instead of aiming for state-of-the-art PoS tagging performance, the authors use word embeddings and a simple classifier to identify "outliers"—words whose distributional profile in a large corpus (BNC) does not align with their traditionally assigned PoS tag. The analysis of these misclassifications reveals interesting linguistic phenomena, such as words on the continuum between nouns and adjectives, verbs that behave like adjectives (e.g., passive participles), and potential inconsistencies or errors in the source corpus annotation.

In my view, the primary contributions are:

1.  **A novel methodology for computational linguistic analysis:** The core idea of using a classifier's "errors" not as a failure but as a tool for linguistic discovery is highly original and thought-provoking. It reframes a standard machine learning setup as an exploratory instrument for probing grammatical categories.
2.  **Qualitative linguistic insights into PoS classes in English:** The paper provides compelling, data-driven evidence for the fuzziness of PoS boundaries. The analysis of outliers systematically identifies well-known linguistic ambiguities (e.g., participles) as well as uncovers potential inconsistencies in a widely-used corpus (e.g., the tagging of currency amounts, years, and specific adjectives).
3.  **A demonstration of the grammatical information encoded in word embeddings:** The secondary experiment, which builds a simple zero-context PoS tagger achieving 84% accuracy, serves as a powerful proof-of-concept that embeddings trained on raw text implicitly capture a significant amount of grammatical and syntactic information, distributed across many dimensions.

### Strengths

1.  **Boldness and Novelty of the Core Idea:** This is the paper's greatest strength. It moves beyond the typical engineering goal of incrementally improving a metric and instead asks a more fundamental linguistic question: can unsupervised models help us redefine or at least re-examine foundational categories like parts of speech? The approach of leveraging classifier disagreements is an elegant and creative way to operationalize this question. This kind of work, which bridges data-driven methods with linguistic theory, is precisely what the field needs more of.
2.  **Rich and Interesting Error Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is compelling and insightful. The authors don't just report error statistics; they dive into the specific cases (nouns vs. adjectives, proper vs. common nouns, verbs vs. adjectives for participles, numerals vs. nouns for years/currency) and connect them to known linguistic debates and annotation challenges. The discovery of a potential systematic error in the BNC regarding adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' being tagged as adverbs is a fantastic demonstration of the method's utility.
3.  **Effective Comparison to a Standard Baseline:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger's error patterns is a crucial part of the paper. It clearly demonstrates that the distributional method uncovers a *different set* of boundary cases. While a standard tagger's errors might be more related to local context ambiguity, the proposed method identifies words whose fundamental distributional "signature" is at odds with its class, providing a distinct and valuable analytical lens.
4.  **Simplicity and Clarity:** The authors use simple, well-understood tools (Skip-gram, logistic regression) to achieve their goal. This is a virtue, as it makes the results easy to interpret and highlights that the contribution lies in the conceptual framework, not in complex model architecture. The paper is well-written and the experiments are clearly explained.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas where the methodology and validation could be strengthened. As per my reviewing philosophy, these are not reasons for rejection but rather suggestions for future work, as the core contribution is strong enough to stand on its own.

1.  **Generalizability of Findings:** The analysis relies entirely on one corpus (BNC) and one embedding model (Skip-gram with a specific parameter set). It is unclear how sensitive the identified "outliers" are to these choices. Would using a different corpus, or a different embedding architecture like GloVe or contextualized embeddings, yield the same or different linguistic insights? While this doesn't invalidate the current findings, it limits their claimed universality.
2.  **Unusual Setup of the Main Classifier:** The initial experiment (Section 3) trains embeddings on `lemma_PoS` strings. This is a clever trick to get distinct vectors for homonyms, but it makes the subsequent "classification" task somewhat artificial. The classifier is not predicting a tag for a word, but rather assessing whether the distributional context of `word_TAG` is consistent with other words having that same tag. This could be explained more explicitly, as it's a "distributional consistency check" more than a standard prediction task.
3.  **Somewhat Disconnected Second Experiment:** The shift in Section 5 to a true PoS prediction task (training on lemmas, predicting tags) feels slightly disconnected from the main analysis. While it effectively demonstrates that embeddings contain PoS information, the link between the specific outliers found in Section 4 and the performance of the tagger in Section 5 is not strongly established. The two parts feel like two separate, albeit related, short papers.
4.  **Analysis of Vector Components is Preliminary:** The paper concludes that PoS information is distributed across ~100 dimensions, but the analysis supporting this (Figure 3) is based on training set accuracy, which is prone to overfitting. A more rigorous analysis using development set performance would be more convincing. The observation about component 94 for conjunctions is interesting but presented as a solitary case, leaving the reader wanting a more systematic investigation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the main experiment in Section 3: What was the motivation for training the embeddings on `lemma_TAG` strings, rather than training on lemmas and using the gold tags as labels for the classifier? Does this approach better capture the distributional profile of a word *in its function as a specific PoS*, and could you elaborate on the trade-offs?
2.  Have you considered a pilot study to test the robustness of your outlier analysis? For instance, would training on a different corpus (e.g., Wikipedia) or using a different model (e.g., GloVe) identify a similar set of "adjective-like nouns" or "adverb-like subordinate conjunctions"? This would significantly strengthen the claim that you are discovering fundamental properties of the words themselves, rather than artifacts of the BNC/Skip-gram combination.
