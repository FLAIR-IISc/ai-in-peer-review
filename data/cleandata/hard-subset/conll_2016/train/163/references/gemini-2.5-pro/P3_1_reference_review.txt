### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in static word embeddings and proposes a methodology for leveraging this information to analyze the boundaries of traditional PoS classes. The work is presented in two main parts. First, the authors train a word embedding model on the BNC corpus where each word type is a concatenation of its lemma and its gold PoS tag (e.g., `love_VERB`). They then train a classifier to predict the PoS tag from these specialized embeddings. The core of this work is a detailed analysis of the classifier's errors, which the authors argue represent "outliers"—words whose distributional properties do not align with their assigned PoS class, thereby highlighting fuzzy linguistic boundaries or corpus annotation errors. Second, the authors train a standard embedding model on lemmatized text and build a simple, context-agnostic PoS predictor. This predictor achieves a respectable accuracy of 84% on the UD Treebank, demonstrating that embeddings alone carry significant PoS information, which is shown to be distributed across many vector dimensions.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel methodology for corpus-driven linguistic analysis:** The strongest contribution is the use of a classifier's prediction errors on word embeddings as a systematic tool for identifying linguistically interesting phenomena. This "outlier detection" approach provides a data-driven method for questioning established PoS categories and discovering potential inconsistencies or errors in gold-standard annotated corpora like the BNC.
2.  **An insightful analysis of PoS class boundaries in English:** The qualitative analysis in Section 4 is detailed and convincing. It successfully uses the proposed method to highlight well-known areas of linguistic ambiguity (e.g., verb participles behaving like adjectives, nouns used attributively) and uncover specific, potentially problematic annotation patterns in the BNC (e.g., the classification of years or currency amounts).
3.  **Empirical validation of PoS encoding in embeddings:** While not entirely novel, the paper provides a clear and well-executed demonstration of how robustly PoS information is encoded in static embeddings. The analysis showing that this information is distributed across approximately 100 dimensions provides valuable empirical evidence that complements existing knowledge.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Innovative use of a standard tool for linguistic discovery:** The central idea of training a classifier not for the sake of prediction, but to use its failures as a diagnostic lens, is both clever and effective. It transforms a standard machine learning pipeline into an exploratory tool for computational linguistics. The comparison with the error patterns of the Stanford PoS Tagger successfully demonstrates that this distributional approach yields a different and arguably more linguistically-grounded set of insights.
2.  **High-quality qualitative analysis:** The paper excels in its discussion of the misclassification errors (Section 4). The authors provide concrete examples for each major error category (PROPN→NOUN, NOUN→ADJ, VERB→ADJ, etc.) and connect them to established linguistic concepts and practical annotation challenges. This analysis is thoughtful, well-argued, and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space, making the findings credible and interesting.
3.  **Clear and reproducible experimental design:** The methodology is described with sufficient detail to be understood and likely reproduced. The authors clearly state the corpus, preprocessing steps, model choices (Skip-gram), and classification setup. The experiments are logical, well-motivated, and directly support the paper's main claims. The decision to map BNC tags to the Universal Dependencies tagset is sound and improves the generality of the findings.
4.  **Significant implications for annotation and tagset design:** The work highlights the practical value of distributional analysis in NLP resource creation. The ability to automatically flag words that are "distributionally strange" for their assigned tag could be a valuable tool for quality control in corpus annotation, helping to refine annotation guidelines and improve the consistency of treebanks.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Potentially confounding experimental setup in the main analysis:** The primary analysis relies on embeddings trained on `lemma_POS` strings. This is an unusual setup where the model is explicitly told to create separate representations for, e.g., `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`. This may be circular: the model learns that these are different things because the input strings are different. It is not clear how this affects the resulting vector space compared to a standard model trained on lemmas, where one might then derive context-dependent representations for classification. A stronger justification for this methodological choice is needed, along with a discussion of potential alternatives and their trade-offs.
2.  **Overstating the novelty of syntax in embeddings:** The introduction and related work section frame the question of whether embeddings contain PoS information as somewhat open ("Do learned semantic vectors help here?"). However, as the authors' own citations show, it has been well-established for many years that distributional models capture syntactic and morphological regularities. The paper's contribution is not in discovering this fact, but in its novel application. A more precise framing would strengthen the paper's positioning by focusing on the unique contribution—the outlier analysis methodology—rather than rediscovering a known property of embeddings.
3.  **Limited empirical scope:** The experiments are confined to English, the BNC corpus, and the Skip-gram model. While the findings are compelling for English, the paper's claims about redefining PoS classes would be much stronger if the methodology were validated on at least one other language, particularly one with a different morphological structure. Similarly, demonstrating that the findings are robust across different static embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText) would increase their generalizability.
4.  **The context-agnostic tagger is underdeveloped:** The second experiment (Section 5), while an interesting proof of concept, feels less rigorous than the first. The 84% accuracy is difficult to interpret without comparison to a simple but crucial baseline, such as a unigram tagger that assigns the most frequent tag to a lemma from the training data. The claim of its usefulness for low-resource languages is highly speculative and would require a more dedicated experiment to support. As it stands, this section adds limited value compared to the excellent analysis in Section 4.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train embeddings on `lemma_POS` strings for the outlier analysis? Did you consider an alternative approach, such as training on lemmas alone and then classifying `(lemma_vector, gold_POS)` pairs? How might that change the nature of the "outliers" you discover?
2.  Your analysis of feature importance suggests PoS information is broadly distributed, yet Figures 1 and 2 show a very sharp, idiosyncratic peak for conjunctions on dimension 94, which you call a "solitary case". Does this suggest that closed-class functional words are encoded differently (perhaps more discretely) than open-class words in the vector space? Did you observe similar prominent spikes for other functional categories like prepositions or determiners?
3.  For the context-agnostic tagger presented in Section 5, could you provide its performance relative to a standard majority-class baseline (a unigram tagger that assigns each lemma its most frequent tag from the training set)? This would help to better situate the 84% accuracy figure and quantify the specific contribution of the distributional information captured by the embeddings.
