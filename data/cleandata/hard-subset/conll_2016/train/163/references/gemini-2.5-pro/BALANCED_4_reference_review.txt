**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its pre-trained Skip-gram embedding. The core of the paper is a detailed analysis of the classifier's errors, which the authors argue are not random but rather highlight linguistically meaningful phenomena. These "outliers" are presented as words that straddle PoS class boundaries (e.g., passive participles that behave like adjectives), point to annotation inconsistencies in the source corpus (the British National Corpus), or reveal systematic tagging errors. A second experiment demonstrates that a reasonably effective PoS tagger (84% accuracy) can be built using only word embeddings, without access to local sentence context, by training on a small set of unambiguous words.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A novel diagnostic methodology for corpus linguistics and annotation quality control. The paper effectively frames an embedding-based classifier not as an end in itself, but as a tool to systematically identify words with atypical distributional properties. The subsequent qualitative analysis of these misclassifications to uncover corpus inconsistencies and explore fuzzy linguistic categories is the primary strength and contribution of the work.
2.  A clear empirical analysis of the relationship between PoS classes and the geometry of a distributional vector space. The paper demonstrates that PoS classes form coherent clusters and provides evidence that this information is distributed across many vector dimensions, offering a useful characterization for researchers working with embeddings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clever and Insightful Methodology:** The central idea of leveraging classification *errors* as a signal for linguistic analysis is elegant and effective. Rather than simply aiming for state-of-the-art prediction, the authors use their model as an analytical probe. The deep dive into the error patterns in Section 4 is the most compelling part of the paper, providing concrete and convincing examples (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns, adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs, participles behaving like adjectives) that support the paper's thesis. This approach has clear value for validating and refining annotated corpora.
2.  **Sound and Well-Explained Experiments:** The two main experiments are logical and well-designed. The first experiment, which trains embeddings on `lemma_PoS` tokens, is a smart way to create distinct representations for different usages of the same lemma, enabling a fine-grained analysis. The second experiment provides a clean, context-free baseline that effectively isolates the contribution of the word embedding itself to PoS prediction. The paper is generally well-written, and the experimental setup is described with sufficient detail for replication.
3.  **Valuable Qualitative Analysis:** The paper excels in its qualitative discussion. The authors connect the quantitative results from their confusion matrices back to established linguistic concepts and practical annotation challenges. The comparison with the error patterns of the Stanford Tagger, while flawed in its setup (see Weaknesses), successfully makes the point that the distributional method surfaces a different and complementary set of issues, strengthening the case for its utility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Misleading Comparison with Standard PoS Tagger:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger in Section 4 is weakened by a crucial methodological choice. The authors state they fed the tagger "separate tokens, to mimic our workflow." This cripples a sequence model like the Stanford Tagger, which relies heavily on sentence context. The resulting error analysis is therefore not of the tagger under normal operating conditions but in a highly artificial, zero-context scenario. While the goal was to create a comparable setup, it makes the comparison less meaningful and potentially unfair. The conclusion that the two methods reveal "different error cases" may still hold, but the evidence presented is less robust than it could be.
2.  **Overstated Claims and Framing:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is an overstatement of the paper's contribution. The work does not propose a new PoS tagset or a formal redefinition of classes. Rather, it provides a powerful data-driven method for *analyzing the boundaries* of existing classes and identifying annotation inconsistencies. A more measured title and abstract that focused on this analytical contribution would more accurately reflect the work and strengthen its impact. Similarly, the novelty of the core finding—that embeddings capture syntactic information—is somewhat overstated in the introduction, as this is a relatively well-established fact. The novelty lies in the *application* of this fact.
3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The primary analysis is based on a single model (Skip-gram) trained on a single, relatively old corpus of English (BNC). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it raises questions about the generalizability of the specific findings. Would the same annotation inconsistencies be found in more modern corpora? How would the methodology perform on morphologically rich languages where lemmatization is more challenging and PoS ambiguity is higher? Demonstrating the approach on another language or a different corpus would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger: Given that feeding isolated tokens to a sequence tagger is an unusual use case, could you elaborate on this choice? How might the Stanford Tagger's confusion matrix differ if it were run on full sentences from the BNC, and how would this impact your comparative analysis?
2.  In Experiment 1, you trained embeddings on concatenated `lemma_PoS` strings. Is it possible that the shared string suffixes (e.g., `_NOUN`, `_VERB`) act as a signal to the model, influencing the embeddings in a way that is separate from the purely distributional context? Have you considered alternative approaches, such as multi-task learning where the PoS tag is a secondary prediction target during embedding training?
3.  Your analysis in Figure 3 suggests PoS information is spread across at least 100 dimensions. This is a very interesting finding. Have you performed any qualitative analysis on the top-ranked dimensions identified by the ANOVA F-value? Do these dimensions appear to correspond to any interpretable linguistic or semantic properties (e.g., concreteness, verb-ness, etc.)?
