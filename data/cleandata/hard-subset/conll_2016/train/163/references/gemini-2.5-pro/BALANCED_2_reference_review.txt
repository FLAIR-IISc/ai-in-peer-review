### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in static word embeddings. The authors conduct two main experiments. First, they train a classifier on word embeddings derived from `lemma_PoS` tokens to predict the PoS tag. They then perform an in-depth qualitative analysis of the classifier's errors, arguing that these "outliers" reveal interesting linguistic phenomena, such as words on the boundary between PoS classes, and expose inconsistencies or errors in the source corpus (BNC). Second, they build a context-free PoS tagger trained on embeddings of unambiguous words, demonstrating that a simple model without access to sentence context can still achieve reasonable performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel method for linguistic and corpus analysis:** The primary contribution is the use of a classifier's prediction errors on word embeddings as a tool for identifying and analyzing words with ambiguous or non-prototypical PoS behavior. The detailed qualitative analysis in Section 4 effectively demonstrates how this method can uncover nuanced linguistic patterns and potential annotation artifacts.
2.  **An insightful comparative error analysis:** The paper effectively contrasts the types of ambiguities highlighted by the embedding-based method with those from a traditional sequence-based tagger (the Stanford PoS Tagger). This comparison strengthens the claim that the proposed method offers a genuinely different and valuable perspective on PoS classification challenges.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Qualitative Analysis:** The core strength of the paper lies in its detailed and insightful analysis of classification errors (Section 4). The authors move beyond simple error statistics to provide compelling linguistic interpretations for why certain words are misclassified. Examples such as passive participles behaving like adjectives (`overgrown`), currency amounts behaving like numerals (`£70`), and years behaving like nouns (`1804`) are well-chosen and convincingly argued. This demonstrates the utility of the method for linguistic discovery and for identifying areas where PoS tagsets and annotation guidelines might be refined.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Creative Experimental Design:** The methodology is clear and cleverly designed to test the paper's hypotheses. The first experiment, training embeddings on `lemma_PoS` concatenated tokens, is an effective way to generate representations that are sensitive to PoS distinctions for homonymous lemmas. The second experiment, building a context-free tagger from a small set of "prototypical" examples, provides a clean and direct measure of the PoS information contained within the static embeddings themselves.
3.  **Effective Empirical Grounding:** The claims are well-supported by empirical results. The high performance of the initial classifier (F1=0.91 on held-out BNC data) validates the underlying premise that embeddings are rich in PoS information. Furthermore, the comparison against the Stanford PoS Tagger's error patterns (Table 3 vs. Table 1) provides strong evidence that the distributional method reveals a different set of boundary cases, focusing more on semantic/distributional similarities rather than local syntactic cues.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Positioning and Novelty of the Core Premise:** The paper frames the discovery that embeddings contain PoS information as a central finding. However, this is a well-established result in the literature, as acknowledged in the related work section. The true novelty lies in the *application* of this principle for systematic error analysis. The paper would be stronger if it were framed more explicitly as a novel methodology for corpus analysis and linguistic investigation, rather than a rediscovery of a known property of embeddings.
2.  **Clarity of the First Experimental Setup:** The description of the first experiment (Section 3) could be clearer. The classifier is trained on embeddings of `lemma_PoS` strings, and the task is to predict the PoS. This setup is somewhat circular, as the "gold" label seems to be part of the input representation's source string. While the goal is to see if, e.g., the embedding of `plain_ADV` clusters more closely with adjective vectors, this needs to be explained more explicitly. Clarifying the precise input/output mapping (e.g., input: `vector('word_TAG')`, output: `TAG`) and the rationale would help readers understand why this is a valid approach for identifying outliers.
3.  **Limited Scope of Embeddings and Analysis:** The study relies exclusively on traditional skip-gram embeddings. While appropriate for demonstrating the concept, the field has largely moved towards contextual embeddings (e.g., ELMo, BERT). Many of the ambiguities identified (e.g., nouns used as adjectival modifiers) are precisely the kind that contextual models are designed to resolve. A discussion of how the findings relate to the capabilities of contextual models would significantly strengthen the paper by situating its conclusions within the current state of the art. Is the goal to analyze the static, context-independent properties of words, or to improve tagging? Acknowledging this limitation and clarifying the scope would be beneficial.
4.  **Potential Confounding Factors in Evaluation:** The evaluation of the context-free tagger in Section 5 could be more rigorous. The classifier is trained on "unambiguous" lemmas from the UD training set and tested on the UD test set. It is unclear if there is any lemma overlap between the classifier's training data and the test set. If so, high accuracy could partially result from memorizing tags for seen lemmas. Reporting performance separately for lemmas seen and unseen during classifier training would provide a more robust assessment of its generalization capability.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the main experiment in Section 3, could you please clarify the training process for the logistic regression classifier? Specifically, is the input the vector for a `lemma_PoS` string (e.g., `vector('plain_ADV')`) and the target label is the PoS part of that string (e.g., `ADV`)? If so, could you elaborate on why this approach is not circular and how it effectively identifies words whose distributional context (as captured by the embedding) mismatches their assigned tag?
2.  For the context-free tagger in Section 5, was the set of lemmas in the UD test set disjoint from the 1,564 "unambiguous" lemmas used to train the classifier? Could you provide a breakdown of the 0.84 accuracy score on lemmas that were seen versus unseen during the classifier's training? This would help to better understand the model's ability to generalize.
3.  The analysis relies on a single skip-gram model configuration (e.g., window size of 2). How sensitive do you believe the identified outlier classes are to hyperparameters like the context window size? For example, would a wider window, which captures more topical information, tend to blur or sharpen the boundaries between classes like NOUN and ADJ?
