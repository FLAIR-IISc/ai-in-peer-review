### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors frame this not as a PoS tagging task, but as a linguistic probe to "redefine" or analyze the fuzzy boundaries between traditional PoS classes.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Method for Corpus and Annotation Analysis:** The core contribution is using a simple classifier trained on word embeddings to identify "outliers"—words whose distributional profile does not match their gold-standard PoS tag. The subsequent error analysis provides compelling evidence of both well-known linguistic ambiguities (e.g., Noun/Adjective, Verb/Adjective participles) and, more significantly, potential inconsistencies or systematic errors in the BNC annotation (e.g., currency units tagged as Nouns, certain adjectives tagged as Adverbs).
2.  **A Novel Comparative Analysis:** The authors strengthen their contribution by comparing the confusion matrix from their distributional classifier with one generated by the Stanford PoS Tagger. This effectively demonstrates that their method uncovers different kinds of "errors" or ambiguities—ones based on broad distributional similarity rather than local contextual or morphological cues—thus highlighting the unique value of the proposed analytical approach.
3.  **Demonstration of PoS Information Density in Embeddings:** The paper shows that a context-agnostic PoS predictor trained on a very small set of prototypical examples can achieve respectable accuracy (84%). Furthermore, it demonstrates that this PoS-related information is not concentrated in a few dimensions but is distributed across a large number of embedding components.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear and Valuable Research Question:** The paper addresses a well-motivated problem. Instead of simply aiming for state-of-the-art PoS tagging performance, it uses embeddings as an analytical tool to explore the linguistic reality of PoS classes. This focus on gaining linguistic insight is a significant strength and is highly appropriate for the CoNLL community.
2.  **Excellent Error Analysis:** Section 4 is the highlight of the paper. The analysis of misclassified words is detailed, insightful, and convincing. The examples provided (e.g., "£70" being predicted as a numeral, years like "1804" as nouns, and participles like "intertwined" as adjectives) are compelling. The discovery of potentially systematic errors in the BNC (e.g., "plain", "clear" tagged as adverbs) is a powerful demonstration of the method's utility for corpus quality control.
3.  **Effective Methodological Choices:** The initial experimental setup in Section 3, while unusual, is clever. By training embeddings on `lemma_TAG` strings, the model learns distinct representations for different functions of the same lemma (e.g., `love_NOUN` vs. `love_VERB`). This allows the classifier to directly assess whether the distributional context of a given `lemma_TAG` pair aligns with other words of the same tag, which is perfect for the stated goal of finding outliers. The use of frequency weighting to balance classes is also a sound choice.
4.  **Strong Empirical Grounding:** The conclusions are well-supported by the experiments. The high F1-score of the initial classifier validates the premise that embeddings capture PoS information. The robustness is demonstrated by testing on multiple datasets (a held-out BNC portion and the UD Treebank). The comparison with the Stanford Tagger in Section 4 provides a crucial baseline that underscores the novelty of the insights derived from the distributional approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on the Main Experimental Setup:** The methodology of training embeddings on `lemma_TAG` concatenations is central to the analysis in Sections 3 and 4, but it is mentioned somewhat matter-of-factly. This is a non-standard approach that effectively creates representations for word *functions* rather than words themselves. The paper would be stronger if it explicitly justified this choice and discussed its implications. For instance, how does this compare to a more standard approach of training on lemmas and then using the gold tags as labels for a classifier? This would help readers better appreciate the novelty and purpose of the design.
2.  **Potentially Disconnected Second Experiment:** Section 5, while interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the paper's main narrative. It switches from the `lemma_TAG` model to a standard lemma-based model and from the BNC to the UD treebank for training. While it successfully demonstrates that a "proper" predictor is possible, its performance is not state-of-the-art, and it distracts from the primary contribution, which is the linguistic analysis in Section 4. The finding that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions could likely have been demonstrated using the main model from Section 3, which would have made the paper more cohesive.
3.  **Evaluation of the Stanford Tagger Baseline:** The comparison to the Stanford Tagger is a key strength, but the evaluation methodology might be suboptimal. The authors state they fed the tagger "separate tokens, to mimic our workflow." This handicaps the tagger by removing its main source of information: sentence context. A standard PoS tagger's errors are highly dependent on context. A more compelling comparison would involve running the tagger on full sentences from the BNC and then analyzing the confusion matrix at the type level. While the current comparison is still useful, this methodological choice weakens the conclusions that can be drawn from it.
4.  **Minor Details and Reproducibility:** The paper mentions that the BNC-to-Universal PoS mappings were created manually and "released online," but the link is anonymized. For a final version, access to this mapping would be critical for reproducibility. Additionally, the analysis of feature importance in Figure 3 is shown on the training set, which is susceptible to overfitting. Showing the same curve on a held-out validation set would be much more informative about the true generalization performance as a function of the number of features.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you further elaborate on the decision to train embeddings on `lemma_TAG` strings? Did you experiment with the more conventional approach of training on lemmas and then classifying them (using BNC tags as labels)? If so, how did the resulting error analysis compare to the one presented in Section 4? My intuition is that your chosen method is more direct for your goal, but a brief discussion of the alternative would be valuable.
2.  In your comparison with the Stanford Tagger, you chose to provide tokens out of context. Could you comment on how you expect the error patterns might change if the tagger were run on full sentences, and why you chose the token-level evaluation for this comparison?
3.  The analysis of feature importance in Figure 3 is based on training set accuracy. Could you provide the same analysis on a held-out test set? This would give a clearer picture of how many features are needed for generalization, as opposed to simply fitting the training data.
