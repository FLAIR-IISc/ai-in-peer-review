### Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates how well part-of-speech (PoS) categories are reflected in distributional word embeddings. The authors propose a methodology centered on training a classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its pre-trained embedding. The primary goal is not to build a state-of-the-art tagger, but to analyze the classifier's errors to identify "outlier" words whose distributional patterns deviate from the prototype of their assigned PoS class. A secondary experiment explores the viability of a simple, context-agnostic PoS tagger built on the same principle for low-resource scenarios.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A methodology for identifying linguistic outliers and annotation inconsistencies.** The core contribution is the use of a simple classifier on top of word embeddings as a diagnostic tool. By analyzing misclassifications, the authors identify linguistically interesting phenomena, such as words on the boundary between PoS classes (e.g., participles as VERB/ADJ, noun modifiers as NOUN/ADJ), and potential systematic annotation errors or inconsistencies in a large corpus like the BNC (e.g., currency amounts tagged as NOUN instead of NUM, or certain adjectives tagged as ADV).
2.  **An empirical analysis demonstrating that different error patterns are revealed compared to standard sequence-based taggers.** The authors compare the confusion matrix from their method with one generated by the Stanford PoS Tagger, showing that their distributional approach highlights different types of ambiguities (e.g., VERB/ADJ) than the sequence model, which makes more errors on other fronts (e.g., VB/NN). This validates the approach as a complementary tool for corpus analysis.
3.  **A demonstration of the potential for low-resource PoS tagging.** The paper shows that a respectable accuracy (~84%) can be achieved by a context-agnostic tagger trained on embeddings with only ~1500 labeled examples, highlighting the rich grammatical information captured in unsupervised representations.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-motivated and insightful analysis:** The paper tackles a classic linguistic question—the fuzzy nature of grammatical categories—with modern computational tools. The idea of using classifier errors not as a failure but as a source of linguistic insight is clever and well-executed. The analysis in Section 4 is the highlight of the paper, providing concrete examples that are linguistically plausible and genuinely interesting, such as the systematic mis-tagging of words like 'plain' and 'clear' in the BNC.
2.  **Sound experimental design:** The two-stage experimental setup is logical. The first experiment (Sec 3-4) cleverly uses embeddings trained on `lemma_POS` pairs to create fine-grained representations for analyzing an existing annotated corpus. The second experiment (Sec 5) switches to lemma-only embeddings to test a more realistic prediction scenario. The use of standard corpora (BNC, UD) and models (Skip-gram, Logistic Regression) makes the work easy to understand and situate.
3.  **Valuable baseline comparison:** The comparison against the Stanford PoS Tagger (Sec 4) is crucial. It effectively demonstrates that the authors' method is not just a weaker version of existing taggers but provides a genuinely different analytical perspective. This strengthens the claim that distributional analysis can uncover issues that sequence-based models, which might rely more on local syntax, might miss or smooth over.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Methodological justification could be clearer:** The choice in Section 3 to train embeddings on concatenated `lemma_POS` strings is central to the outlier analysis, but its implications are not fully discussed. This method learns distinct vectors for `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`, effectively baking the gold tag into the representation. The classifier then learns to map this `love_VERB` vector back to the VERB label. While this serves the goal of finding outliers within the `VERB` class, the setup feels somewhat circular. A clearer justification of why this is preferable to training on raw lemmas and then labeling the resulting single 'love' vector with its various possible tags would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Potentially unfair comparison to the sequence-based tagger:** The Stanford Tagger was run on "separate tokens" to mimic the context-free nature of the authors' predictor. This significantly handicaps the Stanford Tagger, as its main strength (and that of most modern taggers) comes from exploiting sequential context. While the goal was to compare error types, the framing could be misinterpreted as a performance comparison. This limitation should be more explicitly acknowledged, and the conclusion could be nuanced to state that the two methods reveal different types of errors *due to their reliance on different information sources* (global distributional patterns vs. local syntactic context).
3.  **Limited exploration of embedding models:** The study relies exclusively on one Skip-gram model with a single set of hyperparameters. Findings, especially the specific vector components identified as important for certain PoS classes (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions), may not be robust across different models (e.g., GloVe, FastText), corpora, or even different random initializations of the same model. While a full-scale comparison is beyond the scope of one paper, acknowledging this limitation and framing the findings as specific to their setup would be appropriate.

### Questions to Authors
1.  In your first experiment (Section 3), the classifier achieved a remarkable 0.99 F-score on the UD Treebank vocabulary, even higher than on the BNC test set from the same distribution. Do you have a hypothesis for this? Is the UD annotation scheme more distributionally consistent than the BNC's, or could there be significant vocabulary overlap between your BNC-trained model and the UD data that contributes to this high performance?
2.  Could you further elaborate on the choice to train embeddings on `lemma_POS` strings? An alternative could be to train on raw lemmas and then, for a polysemous word like "love", use a weighted average of its instances' contexts to create separate `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN` vectors for the classifier. How do you think such an approach would compare to yours?
3.  The feature analysis in Figure 3 suggests that PoS information is distributed across at least 100 dimensions. This is an interesting finding. Have you examined the top-ranked features (e.g., the top 10) to see if they correspond to any interpretable linguistic or contextual properties beyond the single example of component 94 for conjunctions?
