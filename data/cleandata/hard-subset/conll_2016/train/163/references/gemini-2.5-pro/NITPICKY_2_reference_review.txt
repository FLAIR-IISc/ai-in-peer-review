**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors conduct two main experiments. First, they train a word embedding model on the British National Corpus (BNC) where each word type is a concatenation of its lemma and PoS tag (e.g., "love_NOUN", "love_VERB"). They then train a logistic regression classifier on these embeddings to predict the PoS tag. The primary goal is to analyze the classifier's errors ("outliers") to identify words that lie on the boundaries of traditional PoS classes or highlight inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation. Second, they build a context-independent PoS tagger by training a similar classifier on embeddings derived only from lemmas, using a small set of "unambiguous" words from the Universal Dependencies (UD) Treebank for training. They conclude that embeddings robustly capture PoS information distributed across many dimensions, and that their error analysis method is a useful tool for linguistic investigation.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the qualitative analysis of classification errors (Section 4). By identifying words whose distributional vectors are more similar to a different PoS class than their annotated one, the authors provide a data-driven method for exploring the fuzzy boundaries between grammatical categories (e.g., participles as verb/adjective hybrids, years as noun-like numerals) and for flagging potential systematic annotation errors in a gold-standard corpus.
2.  A secondary contribution is the demonstration that a simple, context-agnostic PoS classifier trained on a very small seed set of unambiguous words (Section 5) can achieve respectable performance, suggesting potential applications in low-resource settings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Interesting Core Idea:** The central premise of using a classifier's prediction errors on word embeddings as a lens for linguistic analysis is clever. It provides a systematic, corpus-based approach to identifying "outliers" that challenge traditional PoS definitions, moving beyond anecdotal evidence.
2.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is the strongest part of the paper. The authors identify several well-motivated and linguistically interesting phenomena, such as the adjectival nature of certain nouns used as modifiers ("hypertext_NOUN"), the verb-adjective ambiguity of passive participles, and the noun-like behavior of numerals representing years. This demonstrates the utility of the proposed method.
3.  **Practical Application for Corpus Curation:** The method successfully identified potential systemic errors in the BNC annotation (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' tagged as adverbs). This highlights a valuable practical application of this work as a semi-automated tool for quality control and consistency checking in annotated corpora.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Serious Methodological Flaw in the Main Experiment:** The primary experiment (Section 3) trains embeddings on `lemma_PoS` strings. A classifier is then trained to predict the PoS from the resulting vector. This experimental design is borderline circular. The input to the embedding model already contains the gold PoS label, explicitly forcing the model to create different vectors for, e.g., `love_NOUN` and `love_VERB`. The subsequent high classification accuracy (0.979 F-score) is therefore unsurprising and less meaningful. The model is not discovering PoS from distributional patterns alone, but rather learning to distinguish vectors that were *defined* to be different based on their PoS. This fundamental issue significantly undermines the claims about embeddings capturing PoS information in a purely unsupervised manner.
2.  **Deeply Flawed Comparison with a Baseline Tagger:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is invalid. The authors hobbled the tagger by feeding it isolated tokens instead of full sentences, removing its access to sequential context, which is the primary information source for such models. To then compare its confusion matrix to their method and claim their method reveals "different error cases" is misleading. The comparison does not show the superiority of their method, but rather the unsurprising fact that a sequence model performs poorly without sequence information.
3.  **Lack of Rigor and Clarity in Experimental Details:** Several crucial details are either missing, vague, or poorly justified.
    *   Why was "no down-sampling" used for training the Skip-gram model? This is a non-standard choice that over-weights function words and should be justified.
    *   The evaluation on the UD Treebank in Section 3, yielding a 0.99 F-score, is poorly explained. How were vectors for UD lemmas obtained from a model trained on BNC `lemma_PoS` strings? This result seems implausibly high without a clear explanation of the mapping process.
    *   The criterion for selecting "unambiguous" lemmas in Section 5 (alternative tags having a frequency of no more than 1/2 of the dominant tag) feels arbitrary and is not justified.
    *   The discussion around Figure 3 is superficial. Stating that the plateau "may lie somewhere near k = 100" is imprecise.
4.  **Extremely Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is riddled with formatting and presentation errors that suggest a lack of care.
    *   The entire reference section is malformed, appearing as a mix of BibTeX keys and unrendered text (e.g., `Aarts et al\.,? \Q2008\E`). This is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   Citation formatting is inconsistent throughout the text (e.g., "(Biber et al., 1999)" vs. "Petrov et al. 2012").
    *   Figures 1 and 2 are low-resolution and lack axis labels, making them difficult to interpret. The y-axis is completely undefined.
    *   There are several typographical errors (e.g., "concatination").

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please address the criticism that your main experimental setup in Section 3 is circular? Since the PoS tags are part of the input to the embedding model, isn't the classifier simply learning to decode information that was explicitly provided during training, rather than discovering latent grammatical properties from distributional context?
2.  Could you defend the comparison against the Stanford PoS Tagger in Section 4? Given that you deprived the tagger of its essential contextual information, how can this be considered a fair or meaningful comparison?
3.  Please clarify the exact procedure for the evaluation on the UD Treebank in Section 3 that resulted in an F-score of 0.99. Specifically, given a lemma from UD, how did you select the corresponding vector from your BNC model, which was trained on `lemma_PoS` pairs?
