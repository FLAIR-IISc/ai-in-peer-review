### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors first train a word embedding model on the BNC corpus where each word is concatenated with its PoS tag (e.g., `love_VERB`). They then train a logistic regression classifier to predict the PoS tag from the corresponding embedding. The high accuracy of this classifier supports the hypothesis that embeddings capture PoS-related distributional patterns. The paper's main focus is an in-depth analysis of the classifier's errors, which are treated as "outliers". The authors argue these outliers reveal either linguistically ambiguous words situated at the boundaries of PoS classes (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives) or potential inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation. A second experiment demonstrates that a classifier trained on embeddings of untagged lemmas, using a small set of unambiguous words for supervision, can achieve reasonable PoS tagging accuracy (84%) without sentence context.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A methodology for corpus analysis and error detection using distributional models.** The primary contribution is the application of a simple classifier on word embeddings to systematically identify and analyze words whose distributional behavior is atypical for their annotated PoS class. This provides a data-driven tool for probing the consistency of PoS tagsets and identifying potential annotation errors or areas where annotation guidelines may need refinement.
2.  **A comprehensive analysis of the relationship between distributional vectors and PoS classes.** The work provides strong empirical evidence that PoS identity is robustly encoded across many dimensions of a word vector. The analysis of "prototypical" PoS vectors and the feature importance curve in Section 5 effectively demonstrates that this information is distributed rather than localized to a few dimensions.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis.** The core strength of the paper lies in Section 4, which provides a detailed qualitative analysis of the classifier's prediction errors. The authors move beyond quantitative metrics to offer compelling linguistic interpretations for the misclassifications. The examples provided (e.g., nouns like 'hypertext' used adjectivally, passive participles like 'overgrown' acting as adjectives, and years like '1804' behaving as nouns) are convincing and effectively highlight both genuine linguistic ambiguities and potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation.
2.  **Novel Application for Linguistic Probing.** While the idea that embeddings capture syntax is not new, using them as a tool to audit a gold-standard corpus for consistency is a novel and valuable application. The comparison of errors made by the distributional classifier with those from a standard PoS tagger (Stanford Tagger) successfully illustrates that this approach can uncover different kinds of systemic issues, making it a complementary tool for corpus linguistics and annotation projects.
3.  **Clear and Replicable Methodology.** The experimental design is straightforward, technically sound, and clearly described. The use of standard models (Skip-gram, Logistic Regression) and well-known corpora (BNC, UD) facilitates reproducibility. Decisions such as weighting training instances by frequency to handle class imbalance are well-justified and demonstrate methodological rigor. The evaluation on an out-of-domain corpus (UD Treebank) further strengthens the claims about the robustness of the findings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Circular Experimental Setup.** In the main experiment (Section 3), embeddings are trained on `lemma_POS` tokens. This means that `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN` are treated as distinct types and learn separate vectors. The classifier then predicts the POS from a vector that was learned with explicit POS information included in its source token. This setup is not fully explained and may be perceived as circular. While the goal is to see if the contexts of a `lemma_POS` token align with the contexts of other tokens with the same POS, this indirect approach complicates the interpretation of how much information is being added by the `_POS` suffix during training versus what is learned from context alone.
2.  **Unfair Baseline Comparison.** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger in Section 4 is weakened by the experimental setup. The tagger was run on individual tokens rather than complete sentences, which fundamentally cripples a sequence model that relies on local context for disambiguation. While the authors state this was done to "mimic our workflow," their workflow inherently uses no local sentence context, whereas the tagger is designed to use it. This makes the comparison of error patterns potentially misleading, as the tagger is not being evaluated under its intended operational conditions.
3.  **Overstated Claims in Title and Framing.** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" suggests a more foundational contribution than what is delivered. The paper does an excellent job of *probing* and *analyzing* existing PoS boundaries and identifying problematic cases, but it does not propose a new, data-driven taxonomy or a concrete redefinition of the classes themselves. The contribution is more analytical than theoretical.
4.  **Limited to English.** The claims about the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS classes are presented as general, but the experiments are confined to English. Since the nature of PoS ambiguities and class boundaries varies significantly across languages (e.g., in morphologically rich languages), providing evidence from at least one typologically different language would have substantially broadened the impact and generalizability of the findings.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the main experiment in Section 3: Could you clarify the rationale for training embeddings on `lemma_POS` strings? How does this setup ensure that the classifier is learning from distributional context rather than from information implicitly encoded by the `_POS` suffix itself during the embedding training process? Would a simpler method—training embeddings on lemmas only and then classifying them based on similarity to PoS-class centroids—achieve a similar analytical goal?
2.  The comparison to the Stanford PoS Tagger involved running the tagger on isolated tokens. Given that this is a significant departure from how such sequence models are designed to be used, could you elaborate on why this comparison is still valid for contrasting error types? How might the results in Tables 3 and 4 change if the tagger were run on full sentences, and would that alter your conclusions?
