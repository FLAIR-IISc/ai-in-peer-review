### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors propose a methodology to analyze the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes by training a simple classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its embedding. The core of the work lies in analyzing the classifier's errors to identify "outlier" words and word groups that do not conform to the typical distributional patterns of their assigned class, thereby revealing linguistic nuances and potential annotation inconsistencies.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A methodology for data-driven linguistic analysis:** The primary contribution is the application of a simple classification framework on word embeddings as a tool for linguistic inquiry. The detailed error analysis in Section 4 provides concrete, interesting insights into the graded nature of PoS categories (e.g., noun-like numerals such as years, adjective-like nouns used as modifiers) and identifies potential systematic errors in a well-established corpus like the BNC.
2.  **Demonstration of context-free PoS prediction:** The paper demonstrates that a surprisingly effective PoS tagger (84% accuracy) can be built using only word-level distributional information, without access to sentence context. This experiment effectively highlights the robustness with which syntactic roles are encoded in word embeddings learned from raw text.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful and Well-Grounded Error Analysis:** The paper's greatest strength is the qualitative analysis of the classifier's mispredictions in Section 4. The authors move beyond simply reporting an accuracy score and delve into *why* the model makes certain errors. The findings are linguistically plausible and interesting, such as identifying passive participles that behave like adjectives, currency amounts that behave like numerals, and years (`1820s`) that behave like nouns. This analysis provides strong evidence that the method can serve as a valuable tool for corpus linguists and lexicographers to probe category membership.
2.  **Simple, Interpretable, and Effective Methodology:** The experimental approach is clear, simple, and effective. By using a basic logistic regression classifier, the authors ensure that the results are attributable to the information present in the embeddings, rather than the complexity of a deep learning model. The comparison of their error patterns with those from a state-of-the-art tagger (Stanford Tagger) is a particularly strong point, as it successfully demonstrates that their method uncovers a different, and arguably more linguistically nuanced, set of boundary cases.
3.  **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed and use standard resources (BNC, Universal Dependencies tagset, Skip-gram model). Key methodological decisions are sound, such as weighting training instances by corpus frequency to mitigate class imbalance for closed-class words. The validation on a separate, differently annotated corpus (UD Treebank) demonstrates the robustness and generalizability of the findings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Confounding Experimental Design in the Main Analysis:** The main analysis (Sections 3-4) is based on a distributional model trained on `lemma_POS` strings (e.g., `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`). This means the model is explicitly trained to produce different vectors for the same lemma with different PoS tags. Consequently, the classifier is not discovering PoS information from a general "semantic" vector; rather, it's learning to map from vectors that were already differentiated based on their gold PoS tags during training. While this setup is valid for finding outliers *within* pre-defined classes, it weakens the claim of discovering PoS clusters in a purely unsupervised fashion. The authors should be more explicit about this design choice and its implications for their conclusions. The experiment in Section 5 (training on raw lemmas) is a cleaner demonstration of PoS information in embeddings, but the core linguistic analysis relies on the confounded model.
2.  **Overstated Claims and Positioning:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" and some of the framing are slightly overstated. The work does not propose a new PoS tagset or a formal redefinition. Instead, it offers a powerful method for *probing the boundaries* of existing PoS schemes and identifying problematic cases. A more modest framing would be more accurate. Furthermore, while the related work section is adequate, it could better situate the work in the broader context of "probing" classifiers and analyses of syntactic information in embeddings, which is a well-established subfield.
3.  **Insufficient Detail on Classifier and Data Splits:** The paper lacks some important experimental details. The hyperparameters for the logistic regression classifier are not mentioned. The criteria for selecting the top 10,000 words for training and words with frequencies between 100-500 for testing feel somewhat arbitrary. While the results are convincing, providing more justification for these choices and more details about the classifier would improve the reproducibility and scientific rigor of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The core analysis in Sections 3 and 4 relies on embeddings trained on `lemma_POS` strings. This pre-separates vectors for homonymous words like `love_NOUN` and `love_VERB`. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice, as opposed to training on raw lemmas and assigning gold PoS labels to the resulting single vector for `love`? How does this initial separation based on gold tags influence the interpretation of your findings, particularly regarding the discovery of PoS clusters from distributional patterns?
2.  In Section 5, you create a training set of 1,564 "unambiguous" lemmas from the UD Treebank. The definition of unambiguous seems crucial for this experiment. Could you provide more detail on the threshold used to determine a "dominant" tag (i.e., frequencies of other tags not exceeding 1/2 of the dominant one) and comment on how sensitive your results might be to this specific threshold?
