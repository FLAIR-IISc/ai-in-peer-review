### Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary of the paper and contributions:**

This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors' primary contribution is a novel methodology for linguistic analysis: they train a classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its embedding and then perform a detailed analysis of the classifier's errors. This error analysis is used to identify words and word subclasses that lie at the boundaries of traditional PoS categories (e.g., participial adjectives, nominal years) and to uncover potential systematic annotation errors in the British National Corpus (BNC). A secondary contribution is a proof-of-concept demonstration that a simple, type-level PoS tagger can be built using embeddings trained on raw text and a small seed set of unambiguously tagged words, achieving reasonable accuracy without any local sentence context.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Insightful Application of Embeddings:** The main strength of this paper is its use of a standard NLP tool (a classifier on top of word embeddings) not for a performance-driven task, but as an exploratory tool for linguistic analysis. Instead of treating classification errors as failures, the authors treat them as signals of linguistic interest. This approach of using a model's "mistakes" to probe the fuzzy boundaries of grammatical categories is a valuable and creative contribution.

2.  **High-Quality Error Analysis:** Section 4 is exemplary. The authors move beyond a simple confusion matrix to provide concrete linguistic explanations for the observed misclassifications. The analysis successfully highlights well-known linguistic phenomena, such as the adjective-like nature of participles and the noun-like usage of years (e.g., "1960s"). More impressively, the method appears to uncover systematic annotation errors in a widely-used corpus (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' tagged as adverbs in the BNC), demonstrating its utility for corpus quality control.

3.  **Strong Justification of the Method:** The authors effectively justify their approach by comparing the types of errors their distributional classifier makes with those made by a state-of-the-art sequential tagger (the Stanford PoS Tagger). This comparison in Section 4 demonstrates that their method uncovers a different, and arguably more linguistically interesting, set of boundary cases than a standard tagger would, validating it as a complementary analytical technique.

4.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The methodology is generally sound and well-considered. The initial experiment's use of `lemma_POS` types (e.g., 'love_NOUN' vs 'love_VERB') is a clever way to isolate the distributional patterns of a lemma in a specific grammatical function. The decision to weight training instances by corpus frequency is a smart way to handle the class imbalance between open and closed PoS classes, ensuring the model can learn representations for function words.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overstated Claims in Title and Framing:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is a significant overstatement of the paper's contribution. The work does not propose a new, revised PoS tagset or a formal redefinition of any category. Rather, it provides data-driven evidence that supports existing linguistic knowledge about fuzzy boundaries and identifies potential inconsistencies in existing schemes. The work is more about *exploring* or *probing* PoS boundaries than *redefining* them. A more modest framing would be more accurate.

2.  **Lack of Detail and Potential Circularity in the Main Experiment:** The description of the main experiment in Section 3 could be clearer. The model is trained on `lemma_POS` word types. The embeddings for these types are then used to train a classifier to predict the POS component of the type. While the vector for `love_VERB` is learned from its external context, not its tag, the fact that all its training instances are filtered to be verbal contexts means the tag information is heavily baked into the input representation from the start. This setup feels somewhat circular, or at least self-confirming. The paper would be stronger if the authors explicitly discussed this choice and justified why it is a valid method for identifying outliers whose contexts *don't* align with their tag, despite this pre-selection.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The analysis is conducted exclusively on English, using one corpus (BNC) and one embedding algorithm (word2vec Skip-gram). The conclusions, while interesting, may be specific to the properties of English grammar, the BNC's annotation scheme, or the word2vec architecture. The paper's claims about the general utility of this method would be substantially stronger if validated on another language (especially one with richer morphology) or with a different embedding model (e.g., GloVe, fastText) to ensure the findings are robust.

4.  **Unreconciled Findings on Feature Importance:** The paper presents two seemingly contradictory findings about how PoS information is encoded. In Section 3, the authors highlight a single vector component (dimension 94) as an "idiosyncratic feature of conjunctions." However, in Section 5, they conclude that PoS information is "distributed among at least one hundred components." These two statements are not mutually exclusive (a feature could be necessary but not sufficient), but the paper does not attempt to reconcile them, leaving the reader to wonder if the finding about dimension 94 was an over-interpretation of an averaged prototype vector.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given that the paper does not propose a new PoS tagset, would you consider reframing the contribution as a method for "Exploring Part-of-Speech Boundaries" or "Identifying Annotation Inconsistencies" rather than "Redefining" them?

2.  Could you elaborate on the methodological choice in Section 3 to train embeddings on `lemma_POS` tokens? Specifically, can you address the concern that this might be a circular setup and explain more clearly how this design enables the robust detection of outliers?

3.  How do you reconcile the specific finding of component 94 being a key feature for conjunctions with the more general finding that PoS information is broadly distributed across ~100 dimensions? Was the visualization of the prototypical vector potentially misleading, or is there a more nuanced interpretation?

4.  The experiment in Section 5 demonstrates a simple context-free tagger. Did you analyze the errors of this tagger as well? It seems this tagger, trained on raw lemmas, would provide a more direct test of which words have ambiguous distributional profiles, without the potential circularity of the `lemma_POS` setup. How would that error analysis compare to the one presented in Section 4?
