### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors pursue this in two main ways. First, they train a Skip-gram model on the BNC corpus where each token is a `lemma_POS` string (e.g., `run_VERB`). They then train a logistic regression classifier to predict the PoS tag from a word's embedding. By analyzing the classifier's errors ("outliers"), they identify words and word classes whose distributional behavior does not align with their assigned PoS tag, revealing linguistic ambiguities (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives) and potential systematic annotation errors in the BNC. Second, they demonstrate that a simple, context-unaware PoS tagger can be built by training embeddings on raw lemmas and then training a classifier on a small set of "unambiguous" words, achieving a respectable 84% accuracy. The overall argument is that distributional models are a valuable tool for linguistic analysis of grammatical categories.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A methodology for using word embeddings for corpus analysis and error detection.** The primary contribution is the novel method of identifying "outlier" words by training a classifier on embeddings derived from `lemma_POS` tokens and analyzing its misclassifications. The subsequent analysis in Section 4 is insightful, successfully using this method to uncover both well-known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the verb/adjective nature of participles) and specific, actionable inconsistencies or errors in the BNC's annotation scheme (e.g., the classification of years as numerals, or adjectives like 'plain' being tagged as adverbs).

2.  **A compelling demonstration of the strength of PoS information encoded in static embeddings.** The paper reinforces the idea that significant grammatical information is captured in word embeddings. The experiment in Section 5, which achieves 84% accuracy with a context-unaware tagger trained on only ~1500 unambiguous lemmas, provides a strong, quantifiable measure of this. The analysis showing that this information is distributed across at least 100 dimensions of the embedding space is also a valuable finding.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful and well-motivated linguistic analysis.** The paper's core strength is not just in applying a computational method, but in using its results to engage in genuine linguistic inquiry. The analysis in Section 4 is excellent. It goes beyond simply listing error types and provides plausible linguistic explanations for why the model makes these "mistakes," effectively using the model as a lens to examine the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes. The discovery of systematic errors in the BNC concerning adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' is a particularly strong result that validates the utility of the approach.

2.  **Sound and clear experimental methodology.** The experiments are well-designed and clearly explained. The use of the BNC and Universal Dependencies provides a solid foundation. Methodological choices, such as weighting training instances by corpus frequency to handle class imbalance for closed-class words, are thoughtful and well-justified. The comparison of their method's error patterns with those of the Stanford PoS Tagger effectively demonstrates that their approach yields different and complementary insights.

3.  **The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.** The authors clearly state their hypothesis, describe their methods step-by-step, and present their results in a logical and compelling narrative. The tables are informative and the overall structure makes the paper's arguments accessible and persuasive.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potential circularity in the main experimental setup.** The primary analysis in Sections 3 and 4 is based on a model trained on `lemma_POS` tokens. A classifier is then trained to predict the PoS component from the resulting embedding. This setup feels somewhat circular. The embedding for `love_VERB` is learned from its co-occurrence with other `*_POS` tokens, meaning the vector space is already explicitly structured by the PoS tags from the outset. Consequently, it's difficult to be certain whether the "outliers" are words whose *distributional patterns* are anomalous, or simply words where the *BNC's tagging* is inconsistent with the dominant tagging patterns. A more convincing approach might have been to use contextualized embeddings or to cluster the contexts of a given lemma to see if they form distinct PoS-related groups without pre-injecting the tag into the vocabulary. The experiment in Section 5 does this correctly, but the paper's main analysis rests on the more questionable `lemma_POS` method.

2.  **Unclear handling of ambiguity in key evaluations.** The paper reports an F-score of 0.99 on the Universal Dependencies (UD) treebank in Section 3 and an accuracy of 0.84 in Section 5. The handling of ambiguous words in these evaluations is not sufficiently explained. For the 0.99 F-score, it is unclear how a lemma from UD (e.g., "work") was mapped to a `lemma_POS` vector from the BNC model. If the UD gold tag was used to select the corresponding vector (e.g., picking `work_NOUN`'s vector for a noun instance), then the evaluation is trivial as the answer is provided in the input. For the 84% accuracy result in Section 5, the model is trained on raw lemmas and tested on UD tokens. For an ambiguous word like "back" (NOUN, VERB, ADV, ADJ), it seems only a single vector exists. It is not explained how the classifier could possibly disambiguate the correct tag for a given instance of "back" without any sentence context. This is a critical detail that undermines the reported performance figures.

3.  **Overstated claims in the title and introduction.** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," suggests a fundamental contribution to linguistic theory. However, the paper primarily presents a useful methodology for analyzing existing PoS schemes and annotated corpora. While it highlights known ambiguities and inconsistencies, it doesn't propose a new, data-driven PoS tagset or a formal redefinition. The findings are more about validating intuitions about fuzzy boundaries and providing a tool for corpus quality control, which is a valuable but different contribution than what the title implies.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the main experiment in Section 4, could you elaborate on the potential circularity of training embeddings on `lemma_POS` strings and then predicting the PoS? How can you be sure that the classifier's errors reflect fundamental distributional properties of the lemmas, rather than simply inconsistencies in the application of the `_POS` labels within the BNC itself?

2.  For the evaluation on the UD Treebank in Section 3 that yielded an F-score of 0.99, how were ambiguous lemmas handled? Specifically, if a lemma in the UD test set has multiple possible PoS tags, how did you select the appropriate `lemma_POS` vector from your BNC-trained model as input to the classifier?

3.  In the Section 5 experiment, your context-unaware tagger is evaluated on tokens from the UD test set. For a polysemous lemma like "book", which has a single embedding, how does the classifier predict whether it is a NOUN or a VERB in a specific instance without access to any sentence context? Does the classifier always output the lemma's most frequent tag? Please clarify the evaluation protocol.
