### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its pre-trained embedding. They find the classifier performs well, and then focus their analysis on its errors, arguing that these "outliers" are linguistically interesting. They contend that these misclassifications reveal words that lie on the boundaries between traditional PoS classes, highlight inconsistencies in corpus annotation (BNC), and can even uncover systematic tagging errors. They compare this error analysis to that of a standard PoS tagger to demonstrate the unique insights provided by their distributional approach. Finally, they build a context-independent PoS tagger using embeddings trained on raw text, showing it achieves reasonable accuracy, and analyze which embedding dimensions are most informative for the task.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A methodology for linguistic analysis using classification errors:** The main contribution is the novel use of a simple classifier not for prediction, but as an analytical tool. By systematically identifying and examining words whose distributional profiles do not match their annotated PoS tag, the authors provide a data-driven method to probe the fuzzy boundaries of grammatical categories and identify potential corpus annotation issues.
2.  **A detailed case study of PoS classes in English:** The analysis in Section 4 provides a series of insightful, concrete examples of the methodology's utility. The findings regarding verb-adjective distinctions for participles, noun-numeral ambiguities for years and currencies, and the identification of potentially mis-tagged adverbs in the BNC are compelling and well-argued.
3.  **A demonstration of the strength of type-level PoS information in embeddings:** While not entirely novel, the experiment in Section 5 effectively demonstrates that a significant amount of PoS information can be extracted from word embeddings alone, without any local sentence context, providing a strong baseline for the inherent "grammaticality" encoded in these representations.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful and Creative Analytical Framework:** The paper's core strength lies in its reframing of classification errors as a source of linguistic insight rather than model failure. This is a creative and productive way to use standard machine learning tools for linguistic discovery. The analysis in Section 4 is compelling, moving beyond simple error counts to provide qualitative interpretations that are well-grounded in linguistic intuition (e.g., the dual nature of participles, the noun-like function of years). This approach is well-suited to the interests of the CoNLL community.
2.  **Clear and Methodical Experiments:** The experimental design is, for the most part, clearly explained and easy to follow. The use of standard resources like the BNC and Universal Dependencies, along with well-known techniques like skip-gram and logistic regression, makes the work transparent and reproducible. The step-by-step process of training, identifying outliers, and analyzing them is logical and sound.
3.  **Effective Comparison to a Standard Tagger:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger in Section 4 is a valuable addition. It successfully demonstrates that the proposed distributional method is not merely a weaker version of a state-of-the-art tagger but a complementary tool that highlights different kinds of linguistic phenomena. The finding that the Stanford tagger's errors are more focused on token-level ambiguities (e.g., verb vs. noun for the same word form), while the distributional method identifies type-level distributional similarities (e.g., nouns that behave like adjectives), strongly supports the unique value of the authors' approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflated Experimental Design in the Main Analysis:** The primary experimental setup described in Section 3 has a significant methodological flaw. Embeddings are trained on tokens formed by concatenating lemmas and their PoS tags (e.g., `love_VERB`). A classifier is then trained to predict the PoS tag from these very embeddings. This creates a circular dependency: the information the classifier is meant to "discover" was explicitly provided to the embedding model during training. While the fact that errors still occur is interesting, framing this as a classifier "inferring" PoS information from distributional patterns is misleading. The experiment in Section 5 is the correct way to test this hypothesis, but the paper's central analysis in Section 4 is based on this circular setup, which undermines the strength of its claims about what the embeddings have learned independently.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty and Baselines:** The technical components of the paper (skip-gram, logistic regression) are standard. The novelty is in their application, but the evaluation of the resulting PoS tagger in Section 5 is incomplete. The reported 84% accuracy is presented in isolation. To properly interpret this result, a comparison to a simple but crucial baseline, such as a unigram tagger that assigns the most frequent tag to each lemma based on the training data, is necessary. Without this, it is difficult to ascertain how much of the performance is attributable to the rich distributional information in the embeddings versus simple lexical priors.
3.  **Potential for Over-interpretation and Lack of Statistical Rigor:** While the qualitative error analysis is a strength, some conclusions are drawn from very small numbers of instances (e.g., 2 SCONJ or 3 ADP misclassifications in Table 2). This risks being anecdotal. The paper would be strengthened by incorporating statistical significance tests or confidence intervals to support claims about error patterns. Furthermore, the analysis of feature importance in Section 5 (Figure 3) is presented without error bars, making it difficult to assess the stability of the observed trend. The discussion of "prototypical" vectors and specific components (e.g., dimension 94) is intriguing but remains at a surface level.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the main experiment in Section 3: You trained embeddings on `lemma_POS` tokens, meaning the PoS tag was part of the input string for the embedding model. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice and address the concern that this creates a circular setup where the classifier predicts information that was explicitly encoded during training? How would your analysis in Section 4 differ if it were based on the cleaner experimental setup from Section 5 (embeddings trained on raw text)?
2.  To contextualize the 84% accuracy of your context-agnostic tagger (Section 5), could you provide the performance of a majority-class unigram baseline (assigning each lemma its most frequent tag from the training set)? This would help clarify the added value of the distributional information captured by the embeddings.
3.  Your error analysis focuses on the single best prediction of the classifier. Have you examined the classifier's confidence for these "outlier" cases? For instance, are these words where the model is highly confident in its incorrect prediction, or are they cases of high uncertainty where the probabilities for the correct and incorrect tags are very close? This could add valuable nuance to your linguistic interpretations.
