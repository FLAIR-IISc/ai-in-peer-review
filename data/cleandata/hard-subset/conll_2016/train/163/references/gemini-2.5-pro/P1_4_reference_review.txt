### Summary of the paper
The paper investigates the extent to which distributional word embeddings capture Part-of-Speech (PoS) information. The authors' primary goal is not to build a state-of-the-art PoS tagger, but rather to use a simple classification model as an analytical tool to probe the boundaries of traditional PoS classes. They train a Skip-gram model on the BNC corpus, where each token is a `lemma_PoS` string. Then, they train a logistic regression classifier to predict the PoS tag from a word's embedding. By analyzing the misclassifications of this classifier, they identify "outlier" words whose distributional patterns are more similar to a different PoS class. This analysis reveals well-known linguistic ambiguities (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives), highlights potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation scheme (e.g., currencies tagged as nouns), and discovers systematic tagging errors. In a second experiment, they demonstrate that a simple context-unaware tagger trained on embeddings of lemmas alone can achieve a respectable 84% accuracy, suggesting that PoS information is robustly encoded and distributed across many dimensions of the embeddings.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel methodology for linguistic analysis:** The primary contribution is the use of a simple classifier's errors on word embeddings as a diagnostic tool to systematically identify and analyze words that lie at the boundaries of PoS categories. This "failure analysis" approach provides a data-driven method for probing the validity and consistency of established PoS tagsets.
2.  **Detailed analysis of PoS boundary cases in English:** The paper provides a compelling qualitative analysis of the classifier's errors (Section 4), identifying several interesting phenomena. These include the noun/adjective distinction for words used as modifiers, the verb/adjective nature of participles, and plausible annotation inconsistencies in the BNC regarding numerals (years, currencies) and adverbs (e.g., 'plain', 'clear').
3.  **Quantitative evidence for the distributed nature of PoS information in embeddings:** The analysis in Section 5 demonstrates that PoS predictability is not concentrated in a few embedding dimensions but is distributed across at least 100 dimensions. This reinforces the distributed representation hypothesis in the context of grammatical features.

### Strengths
1.  **Clever and insightful problem framing:** The paper's strength lies in its framing. Instead of pursuing incremental gains in PoS tagging accuracy, it uses a simple, well-understood model as a lens to investigate a fundamental linguistic question about the nature of grammatical categories. This approach is refreshing and yields interesting insights that would be obscured by a more complex, state-of-the-art model.
2.  **Strong qualitative error analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is the highlight of the paper. It is well-structured, connects computational results to linguistic intuition, and provides concrete examples that are convincing. The discovery of systematic errors in the BNC (adjectives like 'plain' tagged as adverbs) is a particularly strong result, demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed method for corpus quality control.
3.  **Effective comparison demonstrating a unique perspective:** The comparison against the Stanford PoS Tagger is effective in showing that the proposed distributional method uncovers a different set of boundary cases and errors. While a standard tagger's errors are heavily influenced by local context and ambiguity, the distributional method's errors are based on a word's global, context-aggregated behavior, providing a complementary view on PoS classification challenges.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Potentially circular experimental setup in the main analysis:** The primary experiment (Sections 3 & 4) trains the embedding model on `lemma_PoS` concatenated strings (e.g., `love_VERB`, `love_NOUN`). This means the model learns distinct vectors for the same lemma depending on its gold PoS tag. The classifier is then trained to predict the PoS tag from this PoS-conditioned vector. This setup feels circular. The classifier may simply be learning to decode the PoS information that was explicitly baked into its input vector during the embedding training stage, rather than discovering PoS affiliation from purely distributional patterns of the lemma itself. This concern undermines the claim that the analysis reveals how a word's distribution conflicts with its tag; instead, it shows how the distribution of a `word-in-a-PoS-role` compares to others.
2.  **Overstated claims and title:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is an overstatement. The work provides evidence to probe and question existing boundaries but does not propose or formalize any new definitions. Similarly, calling the model in Section 5 a "full-fledged PoS tagger" is misleading. It is a context-unaware lexical tagger, and while its performance is interesting as a baseline, it is not comparable to actual taggers used in practice. A more measured framing would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Unfair comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger:** The Stanford Tagger was evaluated on isolated tokens to "mimic our workflow." This cripples the tagger, which is fundamentally designed to use sequential context. This experimental choice makes the comparison unfair and potentially misleading. While it successfully shows the two methods are different, the analysis of the Stanford tagger's errors under these artificial conditions may not be representative of its actual error patterns on real text, weakening the conclusions drawn from the comparison.
4.  **Limited scope of evaluation:** The analysis is confined to English, the BNC, and a single embedding architecture (Skip-gram with specific hyperparameters). The claims about the nature of PoS classes are general, but the evidence is specific. The conclusions would be much stronger if the method were demonstrated on another language (especially one with richer morphology) or with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText, or contextualized embeddings).

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the main experiment (Section 3), could you clarify the rationale for training embeddings on `lemma_PoS` tokens? Given that the input vectors to the classifier are already conditioned on the gold PoS tag, how does this setup avoid circularity? Does this not test the similarity of `(lemma, tag)` contexts rather than the inherent distributional properties of the `lemma` itself?
2.  The comparison to the Stanford PoS Tagger was performed on isolated tokens, removing the context which is the primary source of information for such models. Could you justify this choice further? Do you believe a comparison on full sentences, while architecturally different from your model, might provide a more meaningful comparison of the *types* of linguistic phenomena that each approach finds difficult?
