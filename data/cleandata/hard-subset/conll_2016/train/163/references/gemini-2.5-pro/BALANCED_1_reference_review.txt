### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in static word embeddings. The authors train a simple classifier to predict PoS tags for words based solely on their pre-trained skip-gram vectors. The primary application is a novel form of linguistic and corpus analysis, where the classifier's errors are used to identify "outlier" words whose distributional properties conflict with their gold PoS tags in the British National Corpus (BNC). The analysis of these outliers reveals well-known fuzzy grammatical boundaries (e.g., participles as verb/adjective hybrids), potential annotation inconsistencies (e.g., treatment of years and currency), and systematic tagging errors in the source corpus. The authors demonstrate that this method provides insights distinct from those gained by analyzing the errors of a standard PoS tagger. A second experiment shows that a similar classifier, trained on a small set of unambiguous words, can achieve a respectable 84% accuracy on a context-free PoS tagging task.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A methodology for data-driven linguistic inquiry and corpus error analysis.** The core contribution is the use of a simple embedding-based classifier not for prediction, but as a tool to systematically surface words that challenge a given PoS tagset. The qualitative analysis in Section 4 is insightful and demonstrates the utility of this approach for identifying linguistically interesting phenomena and potential annotation artifacts.
2.  **A clear empirical analysis of PoS information encoded in static embeddings.** The paper provides a well-structured empirical demonstration of how much PoS information is captured by standard word embeddings. The high classifier performance and the analysis of prototypical PoS vectors effectively quantify this relationship, confirming and building upon prior work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and insightful application for linguistic analysis.** The paper's primary strength is its shift in focus from pure predictive performance to linguistic discovery. Instead of treating classifier errors as failures, the authors treat them as objects of study. The analysis in Section 4 is the highlight of the paper, providing concrete examples (e.g., `white-collar_ADJ` as a noun, passive participles as adjectives) that are well-grounded in linguistic theory. This positions the work as a valuable tool for corpus linguists and lexicographers.
2.  **Effective comparative analysis.** The comparison against the error patterns of the Stanford PoS Tagger (Tables 1/2 vs. 3/4) is particularly compelling. It successfully argues that the proposed distributional method is not merely a weaker version of a standard tagger, but rather a complementary tool that reveals a different set of issues, focusing more on semantic-distributional class membership rather than local syntactic disambiguation. This strengthens the paper's claim to utility.
3.  **Clear experimental design and presentation.** The paper is well-written and the experiments are easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the progression from the outlier detection task (Sec 3-4) to the context-free prediction task (Sec 5) is logical. The visualizations of prototypical vectors (Figures 1-2) and the feature importance analysis (Figure 3) are effective additions that support the paper's central claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated claims and limited technical novelty.** The title's claim of "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is a significant overstatement. The work effectively *probes* existing PoS boundaries and finds inconsistencies in their application, but it does not propose or derive a new, coherent PoS taxonomy. The underlying technical finding—that word embeddings capture syntactic information—is well-established. While the application is novel, the paper would be stronger if it more precisely framed its contribution in relation to prior work that has already demonstrated PoS clustering in embedding spaces.
2.  **Unclear motivation for the `lemma_PoS` embedding strategy.** In the first experiment, the authors train embeddings on concatenated `lemma_PoS` tokens (e.g., `love_VERB`). It is not immediately obvious how the skip-gram model learns distinct representations for homonyms like `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`, given that their contexts consist of the same vocabulary of raw words (e.g., `the_DET`). The distinction must be learned from subtle statistical differences in the distributions of these context words, but this mechanism is not explained or justified. A simpler and more direct approach might have been to train embeddings on raw lemmas and use the BNC tags as external labels for the classifier, as is done in Section 5. The rationale for the more complex `lemma_PoS` setup needs to be clarified.
3.  **Lack of sensitivity analysis and limited evaluation baselines.** The experiments are conducted with a single set of hyperparameters (e.g., window size of 2, skip-gram model). The choice of a small, local window is particularly relevant, as it likely emphasizes syntactic interchangeability. The conclusions would be more robust if the authors discussed how different hyperparameters or embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText) might affect the results. Furthermore, the 84% accuracy of the context-free tagger in Section 5 is presented without a crucial baseline: a simple majority tag classifier that assigns the most frequent PoS tag to each lemma based on the training data. This baseline is essential for understanding how much of the performance is attributable to the distributional information versus simple word-tag frequency.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for training embeddings on `lemma_PoS` tokens in Section 3? Specifically, how does the model differentiate homonyms with different tags (e.g., `run_VERB` vs. `run_NOUN`) when their local context windows often contain identical tokens? Have you experimented with the simpler approach of training on raw lemmas and using the gold tags as labels for the resulting vectors?
2.  The title suggests an effort to "redefine" PoS classes. While the analysis is valuable for probing PoS boundaries, it stops short of proposing an actual revised classification scheme. Could you please clarify this claim? For instance, what would a concrete "redefinition" suggested by your findings look like (e.g., merging certain classes, creating new ones for participles, etc.)?
3.  For the context-free tagger in Section 5, how does its 84% accuracy compare against a strong baseline that assigns the most frequent tag for each lemma, as determined from the training corpus? This would help to better isolate the contribution of the distributional vector itself.
