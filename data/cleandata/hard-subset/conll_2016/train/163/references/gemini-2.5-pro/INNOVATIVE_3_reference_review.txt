### Paper Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to analyzing and questioning traditional Part-of-Speech (PoS) categories using distributional word embeddings. The core idea is to train a classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its embedding and then to perform a qualitative analysis on the words that are misclassified. The authors argue that these "outliers" are not simply errors, but linguistically interesting cases that lie on the fuzzy boundaries between grammatical classes or that highlight inconsistencies and errors in the source corpus (the BNC). A secondary experiment demonstrates that a simple, context-unaware PoS tagger can be built using this principle, suggesting potential for low-resource scenarios.

I see the main contributions as follows:

1.  **A novel framework for computational linguistic inquiry:** The paper's primary contribution is not a new model or a state-of-the-art result, but a creative and insightful reframing of a standard machine learning task (classification) as a tool for linguistic discovery. By focusing on the model's "failures," the authors use word embeddings as a lens to probe the continuous and often ambiguous nature of PoS classes in a data-driven manner.
2.  **A detailed qualitative analysis revealing PoS boundary cases and corpus artifacts:** The error analysis in Section 4 is a significant contribution in itself. It provides concrete, well-analyzed examples of various phenomena, such as the noun-adjective continuum, the adjectival nature of passive participles, and systematic tagging errors within the BNC, demonstrating the practical utility of the method for both linguistic theory and corpus maintenance.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Bold Vision:** This is exactly the kind of work that pushes the field forward. Instead of offering an incremental improvement to PoS tagging accuracy, the paper uses modern NLP tools to return to a fundamental linguistic question about the nature of grammatical categories. The proposal to use a classifier's errors as a primary source of insight is a bold and refreshing perspective. It champions the use of computational models not just for engineering better systems, but for generating new linguistic hypotheses.
2.  **Rich and Insightful Analysis:** The heart of the paper, the qualitative analysis in Section 4, is exceptionally well-executed. The authors go beyond simply listing error types and provide thoughtful linguistic interpretations for why certain words are misclassified. The discovery of systematic errors in a well-established corpus like the BNC (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' tagged as adverbs) is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness. This analysis provides a compelling case for the idea that distributional patterns can challenge and refine pre-existing linguistic annotations.
3.  **Simplicity as a Virtue:** The methodological approach is refreshingly simple (standard Skip-gram embeddings and logistic regression). This is a major strength, as it ensures that the fascinating results are not an artifact of a complex, opaque architecture but emerge from the fundamental distributional properties captured in the vector space. This clarity makes the paper's central argument more direct and convincing.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few points that could be strengthened. I view these as minor flaws that do not detract from the overall significance of the work.

1.  **Slightly Flawed Comparison to the Sequential Tagger:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is the paper's weakest point. By design, the authors feed the tagger isolated tokens, crippling its ability to use sequential context, which is its primary strength. While the authors note this is done to "mimic our workflow," it makes the comparison unfair and the conclusion that the methods "reveal different error cases" somewhat obvious. The value of this comparison could be improved by framing it more explicitly as a contrast between a global, type-level distributional model and a local, token-level sequential model, and discussing why this distinction *should* lead to different error profiles.
2.  **The PoS Tagger Experiment Feels Preliminary:** The second experiment building a full PoS tagger in Section 5 is an interesting proof-of-concept, but it feels underdeveloped compared to the rich analysis in the first part of the paper. The analysis of feature importance, for instance, concludes that information is distributed across ~100 dimensions but doesn't delve deeper. While this doesn't undermine the paper's core contribution, this section could either be expanded or framed more clearly as a promising direction for future work.
3.  **Initial Methodological Description Could Be Clearer:** The method of creating `lemma_POS` word types for training the initial embeddings is a clever solution for handling homonyms. However, this could initially be misinterpreted by a reader as a circular setup (predicting a tag from an embedding that was explicitly trained using that tag). A sentence or two at the beginning of Section 3 explicitly justifying this design choice—to create distinct representations for words like `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN` based on their unique contexts—would preempt potential confusion and strengthen the methodological exposition.

### Questions to Authors

The comparison to the Stanford Tagger, while flawed in its current form, brings up an interesting conceptual point. Your model operates on type-level, corpus-wide distributional information, whereas a standard HMM/CRF tagger leverages local, token-level sequential context. Could you elaborate on how you see this fundamental difference in information sources leading to the distinct error patterns you observed? A deeper discussion of this point could turn a methodological weakness into a valuable conceptual insight about the different kinds of syntactic knowledge captured by these different model classes.
