### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors propose a methodology based on training a classifier to predict PoS tags from word vectors and then analyzing the classification errors to identify words that lie at the boundaries of traditional PoS categories.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  An error analysis methodology for identifying words with ambiguous or non-prototypical PoS behavior. The comparison of error patterns from their embedding-based classifier against those from a standard PoS tagger (Stanford) effectively demonstrates that this approach can uncover different and potentially more nuanced types of categorical ambiguity.
2.  A qualitative analysis that uses this methodology to identify several well-known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the adjective-like nature of some nouns, the verb/adjective ambiguity of participles) and potential inconsistencies or systematic errors in the British National Corpus (BNC) annotation.
3.  A secondary experiment demonstrating that a simple, context-agnostic PoS tagger trained on embeddings of raw lemmas can achieve a respectable accuracy (0.84), confirming that significant PoS-related information is captured in embeddings even without explicit tag information during training.

**Strengths**

1.  **Complementary Analytical Tool:** The core idea of using the failures of an embedding-based classifier as a diagnostic tool for linguistic analysis is sound. The comparison in Section 4 between the confusion matrix of their method and that of the Stanford PoS Tagger is the paper's strongest point. It clearly shows that the two methods highlight different kinds of ambiguities. While the Stanford tagger frequently confuses major open classes (VB-NN, JJ-NN), the proposed method identifies more subtle issues, such as the adverbial nature of certain subordinate conjunctions or the nominal behavior of numerals representing years. This suggests the proposed method is not redundant and can serve as a useful complementary tool for corpus analysis and lexicon development.

2.  **Concrete and Plausible Findings:** The analysis in Section 4, while qualitative, yields several concrete and linguistically plausible findings. The identification of currency amounts (`£70`) classified as numerals instead of nouns, or years (`1804`) classified as nouns instead of numerals, points to genuine inconsistencies in annotation schemes. The discovery of adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' being systematically mis-tagged as adverbs in the BNC is a practical demonstration of the method's utility for corpus quality control.

3.  **Soundness of the Secondary Experiment:** The experiment in Section 5 is well-conceived and serves as an important sanity check. By training embeddings on untagged lemmas and building a classifier from a small set of "unambiguous" words, the authors provide a more conventional and convincing demonstration that PoS information is inherently learned by the embedding model. Achieving 84% accuracy with a context-agnostic, type-level classifier is a solid result that grounds the paper's premise in established methodology.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overstated Claims and Framing:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," and parts of the introduction imply a much more foundational contribution than is delivered. The paper does not propose a new, data-driven PoS tagset or a formal redefinition of class boundaries. Rather, it uses an existing technique (classification on embeddings) to explore the fuzziness of a pre-existing tagset. The contribution is analytical, not definitional. A more accurate framing would focus on the work as a method for analyzing corpus annotation and exploring categorical ambiguity.

2.  **Methodological Circularity in the Main Experiment:** The primary experimental setup (Sections 3 and 4) is somewhat circular. The embeddings are trained on `lemma_POS` tokens, meaning the PoS information is explicitly fed into the model during training. A classifier is then trained to predict the `POS` from the resulting `lemma_POS` vector. It is therefore unsurprising that the classifier achieves very high accuracy, as it is largely recovering information that was provided upfront. While the authors' focus is on the *errors*, this setup makes the baseline performance less meaningful and complicates the interpretation of what the model has "learned." A more robust design would have been to use the methodology from Section 5 for the main analysis as well: train embeddings on raw text and then perform the classification and error analysis.

3.  **Lack of Quantitative Validation for Outlier Analysis:** The analysis of outliers in Section 4 is insightful but remains largely qualitative and anecdotal. The claims would be substantially strengthened by quantitative evidence. For example, when arguing that `materialist_NOUN` behaves like an adjective, the authors could provide statistics from the BNC showing the frequency with which it appears in pre-nominal modifier position, compared to prototypical nouns and adjectives. Without such validation, the analysis relies heavily on the reader's acceptance of the authors' linguistic intuition.

4.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Methods:** While the paper compares its error patterns to those of a standard tagger, it does not compare its *method* for identifying outliers to other established techniques. For instance, one could identify words with ambiguous PoS membership by analyzing the output distributions of a probabilistic tagger (e.g., finding words with high entropy over the tag set). A comparison against such a baseline would be necessary to establish that using embedding-based classifiers is a superior or uniquely insightful approach for this specific task.
