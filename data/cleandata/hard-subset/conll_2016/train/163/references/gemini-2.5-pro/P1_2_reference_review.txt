### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a method for analyzing the fuzzy boundaries between Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes using distributional word embeddings. The primary methodology involves training a classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its pre-trained word embedding. The authors' main focus is not on the classifier's performance itself, but on its errors. They hypothesize that words misclassified by this model are linguistic "outliers" that do not conform to the typical distributional patterns of their assigned PoS class. Through a detailed analysis of these errors on the British National Corpus (BNC), they identify several interesting phenomena, such as the adjectival nature of some nouns, the verbal nature of participles, inconsistencies in annotating numerals and currency, and even potential systematic errors in the BNC's original annotation. The paper contrasts this error analysis with that of a standard PoS tagger to show its unique insights and concludes by demonstrating a simple, context-unaware PoS tagger built on the same principles.

### Contributions

1.  **A methodology for linguistic analysis via classifier error analysis.** The main contribution is the novel use of a simple classifier not as a final tool, but as a diagnostic probe. By analyzing the misclassifications of an embedding-to-PoS model, the authors provide a systematic, data-driven framework for identifying words and subclasses that lie at the boundaries of traditional PoS categories. This approach is valuable for corpus linguistics, annotation guideline refinement, and linguistic discovery.
2.  **A qualitative analysis of PoS boundary cases in English.** The detailed error analysis in Section 4 is a significant contribution. It moves beyond simply reporting a confusion matrix to offer plausible linguistic interpretations for the observed "errors," such as nouns with adjectival usage (`hypertext`), verbs used primarily as passive participles (`intertwined`), and the noun-like function of years (`1820s`). This analysis validates the proposed methodology and provides concrete insights into the English PoS system.
3.  **A demonstration of the strength of PoS information in static embeddings.** The paper effectively shows that a significant amount of PoS-related information is encoded in word embeddings, separable even by a simple linear classifier. The experiment in Section 5, which achieves 84% accuracy with a context-unaware tagger trained on only ~1500 unambiguous examples, serves as a strong proof-of-concept for this idea.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Methodology:** The core idea of using a classifier's errors as a tool for linguistic discovery is compelling and well-motivated. Instead of treating misclassifications as failures to be minimized, the authors reframe them as data points worthy of investigation. This provides a fresh perspective on how machine learning models can be used to inform linguistic theory and practice, rather than just perform an engineering task.
2.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The paper excels in its qualitative analysis (Section 4). The authors provide specific examples for each major error category and connect them to known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the verb-adjective continuum for participles) or annotation challenges (e.g., currency amounts). The discovery of potentially systematic errors in the BNC (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' tagged as adverbs) is a particularly strong piece of evidence for the utility of this method for corpus quality control.
3.  **Effective Comparison to a Standard Tagger:** The comparison of their method's confusion matrix with that of the Stanford PoS Tagger is a crucial element. It successfully demonstrates that the distributional approach highlights different, and arguably more nuanced, types of boundary cases than a sequence-based tagger. This strengthens their claim that their method offers a complementary and valuable analytical lens.
4.  **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the experiments are described clearly. The authors provide sufficient detail about their data (BNC, UD), model (Skip-gram), parameters, and classifier (logistic regression) to allow for the work to be reproduced.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Claims in Title and Framing:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is an overstatement. The paper does not propose a new, coherent PoS tagset or a formal redefinition of categories. Rather, it provides an excellent *analysis* of the boundaries and inconsistencies within an *existing* system (Universal PoS tags on the BNC). The contribution is analytical and diagnostic, not definitional. A more accurate framing would strengthen the paper by aligning its claims with its actual achievements.
2.  **Methodological Circularity in the Main Experiment:** In Section 3, the distributional model is trained on `lemma_POS` strings (e.g., `love_VERB`). This means the gold PoS information is "baked into" the input representation *before* the classifier is trained. The classifier is then trained to predict the PoS from the embedding of `lemma_POS`. This setup feels circular and makes the high performance less surprising. It is not learning PoS from raw distributional patterns alone, but rather learning to associate the distributional patterns of an already-tagged word with its tag. This choice requires much stronger justification, as it differs from the more standard approach used in Section 5.
3.  **Lack of a Simple Baseline for the Context-Unaware Tagger:** In Section 5, the authors build a context-unaware tagger that achieves 84% accuracy and rightly note it cannot be compared to state-of-the-art context-aware taggers. However, to properly evaluate how "impressive" this result is, it should be compared to a more appropriate baseline, such as a simple unigram tagger that assigns each word its most frequent PoS tag from the training data. Without this baseline, it is difficult to assess how much the distributional embeddings are contributing beyond simple frequency information.
4.  **Oversimplified View of Prototypicality:** The paper creates "prototypical" PoS vectors by averaging the embeddings of all words in a class. This assumes a unimodal distribution for each PoS class. This is likely not true; for example, the class of verbs contains distributionally distinct subclasses like transitive, intransitive, and auxiliary verbs. Averaging them all together may obscure important internal structure. While not a central flaw, acknowledging this limitation would make the analysis more robust.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train the first model (Section 3) on `lemma_POS` strings? This seems to introduce the answer (the PoS tag) into the input representation. How do you believe this affects the resulting embeddings and the subsequent error analysis, compared to training on raw lemmas and then associating the resulting embeddings with their gold tags?
2.  For the context-unaware tagger experiment in Section 5, what is the performance of a majority-class unigram baseline (i.e., assigning each lemma its most frequent tag from the UD training set)? This would help to better contextualize the 84% accuracy achieved by the embedding-based classifier.
3.  Considering the analytical nature of your contribution, would you be open to revising the title to better reflect the work as an "analysis of PoS boundaries" or "investigation of PoS inconsistencies" rather than a "redefinition"?
