**Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"**

### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors conduct two main experiments. First, they train a Skip-gram model on the British National Corpus (BNC) where each token is a `lemma_POS` pair (e.g., `love_VERB`). They then train a classifier to predict the PoS tag from the resulting embedding. The paper's primary focus is a detailed qualitative analysis of this classifier's errors, arguing that these "misclassifications" are not random but systematically highlight linguistically interesting phenomena, such as words on the boundary between classes (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives) and potential annotation inconsistencies within the BNC. The second experiment demonstrates that a simple classifier trained on standard word embeddings (without PoS tags in the input) and a small seed set of unambiguous words can achieve reasonable PoS tagging accuracy, suggesting potential applications in low-resource settings.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A methodology for linguistic inquiry using classifier error analysis.** The paper's most significant contribution is its framing of a classification task not as an end in itself, but as a tool for corpus exploration. By interpreting the model's errors as signals of distributional ambiguity, the authors provide a compelling method for identifying and analyzing words that challenge traditional, discrete PoS categories.
2.  **An insightful qualitative analysis of PoS boundaries in English.** The detailed breakdown of error patterns in Section 4 is the highlight of the paper. It offers concrete, data-driven evidence for well-known linguistic ambiguities (e.g., noun/adjective, verb/adjective via participles) and uncovers specific, potential annotation inconsistencies in a widely used corpus.
3.  **A clear demonstration of the PoS information encoded in static embeddings.** While not a novel finding in itself, the paper provides a well-executed empirical study showing how robustly PoS identity is encoded in word vectors, even to the point where a simple model with no sentence context can perform reasonably well.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Productive Use of Error Analysis:** The central idea of leveraging classifier errors for linguistic discovery is both clever and effective. Instead of treating errors as model deficiencies, the authors use them as a lens to examine the fuzzy boundaries of grammatical categories. This approach turns a standard machine learning pipeline into a valuable tool for descriptive linguistics and corpus analysis, which is a perfect fit for a computational linguistics venue.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Qualitative Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 is exemplary. The authors move beyond simply reporting confusion matrices to provide plausible linguistic explanations for the observed patterns. The examples are well-chosen and effectively illustrate how distributional patterns can diverge from formal PoS annotation (e.g., currency amounts like `£70` being classified as numerals, or years like `1804` as nouns). The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger's errors successfully shows that the proposed distributional method reveals a different and arguably more nuanced set of boundary cases.
3.  **Well-Structured and Clearly Presented Research:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly rooted in linguistic theory regarding the continuous nature of grammatical classes. The two experiments build on each other logically, with the first establishing the principle via an "oracle" setup (`lemma_POS`) and the second testing its practical viability from scratch.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Finding Regarding Embeddings and Syntax:** The fact that distributional embeddings capture syntactic information, including PoS, is a foundational and well-established property of these models, demonstrated since the original word2vec papers. The paper would be strengthened by more explicitly acknowledging this and positioning its contribution not in *discovering* this link, but in *exploiting* it for detailed linguistic analysis. The current framing sometimes presents this known property as a new finding.
2.  **Unconventional Experimental Design Choices Could Be Better Justified:**
    *   The use of `lemma_POS` tokens in the first experiment is central to the analysis, but it is an "oracle" setup that pre-supposes access to gold PoS tags during embedding training. This makes the classification task fundamentally different from standard PoS tagging. A discussion on the implications of this choice—for example, how it sidesteps the challenge of homonymy—would be beneficial.
    *   The train/test split is based on word frequency (most frequent 10k for training, next 17k for testing). This is a non-standard evaluation protocol. A standard randomized split would be a more robust choice. The authors should provide a clear justification for why a frequency-based split is preferable for their research questions, as it could introduce frequency as a confounding variable.
3.  **Claims About Low-Resource Applicability Are Speculative:** The second experiment is presented as a proof-of-concept for low-resource PoS tagging. While interesting, the claim is under-supported. The experiment is performed on English, using a large, high-quality corpus (BNC) to train the embeddings. The specific challenges of low-resource languages—such as a lack of large corpora for training good embeddings and high morphological complexity—are not addressed. To make this claim more credible, the paper would need to either test on a genuinely low-resource language or, at a minimum, discuss how these challenges might affect the proposed method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Experiment 1, you train embeddings on `lemma_POS` tokens, which effectively creates separate vocabulary entries for, e.g., `love_NOUN` and `love_VERB`. This seems to make the PoS classes highly separable by design. How do you think this setup influences the kinds of "outliers" you find? Could an alternative approach, where you train on lemmas and then perform multi-label classification for ambiguous words, reveal a different or complementary set of boundary cases?
2.  The error analysis in Section 4 is based on manual inspection. Have you considered a more quantitative validation for your claims? For instance, for the verbs misclassified as adjectives (which you hypothesize are mostly passive participles), could you verify this by showing that these specific verbs appear in passive constructions significantly more often than correctly classified verbs in a held-out portion of the corpus?
3.  Regarding the proposed low-resource application (Section 5), the method relies on pre-trained embeddings and a list of unambiguous lemmas. How do you foresee this approach working for morphologically rich languages where lemmatization is a non-trivial challenge and a single lemma may correspond to dozens of surface forms with varying distributional signals?
