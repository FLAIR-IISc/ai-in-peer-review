**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a simple classifier on word vectors to predict PoS tags and then focus on analyzing the classification errors. They argue that these "outliers" highlight interesting linguistic phenomena, such as words on the boundary between classes (e.g., participles behaving as adjectives), and reveal inconsistencies or errors in the gold-standard annotation of the British National Corpus (BNC).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A compelling analysis of PoS class boundaries and corpus annotation issues using distributional models.** The core strength of the paper lies in Section 4, where the authors systematically examine the misclassifications of a simple embedding-based classifier. They provide insightful, linguistically-grounded explanations for why certain words are misclassified, effectively using the model as an analytical tool to probe the fuzzy nature of PoS categories and identify potential systemic errors in a widely-used corpus.
2.  **A demonstration of the strong PoS signal present in word embeddings.** While not a novel discovery, the paper provides strong empirical evidence through two experiments. The first shows high accuracy in identifying a word's PoS from its embedding (when the embedding is trained on `lemma_POS` pairs), and the second demonstrates that a reasonable PoS tagger (0.84 accuracy) can be built without sentence context, using only embeddings and a very small set of prototypically-tagged words for training.

**Strengths**

1.  **Creative and Insightful Application of Standard Methods:** The paper's primary novelty is not in its methodology (Skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression), but in its application. Instead of aiming for state-of-the-art PoS tagging, the authors use these tools as a "magnifying glass" for linguistic inquiry. The error analysis is thoughtful and yields genuinely interesting findings, such as the classification of currency strings as numerals, years as nouns, and the identification of potentially systemic errors in the BNC's tagging of adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear'.
2.  **Clear and Effective Comparison:** The comparison of their method's error patterns with those of the Stanford PoS Tagger (Table 3 vs. Table 1) is a strong rhetorical move. It successfully demonstrates that their distributional approach highlights different, and arguably more linguistically fundamental, types of class confusions (e.g., VERB-ADJ, NUM-NOUN) compared to a context-aware tagger, thereby justifying their approach as a distinct analytical tool.
3.  **Well-Written and Methodologically Transparent:** The paper is exceptionally clear and easy to follow. The experimental setup, motivations, and analysis are described in sufficient detail. The choice to weight training instances by corpus frequency is a sensible way to handle the class imbalance between open and closed-class words, and the authors explain their reasoning clearly.
4.  **Interesting Secondary Finding on Low-Resource Tagging:** The experiment in Section 5, while not achieving SOTA performance, is an interesting proof-of-concept. It shows that PoS information is so robustly encoded in embeddings that a decent tagger can be bootstrapped from a small (1564-word) set of unambiguous examples. This has potential implications for low-resource languages where large annotated corpora are unavailable but raw text for training embeddings is.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Circularity in the Main Experiment:** The central analysis in Sections 3 and 4 is based on a model trained on a corpus where words were represented as `lemma_POS` strings (e.g., `love_VERB`, `love_NOUN`). This means the PoS tag was part of the input to the embedding model itself. The model is explicitly trained to create different vectors for homonyms with different tags. The classifier then learns to map these pre-separated vectors back to their tags. While this still relies on the different distributional contexts of `love_VERB` vs. `love_NOUN` to place their vectors correctly, it feels circular. The claims would be much stronger if this analysis were performed on embeddings trained on the raw BNC lemmas (as was done for the experiment in Section 5), to show that these PoS distinctions emerge naturally from distributional patterns alone, without being explicitly encoded in the vocabulary.
2.  **Overstated Title and Claims:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is a significant overstatement of the paper's contribution. The work does not propose a new, revised tagset or a formal redefinition of PoS classes. Rather, it *explores the boundaries* of existing classes and *questions specific annotation decisions*. It is an excellent piece of linguistic analysis and corpus diagnostics, but it is not a work of redefinition. The framing throughout the paper is more exploratory than the title suggests.
3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments and analysis are conducted entirely on English, using the BNC and the English UD Treebank. While the findings are compelling for English, the paper makes general claims about "parts of speech" and "grammatical categories." The validity of these specific findings (e.g., the NUM/NOUN confusion for years) is highly dependent on the syntactic behavior of these items in English. The contribution would be stronger if it included even a preliminary analysis on a typologically different language to show the generality of the *method*, even if the specific linguistic findings differ.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the reasoning behind training the first distributional model (Section 3) on `lemma_POS` concatenated strings? The core claim is that embeddings capture PoS information from context. This experimental design seems to partially "bake in" the PoS label into the input word representation, which complicates the interpretation of the classifier's success. Why was this approach chosen over training on lemmas alone, as was done in Section 5?
2.  Regarding the title, do you believe the work truly "redefines" PoS classes, or would a title like "Probing Part-of-Speech Boundaries with Distributional Semantic Models" more accurately reflect the exploratory and analytical nature of the contribution?
3.  The analysis of classifier errors on BNC data is very insightful. Have you performed any manual cross-verification for the cases you identify as probable systematic errors (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' tagged as adverbs)? For instance, by checking these specific constructions against other manually annotated corpora or English grammar references to confirm they are indeed errors rather than an unusual but internally consistent aspect of the CLAWS4 annotation scheme?
