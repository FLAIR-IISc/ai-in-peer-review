### Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates the fuzzy boundaries between Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes using distributional semantic models. The core methodology involves training a logistic regression classifier to predict a word's PoS tag based solely on its pre-trained word embedding. The authors' primary experiment uses embeddings trained on a PoS-aware version of the British National Corpus (BNC), where each word type is a concatenation of its lemma and PoS tag (e.g., `love_NOUN`, `love_VERB`). They then conduct a detailed analysis of the classifier's errors, arguing that these "outliers" reveal either linguistically interesting subclasses of words (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives) or systematic inconsistencies and errors in the BNC's annotation scheme (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns instead of numerals). The authors compare their error analysis to that of a standard PoS tagger to demonstrate the unique insights provided by their distributional approach. A second experiment shows that a similar classifier, trained on a small set of unambiguous words, can achieve a respectable 84% accuracy on a test set, highlighting the significant amount of PoS information encoded in embeddings even without sentence context.

### Contributions
1.  **A novel diagnostic methodology for corpus analysis:** The primary contribution is the use of an embedding-based classifier's errors not as a failure, but as an analytical tool to probe the consistency of a corpus's annotation scheme and explore linguistic phenomena at the syntax-semantics interface. This method of identifying "distributional outliers" provides a fresh, data-driven perspective on PoS classification challenges.
2.  **Specific, data-driven linguistic and annotation-related insights:** The paper provides a compelling analysis of several PoS boundary cases in English, backed by concrete examples. It successfully identifies and categorizes phenomena such as the adjectival nature of certain nouns and passive participles, and points out clear inconsistencies in the BNC tagset regarding entities like years, decades, and currency amounts. The discovery of a potential systematic error in the BNC's tagging of words like 'plain' as adverbs is a particularly strong finding.

### Strengths
1.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The main strength of the paper is its focus on analyzing the model's failures. Rather than simply aiming for state-of-the-art performance, the authors use their model as a lens to examine the data. The analysis in Section 4 is detailed, well-argued, and provides convincing evidence for their claims. The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger's error patterns effectively demonstrates that their approach reveals a different and valuable set of issues.
2.  **Sound and Creative Experimental Design:** The initial experiment's design—training embeddings on `lemma_POS` pairs—is a clever way to isolate the distributional properties of words *as they function as a specific part of speech*. This allows for a very clean setup for identifying outliers. Furthermore, the use of frequency weighting to balance the training data is a methodologically sound choice that addresses a common problem in PoS classification.
3.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors clearly state their hypothesis, describe their methods, and present their results and analysis in a compelling narrative. The examples provided are illustrative and effectively support their arguments.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Overstatement of Claims in Title and Framing:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is an overclaim. The work does an excellent job of *critiquing* and *analyzing* an existing PoS scheme by highlighting its fuzzy boundaries and inconsistencies, but it does not propose a new, redefined set of classes. The findings support the idea that PoS is a continuum, but the paper stops short of offering a concrete alternative framework.
2.  **Ambiguity in the Unit of Analysis (Type vs. Token):** The methodology largely operates on a *type level* (specifically, `lemma_POS` types or lemmas), which is a significant simplification. PoS tagging is fundamentally a *token-level* task that depends on sentence context. The paper is not always explicit about this distinction. For instance, the context-free tagger in Section 5 is tested on tokens from the UD Treebank, but its input is only the lemma. This makes it unclear how it would handle a word like "building," which can be a noun or a verb depending on context. This limitation makes it difficult to compare the results to traditional taggers and somewhat constrains the applicability of the findings.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Model Dependence:** The conclusions are drawn from a single distributional model (Skip-gram) with a fixed set of hyperparameters (e.g., window size of 2). The authors generalize their findings to "distributional semantic models" as a whole, but these findings could be an artifact of their specific modeling choices. A small, symmetric window strongly favors local syntactic information. It would be valuable to discuss how results might differ with other architectures (e.g., GloVe, FastText) or a larger context window, which might capture more topic-level information and potentially blur the PoS signals. The paper would be strengthened by acknowledging these limitations.
4.  **Details of the "Prototypical" Tagger are Under-specified:** In Section 5, the classifier is trained on "unambiguous lemmas" from the UD Treebank, defined by a heuristic based on the frequency of the dominant tag. This process is not fully detailed. The choice of this threshold could significantly impact the results, as it may be selecting for the easiest, most prototypical examples and thus inflating performance. A more detailed description of this training set's construction and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Regarding the context-free tagger in Section 5: How does your system handle lemmas that are highly ambiguous across PoS classes (e.g., 'like', 'round', 'book')? Since the classifier takes only the lemma as input, does it always predict the most frequent tag, or does the distributional vector for the un-tagged lemma capture an 'average' of its uses that allows for a more nuanced prediction?
2.  The outlier analysis in Section 4 relies on a model trained with gold PoS tags. How do you envision this methodology being used in a more practical scenario to improve a corpus where the tags are presumed to be noisy? Would you propose an iterative process of training, identifying/correcting outliers, and retraining?
3.  You note that PoS information is distributed across at least 100 dimensions. This contrasts with the very strong signal you found for conjunctions in a single component (dim 94). Do you have any hypotheses as to why conjunctions, a closed class, might exhibit such a localized feature while open classes do not?
