### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel, data-driven approach to investigate the boundaries and potential inconsistencies of traditional Part-of-Speech (PoS) classes. The authors train a simple classifier to predict a word's PoS tag from its pre-trained distributional embedding. The core idea is not to build a state-of-the-art tagger, but to analyze the classifier's *errors*. These misclassifications are treated as a signal, highlighting "outlier" words whose distributional behavior aligns more closely with a PoS class different from their gold-standard annotation. The work provides a qualitative analysis of these cases, suggesting the method can uncover both genuine linguistic ambiguities and systematic annotation errors in corpora like the BNC.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A clever reframing of model error as a tool for linguistic discovery.** The central methodology—using a classifier's mistakes on word type embeddings to identify words at the fuzzy boundaries of grammatical categories—is a highly original and insightful approach to computational linguistic analysis.
2.  **A compelling demonstration of how distributional models can empirically probe established linguistic categories.** The qualitative analysis in Section 4 successfully uses the model's "confusion" to highlight well-known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the verb-adjective continuum for participles) and potential corpus artifacts in a purely data-driven way.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's greatest strength is its research question. It moves beyond the typical engineering goal of improving PoS tagging accuracy and instead uses computational tools to probe the fundamental nature of PoS classes themselves. The ambition to "redefine" these classes, even if not fully realized, represents a bold and valuable direction for the field. This is precisely the kind of work that can spark new conversations.
2.  **Insightful Use of Error Analysis:** The idea of treating prediction errors not as failures but as data points for analysis is elegant and powerful. The authors show this method can systematically identify interesting linguistic cases that might otherwise be lost in aggregate accuracy metrics. The comparison with the Stanford Tagger's errors effectively argues that their distributional approach reveals a different, and arguably more semantically grounded, set of boundary phenomena.
3.  **Strong Qualitative Results:** The analysis of outliers is the heart of the paper and is very well executed. The examples provided (e.g., currency amounts behaving like numerals, passive participles like adjectives, certain prepositions like adjectives) are convincing and intuitively plausible. This demonstrates that the method is not just finding noise but is latching onto genuine patterns of language use that challenge rigid categorical annotation.
4.  **Simplicity and Focus:** The authors wisely use simple, well-understood components (Skip-gram, Logistic Regression). This methodological simplicity keeps the focus squarely on the central conceptual contribution and the linguistic analysis, which is where the paper truly shines.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Potentially Confounding Experimental Setup:** The main experiment in Section 3 trains embeddings on `lemma_PoS` strings. This means the model learns separate vectors for `love_NOUN` and `love_VERB` from the start. This choice feels somewhat circular; the PoS information is baked into the "word" representation that the subsequent classifier is trained on. While the analysis of whether `overgrown_VERB`'s vector is closer to the `ADJ` centroid than the `VERB` centroid is still valid, the overall approach is less direct than training on raw text and then analyzing how a classifier handles polysemous word embeddings. This methodological choice could be clarified and better justified.
2.  **The "Redefinition" Promise is Underdeveloped:** The title makes a bold claim about "redefining" PoS classes, but the paper primarily offers a method for *critiquing* existing classes. It successfully identifies outliers and boundary cases but stops short of proposing what a new, distributionally-derived taxonomy might look like. Is the goal a more continuous, probabilistic view of PoS? Or a revised set of discrete classes? The paper raises this fascinating question but doesn't pursue an answer, which feels like a missed opportunity to follow through on its bold premise.
3.  **Superficial Feature Analysis:** The analysis in Section 5, which concludes that PoS information is distributed across ~100 dimensions, feels like a standard finding for dense vector representations and adds little to the paper's main argument. A more incisive analysis could have attempted to characterize what these key dimensions represent or how they distinguish between specific PoS pairs (e.g., ADJ vs. NOUN), which would have provided a stronger conclusion to the investigation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train the initial model on `lemma_PoS` concatenated strings? An alternative would be to train embeddings on raw lemmas and then assign gold labels to these (ambiguous) lemma vectors for classification. What advantages did your chosen method offer for the specific goal of identifying outliers?
2.  The error analysis is compelling but qualitative. Have you considered more quantitative approaches to characterize the identified outliers? For instance, could the misclassified `VERB -> ADJ` words be clustered to see if they form coherent semantic subclasses (e.g., stative verbs, resultative participles)?
3.  Given the ambition of your title, do you see this work leading towards a new model of PoS that is perhaps probabilistic or continuous, rather than categorical? What do you envision as the next major step in a research program aimed at truly "redefining" PoS classes based on distributional evidence?
