**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors propose using a classifier trained on word vectors to identify "outlier" words whose distributional profiles do not match their annotated PoS tags. The primary contribution is a qualitative analysis of these outliers, which reveals linguistically ambiguous cases (e.g., passive participles acting as adjectives) and potential inconsistencies or errors in the British National Corpus (BNC) annotation. A secondary contribution is a demonstration that a simple, context-agnostic classifier trained on a small set of "prototypical" word embeddings can achieve reasonable PoS tagging accuracy, suggesting that embeddings robustly capture PoS information.

**Strengths**

1.  The core idea of leveraging a classifier's errors on distributional vectors as a diagnostic tool for linguistic analysis is novel and interesting. Rather than simply aiming for state-of-the-art performance, the work uses a standard ML setup to probe the fuzzy boundaries of grammatical categories, which is a valuable approach.

2.  The qualitative error analysis presented in Section 4 is the most compelling part of the paper. The examples provided (e.g., nouns like '£70' being classified as numerals, years like '1804' being classified as nouns, and passive participles like 'overgrown' being classified as adjectives) are insightful and effectively highlight well-known linguistic ambiguities and potential annotation inconsistencies. This methodology could be a useful tool for corpus quality control and linguistic discovery.

3.  The comparison against the errors made by the Stanford PoS Tagger, while methodologically problematic (see Weaknesses), successfully demonstrates that the distributional approach uncovers a different class of errors. This supports the authors' claim that their method provides a complementary perspective for analyzing PoS systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Critical Methodological Opacity and Dubious Results:** The description of the first experiment in Section 3 is concerning. The model is trained on a corpus where tokens are `lemma_POS` concatenations. A classifier is then trained to predict the PoS tag from the vector for `lemma_POS`. This setup is circular. Furthermore, the evaluation on the Universal Dependencies (UD) Treebank, yielding an F-score of 0.99, is entirely unexplained and seems technically implausible. The BNC-trained model would not have vectors for `lemma_POS` combinations from UD. How was this mapping performed? A near-perfect result under these circumstances is highly suspect and requires a thorough explanation that is currently absent.

2.  **Fundamentally Flawed Comparison Experiment:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger in Section 4 is invalid. The authors state they fed the tagger "separate tokens" rather than full sentences. Modern PoS taggers like Stanford's are sequence models that rely heavily on sentence context. Evaluating it in a context-free manner cripples its performance and produces an error profile that is not representative of its actual use or capabilities. This invalidates any conclusions drawn from the comparison in Tables 3 and 4.

3.  **Lack of Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** Several key methodological choices are presented without justification.
    *   The word2vec model was trained with "no down-sampling." This is a non-standard choice that heavily weights high-frequency function words. Its impact on the resulting vector space is not discussed.
    *   In Section 5, "unambiguous" lemmas for training are defined using an arbitrary frequency threshold ("not exceeding 1/2 of the dominant assignment frequency"). The sensitivity to this threshold is not explored.
    *   The training instances for the classifier were "weighted" by duplicating them according to corpus frequency. While the goal is understandable, this is a crude method compared to standard instance or class weighting in logistic regression, and its specific implementation is not detailed.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Professionalism:** The paper suffers from numerous presentation issues that undermine its credibility. The references section is completely malformed, with inconsistent formatting, stray characters (`?`, `\Q`, `\E`), and missing information, making it unusable. Figures are low quality, with unlabeled axes and difficult-to-read fonts. In-text citations are inconsistent (e.g., "(Biber et al., 1999)" vs. "(Houston, 1985)"). The claim in the title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is a significant overstatement of the work's contribution, which is more of an exploration than a redefinition. This overall sloppiness suggests a rushed submission and a lack of attention to detail that extends to the research methodology itself.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a detailed step-by-step explanation of the evaluation process in Section 3 that resulted in a 0.99 F-score on the UD Treebank? Specifically, how were words from the UD corpus, which were not in the `lemma_POS` format of your BNC training data, processed by the classifier?

2.  Please justify the decision to evaluate the Stanford PoS Tagger on isolated tokens. Given that it is a sequence model, how can this be considered a fair or meaningful comparison against your context-agnostic method?

3.  What was the rationale for training the word2vec model without down-sampling? How do you believe this impacted the embeddings for the open-class words that are the primary focus of your error analysis?
