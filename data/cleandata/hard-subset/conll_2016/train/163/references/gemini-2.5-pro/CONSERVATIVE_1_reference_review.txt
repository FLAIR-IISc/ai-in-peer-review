### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags for words from the British National Corpus (BNC). The primary focus is on analyzing the classifier's errors ("outliers") to identify words that exhibit distributional properties of a different PoS class than their gold annotation. A secondary experiment attempts to build a standalone, context-independent PoS tagger trained on embeddings.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A qualitative analysis of classification errors, which successfully identifies several well-known areas of linguistic ambiguity (e.g., the verb/adjective nature of participles) and potential inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation scheme (e.g., the classification of years and currency amounts).
2.  A demonstration that even a simple classifier trained on word embeddings can achieve moderately high accuracy in PoS prediction without access to local sentence context, confirming that embeddings robustly capture this type of information.

### Strengths

1.  **Interesting Qualitative Error Analysis:** The core strength of the paper lies in Section 4. The analysis of misclassified words provides a compelling, data-driven exploration of the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes. The examples provided (e.g., nouns like 'hypertext' behaving as adjectives, passive participles like 'overgrown' behaving as adjectives, and currency amounts like '£70' behaving as numerals) are intuitive and serve as good illustrations of known linguistic phenomena. This use of a simple classifier as a probe for linguistic analysis is a sound approach.
2.  **Focus on Annotation Artifacts:** The paper successfully uses its method to highlight potential inconsistencies and even systematic errors within the BNC tagset. The finding that words like 'plain' and 'clear' are frequently mistagged as adverbs is a valuable, concrete result that demonstrates the utility of this kind of distributional audit of a corpus.
3.  **Simple and Interpretable Model:** The use of a simple logistic regression classifier is appropriate for the goal of analysis. It avoids the black-box nature of more complex models and allows for a clearer link between the input features (embedding dimensions) and the classification outcome, which supports the linguistic investigation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Methodological Concerns with Embedding Training:** The primary experimental setup in Section 3 is unconventional and its motivation is unclear. The authors train embeddings on a corpus where tokens are concatenations of their lemma and PoS tag (e.g., `love_VERB`). This seems to bake the target label into the input representation for the embedding model itself. A more standard and methodologically sound approach would be to train embeddings on the raw lemmas and then use the gold PoS tags as labels for a separate classification task. The current approach risks circularity and makes it difficult to ascertain whether the model is learning about PoS from distributional context alone or from the explicit tag strings attached to the words.
2.  **Insufficient and Flawed Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to the Stanford PoS Tagger is not rigorous. The authors state they ran the tagger on "separate tokens, to mimic our workflow." This fundamentally cripples a state-of-the-art tagger, whose primary strength comes from sequential context. A meaningful comparison would involve running the tagger properly on full sentences and then analyzing its confusion matrix. As presented, the comparison does not fairly represent what an established method would find and serves only to make the authors' approach seem more unique than it may be. The claim that their method "reveals different error cases" is not well-supported without a fair comparison.
3.  **Overstated Claims and Low Performance of Standalone Tagger:** The title's claim of "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is a significant overstatement. The work does not propose a new, redefined set of classes; rather, it highlights ambiguities within an existing classification scheme. Furthermore, the performance of the standalone tagger in Section 5 (84% accuracy) is extremely low compared to established methods, which routinely exceed 97% accuracy on English. While the authors correctly note the absence of sentence context, presenting this result as "decent" or "impressive" is misleading. It primarily serves to show that context-free tagging from pre-trained embeddings is not a viable alternative to established sequential models. The suggestion that this could be used for resource-poor languages is speculative and unsupported by evidence.
4.  **Limited Novelty of Core Premise:** The fundamental observation that word embeddings capture syntactic and PoS information is well-established in the literature, as the authors themselves note by citing Mikolov et al. (2013b) and Tsvetkov et al. (2015). While the application to error analysis is interesting, many of the specific findings (e.g., noun/adjective and verb/adjective ambiguity) are well-documented linguistic facts. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly situated its findings with respect to prior work on computational analysis of PoS ambiguity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the rationale for training the initial word embedding model on `lemma_TAG` strings? How does this methodology ensure that the learned PoS information comes purely from distributional patterns, rather than from the explicit tags themselves being part of the vocabulary items? A more conventional approach would be to train on lemmas and use tags as external labels.
2.  The comparison to the Stanford PoS Tagger was conducted by providing it with isolated tokens, which negates its ability to use context. Could you justify this decision? A comparison against the error patterns of a properly utilized sequential tagger would be a much stronger baseline to demonstrate the unique insights provided by your distributional approach. Without it, the claim that your method uncovers different kinds of errors is difficult to evaluate.
