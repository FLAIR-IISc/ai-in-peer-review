### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which part-of-speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a classifier on word embeddings to predict PoS tags and then analyze the classification errors to identify words that exhibit distributional properties of a different class. They argue that this method can highlight linguistically ambiguous cases and uncover annotation inconsistencies in corpora like the BNC. A second experiment attempts to build a standalone PoS predictor using only word embeddings, without sentence context.

The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  An error analysis (Section 4) that uses a distributional model to identify specific, plausible instances of annotation inconsistencies or systematic errors within the British National Corpus (BNC). For example, the classification of currency amounts as nouns rather than numerals, and certain adjectives like 'plain' and 'clear' being erroneously tagged as adverbs.
2.  A demonstration that even a simple classifier trained on pre-trained word embeddings, without access to sequential context, can achieve a non-trivial level of accuracy in PoS prediction.

### Strengths

1.  **Method as an Analytical Tool:** The central idea of using a model's "failures" not as a flaw of the model but as a lens to inspect the underlying data is compelling. Rather than aiming for state-of-the-art performance, the authors use their classifier to probe the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes, which is a valid and interesting line of inquiry.
2.  **Concrete Findings on Corpus Annotation:** The analysis in Section 4 provides specific and convincing examples of potential inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation scheme. The discussion around currency/percentages, years/decades, and passive participles highlights well-known linguistic gray areas. The identification of potentially systematic errors (e.g., 'plain' tagged as ADV) is a particularly strong and useful outcome, demonstrating the utility of this approach for corpus quality control.
3.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The experiments are described logically, and the results of the error analysis are presented with clear examples that support the authors' interpretations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Fundamentally Flawed Comparison to Established Methods:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is methodologically unsound and invalidates the conclusions drawn from it. The authors state they fed the tagger "separate tokens" rather than whole sentences. State-of-the-art taggers are sequence models; their primary strength comes from leveraging contextual information. By removing the context, the authors are not evaluating the tagger under its designed operating conditions but rather in a crippled, unigram-like mode. Consequently, the resulting confusion matrix (Table 3) is not representative of the tagger's behavior, and the claim that the distributional predictor "reveals different error cases" is built on an unfair and misleading comparison.
2.  **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation for the PoS Predictor:** The experiment in Section 5, which presents a context-agnostic PoS predictor, lacks a crucial baseline. The reported accuracy of 0.84 is difficult to interpret without comparison to a simple unigram baseline (i.e., assigning each word its most frequent tag from the training data). It is plausible that such a simple heuristic would achieve a comparable score, which would diminish the claimed impressiveness of the result. Without this baseline, the performance of the embedding-based classifier is not properly contextualized.
3.  **Overstated Novelty and Claims:** The title's claim of "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is a significant overstatement. The paper does not propose a new, coherent set of PoS classes. Instead, it observes well-established linguistic phenomena (e.g., the adjective-like nature of participles, the similarity of nouns and proper nouns) through the lens of distributional models. Furthermore, the core finding that embeddings capture syntactic information is not new, as the authors acknowledge in their related work. The contribution is an application of this known property, not a fundamental redefinition or discovery.
4.  **Unconventional and Potentially Confounding Initial Setup:** The methodology in Section 3 involves training embeddings on `lemma_POS` concatenated strings. This is a highly unusual approach. A more standard and interpretable method would be to train embeddings on the raw text (lemmas only) and then use the associated PoS tags as labels for a classifier. The chosen method bakes the "gold" label into the input representation, which may artificially inflate the separability of the classes and makes it difficult to ascertain whether the learned representations are truly capturing inherent distributional properties or simply memorizing patterns associated with the appended tags.
