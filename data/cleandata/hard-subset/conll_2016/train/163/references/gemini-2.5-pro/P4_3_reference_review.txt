**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the degree to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors first train a word embedding model on the British National Corpus (BNC), where each word type is a concatenation of its lemma and PoS tag (e.g., `love_VERB`). They then train a logistic regression classifier on these embeddings to predict the PoS tag. The primary focus is a detailed qualitative analysis of the classifier's misclassifications, which are treated as "outliers." These outliers are used to identify words with ambiguous PoS characteristics that lie at the boundaries of traditional classes, and to uncover potential inconsistencies or systematic errors in the BNC's automatic annotation. The error patterns are contrasted with those from a standard PoS tagger to demonstrate the unique perspective offered by the distributional approach. A second experiment shows that a context-agnostic PoS tagger, trained on embeddings of lemmas without PoS tags, can achieve non-trivial accuracy using a small set of prototypical words for training.

**Main Contributions**

1.  A systematic methodology for using a simple classifier on word embeddings to identify and analyze "outlier" words. The strength of the paper lies in its detailed qualitative error analysis, which provides concrete linguistic insights into fuzzy PoS class boundaries (e.g., noun/adjective, verb/adjective) and highlights potential annotation inconsistencies in a large corpus (e.g., treatment of years, currencies).
2.  A demonstration that a simple, context-agnostic PoS predictor trained on a small seed set of unambiguous words can achieve reasonable performance. This underscores how robustly PoS-related distributional patterns are captured in static word embeddings, even without access to local sentence context.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Methodology and Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The paper's core strength is the well-executed analysis in Section 4. The methodology of training a classifier and inspecting its failures is a clear and effective way to probe the structure of the embedding space. The authors provide numerous concrete examples (e.g., currency amounts like `£70` being classified as numerals instead of nouns, passive participles like `overgrown` being classified as adjectives) that are linguistically plausible and effectively illustrate the fuzzy nature of PoS boundaries. This analysis serves as a valuable case study for using embeddings as a tool for linguistic and corpus exploration.
2.  **Effective Comparison to a Standard Tagger:** The comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger is a valuable addition. By showing that the two methods produce different confusion matrices, the authors successfully argue that their distributional approach offers a complementary perspective. It reveals different kinds of ambiguities—those based on global distributional patterns rather than local contextual cues—and strengthens the claim that this method is a useful analytical tool rather than just a poor-performing tagger.
3.  **Technically Sound and Reproducible Experiments:** The experimental setup is straightforward and well-described. The use of separate training and test sets based on word frequency is a sensible design choice. The decisions made, such as weighting training instances by corpus frequency to handle class imbalance, are well-justified. The analysis of feature importance (Section 5) provides additional evidence for the distributed nature of syntactic information in embeddings, which is a known but well-demonstrated finding.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Confounding Effect of `lemma_PoS` Input Representation:** The main experiment (Sections 3-4) is based on a model trained on `lemma_PoS` strings. This means that embeddings for `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN` are learned as entirely separate items. While this is effective for showing that words with the *same tag* cluster distributionally, it complicates the interpretation of the outlier analysis. The classifier is not predicting the PoS for a lemma; it is confirming or denying the PoS for a pre-tagged lemma. An error on `materialist_NOUN` (predicted as ADJ) does not necessarily mean the lemma `materialist` is distributionally more adjectival overall, but rather that the specific contexts where it was tagged as a noun in the BNC are adjectival. This circularity limits the scope of the claims about "redefining part-of-speech classes" and should be more clearly acknowledged and discussed.
2.  **Overstated Novelty and Claims:** The central finding that word embeddings capture PoS information is well-established in prior work, which the paper cites. The primary novelty is the application of this fact to error analysis. However, the paper's framing, including the title "Redefining part-of-speech classes," suggests a more foundational contribution than what is delivered. The analysis identifies well-known areas of linguistic ambiguity and potential corpus artifacts rather than proposing a new, data-driven PoS scheme. Tempering these claims would strengthen the paper by aligning them more closely with the presented evidence.
3.  **Limited Evaluation of the Context-Free Tagger:** The experiment in Section 5 presents a context-free tagger with 0.84 accuracy. While interesting, this result is difficult to interpret without a proper baseline. A crucial baseline would be a simple unigram model that assigns the most frequent tag for each lemma from the training data. Without this, it is unclear how much of the performance is attributable to the rich information in the embeddings versus simple frequency-based heuristics. Furthermore, the criteria for selecting "unambiguous" lemmas for training are not formally defined ("frequencies of other PoS assignments not exceeding 1/2 of the dominant assignment frequency"), which affects reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the implications of training your primary model on `lemma_PoS` strings? Does this approach prevent you from analyzing ambiguity for a truly polysemous lemma (e.g., `show`), since `show_NOUN` and `show_VERB` are treated as unrelated types during embedding training?
2.  For the context-free tagger in Section 5, what is the accuracy of a majority-class baseline that assigns the most frequent PoS tag observed for each lemma in the training data? This would help contextualize the 0.84 accuracy achieved by the classifier.
3.  In your comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger, you state you processed "separate tokens, to mimic our workflow". Does this mean the tagger was run on single words without any sentence context? As standard taggers are heavily reliant on context, this would be an unconventional usage. Please clarify the exact input provided to the Stanford Tagger for this comparison experiment.
