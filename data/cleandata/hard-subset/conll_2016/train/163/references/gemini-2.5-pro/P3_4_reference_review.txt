### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors train a Skip-gram model on the BNC corpus, where each word token is represented as a `lemma_PoS` string. They then train a logistic regression classifier to predict the PoS tag from a word's embedding. The central analysis focuses on the classifier's errors, interpreting them as "outliers" that reveal either linguistic ambiguities at class boundaries (e.g., participles being verb-like and adjective-like) or inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation scheme. A secondary experiment builds a standalone PoS predictor from embeddings trained on lemmas alone, demonstrating that even without sentence context, embeddings can achieve reasonable PoS tagging accuracy.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A detailed, qualitative analysis of PoS class ambiguities using distributional models.** The core strength of the paper lies in Section 4, where the authors use misclassifications not as a model failure, but as a lens for linguistic inquiry. They provide concrete, data-driven evidence for well-known linguistic phenomena (e.g., the noun-adjective continuum) and uncover potential systematic inconsistencies in a major corpus (e.g., the tagging of years, currency, and certain adverbs in the BNC).
2.  **A demonstration of a simple yet effective methodology for corpus analysis.** The paper showcases a straightforward framework (embeddings + simple classifier) for identifying words and word classes that deviate from prototypical distributional patterns. This serves as a useful case study for using computational tools for linguistic discovery and corpus quality control.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Linguistic Motivation and Insightful Analysis:** The paper is grounded in a classic linguistic problem—the continuous nature of grammatical categories—and successfully uses modern computational methods to explore it. The error analysis in Section 4 is excellent, moving beyond simple error counts to provide compelling linguistic interpretations for specific misclassification patterns (PROPN↔NOUN, NOUN↔ADJ, VERB→ADJ for participles, NUM↔NOUN for years/currency). This is precisely the kind of analysis that benefits the computational linguistics community.
2.  **Effective Use of a Comparison Baseline:** The comparison of their distributional classifier's confusion matrix with that of the Stanford PoS Tagger is a valuable addition. It effectively demonstrates that their method uncovers a *different set* of boundary cases and potential errors. While the Stanford tagger highlights common confusions like NNP→NN, the authors' method identifies more subtle distributional issues, like SCONJ→ADV, which strengthens their claim that this is a complementary tool for linguistic analysis.
3.  **Methodological Simplicity and Clarity:** The approach is simple, transparent, and easy to reproduce. The authors use standard, well-understood components (Skip-gram, logistic regression) on a widely available corpus (BNC). This clarity allows the focus to remain squarely on the linguistic insights derived from the model's behavior, rather than on complex model architecture. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflation of Input Representation and Target Label:** The primary methodological choice in Section 3 is highly problematic. The authors train their embedding model on a corpus where tokens are `lemma_PoS` concatenations (e.g., `love_VERB`, `love_NOUN`). This means the ground-truth PoS tag is *part of the input* to the embedding model. Consequently, the model is not learning the PoS of "love" from its context alone; it is learning separate representations for `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN` based on their respective contexts. This approach guarantees that words with different PoS tags get different vectors, but it conflates the information source. The central claim is that distributional vectors reveal PoS affiliation, but here, the PoS affiliation is pre-encoded into the vocabulary. A much cleaner experiment would be to train embeddings on lemmas only and then use the BNC tags as external labels for training the classifier. The experiment in Section 5 does this, but the main analysis in Sections 3 and 4 is based on the flawed `lemma_PoS` setup.
2.  **Unconventional Treatment of Class Imbalance:** In Section 3, the authors address class imbalance by repeating training instances for the classifier proportional to their corpus frequency. While the motivation is clear (to give closed-class words more weight), this is a non-standard approach. It would be more conventional and principled to use standard techniques like `class_weight` parameters in the logistic regression model or stratified sampling. The current method makes frequent words "more influential," but this might just be amplifying the distributional patterns of a few high-frequency words rather than learning a general pattern for the entire class. This choice requires stronger justification.
3.  **Overstated Claims and Limited Scope:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is too strong for the scope of the work presented. The paper provides interesting evidence for fuzzy boundaries and annotation inconsistencies in one corpus for one language (English). It does not propose a new, coherent tagset or a formal method for redefining classes. The findings are analytical and diagnostic, which is valuable, but "redefining" implies a more constructive, theoretical contribution that isn't delivered.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the rationale for using `lemma_PoS` as the input vocabulary for the main experiment in Sections 3-4? This seems to bake the answer (the PoS tag) into the question (can embeddings distinguish PoS?). Did you perform a control experiment where embeddings were trained on lemmas alone, and if so, did the resulting error analysis lead to similar or different linguistic insights?
2.  Regarding the frequency-weighting scheme for the classifier: can you justify this choice over more standard methods for handling class imbalance, such as using the `class_weight='balanced'` option in scikit-learn's logistic regression or other sampling techniques? How sensitive are your results and the resulting error analysis to this specific choice?
3.  The visualization of "prototypical" PoS vectors by averaging all word vectors in a class can be misleading if the class distribution is not unimodal and convex. For instance, the class VERB contains transitive, intransitive, and auxiliary verbs, which may have very different distributional patterns. Have you considered using dimensionality reduction techniques like t-SNE or UMAP to visualize the entire distribution of words within a PoS class to see if they form single cohesive clusters or multiple sub-clusters? This might reveal more nuanced intra-class structures than a simple average.
