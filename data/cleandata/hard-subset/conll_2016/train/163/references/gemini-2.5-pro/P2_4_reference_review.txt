### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in static word embeddings. The authors train a logistic regression classifier on Skip-gram vectors to predict a word's PoS tag. The core of the paper is a detailed qualitative analysis of the classifier's errors, which the authors argue highlight linguistically interesting "outliers" that lie on the fuzzy boundaries between traditional PoS classes. They use this methodology to identify subclasses of words (e.g., passive participles that behave like adjectives) and to uncover potential systematic annotation inconsistencies in the British National Corpus (BNC). A second experiment demonstrates that a context-unaware PoS predictor, trained on a small set of unambiguous words, can achieve reasonable accuracy, reinforcing the finding that embeddings are rich with grammatical information.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A methodology for data-driven linguistic analysis and corpus error detection.** The primary contribution is not a new model, but a novel application of a simple model. By systematically analyzing the misclassifications of an embedding-based classifier, the paper provides an effective method for identifying words and word classes that challenge traditional PoS boundaries and for spotting potential annotation errors in gold-standard corpora.
2.  **An insightful qualitative analysis of PoS class boundaries in English.** The paper moves beyond quantitative results to offer compelling linguistic interpretations of its model's failures. The specific examples provided for noun/adjective, verb/adjective, and noun/numeral confusions serve as valuable, data-backed evidence for the "continuum" of grammatical categories.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful and Detailed Qualitative Analysis:** The paper's main strength is the thorough error analysis in Section 4. Instead of treating misclassifications as mere failures, the authors use them as a lens for linguistic inquiry. The discussion of why nouns like 'hypertext' are confused with adjectives, why passive participles are confused with adjectives, and why date/currency tokens blur the line between noun and numeral is compelling. This approach successfully leverages a computational model as an exploratory tool for linguistics.
2.  **Effective Demonstration of Corpus Error Detection:** The work makes a strong case for using this method to find annotation inconsistencies. The discovery that adjectives like 'plain' and 'sharp' are systematically mistagged as adverbs in the BNC is a concrete and valuable finding. Furthermore, the comparison with the Stanford PoS Tagger's error patterns convincingly shows that the distributional approach uncovers a different, and arguably more nuanced, set of issues than a standard sequential tagger would.
3.  **Clear and Sound Experimental Design:** The methodology is well-motivated and clearly explained. The two-stage approach—first analyzing `lemma_POS` vectors to find outliers, then building a predictor from standard `lemma` vectors—is logical. Technical decisions, such as weighting training instances by corpus frequency to handle class imbalance, are well-justified and demonstrate a careful approach. The validation on multiple datasets (BNC and UD Treebank) adds credibility to the findings.
4.  **High Relevance to the Field:** The paper addresses fundamental questions about the nature of grammatical categories and their representation in computational models, which is highly relevant to the CoNLL community. The findings have potential implications for the design of annotation schemes, the development of more robust PoS taggers, and the interpretation of what linguistic properties are captured by word embeddings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overstated Claims of "Redefining" PoS Classes:** The title "Redefining part-of-speech classes" is an overstatement. The paper does an excellent job of identifying problematic cases and highlighting fuzzy boundaries, but it does not propose a new, coherent PoS scheme or a concrete framework for redefinition. The analysis is exploratory and identifies problems with existing schemes, rather than defining a new one. A more measured title like "Exploring Part-of-Speech Boundaries..." would more accurately reflect the contribution.
2.  **Reliance on a Single Distributional Model:** All conclusions are derived from one specific model: Continuous Skip-gram trained on the BNC with a fixed set of hyperparameters. It is unclear how generalizable these findings are. Would a different model (e.g., GloVe, FastText) or a larger, more diverse training corpus identify the same "outliers"? The paper's claims would be substantially stronger if it demonstrated that these boundary cases are model-agnostic phenomena rather than potential artifacts of a specific training setup.
3.  **Potential for Circularity in the Primary Experiment:** The main analysis in Section 3 is based on a model trained on `lemma_POS` strings. This means the model learns distinct vectors for `love_VERB` and `love_NOUN`. The classifier is then trained to recover the PoS from these vectors. The model is essentially learning to associate the `_VERB` suffix with its co-occurring contexts, making the high classification accuracy less surprising. While this setup is clever for identifying outliers where the lemma's context contradicts the tag's expected context, the authors should more explicitly address this potential circularity and justify why this artificial setup is a valid tool for their analysis.
4.  **Limited Novelty of the Predictive Model:** While the analysis is novel, the finding that embeddings contain PoS information is well-established, as noted by the authors. The PoS predictor built in Section 5 achieves 0.84 accuracy, which, while impressive for a context-free model, is far from the state-of-the-art and has limited practical application. The paper's framing could more clearly position this experiment as a proof-of-concept to quantify the strength of the PoS signal in embeddings, rather than presenting it as a viable tagging method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The error analysis in Section 4 attributes some misclassifications of proper nouns (e.g., 'luftwaffe') to a lack of sufficient contexts. However, the classifier achieves a very high F-score of 0.99 on the UD English Treebank. Could this high performance be because the UD vocabulary is better represented in your BNC-trained model (i.e., contains more frequent words) compared to your BNC test set? Could you provide a breakdown of vocabulary overlap between the UD test set and your model's vocabulary?
2.  Your analysis is based entirely on static Skip-gram embeddings. How do you think your findings would change with contextualized embeddings (e.g., from BERT)? For example, would a contextual model still flag 'plain' as an outlier in the phrase "makes it plain that..." if it were tagged as an adverb, given that the model has access to the immediate syntactic structure?
3.  The predictor in Section 5 is trained on only 1,564 "unambiguous" lemmas. Could you elaborate on the precise criteria used to define "unambiguous" (e.g., what was the frequency ratio threshold for the dominant tag)? How sensitive is the 0.84 accuracy figure to this choice of a small, "clean" training set?
