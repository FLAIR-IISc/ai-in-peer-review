### Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates the extent to which Part-of-Speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. The authors propose a methodology to identify "outlier" words—those whose distributional context makes them behave more like a different PoS class than their annotated one. They train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings derived from the BNC, where words are represented as `lemma_POS` tokens. Errors made by this classifier are then analyzed to reveal linguistic ambiguities, fuzzy class boundaries (e.g., between nouns and adjectives), and potential annotation inconsistencies or errors in the BNC. The analysis is supported by a comparison with errors from a standard PoS tagger. A second experiment demonstrates that a context-free PoS tagger can be trained on a small set of unambiguous words, achieving a respectable accuracy of 84% on the UD English Treebank, suggesting that embeddings alone carry a strong PoS signal.

### Contributions
1.  **A Novel Method for Linguistic Analysis:** The primary contribution is a novel, data-driven methodology for exploring the fuzzy boundaries of PoS classes. By training a classifier on embeddings of `lemma_POS` tokens, the authors create a tool to systematically identify words whose distributional properties conflict with their canonical PoS tag. This provides a quantitative framework for investigating classic linguistic questions about grammatical categories.
2.  **Targeted Analysis of Corpus Annotation:** The paper presents a compelling analysis of the classifier's errors, using them to highlight specific, well-motivated cases of linguistic ambiguity (e.g., participles as adjectives) and potential inconsistencies in the BNC's annotation scheme (e.g., the treatment of years, currency, and certain adverbs). This demonstrates the utility of the method as a tool for corpus quality control and annotation refinement.
3.  **Demonstration of Context-Free PoS Prediction:** The paper provides a clear demonstration of the strength of the PoS signal within word embeddings by building a purely lexical (context-free) PoS tagger. While not state-of-the-art, its 84% accuracy, achieved with a small seed lexicon, is impressive and highlights the potential of this approach for low-resource scenarios.

### Strengths
1.  **Clever and Well-Motivated Methodology:** The core idea of using classification errors on `lemma_POS` embeddings to find "outliers" is insightful. It cleverly turns a standard machine learning setup into an exploratory tool for linguistic analysis. This approach is more directed and interpretable than simple clustering, which the authors note was unsuccessful.
2.  **Strong and Insightful Error Analysis:** The paper's main strength lies in Section 4. The authors don't just report errors; they delve into them and connect the findings to established linguistic knowledge. The examples provided (e.g., 'hypertext_NOUN' being adjectival, 'overgrown_VERB' behaving like an adjective, currency amounts like numerals) are convincing and effectively illustrate the "continuum" of grammatical categories mentioned in the introduction. The claim of finding systematic BNC tagging errors for words like 'plain' and 'clear' is a significant finding.
3.  **Effective Comparison and Baselines:** The comparison with the error patterns of the Stanford PoS Tagger is highly effective. It successfully shows that the proposed distributional method uncovers a different set of boundary cases and potential errors, justifying it as a complementary analytical tool rather than a redundant one. The inclusion of majority-class and single-feature baselines in Section 3 also properly contextualizes the classifier's high performance.
4.  **Clear Presentation and Structure:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the experiments are described logically, and the analysis flows directly from the results. The progression from the primary analytical experiment to the secondary PoS prediction experiment is sensible and strengthens the overall argument.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Potential Circularity in the Main Experiment:** The methodology in Section 3, while clever, has a potentially circular aspect that is not fully addressed. The embeddings are trained on a corpus where PoS tags are already part of the tokens (`lemma_POS`). This means the context for a word like `love_VERB` consists of other tokens that also have explicit PoS tags (e.g., `a_DET`, `beautiful_ADJ`). This likely amplifies the PoS signal in the embeddings in a way that wouldn't happen with a standard, untagged corpus. While this serves the goal of finding outliers within the BNC's own system, it makes it harder to claim that the findings are about general distributional properties independent of the BNC's specific annotations. The paper would be stronger if it discussed this potential confound.
2.  **Overstated Title and Claims:** The title, "Redefining part-of-speech classes," is an overstatement of the paper's contribution. The work provides evidence for the fluidity of PoS boundaries and suggests refinements to annotation, but it does not propose or formalize a new, "redefined" set of classes. Similarly, claims about finding "systematic errors" in the BNC are strong and would require more rigorous proof, such as consulting the original CLAWS4 annotation manual to see if these cases represent deliberate choices rather than mistakes. A more cautious framing would be more appropriate.
3.  **Lack of Detail in the PoS Tagger Experiment:** The second experiment in Section 5 is interesting but lacks a crucial baseline. An accuracy of 0.84 for a context-free tagger is presented as impressive, but it's unclear how this compares to a simple unigram baseline (i.e., for each lemma, assign its most frequent tag from the training data). Given that many words are unambiguous, such a baseline could be quite high, and is necessary to properly evaluate the added value of the distributional vectors.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to train embeddings on `lemma_POS` tokens? How does this setup distinguish between a lemma's inherent distributional ambiguity versus artifacts introduced by having explicit PoS tags in the training context windows? Have you considered an alternative setup where embeddings are trained on untagged text, and the classifier is trained to predict the BNC tag for each lemma?
2.  In Section 4, you identify what appear to be systematic errors in the BNC (e.g., adjectives like 'plain' tagged as adverbs in constructions like "makes it plain that..."). Have you consulted the BNC or CLAWS tagger documentation to confirm whether these are indeed errors, or if they reflect a specific linguistic theory or annotation guideline where these words are intentionally tagged as adverbs in these syntactic frames?
3.  For the context-free PoS tagger in Section 5, could you provide the performance of a majority-class unigram baseline (assigning each lemma its most frequent tag from the training set)? This would help contextualize the 84% accuracy and clarify how much of the performance is attributable to the rich information in the embeddings versus the simple lexical distribution of PoS tags.
