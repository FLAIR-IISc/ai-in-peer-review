## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:**

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize and demonstrate that word embeddings, trained on a large corpus, encode information about PoS classes, allowing for accurate PoS prediction. They train a classifier on these embeddings, analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic "outliers" and boundary cases, and finally, attempt to build a PoS tagger directly from the embeddings. The analysis of misclassifications reveals insights into annotation inconsistencies and subtle linguistic phenomena.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper provides compelling evidence that unsupervised distributional models capture significant information about traditional PoS categories, achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and syntactic structure.
2. **Error analysis as a diagnostic tool for annotation quality:** The analysis of misclassified words reveals potential inconsistencies and areas of ambiguity in the BNC corpus annotations. This demonstrates a valuable application of distributional models – identifying areas requiring linguistic re-evaluation.
3. **Exploration of PoS boundaries and linguistic nuances:** The identification of “outlier” words highlights cases where traditional PoS boundaries are blurry, providing linguistic insights into phenomena like passive participles functioning as adjectives or numerals incorporating nominative elements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently high accuracy achieved by the PoS classifier (F-score of 0.979 on training data, 0.91 on unseen data, 0.99 on UD Treebank) strongly supports the core hypothesis. This validates the utility of distributional semantics for syntactic analysis.
2. **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors use a well-established corpus (BNC), a standard embedding technique (Skipgram), and a clear experimental setup. The consideration of word frequency during training is particularly commendable, addressing a potential bias.  The 10-fold cross-validation strengthens the robustness of their findings.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. It goes beyond simply reporting accuracy and delves into *why* the model fails, providing valuable linguistic insights and highlighting potential annotation issues.  The comparison to errors from the Stanford tagger adds further weight.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, logically flows from introduction to analysis, and is generally easy to follow. The inclusion of examples (e.g., Figures 1 & 2) aids comprehension.
5. **Demonstration of a Self-Contained System:**  Successfully training a PoS tagger *without* initial tag information (using only distributional vectors) is a significant achievement, showcasing the potential of this approach for resource-poor languages.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** While the application to PoS analysis is well-executed, the core methodology (using Skipgram embeddings and a logistic regression classifier) is not novel. The main contribution lies in the *application* and *analysis* rather than a breakthrough in techniques.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Tagging Systems:** The paper mentions the performance of the Stanford tagger in the context of error analysis, but doesn't provide a direct comparison of the distributional model-based tagger to current state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., those based on BiLSTMs or Transformers). This makes it difficult to assess the practical competitiveness of the proposed approach.
3. **Superficial Discussion of Vector Components:** The authors mention that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, but the analysis remains relatively superficial.  A more in-depth investigation into *which* components are most informative for specific PoS tags could be highly valuable. The mention of component 94 being specific to conjunctions feels isolated.
4. **Corpus Specificity:** The results are heavily reliant on the characteristics of the BNC. While the UD Treebank testing provides some generalization, further evaluation on other corpora with different annotation schemes and linguistic features would strengthen the findings.
5. **Ambiguity in "Outlier" Definition:** The definition of “outlier” (words misclassified by the model) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled approach to defining outliers, perhaps based on confidence scores or distances in the embedding space, could be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a direct comparison of your distributional model-based tagger’s performance (precision, recall, F-score) against a state-of-the-art PoS tagger (e.g., a Transformer-based model) on the UD Treebank or another commonly used benchmark dataset?
2.  Could you elaborate on your analysis of the embedding components? Are there specific dimensions that consistently correlate with particular PoS tags, beyond the example of component 94?  Perhaps a visualization of these relationships would be insightful.
3.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling)?  Did you experiment with different configurations?
4.  You mention potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC. Could you provide specific examples of these inconsistencies, and how they manifest in the misclassified words?
5.  Would the addition of morphological features (e.g., prefixes, suffixes) to the input of the classifier improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clear, but some sentences are slightly verbose.
*   The links to the data and code (mappings between BNC and Universal tags) are essential for reproducibility and should be prominently displayed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between distributional semantics and syntactic structure. While the methodology is not groundbreaking, the thorough empirical analysis and the insightful discussion of linguistic nuances are significant strengths.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art taggers and a more detailed investigation of embedding components, would further strengthen the paper. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**
