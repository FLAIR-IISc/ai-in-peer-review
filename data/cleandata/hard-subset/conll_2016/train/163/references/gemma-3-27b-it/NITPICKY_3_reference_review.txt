## Review

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantics and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, proposing to leverage learned word embeddings to refine and potentially redefine traditional PoS categories. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC and use a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings, focusing on misclassifications as indicative of linguistic interesting boundary cases. They further explore the representation of PoS through averaging embeddings and assess the impact of different feature subsets on classification performance.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides a solid empirical demonstration that distributional semantic models *do* encode significant information about PoS, achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction solely from word embeddings. This reinforces the connection between distributional and linguistic representations.
2. **Identification of Annotation Inconsistencies/Boundary Cases:** The analysis of misclassified words offers a valuable tool for identifying potential inconsistencies in the BNC’s PoS annotation and highlighting linguistic instances where traditional categorization is ambiguous or debatable (e.g., currency amounts, passive participles).
3. **Preliminary Exploration of Feature Distribution:** The investigation of which embedding dimensions contribute most to PoS prediction, while preliminary, provides a starting point for understanding how syntactic/grammatical information is encoded in vector space.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a well-established corpus (BNC), a standard embedding model (skip-gram), and a clear classification pipeline. The weighting of training instances by corpus frequency addresses a crucial issue of class imbalance, and the use of 10-fold cross-validation provides robust performance estimates.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. Going beyond simply reporting accuracy, the authors categorize and interpret the errors, drawing insightful conclusions about PoS boundaries and annotation quality. The comparison with errors identified by the Stanford tagger strengthens these findings.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in sufficient detail, and the results are presented in a concise and accessible manner.
4. **Relevance to the Community:** The findings have implications for both NLP researchers working on distributional semantics and computational linguistics specialists interested in PoS tagging and the nature of grammatical categories.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** The core method – training embeddings and using them for classification – is not novel. While the application to PoS refinement is interesting, the paper lacks a genuinely new technical contribution. The authors acknowledge this to some extent.
2. **Superficial Dimensionality Analysis:** While the attempt to identify important embedding dimensions is commendable, the analysis is limited. Simply ranking dimensions by ANOVA F-value doesn’t provide a deep understanding of *what* linguistic features those dimensions represent. The conclusion that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions is not particularly surprising or insightful. The visualisations in section 3, while present, are not sufficiently analysed.
3. **Over-reliance on BNC:** The study is heavily reliant on the BNC, which, as the authors themselves acknowledge through the error analysis, has known annotation inconsistencies. While the authors attempt to mitigate this, the results remain potentially biased. The use of UD Treebank provides some external validation, but the coverage is limited by vocabulary overlap.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Tagging:** The performance of the classifier (93.8% accuracy on the training set, 84% on unseen data) is reported but not compared to the performance of established PoS taggers on the same data. This makes it difficult to assess the actual value of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the description of the taggers used for comparison is incomplete; the Stanford Tagger performance is presented without information about settings and tokenization.
5. **Minor Formatting Issues:** There are a few minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistencies in citation style, Figures 1 and 2 lack clear axis labels). These are not major, but reflect a lack of meticulous attention to detail.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the top-ranked embedding dimensions in Section 5, attempting to interpret the linguistic features they capture?
2.  How do the results change when using a more recent or sophisticated embedding model (e.g., BERT, GloVe)?
3.  Can you provide a comparison of your classifier’s performance to state-of-the-art PoS taggers on the BNC or UD Treebank?
4.  You mention inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Do you have any hypothesis for *why* these inconsistencies exist? Are certain taggers more robust to these issues?
5.  You state that the classifier "knows absolutely nothing about a word’s context in the current sentence." While true, is there a potential for integrating contextual information (e.g., using a biLSTM on top of the embeddings) to further improve performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of distributional semantics for capturing all aspects of PoS information (e.g., subtle semantic distinctions, complex syntactic constructions).
*   The annotation of Figures 1 and 2 should be made clearer, with labels indicating the axes and units.
*   The citation of “Aarts et al., 2008” is incomplete and needs proper referencing.



Overall, this paper presents a well-executed and insightful exploration of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the method lacks substantial novelty, the thorough error analysis and the demonstration of PoS information in embeddings contribute valuable knowledge to the field. However, the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art taggers and the somewhat superficial dimensionality analysis weaken the overall impact. **I lean towards rejection**, primarily due to the limited novelty and the absence of a compelling demonstration that this approach offers a significant advantage over existing methods. The meticulousness demanded by a high-quality conll paper is not entirely met.
