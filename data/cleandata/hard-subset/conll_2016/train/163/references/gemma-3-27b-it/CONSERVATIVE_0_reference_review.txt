The paper explores the relationship between distributional semantics and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, investigating whether word embeddings can reveal insights into PoS boundaries and potentially improve tagging accuracy. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC corpus and use it to classify words based on their PoS tags. They identify “outlier” words—those misclassified by their model—as potentially revealing interesting linguistic phenomena and inconsistencies in existing annotations. They also demonstrate the feasibility of training a PoS classifier directly from distributional vectors, without relying on contextual information.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration – though not a particularly surprising one – that distributional semantic models *do* encode information about lexical category. This is largely a confirmation of existing literature, albeit with a more detailed error analysis. A secondary, more interesting contribution is the identification of potential annotation errors in the BNC, highlighted by discrepancies between the model’s predictions and the corpus’s existing tags. Finally, the demonstration that a reasonably accurate PoS classifier can be trained *solely* from distributional vectors, excluding contextual information, is a noteworthy, though incremental, advance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors employ a well-established methodology, using a standard distributional semantic model (skip-gram) and a common corpus (BNC). The inclusion of a baseline (logistic regression) and comparison to the Stanford tagger adds credibility to their findings. The weighting of training instances by corpus frequency is a sensible approach to address class imbalance.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The focus on misclassified words (“outliers”) is a valuable aspect of the work. The authors don’t simply report accuracy scores; they delve into *why* the model makes errors, offering potential explanations based on linguistic considerations. This error analysis is the strongest part of the paper.
3. **Confirmation of Existing Knowledge:** While not groundbreaking, the paper provides further evidence supporting the well-established connection between distributional semantics and syntactic categories. It reinforces the idea that semantic representations capture information about word usage and grammatical roles.
4. **Demonstration of Context-Free Prediction:** Showing that a usable PoS tagger can be built *without* contextual information is intriguing, highlighting the information implicitly captured in the distributional representations. The accuracy achieved (84%) is respectable given the constraint.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core finding—that word embeddings contain PoS information—is not novel. This has been demonstrated repeatedly in prior work (adequately cited). The paper largely confirms existing understanding rather than presenting a significant breakthrough. The improvements over baseline are incremental.
2. **Limited Scope of Innovation:** The proposal to detect annotation errors, while potentially useful, isn’t thoroughly explored. The analysis of the identified errors is somewhat superficial. A more systematic comparison to manually verified annotations would be needed to strengthen this claim.
3. **Overreliance on a Single Corpus:** The entire evaluation is performed on the BNC. The generalizability of the findings to other corpora and languages is uncertain. Testing on more diverse datasets is crucial. The brief evaluation on the UD Treebank doesn’t fully address this concern.
4. **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Tagging:** The comparison to the Stanford tagger is useful, but a more comprehensive comparison to modern, state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., those based on BERT or other transformer models) would provide a more meaningful benchmark. It's unclear how the distributional approach compares to current best practices.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for applying this approach to resource-poor languages. Could you elaborate on the practical challenges of creating a seed lexicon of “unambiguous” words in such scenarios?
2.  The error analysis highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you considered reporting these findings to the BNC maintainers? What steps could be taken to improve the corpus’s annotation quality?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the selection criteria for the 10,000 most frequent words used to train the initial classifier? Were there any considerations given to the distribution of PoS tags within this subset?
4. How sensitive are the results to the embedding size (300) and other hyperparameters of the skip-gram model (window size, negative sampling)? Did you perform any hyperparameter tuning?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 and 2 are not particularly informative. They show average vectors, but don’t offer a clear visual demonstration of what differentiates conjunctions.
*   The discussion of vector components (Section 5) is interesting, but lacks depth. A more detailed analysis of the semantic content of specific components would be valuable.
* The phrase “we plan to extend it” in the conclusion is rather vague and doesn't specify concrete next steps.
