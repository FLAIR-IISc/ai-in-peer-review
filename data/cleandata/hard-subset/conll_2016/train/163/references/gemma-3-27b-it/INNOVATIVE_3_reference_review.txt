## Review

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantics and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, with the intriguing hypothesis that unsupervised distributional models implicitly encode information about grammatical categories, potentially even revealing inconsistencies or ambiguities in existing annotation schemes. The authors train a skipgram model on the BNC, use it to predict PoS tags, and analyze the resulting errors to identify linguistic “outliers.”

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper convincingly shows that readily available, off-the-shelf distributional semantic models *do* contain information relevant to PoS, achieving strong classification performance without relying on explicit syntactic features. This isn't entirely novel, but the consistent performance across datasets is a valuable affirmation.
2. **Error analysis as a linguistic probe:** The core contribution lies in using errors made by the PoS predictor – the “outliers” – as a way to highlight potential issues or nuances in existing PoS annotation. The identification of currency amounts misclassified as numerals, and the surfacing of inconsistent adverb/adjective tagging, is a genuinely insightful application of the method.
3. **Feasibility of tag prediction without explicit supervision:** The final experiment, showing respectable PoS prediction accuracy *without* PoS tags during model training, is a promising, albeit preliminary, step towards leveraging distributional models for low-resource language tagging.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold and intriguing premise:** The paper tackles a fundamental question about the relationship between semantic representation and grammatical structure. The idea that unsupervised models can reveal insights into language structure is ambitious and inherently interesting.
2. **Focus on linguistic insights through error analysis:**  The authors don’t simply report accuracy numbers; they meticulously analyze the errors, drawing meaningful conclusions about potential annotation inconsistencies and grammatical nuances. This is the strongest aspect of the paper.  The examples provided (currency as numerals, ambiguous adjective/adverb tags) are compelling and illustrate the method’s potential.
3. **Robust experimental setup:** Utilizing the BNC and UD Treebank provides a solid foundation for evaluation, and the weighting of training instances by corpus frequency is a thoughtful methodological detail. The cross-validation and testing on unseen data further strengthen the results.
4. **Clear and concise writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, effectively communicating the core ideas and findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in the core method:** Using distributional semantics for PoS tagging is not a new idea. The core technique – training embeddings and using them for classification – is well-established. The paper's strength lies not in a novel *method* but in the *application* of a known method to a specific linguistic problem.
2. **Superficial exploration of embedding dimensions:** While the paper acknowledges the distributed nature of PoS information within the embeddings, the analysis of which dimensions are most relevant is cursory. Identifying specific dimensions corresponding to grammatical features would significantly strengthen the findings. The mention of component 94 for conjunctions feels like an isolated observation.
3. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art taggers:** While the final experiment shows reasonable accuracy, the paper doesn’t directly compare the distributional model’s performance to current state-of-the-art PoS taggers. Demonstrating competitive performance (even with the limitation of no contextual information) would be impactful.  Simply stating the number is insufficient; a comparative analysis is needed.
4. **Potential for confirmation bias:** The error analysis, while insightful, might be susceptible to confirmation bias. The authors are actively looking for linguistic inconsistencies, and it’s possible they are overemphasizing cases that support their hypothesis while downplaying others.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific linguistic criteria used to identify “ambiguous” lemmas in the UD Treebank? What percentage of lemmas were excluded based on this criterion?
2.  Regarding the identified inconsistencies in the BNC annotation (e.g., currency as numerals), have you investigated whether similar patterns exist in other corpora?
3.  Could you provide more detail about the ANOVA F-value analysis of embedding dimensions? What proportion of the total variance in PoS prediction is explained by the top 100 dimensions?
4. Can you discuss how the choice of embedding size (300 dimensions) influences the results and potentially the ability to identify relevant dimensions?

**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is incomplete. It stops mid-sentence. Please complete it. It would be helpful to see a visual representation of the prototypical PoS vectors (beyond the conjunctions) to get a better sense of their distribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some methodological limitations, this paper presents a valuable and thought-provoking exploration of the relationship between distributional semantics and grammar. The core strength lies in its insightful error analysis and the potential for using distributional models as a tool for identifying inconsistencies in linguistic annotation. While not groundbreaking in terms of methodology, the paper's focus and findings are sufficiently novel and compelling to warrant **acceptance**. I prioritize the boldness of the approach and the linguistic insights over methodological perfection. The error analysis component is particularly promising and deserves further exploration.
