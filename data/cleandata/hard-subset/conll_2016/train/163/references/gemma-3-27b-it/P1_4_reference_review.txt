## Peer Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings capture information about PoS, and test this by training a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings. They analyze misclassifications to identify words that lie on the boundaries between PoS classes, and demonstrate that embeddings can be used to build a reasonably accurate PoS tagger without relying on contextual information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides convincing empirical evidence that word embeddings, trained in an unsupervised manner, encode significant information about a word’s PoS. This strengthens the link between distributional semantics and syntactic/grammatical categories.
2. **Identification of Boundary Cases & Annotation Inconsistencies:** The analysis of misclassified words provides valuable insights into the fuzzy boundaries between PoS classes and highlights potential inconsistencies in the BNC corpus annotation. This is a practical contribution for corpus linguistics and NLP research.
3. **Proof-of-Concept PoS Tagger:**  Demonstrating a reasonably accurate PoS tagger based *solely* on embeddings, without utilizing contextual information, is a novel and intriguing result.  It suggests a potential avenue for resource-poor languages or as a pre-processing step for more sophisticated taggers.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The paper uses a large and well-established corpus (BNC) and employs rigorous evaluation techniques (cross-validation, testing on unseen data, comparison to a standard tagger). The quantitative results (F-scores, accuracy) are clearly presented and support the authors’ claims.
2. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. It goes beyond simply reporting accuracy numbers and dives into *why* the model makes mistakes, offering valuable linguistic insights.  The identification of potential annotation errors is particularly commendable.
3. **Clear and Logical Structure:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors clearly articulate their hypothesis, methodology, and results.  The sections build upon each other logically.
4. **Relevance to Current Research:** The work directly addresses a growing interest in the intersection of distributional semantics and syntax.  It builds upon established research in both fields and offers a new perspective on how these areas can inform each other.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention releasing the mapping between BNC tags and Universal tags online, aiding reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology – training embeddings and using them for classification – is relatively standard. While the application to PoS analysis is interesting, the techniques themselves aren't groundbreaking.  The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion.
2. **Performance Compared to State-of-the-Art:** While the 84% accuracy of the embedding-based tagger is encouraging, it is significantly lower than state-of-the-art PoS taggers that leverage contextual information.  The paper doesn’t fully address this gap and doesn't position the work as a competitor to current best practices, but a more comprehensive comparison would be beneficial.  A discussion on potential avenues for improving performance, like combining embeddings with other features, would strengthen the work.
3. **Focus on English:** The results are limited to English. While English is a widely studied language, the findings may not generalize to other languages with different grammatical structures. The authors acknowledge this and mention future work on other languages, but it's a current limitation.
4. **Reliance on Universal Dependencies:** The use of Universal Dependencies for comparison and testing is helpful, but the reliance on it introduces another layer of potential annotation issues. Any differences observed might be due to discrepancies between BNC and UD annotations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the classifier was trained on weighted instances to address class imbalance.  Could you elaborate on the specific weighting scheme used and provide a justification for its choice?
2.  The error analysis highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation.  Have you considered quantifying the extent of these inconsistencies and their impact on the overall results?
3.  You found that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.  Do you have any hypotheses about *why* this is the case? Is it inherent to the ambiguity of language, or a limitation of the embedding model?
4.  You mention future work on other languages.  Are there any specific languages you plan to investigate, and how do you anticipate the findings might differ based on the typological characteristics of those languages?
5.  Could you elaborate on the computational resources (e.g., hardware, training time) required to train the embedding model and the classifier?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures showing prototypical vectors (Figures 1 and 2) are somewhat difficult to interpret without more explanation of the axes. Providing labels or a more detailed description would improve clarity.
*   The comparison to the Stanford tagger is very insightful, but the description of how the Penn Treebank tags were mapped to the Universal Tagset could be expanded.




Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the methodology isn't groundbreaking, the empirical results, detailed error analysis, and proof-of-concept tagger are strong points. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
