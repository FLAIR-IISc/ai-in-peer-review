## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:** This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize, and demonstrate through experimentation on the BNC and UD Treebank, that word embeddings capture information about PoS, allowing for accurate PoS prediction and identification of “outlier” words – those whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned tag. They train a logistic regression classifier on word embeddings and analyze misclassifications to uncover potential inconsistencies in PoS annotation and boundaries. They also show that a classifier trained solely on distributional vectors can achieve reasonable PoS prediction accuracy.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides compelling evidence that distributional semantic models implicitly encode information about PoS classes, going beyond the previously established notion that embeddings merely *reflect* syntactic contexts.
2. **Novel Error Analysis for Corpus Refinement:**  The identification of misclassified words, and the insightful analysis of these outliers, offers a valuable methodology for detecting potential annotation errors and ambiguous cases within established corpora (BNC in particular). This is a tangible contribution that could directly benefit corpus linguistics.
3. **Demonstration of Zero-Shot PoS Prediction:** The ability to train a reasonably accurate PoS predictor solely from distributional information, without relying on pre-tagged data during training (beyond defining prototypical examples), is a significant step towards leveraging unsupervised learning for linguistic tasks.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Support:** The authors present a thorough and well-documented experimental setup using established corpora (BNC, UD Treebank) and standard techniques (skip-gram embeddings, logistic regression).  The consistently high accuracy scores (97.9% weighted F-score on training data, 91% on unseen BNC data, 99% on UD Treebank) strongly support their claims.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words (Sections 4) is a standout feature. The authors don't just report errors; they *interpret* them, identifying patterns related to proper nouns, nominal-adjectival ambiguities, and specific tagging inconsistencies in the BNC. This demonstrates a deep understanding of linguistic nuances and the limitations of current tagging schemes.
3. **Methodological Rigor:**  The careful consideration of word frequency weighting during classifier training is commendable, addressing a potential bias towards more frequent (and often simpler) word classes. The use of 10-fold cross-validation and comparison to baseline classifiers further strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and understand their experimental approach.
5. **Relevance to the Field:** The work bridges the gap between distributional semantics and traditional linguistics, contributing to ongoing discussions about the nature of PoS and the limitations of discrete tagging schemes.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Methodology:**  The core methodology – training word embeddings and using them for classification – is well-established. While the *application* to PoS analysis and the specific error analysis are novel, the underlying techniques themselves are not groundbreaking. The authors acknowledge this, but it's important to emphasize.
2. **Superficial Discussion of Embedding Dimensions:** While the paper notes that PoS information is “distributed among several axis of the vector space”, the analysis of *which* dimensions contribute to PoS differentiation (beyond the conjunction example) remains somewhat superficial.  The mention of Tsvetkov et al. (2015) suggests potential for deeper analysis, but it's not fully explored. A more detailed investigation of which embedding dimensions correlate with specific linguistic features would strengthen the findings.
3. **Reliance on Universal Dependencies as a “Gold Standard”:** While UD is a valuable resource, treating it as an unquestioned ground truth for identifying BNC tagging errors is problematic.  The UD Treebank itself has its own annotation conventions and biases, and simply comparing BNC tags to UD tags doesn't necessarily reveal *errors* in the BNC; it reveals *differences* in annotation philosophy.  This needs to be explicitly acknowledged.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art PoS Taggers:** The paper briefly mentions that the achieved accuracy isn't comparable to SOTA taggers, but a more detailed comparison, including a discussion of the different advantages and disadvantages of the distributional approach (e.g., zero-shot capability vs. context-aware accuracy), would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the potential for identifying annotation errors in the BNC using your method. Could you provide a more quantitative assessment of the proportion of identified errors that were demonstrably *errors* (after manual review), rather than simply differences in annotation convention?
2.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of applying the distributional model approach to languages with richer morphology or more flexible word order than English? How might the method need to be adapted to handle these complexities?
3.  Beyond the conjunction example, can you provide further analysis of the embedding dimensions that are most informative for distinguishing between different PoS classes?  Perhaps visualisations or correlation analyses?
4. You used a skip-gram model. Did you explore other embedding models (e.g., CBOW, FastText) and did they yield different results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are helpful and well-labeled.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The formatting is generally good.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the core methodology isn’t revolutionary, the careful experimentation, insightful error analysis, and demonstration of zero-shot PoS prediction are significant achievements. The weaknesses are relatively minor and addressable through further research. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above.
