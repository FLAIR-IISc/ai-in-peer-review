## Peer Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:** This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings inherently encode PoS information, allowing for the identification of “outlier” words that challenge conventional PoS boundaries. They train a skip-gram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, analyze misclassifications to highlight linguistic ambiguities, and demonstrate the feasibility of building a PoS tagger solely from distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS Information within Embeddings:** The paper provides empirical evidence, through a reasonably accurate PoS classifier trained on word embeddings, that distributional models capture significant information about lexical categories. This validates the distributional hypothesis in the context of grammatical classes.
2. **Identification of Boundary Cases & Annotation Inconsistencies:** The analysis of misclassified words reveals interesting linguistic phenomena – ambiguous cases like participles, currency amounts, and instances where the model highlights potential errors in the BNC’s original annotation. This is a valuable contribution to understanding the limitations of existing PoS tagsets and corpora.
3. **Feasibility of Distributional-Only PoS Tagging:** The successful training of a PoS tagger solely using distributional information (without leveraging contextual features) is a noteworthy result, suggesting potential applications in low-resource settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Research Question:** The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the inherent flexibility of PoS boundaries and the potential for data-driven refinement. The research question – can distributional models reveal insights into PoS – is clearly articulated.
2. **Robust Experimental Setup:** The authors utilize a well-established corpus (BNC) and a standard embedding technique (skip-gram).  The inclusion of a baseline classifier (majority class) and comparison to the Stanford tagger strengthens the results. The weighting of training instances by corpus frequency is a smart approach to address class imbalance.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics. The authors delve into *why* the model makes certain errors, offering interesting linguistic observations and potential explanations. The identification of annotation inconsistencies is particularly valuable.
4. **Demonstrated Applicability:** Showing that a useful PoS tagger can be constructed solely from embeddings is an impressive result and demonstrates the utility of the approach. The analysis of component importance provides further insights.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** The core methodology (training embeddings and using them for classification) is not particularly novel.  The primary contribution lies in the *application* of this established technique to the specific problem of PoS refinement and the insights gained from the error analysis. While effective, the methodology isn't groundbreaking.
2. **Dependence on Pre-existing Tags:** While the final experiment aims for distributional-only tagging, the initial steps rely heavily on the BNC's existing PoS annotations for training and evaluation. This creates a circularity.  The study would be stronger if it explored techniques for discovering PoS categories *completely* unsupervised, potentially through clustering.
3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison to the performance of current state-of-the-art PoS taggers that *do* utilize contextual features. While the authors acknowledge the lower performance, a more comprehensive benchmark would better contextualize their results.  It's unclear how much performance is sacrificed by eschewing contextual information.
4. **Visualization Limited:** The figures showing prototypical vectors (Figures 1 & 2) are not particularly informative without more context or detailed explanation of the axes. A more sophisticated visualization technique might highlight the key differentiating features more effectively.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention the possibility of using this approach in low-resource languages. Could you elaborate on the practical steps involved in adapting the method to a language with limited or no existing PoS-tagged data? Specifically, how would you bootstrap the initial categorization of words?
2.  The analysis of misclassifications revealed some inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you considered quantifying the extent of these inconsistencies across the entire corpus?
3.  Regarding the choice of hyperparameters (vector size, negative samples, window size), did you perform any ablation studies to assess the sensitivity of the results to these parameters?
4.  You mention the 100-dimensional threshold for "important" vector components. Is this a hard threshold, or is there a gradual decline in the importance of components beyond that point?  Could you provide a more detailed visualization of the F-value ranking of components?
5.  The paper focuses on the Universal Dependencies tagset. Were there significant differences observed when using the original BNC tagset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The anonymization is effective.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough.



Overall, this is a solid and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between distributional semantics and part-of-speech tagging. While the methodology isn't entirely novel, the careful analysis and insightful observations regarding boundary cases and potential annotation errors are noteworthy. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
