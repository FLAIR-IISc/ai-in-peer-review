## Peer Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings capture PoS information, allowing for classification and identification of words that deviate from typical PoS behavior. They train a skipgram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, and then analyze misclassifications to uncover linguistic insights. The paper further demonstrates the feasibility of building a PoS tagger solely based on distributional information, without relying on contextual features.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of PoS information within word embeddings:** The paper provides robust empirical evidence demonstrating that distributional semantic models encode significant information about PoS, capable of achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and syntactic knowledge.
2. **Method for identifying linguistic outliers & annotation inconsistencies:**  The approach of using a classifier trained on embeddings to identify misclassified words provides a novel method for identifying words with ambiguous or unusual PoS behavior, and even potential errors in existing annotated corpora.
3. **Proof-of-concept PoS tagger based solely on distributional information:**  The authors demonstrate the potential to build a usable PoS tagger *without* utilizing typical contextual features, relying only on word embedding representations. This is a conceptually interesting approach, particularly for low-resource languages.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** The consistently high F-scores achieved by the PoS classifier (0.979 on training data, 0.91 on a held-out BNC test set, 0.99 on the UD treebank) strongly support the central hypothesis. This suggests that the learned embeddings effectively capture PoS information.
2. **Insightful error analysis:**  The detailed analysis of misclassifications is a significant strength. It doesn’t just report errors, but attempts to *interpret* them, leading to interesting observations about the boundaries between PoS categories (e.g., nouns and adjectives, numerals and nouns) and even potential annotation errors in the BNC. This is a valuable contribution to linguistic understanding.
3. **Clear methodology and replication potential:** The paper clearly outlines the data processing steps, model training parameters, and evaluation metrics. The authors also made their mapping between BNC and Universal tags publicly available, facilitating replication and further research.
4. **Interesting exploration of vector components:** The attempt to link specific vector dimensions to PoS affiliation, although preliminary, is a valuable direction.  The findings suggest that PoS information is not concentrated in a few dimensions, but is distributed throughout the embedding space, supporting the holistic nature of distributional semantics.
5. **Novel approach to PoS tagging:** Building a PoS tagger from distributional information alone is conceptually interesting. While the performance is not state-of-the-art, it demonstrates a viable alternative approach that doesn't rely on traditional feature engineering based on context.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach:** The idea of exploring the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS is not entirely new (references acknowledge this). The main novelty lies in the detailed analysis of misclassifications and the standalone PoS tagger, but the foundational concept has been previously explored.
2. **Lack of comparison with stronger baselines:** While the authors compare against a majority class baseline and a single-feature baseline, they do not compare their standalone distributional tagger with more modern, context-aware PoS taggers (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF models). This makes it difficult to assess the true performance of their approach relative to the state-of-the-art.
3. **BNC dependency & potential bias:**  The entire study is based on a model trained on the BNC.  It is unclear how well the findings would generalize to other corpora with different characteristics. The potential biases inherent in the BNC (e.g., its age and genre distribution) are not adequately discussed.
4. **Limited exploration of hyperparameter tuning:**  The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters of the skipgram model (e.g., window size, vector dimensionality). Exploring a wider range of parameters could potentially lead to improved performance.
5. **Superficial discussion of dimensionality and feature importance:** While the paper identifies the number of components needed to achieve high accuracy, a deeper analysis of *which* features are most informative for PoS prediction is lacking.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Baseline comparison:** Could you compare your standalone distributional tagger to a more standard, context-aware PoS tagger (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) trained on the same data? This would provide a more meaningful assessment of your approach's performance.
2. **Generalizability:** Have you experimented with training and evaluating your model on other corpora besides the BNC, to assess the generalizability of your findings?
3. **Feature Interpretation:** Can you provide more insights into what the most important vector components *represent* linguistically? Are there any interpretable patterns or semantic features associated with these dimensions?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameters of the skipgram model? Have you explored a range of values for parameters like window size, vector dimensionality, and negative sampling rate?
5. **Error Category Analysis:** You identify errors related to numerals and adjectives/nouns.  Could you provide more examples of these errors, and elaborate on the linguistic motivations behind them?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (Figures 1 and 2) are visually uninformative and could be improved.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the Universal Dependencies tagset itself, as it might contribute to some of the misclassification errors observed.
*   The use of anonymization is noted, but clarification of what exactly was anonymized would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-executed and insightful study exploring the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging.  While the core idea isn’t entirely novel, the thorough empirical analysis, the identification of linguistic outliers, and the demonstration of a standalone distributional tagger are valuable contributions.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors addressing the questions and weaknesses raised in this review.
