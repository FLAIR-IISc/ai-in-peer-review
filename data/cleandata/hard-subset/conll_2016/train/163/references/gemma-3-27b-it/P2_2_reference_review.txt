## Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings inherently encode PoS information, and test this by training a classifier on word embeddings derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). They analyze misclassifications to identify words exhibiting ambiguous PoS behavior, and demonstrate that a classifier trained solely on distributional vectors can achieve reasonable accuracy in PoS prediction. They further explore the distribution of PoS information within the embedding space.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that distributional semantic models capture significant information about traditional PoS tags, even *without* being explicitly trained with PoS supervision. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and syntactic/grammatical structure.
2. **Identification of Linguistic “Outliers”:**  The analysis of misclassified words effectively highlights cases where current PoS annotation schemes may be questionable or where words exhibit behavior spanning multiple PoS categories. This provides a valuable tool for linguistic analysis and potential refinement of tagging schemes.
3. **Demonstration of Zero-Shot PoS Prediction:** The ability to achieve reasonable PoS prediction accuracy using a model trained *without* PoS tags during embedding training demonstrates the potential for applying this approach to low-resource languages or scenarios where labeled data is scarce.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Experimental Setup & Thorough Evaluation:** The authors utilize a well-established corpus (BNC), a standard embedding technique (Skipgram), and rigorous evaluation metrics (F-score, accuracy, cross-validation).  The comparison with a Stanford PoS tagger and testing on the UD Treebank provide valuable benchmarks and demonstrate the generalizability of their findings.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is the strongest aspect of this paper. It goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers and dives into *why* the model makes mistakes, revealing interesting linguistic phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies. The discussion of currency amounts, passive participles, and the overlap between nouns and adjectives is particularly compelling.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the methods are explained thoroughly, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.
4. **Novel Exploration of Embedding Space:**  The attempt to understand *how* PoS information is represented within the embedding space (through analysis of prototypical vectors and component importance) is a valuable contribution.  While the initial findings are not definitive, they point to interesting directions for future research.
5. **Practical Implications:** The methodology presented has clear potential applications in areas like corpus linguistics, tagset refinement, and cross-lingual PoS tagging.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Method:** While the experimental setup is sound, the core methodology – training embeddings and using them for PoS classification – is not entirely novel.  Previous work (as acknowledged in the related work section) has explored similar ideas. The primary contribution lies in the detailed analysis and the specific insights gained from it.
2. **Dependency on Existing Tagset:** The entire analysis is fundamentally dependent on the quality and consistency of the BNC tagset. While the authors point out some inconsistencies, the validity of their conclusions is tied to the accuracy of the initial annotations. The paper doesn’t thoroughly explore how different tagsets might impact the results.
3. **Relatively Low Accuracy in Zero-Shot Setting:** While the 84% accuracy in the zero-shot setting is encouraging, it is still significantly lower than the performance of state-of-the-art supervised PoS taggers. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the limitations of this approach and potential avenues for improvement would be beneficial.  Specifically, what limits the performance of the zero-shot tagger?
4. **Lack of Detailed Analysis of Embedding Components:** While the authors attempt to identify important embedding components, the analysis is somewhat superficial. A more in-depth investigation of what these components *represent* linguistically would strengthen the paper. Just stating that component 94 is specific to conjunctions is a good start, but doesn’t provide much insight.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of using the BNC tagset, and how these limitations might have influenced your results?
2.  What specific techniques could be employed to improve the accuracy of the zero-shot PoS tagger?  Would incorporating subword information (e.g., using fastText embeddings) be beneficial?
3.  Regarding the identified "outliers," have you investigated whether these words are similarly misclassified by other PoS taggers?  Would this provide further evidence of annotation inconsistencies?
4.  You mention that the PoS information seems to be distributed among many embedding components.  Can you speculate on *why* this is the case?  Is it an inherent property of the distributional hypothesis, or a consequence of the training process?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more extensive discussion of related work, particularly recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo). While these models weren't used in the experiments, acknowledging their potential relevance would be helpful.
*  The Figures 1 and 2 would be more informative with labelled axes.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and syntax. While the methodology isn’t groundbreaking, the insightful error analysis and the demonstration of zero-shot PoS prediction are significant achievements.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
