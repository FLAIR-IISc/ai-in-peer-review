This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) classes, aiming to refine PoS definitions based on data-driven approaches. The core idea is that word embeddings capture syntactic and semantic information, potentially revealing nuanced boundaries between PoS categories and identifying outliers that challenge traditional classifications. The authors train a skipgram model on the BNC corpus and use a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags from the embeddings.  They analyze misclassifications to identify words with atypical distributional patterns and explore the correspondence between embedding dimensions and PoS affiliation. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating the effectiveness of distributional models in predicting PoS tags with high accuracy, even without contextual information; (2) identifying linguistic outliers – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned PoS, potentially highlighting annotation inconsistencies or ambiguous grammatical behavior; and (3) providing an analysis of which embedding dimensions are most relevant to PoS prediction, suggesting a distributed representation of grammatical information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates compelling empirical results, achieving high accuracy (84% on unseen data) in PoS prediction using only word embeddings. This is significant because it showcases the ability of purely distributional models to capture substantial grammatical information without relying on contextual features typically used in PoS tagging.  The comparison to a majority class baseline and a single-feature baseline (F-scores of 0.07 and 0.22 respectively versus 0.91 and 0.979) strongly supports the claim that the embeddings encode PoS-related data.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a major strength. The authors don't simply report error rates; they delve into specific cases, uncovering interesting linguistic phenomena like the difficulties in distinguishing proper nouns from common nouns, the ambiguity of participles, and inconsistencies in the BNC annotation.  The comparison with the Stanford tagger’s errors further strengthens this point, revealing different types of errors and reinforcing the value of the distributional approach.
3. **Novel Application of Distributional Models:** While distributional semantics have been used in relation to PoS before (as outlined in the related work), this work goes beyond simply improving tagging performance. The authors leverage the models for *discovering* linguistic subtleties and challenging existing categorizations. The identification of words on the boundaries between PoS classes (e.g., nouns behaving like adjectives) is a novel and valuable contribution.
4. **Distributed Representation Analysis:** The investigation of which embedding dimensions are relevant for PoS prediction, while preliminary, offers insights into how grammatical information is encoded in the vector space. The observation that PoS affiliation is distributed across multiple dimensions, rather than concentrated in a few, is a reasonable finding and supports the underlying principles of distributional semantics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Outlier Analysis:** While the error analysis is insightful, it remains largely descriptive. The paper identifies *that* certain words are misclassified, but lacks a deeper investigation into *why* this happens.  More sophisticated linguistic analysis of the misclassified words, potentially involving manual examination of their contexts and grammatical functions, would strengthen the claims. What specific distributional characteristics differentiate the outliers from their correctly classified counterparts?
2. **Reliance on Universal Dependencies Tagset:** The choice to convert BNC tags to the Universal Dependencies tagset is reasonable for broader comparability, but the manual mapping process introduces a potential source of error. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss how it might affect their results. A sensitivity analysis, exploring the impact of different mapping strategies, could be beneficial.
3. **Classifier Simplicity:** The use of a simple logistic regression classifier, while adequate for demonstrating the feasibility of the approach, might be masking potential performance gains. Exploring more sophisticated classification models, such as neural networks, could reveal further insights and improve accuracy. The authors should briefly discuss the limitations of using a linear model.
4. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with State-of-the-Art PoS Taggers:** While the paper compares against a majority class baseline, it lacks a direct comparison with contemporary state-of-the-art PoS taggers on the same dataset. Demonstrating how the distributional approach performs relative to established methods would provide a clearer assessment of its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detailed examples of misclassified words, including their contexts in the BNC corpus?  A qualitative analysis of these contexts would help illustrate the specific linguistic challenges that the model is facing.
2. You mention inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. How prevalent are these inconsistencies, and how might they be affecting your results?
3. Did you experiment with different embedding parameters (e.g., window size, negative sampling rate) and how did these parameters affect the performance of the PoS predictor?
4. What are the specific dimensions of the embedding space that are most correlated with particular PoS tags, beyond the identified component 94 for conjunctions? Can you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the relationship between embedding dimensions and grammatical features?

**Additional Comments:**

* The figures (Figures 1 and 2) could benefit from clearer labeling and explanations.
* A more thorough discussion of related work on distributional semantics and PoS tagging would be helpful, particularly regarding recent advancements in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, ELMo).
* The abbreviation "PoS" is used extensively. Consider defining it at first use in each section to enhance readability.
