## Review for "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings inherently encode information about PoS, and test this by training a classifier on embeddings derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). They analyze misclassifications to identify words that lie on the boundaries between PoS categories, and demonstrate the potential for using embeddings to refine our understanding of these categories. They also show that a classifier trained *without* explicit PoS tags can achieve reasonable accuracy in predicting PoS.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence that distributional semantic models capture substantial information about traditional PoS tags, even without being explicitly trained for that purpose. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and linguistic structure.
2. **Identification of Boundary Cases & Potential Annotation Errors:** The analysis of misclassified words effectively highlights cases where the boundaries between PoS categories are blurry and suggests potential inconsistencies within the BNC annotation scheme. This is a valuable contribution for corpus linguistics.
3. **Proof-of-Concept for Tag-Free PoS Prediction:**  The ability to predict PoS tags with reasonable accuracy from embeddings trained *without* PoS information demonstrates the potential for applying this approach to low-resource languages or scenarios where labeled data is scarce.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors utilize a well-established corpus (BNC), a standard embedding technique (Skipgram), and a clear classification approach (logistic regression). The use of 10-fold cross-validation and comparison to a baseline adds to the credibility of the results. The weighting of training instances by corpus frequency is a smart move to address class imbalance.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. It’s not just about reporting errors, but *interpreting* them linguistically and drawing meaningful conclusions about PoS boundaries and potential annotation issues. The examples provided (e.g., proper nouns as common nouns, currency amounts as numerals) are compelling.
3. **Demonstrated Robustness:** Applying the trained classifier to the UD Treebank and achieving high accuracy further demonstrates the generalization capability of the learned relationships between embeddings and PoS.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the research, and the sections flow logically, building upon each other.
5. **Quantitative and Qualitative Support:** The work combines quantitative results (accuracy scores, F-scores) with qualitative analysis of specific examples, providing a well-rounded assessment.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology – training embeddings and using them for classification – is well-established.  The novelty lies primarily in the *application* to analyzing PoS boundaries and the insightful error analysis, rather than a completely new technical approach. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it limits the paper's impact.
2. **Focus on English:** The findings are specific to the English language. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would benefit from a discussion of how the results might generalize to other languages with different morphological and syntactic structures.
3. **Simplified Classifier:** While the choice of logistic regression is reasonable for a baseline, exploring more sophisticated classification models (e.g., neural networks) could potentially improve performance and provide further insights. The paper acknowledges this implicitly ("modern state-of-the-art PoS taggers") but doesn’t investigate it.
4. **Limited Exploration of Embedding Parameters:**  The paper uses a fixed vector size (300) and other embedding parameters.  Exploring the impact of different parameter settings on PoS discrimination would strengthen the analysis.
5. **Over-Reliance on BNC:** While the BNC is a valuable resource, relying almost exclusively on it for training and evaluation limits the generalizability of the findings. Using additional corpora (even for evaluation) would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the UD Treebank results support the robustness of the classifier.  Could you provide more detail on the percentage of words in the test set that were *not* present in the distributional model’s vocabulary, and explain how this missing vocabulary affects the interpretation of the 99% F-score?
2.  The error analysis highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you considered quantifying these inconsistencies (e.g., calculating the percentage of specific tags that are frequently misassigned)?
3.  You state that the knowledge about PoS affiliation is distributed among many embedding components.  Are there any specific dimensions that consistently exhibit higher relevance across different PoS tags, beyond the example of component 94 for conjunctions?  Could you provide a more systematic exploration of feature importance?
4.  Regarding the "tag-free" PoS prediction, what is the performance when training and testing on different corpora (e.g., training on BNC and testing on UD Treebank)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (1 and 2) are somewhat small and difficult to interpret.  Larger, higher-resolution figures would be helpful.
*   The references are formatted inconsistently.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and linguistic structure. While the methodology isn't groundbreaking, the insightful analysis of errors and the demonstrated potential for tag-free PoS prediction are significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should address the questions to clarify certain aspects of their work and further strengthen their conclusions.
