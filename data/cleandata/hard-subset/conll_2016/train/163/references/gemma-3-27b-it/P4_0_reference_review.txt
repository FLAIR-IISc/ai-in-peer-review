This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, proposing that these models contain information useful for refining PoS definitions and identifying boundary cases. The authors train a skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC) and use it to train a classifier to predict PoS tags. They then analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic patterns and outliers, and demonstrate that a classifier trained solely on distributional vectors can achieve reasonable accuracy. The primary contributions of the work, as I see them, are: 1) Demonstration of the robustness of PoS information embedded within distributional semantic models, achieving high accuracy (97.9% weighted F-score on training data, 91% on unseen test data) in predicting PoS tags from word embeddings; 2) A novel approach to identify potential inconsistencies in PoS annotation guidelines by highlighting words exhibiting distributional patterns more typical of alternative PoS categories; and 3) An exploration of the distributed nature of PoS information within the embedding space, indicating that PoS affiliation isn't captured by a few key dimensions but is instead spread across many.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently high performance of the PoS classifier, both on training and unseen data, provides compelling evidence that distributional semantic models capture significant PoS information. The accuracy of 91% on the unseen test set, despite the simplicity of the logistic regression classifier, is particularly noteworthy. This lends credence to the central hypothesis of the paper.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words goes beyond simply reporting error rates. The authors identify specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., passive participles, currency amounts, nouns with adjectival distribution) that contribute to errors, and relate these to potential ambiguities or inconsistencies in the BNC annotation scheme. This detailed inspection of 'outliers' is a valuable contribution, offering nuanced linguistic insights.
3. **Demonstration of Potential for Corpus Revision:** The identification of systematic errors in the BNC’s PoS tagging, particularly the misclassification of adjectives as adverbs, is a significant finding. This suggests a practical application for distributional semantic models: assisting in the refinement and correction of existing corpora, potentially improving the performance of downstream NLP tasks.
4. **Exploration of Distributed Representation:** The investigation of how PoS information is encoded across multiple dimensions of the embedding space highlights the complexity of these models and reinforces the notion that they don’t rely on simple, easily interpretable features. This contributes to a broader understanding of how distributional semantics represent linguistic knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology – training a skipgram model and using it for PoS prediction – is well-established. While the application to exploring PoS boundaries is interesting, the underlying techniques themselves don’t represent a significant methodological advancement. The paper lacks a substantial novel algorithmic contribution.
2. **Dependence on Existing High-Quality Annotations:** The entire evaluation relies on the accuracy of the BNC and UD Treebank annotations. While the authors acknowledge potential inconsistencies in the BNC, the analysis is inherently limited by the quality of the "gold standard." It's difficult to assess the true extent of annotation errors without independent verification.
3. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Tagging Systems:** The authors present the results of their classifier, but do not directly compare its performance to more sophisticated, state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., those based on neural networks like BERT or LSTM). This makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of their approach relative to existing solutions. Demonstrating improvement over a baseline established by a strong competitor would strengthen the work.
4. **Limited Analysis of Vector Components:** While the authors identify components correlated with conjunctions, they don’t delve deeply into interpreting the semantic meaning of the most informative dimensions. A more thorough analysis of what specific linguistic features are captured by these dimensions could provide valuable insights into how distributional semantics represent grammar.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Have you attempted to quantify the extent of these inconsistencies beyond the examples discussed in Section 4? Could you provide a more systematic comparison with other annotated corpora?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative samples)? Did you perform any parameter tuning, and if so, what were the results?
3.  You state that the classifier “knows absolutely nothing about a word’s context.” However, the embedding itself is trained based on contextual information. Could you clarify how this seemingly contradictory statement impacts your interpretation of the results?
4.  Regarding the ‘prototypical’ vectors: Are these simply averages of the embeddings, or do you employ any weighting schemes to account for word frequency or other factors?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (1 and 2) are helpful in visualizing the prototypical vectors but could benefit from clearer labeling of axes.
*   The paper could be strengthened by including a discussion of the limitations of distributional semantics for representing certain linguistic phenomena, such as long-distance dependencies or semantic ambiguities.
* The references are slightly incomplete; e.g., Aarts & McMahon (2008) is missing publication details.
