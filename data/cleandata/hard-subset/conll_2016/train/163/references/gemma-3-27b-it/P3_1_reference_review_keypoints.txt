- Word embeddings encode robust information about part-of-speech (PoS) classes and can predict PoS from distributional data.  
- A classifier trained on embeddings successfully predicts PoS and reveals words that challenge traditional tagging schemes.  
- The misclassification analysis offers a methodology for identifying linguistic “grey areas” and problematic annotations.  
- A PoS classifier can be trained effectively without initial PoS tags, suggesting promise for low-resource languages.  
- Experiments are well-designed using the BNC, cross-validation, and external testing on the UD Treebank.  
- The paper’s in-depth error analysis interprets misclassifications linguistically and compares them to the Stanford tagger’s errors.  
- The paper is clearly written and well-structured, accessible to NLP and linguistics readers.  
- The work addresses a fundamental NLP question linking form, meaning, and grammatical category.  
- Practical implications include improving PoS taggers and refining annotation guidelines via outlier detection.  
- Methodologically, using embeddings for classification is not novel; the novelty is in application and analysis.  
- The initial supervised experiment relies on gold-standard PoS tags, weakening claims of purely discovering PoS from scratch.  
- The exploration of which vector components correlate with PoS is cursory; deeper, systematic analysis is needed.  
- Comparison to the Stanford tagger is useful but insufficient; stronger benchmarks against state-of-the-art taggers are missing.  
- Question: How does the size of the prototypical word list (1564 words) affect unsupervised performance and was it optimized?  
- Question: Can the authors elaborate on challenges linking specific vector dimensions to linguistic features beyond the conjunction example?  
- Question: How could the error-analysis insights be concretely used to improve BNC annotation guidelines?  
- Question: Would performance vary using full word forms instead of lemmas, and was this explored?  
- Suggestion: Discuss limitations of distributional semantics for capturing grammatical info like long-distance dependencies.  
- Suggestion: Include visualizations (e.g., t-SNE) to illustrate embedding clustering by PoS.  
- Overall: Well-executed, insightful paper with valuable empirical results and practical applications; addressing noted weaknesses would strengthen it.
