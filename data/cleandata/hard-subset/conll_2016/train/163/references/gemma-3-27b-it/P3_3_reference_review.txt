## Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:** This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings inherently contain information about PoS, and test this by training a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings derived from the British National Corpus (BNC). They then analyze misclassifications to identify words with ambiguous or unusual distributional patterns, potentially indicating refinements needed in PoS definitions. They further demonstrate the possibility of building a PoS tagger solely from distributional information, without relying on contextual features.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence demonstrating that distributional semantic models encode information relevant to PoS, achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction based *solely* on embeddings. This reaffirms and expands upon previous work suggesting a link between distributional semantics and syntactic categories.
2. **Error Analysis as a Tool for Linguistic Refinement:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words reveals interesting linguistic phenomena, like the ambiguity between nouns and adjectives, and highlights potential inconsistencies in the BNC tagging scheme. This approach positions distributional models as a valuable tool for identifying areas where traditional linguistic annotation might be refined.
3. **Feasibility of Context-Free PoS Prediction:** The successful training of a PoS tagger using only word embeddings, without leveraging contextual information, is a notable result. This suggests the potential for rapid PoS tagging in resource-scarce languages or scenarios where contextual analysis is computationally expensive.



**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Methodology:** The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing a large, established corpus (BNC) and employing sound machine learning practices (weighted training instances, cross-validation). The comparison to a majority-class baseline and a single-feature baseline provides a clear benchmark for evaluating the model's performance.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The most compelling aspect of the paper is the thorough error analysis.  The authors go beyond simply reporting misclassification rates; they delve into the *types* of errors, identifying linguistic ambiguities and potential annotation issues.  The specific examples provided (currency amounts, passive participles) are particularly illuminating.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the research is clearly stated, and the methodology is explained in a concise and understandable manner.  The inclusion of visualizations (Figures 1 & 2) is helpful for illustrating the vector representations.
4. **Novel Application of Distributional Models:**  While the idea of a relationship between distribution and meaning is foundational, applying it to refine *PoS definitions* and creating a context-free tagger adds a unique and valuable perspective.  
5. **Relevance to the Field:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in linguistics – the nature of part-of-speech categories – and offers a data-driven approach to addressing it.  The findings have implications for both theoretical linguistics and practical NLP applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the Embedding Approach:** The use of a Continuous Skipgram model with standard parameters is relatively standard. While the application to PoS refinement is novel, the underlying model itself doesn't represent a significant methodological advancement. More recent embedding techniques (e.g., contextualized embeddings like BERT, or transformers) might yield even better results and more nuanced insights.
2. **Dependence on Existing Annotation:** The evaluation relies heavily on the quality of the BNC’s existing PoS tags. While the authors rightly identify some potential inconsistencies, the entire analysis is predicated on the assumption that the BNC tags are largely accurate. This creates a circularity – using potentially flawed tags to evaluate the model’s ability to refine them.  Further validation against other tagged corpora would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Taggers:** While the authors demonstrate the feasibility of a context-free tagger, they don’t compare its performance to the current state-of-the-art PoS taggers that *do* utilize contextual information.  A comparison would help to better contextualize the results and highlight the trade-offs between the proposed approach and existing methods.  
4. **Limited Exploration of Embedding Dimensionality:**  The analysis of which embedding components are most relevant to PoS (Figure 3) feels somewhat superficial. While the authors correctly point out that information is distributed, a more detailed investigation into the semantic content of the most informative components could provide further insights.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you considered using more advanced embedding models (e.g., BERT, transformers) and do you anticipate that these would significantly improve the performance of the PoS predictor or yield different insights from the error analysis?
2.  Beyond the Universal Dependencies Treebank, have you validated your findings against other tagged corpora to assess the generalizability of the identified annotation inconsistencies?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed interpretation of the most informative embedding components (e.g., what types of semantic or syntactic features do they appear to capture)?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the context-free tagger to the size of the training vocabulary (the 1564 words) and the choice of ‘unambiguous’ lemmas?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the limitations of the BNC tagging in relation to currency amounts and years is particularly insightful.
*   The conclusion effectively summarizes the key findings and outlines promising avenues for future research.
*   The anonymization note is appreciated.



**Overall Assessment:**  This is a well-executed and insightful study that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the methodological novelty is limited, the thorough error analysis and the demonstration of a context-free PoS tagger are significant achievements. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
