## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:**

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantics and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize and demonstrate that word embeddings, trained on a large corpus, encode information about PoS classes. They train a classifier to predict PoS tags from embeddings and analyze misclassifications to identify words that challenge conventional tagging schemes. They further show that a PoS classifier can be trained effectively *without* initial PoS tags, relying solely on distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical validation of the link between distributional semantics and PoS:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that word embeddings capture surprisingly robust information about PoS, even without explicit supervision. This strengthens the theoretical connection between distributional and linguistic categories.
2. **A methodology for identifying linguistic “grey areas”:** The misclassification analysis provides a valuable methodology for identifying words that fall between traditional PoS boundaries, potentially highlighting areas where current annotation schemes are suboptimal or require refinement.
3. **Demonstration of unsupervised PoS prediction:** Showing that a reasonably accurate PoS classifier can be trained solely from distributional data is a significant result, suggesting potential for low-resource language scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Foundation:** The experiments are well-designed and use a large, established corpus (BNC) and a standard evaluation methodology (cross-validation, external testing on UD Treebank).  The results consistently demonstrate the efficacy of the approach.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is a major strength. It doesn’t just report errors; it actively interprets them in linguistic terms, providing concrete examples of ambiguous cases and potential annotation issues. The comparison with errors from the Stanford tagger is particularly valuable.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors effectively explain the motivation, methodology, and results, making the paper accessible to readers with a background in NLP and linguistics.
4. **Relevance to the Field:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in NLP – the relationship between form, meaning, and grammatical category.  Exploring this connection through the lens of distributional semantics is a timely and relevant contribution.
5. **Practical Implications:** The method for identifying linguistic outliers has practical applications for improving existing PoS taggers and refining annotation guidelines. The unsupervised approach offers a potential path toward PoS tagging for low-resource languages.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:**  The core methodology – training word embeddings and using them for classification – is not novel.  The paper's contribution lies primarily in the *application* of these techniques to the specific problem of PoS boundaries and the insightful analysis of the results, rather than a groundbreaking methodological advance.
2. **Reliance on Pre-Existing Tags:** While the unsupervised experiment is strong, the initial classification experiment *requires* gold-standard PoS tags for training. This slightly weakens the claim that the model is "discovering" PoS classes from scratch. The initial phase serves primarily to validate the approach before the more interesting unsupervised setup.
3. **Superficial Exploration of Vector Components:** The investigation of which vector components correlate with PoS (towards the end of the paper) feels somewhat cursory.  While the authors identify component 94 as relevant for conjunctions, a more systematic exploration of feature importance across *all* PoS classes would be beneficial.  The ANOVA F-value analysis is a good starting point, but further investigation (e.g., using techniques like PCA or feature visualization) could reveal deeper insights.
4. **Limited Comparative Analysis:**  The comparison to the Stanford tagger is useful but limited.  A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., those based on deep learning) would provide a better benchmark for evaluating the performance of the distributional semantics-based approach. The authors address the comparative aspect in the conclusion, but it is not a central element of the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the unsupervised experiment:** What impact does the size of the "prototypical" word list (the 1564 words) have on performance?  Was any experimentation done to optimize this number?
2. **Regarding vector component analysis:**  Could you elaborate on the challenges of interpreting the meaning of specific vector dimensions?  Are there any attempts to link these dimensions to known linguistic features beyond the conjunction example?
3. **Regarding error analysis:**  The paper highlights inconsistencies in the BNC annotation. Could you provide more specific examples of how the insights gained from the error analysis could be used to improve the annotation guidelines?
4. **Concerning the use of lemmas:** While using lemmas is a sensible approach, could the performance vary significantly if full word forms were used instead? Did you explore this possibility?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of distributional semantics in capturing certain types of grammatical information (e.g., long-distance dependencies).
* A visual representation of the word embeddings, perhaps using dimensionality reduction techniques like t-SNE, could further illustrate the clustering of words by PoS.




Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the methodology is not entirely novel, the empirical results, insightful error analysis, and potential practical applications make it a strong candidate for acceptance.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.
