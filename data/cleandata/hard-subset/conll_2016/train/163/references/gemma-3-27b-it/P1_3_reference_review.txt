## Peer Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that embeddings capture information about PoS, enabling classification and identification of “outlier” words – those whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned tag. They train a skip-gram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic phenomena, and finally demonstrate the feasibility of a PoS tagger solely based on distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper convincingly shows that word embeddings, trained in an unsupervised manner, encode significant information about traditional PoS tags, allowing for reasonably accurate PoS prediction. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and syntactic categories.
2. **Identification of annotation inconsistencies & boundary cases:** The analysis of misclassified words reveals potential inconsistencies in the BNC annotation (e.g., adverb/adjective tagging) and highlights words existing on the boundaries between PoS classes (e.g., passive participles, currency amounts). This is a valuable contribution to understanding the fuzziness of PoS boundaries.
3. **Feasibility of a context-free PoS tagger:** The successful training of a PoS tagger *without* relying on contextual information (sentence structure) is a noteworthy result, indicating the robustness of PoS information captured in distributional representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Hypothesis and Methodology:** The paper presents a well-defined hypothesis, a clear methodological approach, and a logical progression of experiments. The use of the BNC and Universal Dependencies provides a solid foundation for evaluation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The classifier achieves high accuracy (97.9% on training data, 91% on unseen data) demonstrating the power of distributional models for PoS prediction.  The consistent performance across different datasets (BNC test set and UD Treebank) strengthens the findings.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words goes beyond simple error reporting. It provides linguistic insights into the limitations of PoS tagging, potential annotation errors, and the inherent ambiguity of certain words. This is a particularly strong aspect of the paper.
4. **Novelty in Context-Free Tagging:** Exploring a PoS tagger *without* contextual information is a novel and potentially useful direction. It suggests potential applications in low-resource settings or as a preliminary step in a more sophisticated tagging pipeline.
5. **Thorough Investigation of Vector Components:** Analyzing the importance of different vector components for PoS prediction (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into how distributional models represent syntactic information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology—training embeddings and using them for classification—is well-established in the field. The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new technique in distributional semantics or machine learning. The novelty lies more in *applying* these techniques to re-examine PoS boundaries.
2. **Focus on English:** The findings are specific to English, a language with relatively well-defined PoS categories.  It's unclear how well these results would generalize to languages with different grammatical structures.  The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion.
3. **Classifier Limitations:** While the classifier’s accuracy is good, it's not state-of-the-art compared to contextualized models (e.g., BERT-based taggers).  The paper doesn’t attempt to compete with these models, but acknowledges this limitation. It would have been helpful to briefly discuss how this approach might complement more powerful methods.
4. **Weighting Scheme Justification:** While the justification for weighting training instances by corpus frequency is reasonable, it feels a bit *ad hoc*. A more rigorous justification, perhaps based on information theory, could strengthen this aspect of the methodology.
5. **Visualization Limited:**  The visualizations (Figures 1 & 2) are not particularly informative.  More sophisticated visualization techniques (e.g., t-SNE or PCA plots of the embedding space, colored by PoS) could provide a more intuitive understanding of the relationships between PoS classes.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalization to other languages:**  Do you anticipate similar results with languages that have different typological characteristics (e.g., languages with freer word order, richer morphology)?  Are there any specific languages where you expect the results to differ significantly?
2. **Complementary approach:** How do you envision this distributional semantic approach being integrated with more powerful, contextualized models (like BERT)? Could it be used for data augmentation, initialization, or error correction?
3. **Weighting scheme alternatives:** Have you experimented with alternative weighting schemes besides corpus frequency?  If so, what were the results?  If not, why did you choose corpus frequency as your primary weighting metric?
4. **Impact of embedding parameters:**  How sensitive are the results to the embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling)? Did you conduct any hyperparameter optimization?
5. **Qualitative examples:** Can you provide more qualitative examples of misclassified words, along with detailed linguistic explanations of why they are challenging for the classifier?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, though there are a few instances of awkward phrasing.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The organization of the paper is logical and easy to follow.
* It would be helpful to explicitly state the computational resources used for training the models.
