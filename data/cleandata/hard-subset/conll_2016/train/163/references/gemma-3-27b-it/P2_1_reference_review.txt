## Review for "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:**

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize and demonstrate that word embeddings capture information about PoS, allowing for relatively accurate PoS prediction. They train a classifier on BNC data with PoS tags and embedding vectors, achieving high accuracy. More interestingly, they analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic “outliers” – words whose distributional patterns challenge conventional PoS assignments, and discuss potential annotation inconsistencies.  They also demonstrate that a PoS classifier can be trained *without* initial PoS tags, relying solely on distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS information within embeddings:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that distributional semantic models inherently encode information about traditional PoS categories, enabling accurate PoS prediction. This reinforces the connection between distributional semantics and linguistic structure.
2. **Identification of linguistic outliers & potential annotation errors:**  The analysis of misclassified words reveals potentially interesting cases where distributional patterns clash with traditional annotation. This highlights areas for re-evaluation of linguistic boundaries and potential errors in existing corpora.
3. **Feasibility of tag-free PoS prediction:** Showing that a reasonable PoS classifier can be trained *without* initial PoS tags is a promising result for low-resource scenarios and bootstrapping PoS tagging systems.



**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The reported accuracy scores (97.9% weighted F-score on training data, 91% on unseen test data, 99% on UD Treebank) are strong and support the central hypothesis.  The use of cross-validation and evaluation on multiple datasets enhances confidence in the findings.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words goes beyond simply reporting error rates.  The identification of specific patterns (e.g., proper nouns misclassified as common nouns, verbs as adjectives) offers valuable linguistic insights and suggests potential areas for refinement in annotation schemes.  The discussion of currency/percentage labels as numerals is particularly astute.
3. **Clear and Logical Structure:** The paper is well-organized, following a logical progression from introduction and related work to methodology, results, and discussion. The writing is clear and accessible.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors mention releasing the mapping between BNC and Universal tags online, facilitating reproducibility. (Although the anonymization prevents verification here)
5. **Demonstration of tag-free training:** The experiment demonstrating PoS prediction without initial tags opens avenues for applications in resource-poor languages and unsupervised learning scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** The core methodology – training embeddings, training a classifier, analyzing errors – is relatively standard. The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of these techniques to the question of PoS boundaries and the insightful error analysis, not the methods themselves.
2. **Superficial Exploration of Vector Components:** While the paper acknowledges that PoS information is distributed across multiple vector dimensions, the exploration of *which* dimensions are most relevant is relatively limited. The mention of component 94 for conjunctions feels somewhat anecdotal. A more systematic investigation of dimension correlations with linguistic features would strengthen the analysis.
3. **Reliance on Existing Annotation:**  The paper heavily relies on the BNC and UD Treebank annotations as the “ground truth.” While these are established corpora, their own limitations and potential biases could influence the results.  The paper acknowledges this to some extent, but a more critical discussion of the annotation schemes would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Tagging:** The paper lacks a direct comparison of the tag-free classifier’s performance to current state-of-the-art supervised PoS taggers. While the authors argue it's not meant to compete, a comparison would provide context for the achieved accuracy.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dimension Analysis:** Can you elaborate on your attempts to identify specific vector dimensions correlated with different PoS classes?  Were there other dimensions beyond component 94 that showed a strong association with particular PoS tags?  Can you share any details regarding techniques used to analyze these dimensions?
2. **Annotation Bias:**  You mention potential inconsistencies in BNC annotation. Could you provide more specific examples of annotation choices that seem problematic based on your analysis? What steps might be taken to address these inconsistencies in future corpora creation?
3. **Tag-Free Classifier Applications:**  What specific low-resource scenarios do you envision for the tag-free PoS prediction model?  What minimal amount of manual annotation would be required to bootstrap a functional PoS tagging system using this approach?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling rate)? Did you experiment with different parameter settings?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-chosen and relevant.
*   The figures are clear and contribute to the understanding of the results.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of distributional semantics in capturing all aspects of PoS (e.g., syntactic constraints, morphological features).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable exploration of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the methodology is not groundbreaking, the insightful error analysis and demonstration of tag-free prediction are significant contributions.  The paper is well-written and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
