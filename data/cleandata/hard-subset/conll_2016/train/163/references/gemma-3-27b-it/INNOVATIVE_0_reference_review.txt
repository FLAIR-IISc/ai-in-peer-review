## Review

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantics and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, positing that semantic models trained on raw text can reveal insights into the often blurry boundaries between grammatical categories. The authors train a skipgram model on the BNC and use it to both predict PoS tags and identify “outlier” words – those misclassified by the model – as potential areas where traditional tagging conventions are questionable. 

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper convincingly shows that pre-trained distributional semantic models *do* encode information relevant to PoS, achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction without explicit tagging during model training. This reinforces the distributional hypothesis in a grammatical context.
2. **Identification of annotation inconsistencies:** The analysis of misclassified words provides a novel method for identifying potential errors or areas of ambiguity within existing PoS annotated corpora (specifically the BNC).  The authors highlight specific cases – numerals representing years, adjectival verbs (participles) – where the distributional model’s ‘disagreement’ signals a potential re-evaluation of tagging choices.
3. **Exploration of distributed PoS representation:** The work attempts to understand *how* PoS information is encoded within the embedding space, demonstrating that it isn’t localized to a few dimensions but rather distributed across many.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  While the connection between distributional semantics and syntax isn’t entirely new, the focus on *using* misclassification as a tool for linguistic analysis – actively seeking out the words that *don't* fit neatly into traditional categories – is a genuinely innovative approach. This is a bold move, prioritizing discovery over incremental improvement.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported accuracy of the PoS predictor is impressive, particularly given that it’s trained on a purely unsupervised representation. The consistent performance across different datasets (BNC and UD Treebank) lends credence to the findings.
3. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is the strongest part of the paper. The examples presented are well-chosen and illustrate concrete cases where distributional semantics can challenge conventional linguistic wisdom.  Identifying systematic errors in the BNC tagging is a valuable contribution.
4. **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.  The inclusion of figures and tables enhances clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Depth:** While the paper raises interesting questions about PoS boundaries, it doesn’t engage deeply with existing theoretical frameworks for understanding grammatical categories (e.g., prototype theory, fuzzy grammar).  A more robust theoretical grounding would strengthen the claims.
2. **Methodological Simplification:** The choice of logistic regression as the classifier, while understandable for simplicity, feels somewhat limiting. Exploring more sophisticated classification techniques might reveal further nuances in the relationship between embeddings and PoS.
3. **Incomplete Validation:** The robustness claim, while supported by the UD Treebank results, could be further strengthened by testing on a wider range of languages and corpora, particularly those with different linguistic typologies.  The focus is heavily on English.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Error Analysis:** The paper presents its findings on BNC tagging errors as novel. A more thorough comparison to existing studies of BNC tagging inconsistencies would clarify the added value of the distributional semantic approach. It’s unclear how much of their findings are already known in the corpus linguistics community.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the “outlier” words are those that “differ from prototypical words of their parts of speech.” How exactly is ‘prototypical’ defined beyond being correctly classified by the model?  Could you elaborate on the characteristics of these prototypical words?
2.  Regarding the identified inconsistencies in BNC tagging, have you contacted the BNC curators to share your findings?  What was their response?
3.  You mention plans to extend the research to other languages. Which languages are you considering, and what challenges do you anticipate in adapting this approach to languages with very different morphological or syntactic structures than English?
4.  While you demonstrate that PoS information is distributed across many vector dimensions, can you pinpoint any specific dimensions that seem to consistently contribute to separating certain PoS classes (beyond the conjunction example)? Would dimensionality reduction techniques help visualize these relationships?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the manual mappings between BNC and Universal tags is broken in the submitted version.
*   Minor: The phrase "judging by absolute amount" is slightly awkward. Consider revising to "in terms of absolute frequency."



I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While not without its weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the insightful error analysis outweigh the concerns. The paper presents a genuinely new perspective on the interplay between semantics and syntax and offers a potentially valuable tool for corpus linguistics research. The authors are prioritizing exploration and discovery, which I find highly commendable.
