- Hypothesis: word embeddings encode substantial PoS information enabling classification and detection of PoS deviations.
- Method: train skip-gram on BNC, use embeddings to train a PoS classifier, analyze misclassifications, and build a distributional-only PoS tagger.
- Contribution: empirically demonstrates that distributional semantic models capture PoS information with high accuracy.
- Contribution: proposes using classifier misclassifications to identify linguistic outliers and annotation inconsistencies.
- Contribution: shows feasibility of a PoS tagger based solely on distributional information, useful for low-resource settings.
- Strength: strong empirical results (high F-scores on BNC and UD) supporting the central claim.
- Strength: insightful error analysis interpreting category boundaries and potential annotation errors.
- Strength: clear methodology and replication potential, with mapping between BNC and Universal tags released.
- Strength: preliminary exploration suggesting PoS information is distributed across embedding dimensions.
- Strength: novel conceptual approach to PoS tagging without contextual features, even if not state-of-the-art.
- Weakness: core idea is not entirely novel; novelty is mainly in error analysis and standalone tagger.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to stronger, context-aware baselines (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF).
- Weakness: reliance on BNC raises concerns about generalizability and corpus-specific biases.
- Weakness: limited exploration of skip-gram hyperparameter sensitivity (window size, dimensionality, negative sampling).
- Weakness: superficial analysis of which embedding dimensions/features are most informative for PoS prediction.
- Question: please compare the distributional tagger to standard context-aware PoS taggers trained on the same data.
- Question: have you tested generalizability by training/evaluating on corpora other than the BNC?
- Question: can you interpret the most important vector components linguistically or identify patterns associated with dimensions?
- Question: how sensitive are results to skip-gram hyperparameters and did you explore ranges for them?
- Question: provide more examples and linguistic motivations for error categories (e.g., numerals vs nouns, adjectives vs nouns).
- Additional: figures are visually uninformative and should be improved.
- Additional: discuss limitations of the Universal Dependencies tagset as a possible source of misclassification.
- Additional: clarify what was anonymized in the submission.
- Overall: well-executed and insightful study; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing weaknesses and questions.
