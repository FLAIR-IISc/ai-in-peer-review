The paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, aiming to redefine PoS classes through data-driven analysis. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC and use a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags, analyzing misclassifications to identify linguistic "outliers" and potential inconsistencies in existing annotations. The primary claimed contribution is demonstrating that distributional models *contain* information about PoS classes, allowing for the identification of words with ambiguous PoS assignments and potential annotation errors. A secondary contribution is the investigation of which vector components are most relevant to PoS affiliation. A third, smaller contribution, is the demonstration of reasonable PoS prediction accuracy *without* contextual information, solely based on word embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Detailed Error Analysis:** The analysis of misclassified words is the strongest aspect of the paper. The authors don’t simply report accuracy numbers; they delve into *why* the model makes errors, revealing interesting cases like passive participles, currency amounts, and adverbs erroneously tagged as adjectives. This provides valuable linguistic insight.
2. **Reproducibility:** The authors explicitly state they manually created mappings between BNC and Universal tags and released them online. This detail is crucial for reproducibility. The clear description of the training process (vector size, negative samples, iterations) also enhances reproducibility.
3. **Novel Application of Distributional Semantics:** While distributional semantics is well-established, applying it to *re-evaluate* existing PoS tagsets and identify potential inconsistencies is a worthwhile pursuit. The idea of using distributional models as a tool for linguistic refinement, rather than just prediction, is novel.
4. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that distributional models, even without explicit PoS information during training, encode information relevant to PoS. The accuracy of the PoS predictor trained solely on embeddings, while not state-of-the-art, is a significant result.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology – training word embeddings and using a classifier – is standard practice. The skip-gram model and logistic regression classifier are not themselves novel contributions. The paper's strength lies in the *application* of these methods to a specific linguistic problem, but the methods themselves lack innovation.
2. **Superficial Vector Component Analysis:** The analysis of vector components, while mentioned as a contribution, is weak. The authors identify component 94 as relevant to conjunctions, but then admit this is a solitary case. The discussion of the number of components needed for adequate accuracy is also insufficient; simply stating a plateau around k=100 lacks rigorous analysis. A more thorough investigation of component meaning is needed.
3. **Over-Reliance on BNC and Limited External Validation:** While testing on the UD Treebank is positive, the reliance on the BNC for both training and initial testing is a concern. The BNC’s tagging conventions, as highlighted in the error analysis, potentially influence the results. More extensive validation on diverse corpora is needed to demonstrate the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Figures 1 and 2, while illustrating prototypical vectors, lack clear labeling and explanation. What do the axes represent? What’s the significance of the peak near ‘100’? Improved visualization and explanation would enhance understanding. The tables are also not referenced sufficiently in the text.
5. **Minor Inconsistencies/Imprecisions:** The phrase "10-fold crossvalidation on the training set" is slightly misleading. Cross-validation implies evaluating on unseen data, which isn't the case if it's done *on* the training set. It's simply repeated training and evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed interpretation of the key vector components identified as relevant to PoS affiliation? What linguistic features do these components seem to capture?
2.  You mention inconsistencies in BNC tagging. How would these inconsistencies affect your findings? Could you quantify the impact of these inconsistencies?
3. Have you investigated the performance of different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText) on this task? Would alternative models yield different insights?
4. How sensitive are your results to the choice of hyperparameters (vector size, window size, negative samples) during embedding training? Did you perform a hyperparameter search?
5.  You note the similarity between proper and common nouns. Could you explore using additional features (e.g., capitalization, presence in a gazetteer) to improve the distinction between these classes?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references section is incomplete (e.g., Aarts & McMahon, Biber et al., Burnard are missing complete citation information).
*   The anonymous link for the mappings between BNC and Universal tags should be verified and accessible.
*   The discussion of the "majority class baseline" classifier is somewhat unhelpful. A more relevant baseline would be a simple lookup table based on corpus frequencies.



**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents interesting observations and a useful error analysis, the lack of methodological novelty and the limited external validation are significant weaknesses. The contributions, while valuable, do not justify acceptance in a highly competitive venue like CoNLL. The authors would significantly strengthen the paper by addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by conducting more comprehensive external validation and providing a more in-depth analysis of the vector components.
