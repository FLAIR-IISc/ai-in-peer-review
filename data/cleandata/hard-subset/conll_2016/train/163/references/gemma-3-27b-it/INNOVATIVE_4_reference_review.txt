This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models and traditional parts-of-speech (PoS), ultimately arguing that these models contain surprisingly rich information about syntactic categories, even allowing for a reasonably accurate PoS tagger trained *without* explicit syntactic supervision. The core contribution is demonstrating that unsupervised distributional semantics can effectively capture PoS information, and that analyzing errors reveals interesting linguistic nuances and potential annotation inconsistencies. A second contribution is the exploration of which vector components are most salient for PoS prediction.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** This work takes a genuinely interesting and relatively unexplored approach. While it's known distributional semantics *encodes* syntactic information, the idea of leveraging this for PoS discovery and analysis, and particularly building a reasonably effective tagger *without* supervised training beyond initial embedding construction, is novel. It challenges the necessity of heavily engineered feature sets and supervised learning pipelines for a fundamental NLP task. This boldness is highly commendable.
2. **Error Analysis as Insight:** The paper doesn’t shy away from analyzing the errors made by its classifier. Crucially, this error analysis isn’t simply a post-hoc justification; it’s the *driving force* of the investigation. Discovering potential annotation errors in the BNC, highlighting ambiguous word classes (like nouns acting as adjectives), and identifying patterns in misclassifications (e.g., currency amounts as numerals) are insightful and demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of linguistic challenges. The connection to UD treebank observations is also a strong point.
3. **Empirical Validation (Despite Limitations):** While the F-score isn’t state-of-the-art, the 91% F-score on unseen data and 99% on UD data demonstrates the method's effectiveness. The comparison to a baseline logistic regression classifier and the ablation study with varying vector components add weight to the claims, even if the experiments aren't exhaustive. The fact that it performs *at all* without explicit tagging information is impressive.
4. **Clear Presentation & Logical Flow:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction sets the stage effectively, the related work is relevant, and the analysis of outliers is compelling. The authors clearly articulate their hypothesis, methods, and findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper reports a single set of hyperparameters for the Skipgram model (vector size 300, 10 negative samples, window size 2). While the focus is on the *concept* rather than pushing SOTA performance, a more thorough exploration of hyperparameters might reveal further improvements and solidify the robustness of the findings.
2. **Model Size & Computational Cost:** The requirement for training a 300-dimensional embedding model necessitates significant computational resources. While not a fatal flaw, it limits the scalability and accessibility of this approach, particularly for lower-resource languages. A discussion of methods to mitigate this cost (e.g., dimensionality reduction) would be beneficial.
3. **Over-Reliance on BNC/UD English:** The results are predominantly evaluated on English, specifically the BNC and UD Treebank. While these are valuable resources, the generalizability of the findings to other languages with different morphological structures and syntactic patterns remains uncertain. While the authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, it's a significant limitation.
4. **Shallow Discussion of Vector Components:** While the analysis of vector components is initiated, it remains somewhat superficial. Simply identifying component 94 as being relevant for conjunctions isn't particularly insightful without further investigation into *what* linguistic features that component encodes. Deeper analysis with probing techniques (e.g. using linear probes) would strengthen this aspect.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. You mention inconsistencies in BNC tagging. Could you provide more specific examples of these inconsistencies, beyond those already presented, and potentially quantify their frequency?
2. How sensitive are your results to the choice of lemma normalization strategy? Did you experiment with different lemmatization approaches, and if so, how did they impact performance?
3. The accuracy drops significantly when moving from the training set to the test set. Can you analyze the characteristics of the test set that contribute to this performance decrease?
4. You state that further research will explore different hyperparameters for training the embedding model. Do you anticipate that these changes will largely affect accuracy, or will they reveal other interesting phenomena?



**Additional Comments:**

* The use of Universal Dependencies for comparison is excellent.
* The formatting of the tables is a little inconsistent.
* The reference to Manning (2015) feels somewhat abrupt – it would be helpful to briefly contextualize its relevance.



Overall, this is a promising and thought-provoking paper. The novelty of the approach and the insightful error analysis outweigh the methodological limitations. Despite some areas for improvement, the work presents a compelling argument for the power of distributional semantics in understanding and potentially redefining our notions of parts of speech. **I recommend acceptance.**
