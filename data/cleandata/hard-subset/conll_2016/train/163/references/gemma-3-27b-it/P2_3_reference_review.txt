## Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:** This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize and demonstrate that word embeddings capture information about PoS, allowing for accurate PoS prediction. They further analyze misclassifications to identify boundary cases and potential inconsistencies in existing PoS annotation schemes. They also show that a classifier trained on these embeddings can achieve reasonable accuracy without relying on contextual information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical validation of PoS information within distributional semantics:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that word embeddings, trained unsupervisedly, encode substantial information about traditional PoS tags. This reinforces the connection between distributional and linguistic knowledge.
2. **Identification of linguistic boundary cases & annotation inconsistencies:** The analysis of misclassified words highlights potentially problematic cases in PoS annotation (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, ambiguous adjective/verb classifications) and provides a novel method for detecting such issues using distributional models.
3. **Demonstration of surprisingly effective zero-shot PoS prediction:** The ability to predict PoS tags with reasonable accuracy *without* contextual information, solely based on word embeddings, is a noteworthy result that suggests the robustness of the PoS signal encoded in these models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The authors employ a well-established corpus (BNC) and a robust evaluation methodology (cross-validation, testing on an independent dataset – UD Treebank). The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The consistent performance across datasets adds confidence to their findings.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassifications is a key strength. It goes beyond simply reporting performance metrics and delves into *why* the model makes errors, revealing interesting linguistic phenomena and potential issues with existing annotation schemes. This analysis is well-reasoned and provides valuable linguistic insights.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner. The organization flows logically, building from motivation to experimentation and analysis.
4. **Novel Application of Distributional Semantics:** Applying distributional semantic models not just *to* PoS tagging, but *to investigate the nature of PoS classes themselves* is a novel and valuable contribution. The work pushes the boundaries of how we can leverage these models for linguistic analysis.
5. **Demonstrates Robustness:**  The results with the Universal Dependencies Treebank show the approach generalizes well beyond the training data, highlighting the robustness of the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** While the *application* is novel, the methods employed (Skipgram, logistic regression) are fairly standard. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a factor to consider. The core technical contribution isn't a breakthrough in model architecture or training.
2. **Superficial exploration of vector components:** While they mention dimensions correlating with linguistic features (citing Tsvetkov et al., 2015), the investigation of *which* components are most important for PoS prediction feels somewhat shallow.  The attempt to identify key components (Section 5) doesn't provide particularly compelling insights beyond the observation that the information is distributed.
3. **Reliance on Universal Dependencies for a “Gold Standard”:** While UD is a valuable resource, relying on it to validate the BNC tagging introduces a potential bias. Different annotation schemes exist, and using UD as a definitive "truth" could overlook nuances in the BNC tagging conventions. A more nuanced comparison acknowledging these differences would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers:** While the authors highlight the zero-shot aspect, a more direct comparison to the performance of a state-of-the-art, context-aware PoS tagger (e.g., using BERT) would better contextualize their results.  Showing how much performance is sacrificed by *not* using contextual information would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific differences between the BNC and Universal Dependencies annotation schemes that might contribute to the discrepancies observed in your error analysis?
2.  You mention that the PoS information is distributed among many vector components.  Could you explore more sophisticated techniques (e.g., feature selection, dimensionality reduction) to identify the most informative components for each PoS tag and provide more detailed analysis of their semantic content?
3. How sensitive are your results to the choice of hyperparameters for the Skipgram model (e.g., vector size, window size, negative sampling)?  Did you experiment with different configurations and, if so, how did they affect performance?
4.  Could you present a direct comparison of your classifier's performance (even in a limited setting) against a state-of-the-art contextualized PoS tagger (e.g., based on BERT or similar architectures)?



**Additional Comments:**

* The citation formatting is inconsistent.
* The figures (1 & 2) are a bit difficult to interpret – improving their visual clarity would be helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and traditional linguistic categories.  While the methods are not groundbreaking, the thorough empirical evaluation, insightful error analysis, and novel application of distributional models make this a worthwhile contribution to the CoNLL community. I recommend **Acceptance**.
