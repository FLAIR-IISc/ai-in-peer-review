- Paper demonstrates that word embeddings encode part-of-speech information and can be used to predict PoS classes accurately with a classifier.
- Main contribution: Empirical evidence that unsupervised distributional models capture traditional PoS categories.
- Main contribution: Error analysis of misclassified words exposes annotation inconsistencies and ambiguity in corpora.
- Main contribution: Identification of PoS boundary cases and linguistic nuances (e.g., participles as adjectives, numeral phenomena).
- Strength: Strong empirical results (high F-scores on training, unseen data, and UD Treebank) supporting the core hypothesis.
- Strength: Rigorous methodology using BNC, Skip-gram embeddings, frequency control, and 10-fold cross-validation.
- Strength: Insightful, detailed error analysis that provides linguistic insights and compares to Stanford tagger errors.
- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful examples and figures.
- Strength: Demonstration of a PoS tagger trained from embeddings alone, useful for resource-poor languages.
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—standard embeddings and logistic regression; contribution is mainly application/analysis.
- Weakness: No direct comparison to state-of-the-art taggers (e.g., BiLSTM/Transformer) to assess practical competitiveness.
- Weakness: Superficial analysis of which embedding components encode PoS information; more in-depth component analysis needed.
- Weakness: Results may be corpus-specific (BNC); more evaluation on varied corpora/annotation schemes is needed.
- Weakness: Definition of “outlier” (misclassified words) is somewhat arbitrary; a more principled definition is required.
- Question: Provide direct performance comparison (precision/recall/F-score) against state-of-the-art taggers on UD or other benchmarks?
- Question: Can you elaborate which embedding dimensions consistently correlate with PoS tags and provide visualizations?
- Question: How sensitive are findings to embedding hyperparameters (vector size, window, negative sampling)?
- Question: Can you give specific examples of BNC annotation inconsistencies found in the misclassifications?
- Question: Would adding morphological features (prefixes/suffixes) to the classifier improve performance?
- Additional comment: Writing is generally clear but occasionally verbose; tighten some sentences.
- Additional comment: Data and code links (BNC-to-Universal mappings) are essential for reproducibility and should be prominent.
- Overall assessment: Solid, insightful paper with valuable empirical analysis; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
