## Peer Review: Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings inherently encode PoS information and can be used to identify lexical outliers – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned PoS. They train a skip-gram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, analyze misclassifications to identify potentially problematic cases, and demonstrate the feasibility of building a PoS tagger directly from distributional representations without relying on contextual information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence, through a well-designed classification experiment and error analysis, that word embeddings trained on raw text contain significant information about word classes. This isn't entirely novel, but the scale and thoroughness of the analysis is valuable.
2. **Identification of potential annotation errors and boundary cases:** The analysis of misclassified words reveals interesting instances where the BNC annotation might be questionable (e.g., adverbs incorrectly tagged as adjectives) or where words genuinely lie on the boundary between PoS categories (e.g., passive participles). This is a practical contribution offering insights for corpus refinement.
3. **Feasibility of context-free PoS prediction:** Demonstrating a reasonable level of accuracy in PoS tagging using *only* distributional information, without considering the surrounding context, is a significant, if preliminary, result. This suggests potential applications in resource-poor settings.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper isn’t solely theoretical; it is grounded in robust experimental results. The authors utilize a large, well-established corpus (BNC) and a clearly defined methodology. The 97% F-score on the training set and 91% on an unseen test set demonstrate the efficacy of their approach.
2. **Thorough Error Analysis:**  The deep dive into misclassified words is a major strength.  Identifying specific patterns of errors (e.g., currency amounts tagged as nouns, proper nouns as common nouns) provides valuable linguistic insights and potential avenues for improving both PoS taggers and corpus annotation schemes.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The methodology is explained in sufficient detail to allow for replication. The connection between related work and the authors’ contribution is also clearly established.
4. **Novel Context-Free Tagger:** The successful construction of a PoS tagger without contextual features is a compelling result, showcasing the power of distributional semantics.  The accuracy achieved, while not state-of-the-art, is notable given the constraints.
5. **Exploration of Feature Distribution:** The analysis of feature importance – showing that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in a few – is a useful finding, supporting the idea that embeddings capture nuanced semantic and grammatical information.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** The idea that distributional semantics encodes syntactic information isn't entirely new.  Prior work (cited in the paper) already demonstrated this.  The novelty lies more in the *application* and the depth of analysis, rather than a groundbreaking theoretical insight.
2. **Reliance on Pre-Tagged Data:** The initial classifier is trained on data already tagged with PoS, which somewhat limits the claim of discovering truly “new” definitions of PoS. While the context-free tagger is a step towards addressing this, the initial training still depends on pre-existing annotations.
3. **Overly focused on BNC idiosyncrasies:** The paper spends a considerable amount of time identifying potentially incorrect tags *within the BNC*. While valuable for corpus linguistics, this focus might detract from the broader implications of the research. A stronger emphasis on generalizable findings would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While acknowledging that the context-free tagger doesn't achieve state-of-the-art performance, a more direct comparison to existing PoS taggers (even with contextual information) would provide a clearer benchmark and demonstrate the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper describes training parameters (vector size, negative samples, etc.) but doesn’t present an ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention inconsistencies in BNC tagging and highlight examples. Have you quantified the extent of these inconsistencies? (e.g., what percentage of the BNC corpus might benefit from revisions based on your analysis?)
2.  The context-free tagger achieves 84% accuracy. How does the performance of this tagger change when a small amount of contextual information is introduced (e.g., using a simple bi-gram model)?
3.  You state the PoS information is distributed among many embedding components. Could you elaborate on whether specific combinations of components are more predictive of certain PoS tags than others? Are there identifiable “sub-spaces” within the embedding space associated with particular grammatical categories?
4.  You mention potential applications in resource-poor languages. What specific strategies would be employed to adapt this approach to languages with limited tagged data?




**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 and 2 are visually helpful but could benefit from clearer labeling of the axes.
*   The references section seems incomplete. Some citations are mentioned in the text but not listed in the references (e.g., Houston, 1985).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the core idea isn't entirely novel, the thorough empirical analysis, identification of annotation inconsistencies, and demonstration of context-free tagging are significant achievements. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
