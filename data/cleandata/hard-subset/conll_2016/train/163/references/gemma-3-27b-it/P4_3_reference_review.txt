This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) classes, aiming to refine PoS definitions through data-driven analysis. The authors train a skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC) and explore how well word embeddings capture PoS information, both by training a classifier to predict PoS tags and by analyzing misclassifications to identify words with ambiguous PoS assignments. The primary contributions are: (1) Demonstration that distributional semantic models encode significant information about PoS, achieving high accuracy in PoS prediction; (2) Identification of linguistic "outliers" – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their conventional PoS, revealing potential annotation inconsistencies or fuzzy boundaries between classes; and (3) Exploration of the distribution of PoS information across vector dimensions, suggesting it's not localized to specific features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates convincingly, through robust classification performance (F-score of 0.979 on the training set, 0.91 on a held-out test set, and 0.99 on the UD Treebank), that distributional semantic models effectively capture information about PoS. This is a significant finding supporting the distributional hypothesis in the context of syntactic categories. The consistent performance across different datasets strengthens the generalizability of the results.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a major strength. Identifying outliers – words that the model assigns a different PoS than the standard annotation – provides valuable insights into the nuances of PoS boundaries and potential annotation errors. The specific examples given (e.g., currency amounts as numerals, vague boundaries between nouns and adjectives) are compelling and illustrate the limitations of traditional PoS categorization. The comparison with errors produced by the Stanford tagger provides a particularly useful contrast.
3. **Novel Application of Distributional Models:**  While the use of distributional models for PoS tagging isn't entirely new, the focus on *redefining* PoS classes and *identifying outliers* represents a novel application. This shifts the perspective from simply predicting tags to understanding the underlying linguistic structure encoded in the models. The successful training of a PoS predictor using only distributional vectors, without reliance on contextual information, is particularly impressive.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a well-defined experimental setup, including careful data preprocessing (converting to the Universal Tagset), hyperparameter tuning (skipgram model), and evaluation metrics (F-score, accuracy). The weighting of training instances by corpus frequency is a thoughtful approach to address class imbalance and improve classifier performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** The core methodology—training a skipgram model and using it for PoS prediction—is fairly standard. While the application to PoS boundary analysis is novel, the technical contribution related to the model itself is minimal. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it impacts the overall significance of the work.
2. **Dependence on Existing Annotations:** The identification of “outliers” is fundamentally reliant on the quality and consistency of the BNC and UD Treebank annotations.  While the authors point out some potential inconsistencies, there’s limited discussion of how annotation bias might influence the results. The paper could benefit from a more critical assessment of the source corpora.
3. **Limited Exploration of Vector Space:**  The analysis of vector components is somewhat superficial. The authors identify some components correlated with conjunctions but don’t offer a comprehensive interpretation of what these dimensions represent linguistically. A more detailed investigation into the semantic and syntactic properties captured by different vector dimensions would strengthen the findings. The statement regarding needing "at least one hundred components" is vague, and a more systematic exploration of the trade-off between number of components and performance could be valuable.
4. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art PoS Taggers:** While the authors demonstrate high accuracy, a direct comparison with the performance of current state-of-the-art PoS taggers (which *do* leverage contextual information) is missing. This would provide a better benchmark for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the identified outliers to the choice of embedding hyperparameters (e.g., vector size, window size, negative sampling)? Would different hyperparameter settings lead to the identification of different outliers?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed linguistic interpretation of the vector components identified as being particularly relevant to PoS affiliation? Are there any clear semantic or syntactic patterns associated with these dimensions?
3.  Have you considered exploring methods to automatically identify potentially erroneous annotations in the BNC or UD Treebank based on the outlier analysis?
4.   You mention that the classifier trained on distributional vectors doesn't leverage contextual information. Have you explored methods to incorporate contextual features into your model to see if performance can be further improved?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (Figures 1 & 2) are somewhat low-resolution and difficult to interpret. Higher-resolution versions would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the most frequent misclassifications in the UD Treebank experiment.
*   The reference list is incomplete; it stops mid-sentence.
