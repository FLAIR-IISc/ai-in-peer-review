This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, with the goal of refining PoS definitions and identifying inconsistencies in existing annotations. The authors train a Continuous Skipgram model on the British National Corpus (BNC) and use the resulting word embeddings to both predict PoS tags and analyze misclassifications, ultimately aiming to reveal nuanced information about PoS boundaries. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that distributional models *do* encode significant PoS information, allowing for reasonably accurate PoS prediction *without* relying on contextual information beyond the distributional representation itself. A secondary contribution is the identification of specific cases of annotation inconsistencies in the BNC, highlighted by the model's misclassifications. A third, smaller contribution is the analysis of feature importance within the embeddings related to PoS prediction.

**Strengths:**

1. **Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The core finding – that unsupervised distributional models capture information relevant to PoS tagging – is significant. Achieving an 84% accuracy on unseen data (UD Treebank) using only distributional vectors for prediction, without any contextual input, is a strong result. This supports the hypothesis that semantic representations derived from corpora implicitly reflect syntactic information. The experimental setup, particularly using a logistic regression classifier, is appropriate for evaluating this claim.

2. **Error Analysis and Annotation Inconsistency Detection:** The thorough analysis of misclassified words provides valuable insights. The identification of specific annotation errors in the BNC (e.g., adverbs incorrectly tagged as adjectives) is a notable contribution, showcasing a practical application of the distributional approach.  Connecting these errors to linguistic subtleties (e.g., the ambiguous boundary between nouns and adjectives) is particularly compelling.  The comparison to error types found using the Stanford tagger demonstrates the complementary nature of the two approaches.

3. **Robustness and Generalizability:** The consistent performance across different datasets (BNC test set and UD Treebank) suggests a degree of robustness and generalizability in the approach. This is strengthened by the attempt to address potential frequency bias in training through weighting, and by explicitly acknowledging potential limitations of the approach (e.g., infrequent words, proper nouns).

4. **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and findings. The authors provide sufficient detail about the data, model parameters, and evaluation metrics to facilitate reproducibility. The release of the mappings between BNC and Universal tags is a positive step for accessibility.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The use of word embeddings for PoS tagging is not novel in itself. Prior work (extensively cited) has already established this connection. The primary novelty resides in the *specific* analysis of misclassifications and the application to identifying annotation errors, rather than the technique of using embeddings for PoS prediction. The paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize *how* this work extends beyond existing literature in terms of the depth or method of analysis.

2. **Classifier Simplification and Baseline Comparison:** While the simplicity of the logistic regression classifier is justified by the focus on the embeddings themselves, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art PoS taggers (beyond the Stanford tagger's confusion matrix) would strengthen the evaluation. Demonstrating the performance gap versus current systems, and *why* this gap exists, is crucial.  The presented accuracy (84%) is respectable, but it’s unclear how it compares to modern sequence tagging models.

3. **Feature Importance Analysis – Limited Insight:** The analysis of vector components related to PoS affiliation (Figure 3) is somewhat superficial. While it confirms that PoS information is distributed across multiple dimensions, the findings lack concrete linguistic interpretation. Simply stating that "at least one hundred components" are important doesn't offer much explanatory power. A deeper investigation into the semantic content of these key components would be valuable.

4. **Ambiguity around “Outliers”:** The definition of "outliers" (misclassified words) is somewhat vague. Are these genuinely linguistically interesting cases, or simply instances where the model struggles due to data sparsity or ambiguity?  More discussion of the limitations of using misclassification rate as a proxy for linguistic interestingness is needed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the distributional model-based PoS tagger compare to more advanced sequence tagging models (e.g., BiLSTM-CRF) trained on the same data, and what specific architectural or data-related factors contribute to any performance differences?

2.  Can you provide a more detailed interpretation of the most important vector components identified in Figure 3? Are there specific semantic themes or concepts that are consistently represented in these dimensions, and how do these relate to PoS classes?

3.  The paper identifies several annotation errors in the BNC. Have you considered attempting to automatically correct these errors using the distributional model, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be interesting to explore the impact of different embedding dimensions (e.g., 100, 500) on the performance and interpretability of the results.
*   The paper consistently refers to “Universal Dependencies” and the corresponding tagset, but a brief explanation of UD’s principles and advantages might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with this framework.
*   The reference list is slightly inconsistent in formatting.
