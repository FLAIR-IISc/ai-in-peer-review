This paper investigates the relationship between distributional semantics and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings contain information about PoS classes and can be used to identify outliers – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned tag. They train a skipgram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, and analyze misclassifications to uncover linguistic insights. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating a high degree of accuracy (F-score ~0.91 on unseen data) in predicting PoS tags from word embeddings alone, suggesting that distributional models capture significant syntactic information; (2) identifying and analyzing outliers – words misclassified by the model – revealing potential annotation errors and interesting linguistic cases (e.g., nouns behaving like adjectives, currency amounts tagged as nouns); and (3) showing that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions of the embedding space, rather than concentrated in a few.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently high accuracy of the PoS classifier (0.979 on training data, 0.91 on a held-out test set) is a significant finding. This clearly demonstrates that distributional models, even without explicit syntactic information, can encode substantial information about word classes. This is particularly impressive given the model's simplicity (logistic regression) and the unsupervised nature of the embeddings. 
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words is the paper’s strongest aspect. The authors don't simply report errors but delve into *why* they occur, uncovering potential annotation inconsistencies in the BNC and highlighting interesting linguistic phenomena like the ambiguity between nouns and adjectives or the treatment of currency amounts. This analysis goes beyond simply improving tagging accuracy and contributes to a deeper understanding of PoS boundaries.  The comparison to errors made by the Stanford tagger further strengthens this point.
3. **Reproducibility & Dataset Release:**  The authors explicitly state that mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags were released online, increasing reproducibility. They also detail the hyperparameters used for training the embedding model, allowing other researchers to replicate their experiments.
4. **Demonstration of Robustness:** Testing the classifier on the UD Treebank (after filtering out unseen words) and achieving a high F-score (0.99) showcases the generalizability of the approach. This indicates the learned representations are not overly specific to the BNC corpus.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Approach:** While the results are solid, the core idea of using distributional models to represent syntactic information is not entirely new (as acknowledged in the Related Work section). The novelty lies primarily in the *application* of these models to detailed error analysis and the identification of specific linguistic phenomena within the BNC. The authors don't propose a fundamentally new method for learning or representing syntactic information.
2. **Classifier Training Details Could Be More Transparent:** While hyperparameters are mentioned, the training process feels somewhat opaque. Specifically, the weighting scheme based on corpus frequency is described but lacks justification beyond alleviating class bias. A more detailed explanation of the rationale for this weighting, and perhaps an ablation study exploring different weighting schemes, would be beneficial.
3. **Superficial Discussion of Dimensionality:** The analysis of how many vector components contribute to PoS prediction is somewhat superficial. The authors state that information is "distributed among at least one hundred components" but provide little insight into *what* these components represent or how they contribute to the classification process. A more in-depth investigation into the semantic or syntactic properties of the most important dimensions would add significant value. The observation regarding component 94 being unique to conjunctions is interesting, but more examples would strengthen the argument.
4. **Limited Scope of Universal Dependencies Experiment:** The authors filter out words absent from the distributional model for the UD Treebank experiment, removing 27% of word types and 10% of tokens. While understandable, this significantly limits the scope and potentially biases the results. The impact of this filtering on the observed performance (F-score of 0.99) isn’t sufficiently discussed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the weighting scheme used during classifier training: Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using corpus frequency as the weight? Did you experiment with other weighting schemes, and if so, what were the results?
2.  You mention that component 94 is idiosyncratic to conjunctions. Are there other vector components that show similar unique associations with specific PoS tags? Could you provide a few more examples?
3.  The UD Treebank experiment filters out words not present in your distributional model. How would you expect the results to change if you had a mechanism to handle out-of-vocabulary words (e.g., using subword embeddings)?
4.  You discuss potential annotation errors in the BNC. Have you investigated whether similar errors exist in other corpora, such as the Penn Treebank, using your distributional approach?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures (1 and 2) are somewhat difficult to interpret. Adding labels to the axes and providing a more detailed explanation of what they represent would be helpful.
*   Consider explicitly stating the statistical significance of the performance improvements (e.g., compared to the majority class baseline).
*   The phrase "prototypical vectors" is used frequently. It might be useful to define this term more precisely.



Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper with valuable insights into the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the approach isn't groundbreaking, the thorough error analysis and demonstrable results make a compelling case for the utility of distributional models in understanding and refining our understanding of part of speech. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed with further experimentation and analysis. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions.
