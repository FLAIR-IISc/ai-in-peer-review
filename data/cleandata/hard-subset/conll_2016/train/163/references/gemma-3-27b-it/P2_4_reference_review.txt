## Review for "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:**

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize that word embeddings capture information about PoS, allowing for classification and identification of words that deviate from typical PoS patterns. They train a skipgram model on the BNC corpus, use it to train a PoS classifier, and then analyze misclassifications to identify potentially problematic cases or refine PoS boundaries. They also demonstrate the feasibility of building a PoS tagger based solely on distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of PoS Information in Embeddings:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence that word embeddings, trained in an unsupervised manner, encode significant information about PoS. The high accuracy of the PoS classifier trained on these embeddings is a strong indicator.
2. **Error Analysis for Linguistic Insight:** The detailed analysis of misclassified words offers valuable linguistic insights.  Identifying specific patterns in errors – such as passive participles being mistaken for adjectives or currency amounts being tagged as numerals – highlights areas where current PoS annotation conventions might be ambiguous or inconsistent.
3. **Feasibility of Distributional-Only PoS Tagging:** Demonstrating a reasonably accurate PoS tagger based solely on distributional information, without relying on contextual features, is a noteworthy achievement and suggests potential applications in low-resource settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently high F-scores achieved by the PoS classifier (0.979 on training data, 0.91 on a held-out test set, 0.99 on UD Treebank) strongly support the central hypothesis. This isn't just a theoretical argument; it's backed up by quantifiable results.
2. **Thorough Error Analysis:** The paper doesn’t simply present accuracy numbers. The in-depth analysis of misclassified words is the most valuable part of the work.  It goes beyond simple error counts and attempts to interpret the *reasons* for the errors, relating them to linguistic ambiguity and potential annotation issues.  This demonstrates a critical and thoughtful approach.
3. **Clear Methodology & Reproducibility:** The authors provide clear details about their data processing, model training, and evaluation procedures.  They even mention releasing the mappings between BNC and Universal tags, enhancing reproducibility.
4. **Novel Application of Distributional Models:**  While the use of distributional models for linguistic analysis isn’t entirely new, the specific focus on refining PoS boundaries and identifying annotation inconsistencies represents a valuable application.
5. **Demonstrated Robustness:** The successful transfer of the trained classifier to the UD Treebank showcases the robustness of the approach and suggests its generalizability beyond the BNC corpus.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Method:**  The core methodology – training word embeddings and using them for classification – is well-established.  The novelty lies more in the *application* to PoS boundary refinement and the insightful error analysis rather than a fundamentally new technique. While this isn't necessarily a fatal flaw, it should be acknowledged.
2. **Reliance on Pre-existing Tags:** While the distributional-only tagger is interesting, it is still *trained* on data with gold-standard PoS tags. The paper doesn’t fully address the bootstrapping problem – how to initiate the process in a truly unsupervised setting with no initial annotation.
3. **Superficial Discussion of Vector Components:** While the paper mentions that PoS information is distributed across many vector components, the analysis of *which* components are most relevant remains somewhat limited. The mention of component 94 for conjunctions feels like an isolated finding rather than a systematic investigation.  A more detailed exploration of component correlations with PoS features would be beneficial.
4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art Tagging:** The paper does not compare the performance of their distributional-only tagger against state-of-the-art supervised PoS taggers. This would help to better contextualize their results and understand the trade-offs.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the challenges of bootstrapping a distributional-only PoS tagger in a completely unsupervised setting? What strategies might be employed to address this issue?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the embedding components that are most strongly correlated with different PoS tags? Perhaps a visualization or a table summarizing these relationships?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of embedding parameters (vector size, window size, negative sampling rate)? Did you explore a range of hyperparameters, and if so, what were the findings?
4.  You mention inconsistencies in BNC annotation.  Did you investigate whether these inconsistencies are reflected in other corpora, such as the Penn Treebank?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The inclusion of figures and tables is helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the core methodology isn’t groundbreaking, the strong empirical results, insightful error analysis, and demonstrated feasibility of distributional-only PoS tagging make it a valuable contribution to the field. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed in future work.  The paper offers a compelling argument that distributional semantic models can be a powerful tool for both understanding and refining our linguistic classifications.
