This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models and part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, hypothesizing that word embeddings capture information about PoS classes. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC, use it to train a PoS classifier, and analyze misclassifications to identify boundary cases and potential annotation errors. They also demonstrate that a classifier trained *without* PoS tags can achieve reasonable accuracy.

The primary contribution of this work is a detailed analysis of errors made by a PoS classifier trained on distributional embeddings, linking these errors to known linguistic challenges such as the ambiguity between nouns and adjectives and the categorization of numerals. This analysis offers some interesting, albeit limited, insights into the limitations of both current annotation schemes and distributional models. A secondary contribution is demonstrating that reasonable PoS prediction is possible *without* initial PoS tag information, suggesting the potential of these models for low-resource languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Careful Error Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting accuracy numbers and delve into *why* the model makes certain errors. Identifying cases like currency amounts misclassified as numerals, or the issues with passive participles, is a valuable contribution, pointing to potential areas for improvement in both tagging schemes and model design. This detailed inspection of the model’s failures provides more concrete insights than simply claiming distributional models “contain information” about PoS.
2. **Robustness Demonstration:** Achieving reasonable performance on the UD Treebank after training on the BNC lends some credibility to the claim that the learned relationships are not merely corpus-specific. This demonstrates a degree of generalizability, which is encouraging.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the data processing steps, model training parameters, and evaluation procedures. The use of a standard corpus (BNC) and established tagsets (Universal POS) facilitates reproducibility and comparison with other work.
4. **Comparison with a Traditional Tagger:** The comparison to the Stanford POS tagger, while not exhaustive, provides a useful benchmark and highlights differences in the types of errors identified by each approach. This comparison reinforces the value of the distributional approach in revealing different facets of PoS ambiguity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Method:** The core method – training word embeddings and using them for PoS tagging – is well-established. The authors do not propose any novel architectural changes or training techniques for the embeddings themselves. The classifier used is a simple logistic regression, and no attempt is made to compare to more sophisticated classification methods. This reliance on established techniques diminishes the overall impact of the work.
2. **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare to a majority-class baseline, a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art PoS taggers (e.g., BERT-based taggers, BiLSTMs) is necessary to establish the true value of their approach. The accuracy of 93.8% on the training set is not particularly impressive given the current landscape of PoS tagging. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the distributional approach offers any real advantage.
3. **Over-Reliance on Established Corpora:** The entire evaluation is performed on the BNC and UD Treebank, both of which are relatively well-behaved corpora. It's unclear how the approach would perform on more challenging datasets with greater noise, ambiguity, or different linguistic characteristics. A demonstration of performance on a more diverse set of corpora would strengthen the claims.
4. **Superficial Analysis of Vector Components:** The discussion of vector components and their relation to PoS tags is limited. While the authors identify component 94 as being relevant to conjunctions, this seems like an isolated finding. A more systematic analysis of the embedding space to understand which dimensions encode PoS information would be beneficial. The claim that PoS information is "distributed among at least one hundred components" feels unsubstantiated without more rigorous analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost of training your model and classifier compared to a comparable BERT-based PoS tagger?
2.  Have you explored more sophisticated classification methods beyond logistic regression? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide more examples of the insights gained from analyzing the vector components and their relationship to PoS tags?
4.  How does the performance of your model change when using different embedding dimensions (e.g., 100, 600)? Does the optimal dimensionality vary depending on the corpus or task?
