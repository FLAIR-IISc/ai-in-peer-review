## Review of "Redefining part-of-speech classes with distributional semantic models"

**Summary:** This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models (word embeddings) and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging. The authors hypothesize and demonstrate that word embeddings capture information about PoS, enabling accurate PoS prediction. They train a classifier on embeddings derived from the BNC, analyze misclassifications to identify linguistic nuances and potential annotation errors, and show the feasibility of a PoS tagger relying solely on distributional information.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of PoS information within distributional models:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence, with F-scores consistently above 0.9, that word embeddings encode significant information about lexical category. While previous work hinted at this, this work offers a thorough and quantitative validation.
2. **Identification of boundary cases and potential annotation errors:** The analysis of misclassified words offers valuable linguistic insights, highlighting ambiguities in PoS boundaries (e.g., nouns vs. adjectives, numerals vs. nouns) and uncovering potential inconsistencies within the BNC corpus.
3. **Feasibility of a context-free PoS tagger:**  The demonstration of a reasonably accurate PoS predictor based solely on word embeddings, without leveraging contextual information, is a significant result. It suggests potential applications in low-resource scenarios and offers a novel perspective on PoS tagging.



**Strengths:**

1. **Solid empirical validation:** The consistent high accuracy of the PoS classifier, both on the training and test data, and when applied to a different corpus (UD Treebank), is a strong argument in favor of the core hypothesis. The use of weighted instances to address class imbalance is a thoughtful methodological choice.
2. **Insightful error analysis:**  The detailed analysis of misclassified words is a key strength. It's not merely about reporting errors, but about interpreting them linguistically, leading to potentially valuable insights regarding PoS boundaries and annotation quality.  The identification of currency amounts and years misclassified as numerals, and the discussion of passive participles, are particularly compelling.
3. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is logically organized, and the methodology is explained clearly. The authors effectively connect their work to relevant literature and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.
4. **Novel approach to PoS tagging:**  The exploration of a context-free PoS tagger, based solely on distributional semantics, represents a novel direction in the field. The results, while not state-of-the-art, demonstrate the potential of this approach.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors released the mapping between BNC tags and Universal tags, which allows for easier replication of results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty in the core method:** Using word embeddings for PoS tagging is not entirely new. The core method – training embeddings and then using them for classification – is a fairly standard approach. The contribution lies more in the thoroughness of the analysis and the specific insights derived, rather than a groundbreaking methodological innovation.
2. **Reliance on pre-existing PoS tags:** The entire evaluation relies on the accuracy of the initial PoS annotations in the BNC and UD Treebank. While the error analysis flags potential inconsistencies, it doesn't offer a way to *resolve* them independently of the initial tags.  A more robust validation would involve comparing the model’s output to a completely independent, manually-annotated gold standard.
3. **Limited exploration of embedding parameters:** The paper focuses on a single set of embedding parameters (vector size = 300, window size = 2, etc.). Exploring the impact of different parameters on performance could provide valuable insights.  For example, a smaller vector size might highlight the most essential PoS-related features.
4. **Lack of comparison with standard PoS taggers:** While the authors mention state-of-the-art taggers, they don't provide a direct comparison of performance. This makes it difficult to assess the practical utility of their context-free approach.
5. **Ambiguity in “outlier” definition:** The paper focuses on misclassifications as "outliers". However, misclassification doesn't necessarily equate to linguistic interest or a need to redefine PoS boundaries. A more rigorous definition of what constitutes an "outlier" would strengthen the analysis.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the distributional model can detect errors in PoS-tagged corpora. Could you elaborate on a systematic procedure for using the model to improve existing annotations?  For example, would you propose automatically re-tagging based on the model’s predictions, or using it as a tool to flag potentially incorrect tags for manual review?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the PoS classifier to the quality of the initial lemmaization step?  Could errors in lemmaization significantly affect the results?
3.  The analysis of vector components in Section 5 suggests that PoS information is distributed.  Could you provide a more detailed exploration of the semantic content captured by the most informative components (e.g., visualizing words with high/low values along those dimensions)?
4.  Given that your approach is context-free, did you consider incorporating any morphological features (e.g., prefixes, suffixes) to improve performance?
5. The paper states a similarity of 0.67 between Proper Noun and Common Noun vectors. Could you present a more extensive similarity matrix of the vectors representing the average embeddings of each POS tag, to provide a visual overview of relationships between them?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures showing the prototypical vectors (Figures 1 & 2) are helpful but could be improved with clearer labeling of the axes.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the distributional hypothesis in the context of PoS tagging.  For example, synonyms might have different PoS tags depending on context.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the relationship between distributional semantics and PoS tagging. While the core method lacks significant novelty, the thorough empirical validation, insightful error analysis, and demonstration of a context-free PoS tagger are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
