## Review

This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) categories, positing that word embeddings can reveal insights into the fluidity of PoS boundaries and even identify annotation errors. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC and use a logistic regression classifier to predict PoS tags, analyzing misclassifications to identify “outliers” and potential inconsistencies in the original BNC tagging. They also demonstrate that a classifier trained *without* explicit PoS information can achieve reasonable accuracy.

The primary contribution, as *I* see it, is a demonstration of the utility of distributional models for *post-hoc* analysis of existing PoS-tagged corpora.  The identification of potential tagging errors and areas of ambiguity, particularly regarding noun-adjective and numeral-noun boundaries, is a valuable outcome. A secondary contribution lies in the confirmation – though not particularly surprising – that distributional models *do* encode information about PoS, allowing for reasonably accurate prediction without contextual information. Finally, the authors present a solid methodological framework for analyzing these distributional patterns.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Soundness:** The experimental setup is generally well-defined and employs established techniques (skip-gram, logistic regression) with clear hyperparameter choices. The use of the BNC, a well-respected corpus, and the comparison to the Universal Dependencies Treebank provide a solid foundation for evaluation.  The weighting by corpus frequency during classifier training is a thoughtful approach to address class imbalance.
2. **Error Analysis is Valuable:** The core of the paper – the analysis of misclassified words – is the most compelling aspect. The authors provide concrete examples of linguistic ambiguities (e.g., passive participles, currency amounts) and potential annotation errors, illustrating how distributional models can highlight these areas. This provides a tangible benefit beyond simply demonstrating predictive accuracy.
3. **Demonstrated PoS Encoding:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that distributional semantic models capture information related to PoS, even without being explicitly trained on PoS tags. The accuracy achieved by the classifier trained solely on embeddings, though not state-of-the-art, is significant given the lack of contextual input.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly articulate their motivation, methods, and findings. The inclusion of tables summarizing error types is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Method:** The use of distributional semantics and logistic regression for PoS-related tasks is far from novel. While the *application* to error analysis is interesting, the core methodology doesn't present a significant advancement in the field. The authors acknowledge existing work in this area but don’t adequately position their contribution as incremental.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparisons to a majority-class baseline and a single-feature baseline are insufficient. A comparison to a strong, established PoS tagger (e.g., Stanford CoreNLP, spaCy) trained on the same data is crucial to contextualize the performance of their distributional model-based classifier. Without this, it’s difficult to assess the true value of their approach. A simple accuracy number isn't enough; performance on the ambiguous cases *specifically* needs to be compared.
3. **Overinterpretation of Findings:** Some of the conclusions drawn from the error analysis feel speculative. While identifying potential annotation errors is valuable, attributing those errors definitively to inconsistencies in the BNC tagging requires more rigorous justification. For example, claiming a “systematic error” based on a limited set of examples is a strong claim that requires more evidence. The author's tendency to frame differences as 'inconsistencies' is concerning.
4. **Limited Exploration of Embedding Space:** While the authors investigate the dimensionality of relevant features, the analysis of the embedding space feels superficial. A more in-depth exploration of the semantic relationships between PoS clusters and the visualization of the embedding space could provide further insights. The discussion of vector component 94 as a solitary case feels unconvincing without further analysis.
5. **Dependence on Gold Standard:** The entire analysis relies heavily on the accuracy of the BNC gold standard annotations. While the authors cite the reported precision of the initial tagger, potential biases or limitations in the original annotation scheme are not adequately addressed.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the results of your distributional model-based classifier compare to those of a state-of-the-art PoS tagger (e.g., Stanford CoreNLP, spaCy) trained on the same data? Please provide a quantitative comparison, focusing specifically on the ambiguous cases identified in your error analysis.
2.  Can you provide more justification for your claims of systematic errors in the BNC tagging? What additional evidence supports these claims beyond the observed misclassifications?
3.  What specific techniques did you use to manually create the mappings between BNC tags and Universal tags?  Could this mapping process introduce biases that affect your results?
4.  You mention the distribution of PoS information across many vector components. Can you elaborate on methods used to identify those components, and can you provide insights on what linguistic features these components represent?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the distributional semantic approach.
*  The claim that “discussions about PoS boundaries definitely should take into consideration this data” is overstated. While the analysis is insightful, it does not fundamentally alter our understanding of PoS boundaries.
