This paper explores the relationship between distributional semantics and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, specifically investigating whether unsupervised word embeddings capture information about grammatical categories and can even reveal inconsistencies in existing annotations. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC and use it to classify words based on their PoS, analyzing misclassifications to identify linguistic 'outliers'. They extend this to a full-fledged PoS tagger and explore the relationship between embedding dimensions and PoS affiliation.

The primary contribution is a demonstration that unsupervised distributional models *do* encode significant information about PoS, enough to achieve strong classification performance and, more interestingly, to highlight potential errors or ambiguities in established PoS annotation schemes. The analysis of misclassified words is particularly valuable, revealing systematic discrepancies between the distributional model’s ‘intuition’ and the BNC’s tagging, specifically concerning nouns/adjectives and numerals/nouns. A secondary contribution is the successful creation of a PoS tagger relying solely on distributional information, bypassing traditional feature engineering. Finally, the exploration of vector components relating to PoS is a useful, albeit preliminary, step towards interpreting the linguistic content of embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of using distributional models to *challenge* and refine established linguistic categories, rather than simply replicating them, is a genuinely fresh perspective. Most work leverages embeddings to *improve* tagging accuracy; this work uses them to question the tags themselves. This boldness is highly commendable.
2. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The detailed examination of misclassified words isn’t just a standard error analysis; it’s a focused investigation into the boundaries of PoS categories. Identifying cases like currency amounts being classified as numerals, or adjectives mislabeled as adverbs, provides concrete examples of areas where annotation guidelines might be inconsistent or overly rigid. This is far more valuable than simply reporting overall accuracy scores.
3. **Robustness Demonstration:** The transfer of the trained classifier to the UD Treebank and its continued high performance is a strong indicator of the generalizability of the findings. This suggests the observed patterns aren’t simply artifacts of the BNC’s specific annotation style.
4. **Minimal Supervision:** Building a reasonably accurate PoS tagger relying *entirely* on unsupervised embeddings, without any manually labeled data during the modeling phase, is a significant achievement. This has implications for low-resource languages or scenarios where labeled data is scarce.
5. **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the complex methodology and findings accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Grounding:** While the paper acknowledges the relationship between distributional semantics and linguistic theory, it doesn’t deeply engage with existing debates about PoS categorization. For example, the concept of "fuzzy" or "graded" categories is mentioned but not thoroughly explored in relation to the findings. A more robust connection to linguistic typology would strengthen the argument.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The PoS tagger’s performance (84% accuracy) is respectable, but lacks context. A direct comparison to a standard, supervised PoS tagger on the same dataset would better highlight the advantages (or limitations) of the distributional approach. While the authors acknowledge existing taggers aren't directly comparable due to the lack of context, some form of benchmark is still valuable.
3. **Dependence on BNC Annotation Quality:** The analysis hinges on the assumption that the BNC’s annotation errors are identifiable through distributional discrepancies. While the findings suggest this is often true, it's difficult to definitively prove that misclassifications reflect annotation errors rather than legitimate ambiguities or edge cases. A manual validation of the identified ‘errors’ would be beneficial.
4. **Superficial Vector Component Analysis:** The analysis of which embedding components correlate with specific PoS tags is somewhat cursory. Simply identifying the most relevant component isn’t particularly informative. A more sophisticated analysis – perhaps using techniques like dimensionality reduction or feature importance metrics – could yield deeper insights.
5. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameters:** The paper focuses on a single set of hyperparameters for the embedding model. A brief exploration of how different parameters (window size, dimensionality, negative sampling) affect the results could strengthen the robustness of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. You identify several potential annotation errors in the BNC. Have you manually validated these cases to confirm they are indeed errors, and not legitimate ambiguities? If so, what percentage of identified 'errors' were confirmed?
2. How sensitive are your findings to the choice of corpus? Would the results be similar if you trained the model on a different corpus (e.g., Wikipedia, a different genre of text)?
3. Can you elaborate on the limitations of comparing your tagger's performance to state-of-the-art supervised taggers? Could you provide *any* quantitative comparison, even if it's based on a different evaluation metric or a smaller dataset?
4. Regarding the vector component analysis, do you have any hypotheses about *why* specific components might correlate with particular PoS tags? Are there linguistic features that could explain these correlations?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is a promising and thought-provoking contribution to the field. The bold approach of using distributional semantics to question established linguistic categories is particularly appealing. While some weaknesses remain, the strengths of the work – especially the insightful error analysis and the robustness of the findings – outweigh the concerns. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the questions raised above.
