The paper explores the relationship between distributional semantic models and traditional part-of-speech (PoS) tagging, positing that word embeddings inherently encode PoS information. The authors train a skip-gram model on the BNC and use it to both classify words by PoS and identify potential outliers – words whose distributional patterns deviate from their assigned tag. They further investigate which vector components are most indicative of particular PoS tags.

The primary contribution, as *I* see it, is a demonstration—albeit a largely confirmatory one—that distributional semantic models, when paired with a logistic regression classifier, can achieve high accuracy in PoS prediction. This isn’t entirely surprising given prior work (referenced within the paper), but the consistent performance across different datasets (BNC test set and UD Treebank) is a positive signal. A secondary, more interesting, contribution is the exploration of misclassified words as potential indicators of annotation inconsistencies or boundary cases between PoS categories. The analysis reveals some plausible examples of such issues within the BNC corpus.

**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Empirical Results:** The classifier achieves impressively high accuracy (97.9% weighted F-score on the training set, 91% on a held-out BNC test set, and 99% on the UD Treebank). These results provide strong evidence supporting the hypothesis that PoS information is encoded within the word embeddings. This level of performance provides a robust foundation for further analysis.
2. **Careful Error Analysis:** The authors don’t simply report the overall accuracy; they delve into the *types* of errors made by the classifier. This error analysis reveals potential issues within the BNC annotation scheme (e.g., adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs) and highlights linguistic grey areas (e.g., proper nouns vs. common nouns, numerals vs. nouns). This is a valuable contribution, offering a data-driven perspective on the complexities of PoS tagging.
3. **Methodological Rigor:** The authors employ standard and well-established techniques (skip-gram model, logistic regression) and utilize a well-regarded corpus (BNC) and tagset (Universal POS). The inclusion of weighting by corpus frequency during training is a thoughtful methodological choice, addressing potential biases inherent in the data.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The explanations are clear and concise, making the work accessible to a broad audience. The inclusion of visualizations (Figures 1 & 2) aids in understanding the concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea – that distributional semantics capture syntactic information – is not new. Numerous papers (cited in the related work) have already demonstrated this. The main contribution feels more like a thorough replication and extension of existing work than a groundbreaking innovation. The method itself (skip-gram + logistic regression) is quite standard.
2. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the authors compare their results to a majority class baseline and a single-feature baseline, they do *not* compare their classifier’s performance to a state-of-the-art, pre-trained PoS tagger on the same datasets. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true value added by their approach. It's quite possible that a readily available tagger would outperform their method with less effort. This omission is a significant weakness.
3. **Over-reliance on the BNC:** While the BNC is a valuable corpus, the results are heavily reliant on its annotations. The identified “errors” in the BNC are, by definition, discrepancies between the distributional model and the BNC’s human tagging. There's no independent verification that the model’s predictions are *correct* – only that they differ from the BNC.
4. **Superficial Exploration of Vector Components:** The analysis of which vector components are most relevant to PoS tags feels somewhat cursory. Simply identifying a strongly expressed feature (like component 94 for conjunctions) is insufficient. A more in-depth investigation of the semantic properties captured by these components would be more insightful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the results of your classifier compare to those of a state-of-the-art, pre-trained PoS tagger (e.g., spaCy, Stanford CoreNLP) on the BNC test set and the UD Treebank? Please provide a direct comparison with quantitative results.
2. The error analysis identifies potential inconsistencies in the BNC. Have you manually investigated these cases to determine whether the BNC tagging is genuinely incorrect, or whether the distributional model is simply capturing a different nuance of meaning?
3. Can you elaborate on the semantic interpretation of the most informative vector components (e.g., component 94)? What linguistic features do these components appear to capture?
4. You mention that training on a corpus without PoS tags is viable. How does the performance of a model trained this way compare to a model trained *with* PoS tags, and what are the trade-offs?



**Additional Comments:**

The Figures 1 and 2 are not sufficiently explained. The axes labels are not readily interpretable to a reader unfamiliar with vector space visualizations.
