- Summary: Shows word embeddings contain PoS information and can identify lexical outliers; trains skip-gram on BNC, builds a PoS classifier, analyzes misclassifications, and builds a context-free PoS tagger.

- Contribution 1: Empirically demonstrates that distributional models encode substantial PoS information via large-scale classification and error analysis.

- Contribution 2: Identifies annotation errors and genuine PoS boundary cases in the BNC through misclassification analysis.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates feasible context-free PoS prediction from embeddings, suggesting applications for resource-poor settings.

- Strength 1: Strong empirical validation using the BNC with high reported F-scores (97% train, 91% test).

- Strength 2: Thorough error analysis revealing systematic tagging issues (e.g., currency, proper/common noun confusions).

- Strength 3: Clear, well-structured presentation and reproducible methodology.

- Strength 4: Novel construction of a context-free PoS tagger with notable accuracy given constraints.

- Strength 5: Shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, not localized.

- Weakness 1: Core idea has limited novelty; prior work already links distributional semantics and syntax.

- Weakness 2: Initial classifier relies on pre-tagged data, limiting claims of redefining PoS from scratch.

- Weakness 3: Overfocused on BNC idiosyncrasies, reducing generalizability of findings.

- Weakness 4: Lacks direct comparison to state-of-the-art contextual PoS taggers for benchmarking.

- Weakness 5: No ablation study exploring sensitivity to hyperparameters (vector size, negatives, etc.).

- Question 1: Have you quantified how much of the BNC might need reannotation based on your findings?

- Question 2: How does the context-free tagger’s performance change with small amounts of contextual information (e.g., bigrams)?

- Question 3: Are there specific embedding subspaces or component combinations that predict particular PoS tags?

- Question 4: How would you adapt this approach for resource-poor languages with limited tagged data?

- Additional comment 1: Figures 1 and 2 need clearer axis labels.

- Additional comment 2: References appear incomplete (some cited works missing).

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical analysis and minor revisions recommended for acceptance.
