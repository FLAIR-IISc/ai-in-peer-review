Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether that signal can be used both to (i) surface “outlier” lexical items that blur PoS boundaries or reflect annotation inconsistencies, and (ii) build a simple PoS predictor without sentence context. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on the BNC, first using lemma+PoS tokenization to study PoS clusters and outliers via a multinomial logistic classifier, and then using lemma-only embeddings with a prototype-based logistic classifier trained on “unambiguous” UD lemmas to predict PoS types and tokens. They provide qualitative error analyses showing linguistically plausible boundary cases (e.g., participles, noun–adjective interchange, numerals as years/currencies) and some likely BNC tagging errors. They also explore the relation between embedding dimensions and PoS using ANOVA feature ranking.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Corpus auditing via distributional diagnostics: A data-driven analysis of misclassifications that reveals PoS boundary phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies (notably in BNC) using a simple classifier over embeddings.
2) A type-level, context-free PoS predictor: A lightweight logistic classifier trained on lemma-only embeddings and “prototype” words reaches 0.84 token accuracy on UD, illustrating the strength of PoS signal in embeddings without context.
3) Empirical observation on representation: Evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.

Strengths
- Linguistically meaningful error analysis: The paper surfaces several well-motivated boundary cases (proper vs common nouns; noun–adjective interchange; passive participles; numerals as years/currencies; time expressions tagged as adverbs) and points to concrete inconsistencies/errors in BNC (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs in certain constructions). This convincingly illustrates the value of embeddings as corpus auditing tools.
- Simple, reproducible methodology: The approach (skip-gram embeddings + multinomial logistic regression) is transparent and should be easy to replicate. Hyperparameters and datasets are specified; the BNC→Universal mapping is said to be released.
- Separation of “tautological” and “proper” experiments: The second experiment (lemma-only embeddings, no PoS in token identity) directly addresses whether embeddings encode PoS without explicit tags in the training text and provides a realistic lower bound for a context-free predictor.
- Useful practical implication: The idea of using a small set of unambiguous prototypes to bootstrap PoS tagging for resource-poor settings (pre-annotation or quality control) is sensible and could be impactful despite modest accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage and evaluation design issues:
  - In the first experiment, embeddings are trained on lemma+PoS tokens, and the classifier predicts PoS from those vectors; contexts also carry PoS-tagged identities. While legitimate for auditing consistency with the gold tagset, this setup makes the task much easier and blurs claims about discovering PoS from “semantics.” More critically, the 10-fold cross-validation appears to split over repeated instances rather than by word type, meaning identical vectors for the same type can occur in train and test folds, inflating performance. Type-level CV is needed.
  - The “UD generalization” result (F≈0.99) is unclear and possibly circular: if inputs were lemma+PoS types from UD, the classifier is being asked to predict the PoS of a token whose identity includes that PoS (albeit only through its training on BNC). Please clarify exactly which vector (lemma or lemma+PoS) was used.
- Unfair baseline comparison: The Stanford tagger is run on isolated tokens (no sentence context), which is not how it is intended to be used; the resulting confusion matrix is therefore not a meaningful baseline for your diagnostics. A more appropriate comparison would be to other type-level diagnostics (e.g., clustering-based PoS induction) or to tagger confusions on full sentences.
- Limited scope and missing baselines:
  - Only English is considered; claims about revising PoS boundaries and generality would be stronger with at least one typologically different language.
  - No comparisons to alternative representations or classic baselines: e.g., PPMI-SVD, Brown clusters, contextual co-occurrence features, or morphological/character features. How much of the PoS signal comes from orthography/morphology vs distribution?
  - No ablations on embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, negative sampling) despite claiming future interest; even a small ablation would help substantiate claims about robustness.
- Novelty relative to prior work: The finding that embeddings encode PoS is well known (e.g., Schütze (1995) and subsequent unsupervised PoS induction; Brown clustering for PoS; Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The principal novelty here is the corpus auditing angle, but positioning relative to unsupervised PoS induction and corpus diagnostics (e.g., Clark 2003; Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010) is underdeveloped, and relevant citations are missing.
- Dimension analysis is under-evaluated: The ANOVA-based feature ranking is reported only with training accuracy curves; no held-out performance vs k is shown. The striking “dimension 94” for conjunctions may be idiosyncratic; stronger claims require test-set curves and stability analyses across runs.

Questions to authors
- Clarify evaluation protocol: For the 10-fold CV on the 10k most frequent types, did you split by type or by (frequency-weighted) instances? Please rerun with type-based splits. For the UD evaluation with F≈0.99, were inputs lemma or lemma+PoS? If the latter, how do you avoid circularity?
- Ambiguity handling: In Section 5, you train on “unambiguous” lemmas but evaluate at token level. What is performance on ambiguous lemmas/tokens specifically, and how does accuracy vary by tag ambiguity?
- Robustness and baselines: Can you report results with (i) PPMI+SVD or GloVe embeddings, (ii) Brown clusters, and (iii) a simple character n-gram classifier? Also, provide an ablation on window size and subsampling.
- Dimension analysis: Please provide held-out accuracy vs top-k features and assess the stability of high-F dimensions across random inits.

Overall
This is a thoughtful, well-motivated study of how distributional representations can aid corpus auditing and shed light on PoS boundary phenomena. However, the main empirical claims are weakened by evaluation design issues (possible leakage and unclear UD setup), limited baselines, and restricted scope. With clarified and corrected evaluation, stronger baselines, and at least one additional language, this work could make a solid contribution, particularly on the diagnostic/auditing angle. As it stands, I lean toward rejection but would be open to acceptance if the above concerns are addressed convincingly.
