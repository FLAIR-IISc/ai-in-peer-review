Summary and claimed contributions (as assessed)
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and how this information can be used to (a) predict PoS and (b) reveal boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies. The authors:

- Train skip-gram embeddings on BNC tokens where lemmas are concatenated with their PoS (lemma_POS), then fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from word vectors. They analyze “outliers” (misclassifications) to characterize PoS boundary phenomena and potential tagging issues.
- Propose a “proper predictor” by training embeddings on lemmas without PoS, using a small set of unambiguous UD lemmas to train a PoS classifier, and evaluate it in a context-free tagging setting.
- Analyze which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS, showing that information is distributed across many dimensions.

Main contributions (in order of significance)
1) A systematic error analysis using an embedding-based PoS classifier that surfaces cross-PoS boundary phenomena and corpus annotation inconsistencies (e.g., ADJ/NOUN alternations, NUM vs NOUN for years and currencies, SCONJ vs ADV), providing concrete lists and qualitative interpretations.

2) A practical, low-resource PoS prediction approach that learns from ≈1.5k unambiguous lemmas and achieves 0.84 token-level accuracy without using sentence context, demonstrating that embeddings alone carry strong PoS cues.

3) Evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (with accuracy increasing up to ~100 top features), informing interpretability and feature selection.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven outlier analysis: The main value lies in the detailed examination of misclassifications that align with known fuzzy boundaries (e.g., participles as ADJ/VERB, nominal modifiers as NOUN/ADJ, numerals vs nouns for years/currencies). This analysis yields concrete, linguistically interpretable cases and flags plausible corpus-level inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs in BNC), which is useful for corpus curation.

- Demonstration of PoS signal in embeddings: The high within-corpus F1 for type-level PoS prediction and the ablation over top-k dimensions empirically support that distributional vectors encode PoS cues, not reducible to a single dimension, complementing prior work with a focused PoS-centric study.

- Low-resource, context-free classifier: The Section 5 experiment shows a simple yet effective bootstrap method (accuracy 0.84 with only ≈1.5k seed lemmas, no context), relevant for preliminary tagging in low-resource scenarios and as a strong type-level prior for downstream taggers.

- Methodological transparency and basic robustness checks: Hyperparameters are specified; frequency-weighted training is motivated to handle closed-class sparsity; a cross-corpus check and a tagger-based comparison are attempted to characterize what errors this method reveals versus a standard tagger.

Weaknesses
- Leakage/confound in the main setup: Training embeddings on lemma_POS tokens and then predicting PoS from those embeddings conflates distributional evidence with explicit tag-marked token identities in all contexts. The reported 0.99 F-score on UD “lemmas” is particularly unclear: unless UD gold tags were used to select lemma_POS vectors, it is not obvious how UD lemmas could be present in a lemma_POS vocabulary. If gold UD tags were used to fetch vectors, this evaluation leaks the label and invalidates the cross-corpus claim. This undermines conclusions drawn from this evaluation. The paper acknowledges the artificiality but still relies on these results for robustness.

- Unfair baseline comparison: The Stanford tagger is run on isolated tokens instead of sentences, which disables its core contextual capabilities. Conclusions that the embedding-based confusion matrix reveals “different” or “better” boundary cases are therefore weak; a fair comparison would use the tagger in its intended sentence-context mode and analyze confusions at type level afterward.

- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Prior studies have shown PoS/morphological information in embeddings and correlations of dimensions with linguistic features. The main novelty here is the outlier-driven analysis for PoS boundaries and annotation issues; methodologically, using logistic regression over embeddings is standard. The contribution is incremental.

- Insufficient validation of error claims: Assertions about annotation errors (e.g., BNC ADJ/ADV issues) are plausible but lack systematic human validation. Without a manual audit or agreement study, it is hard to quantify what fraction of “errors” are genuine misannotations versus legitimate boundary phenomena or distributional sparsity.

- Incomplete analysis and ablations: 
  - The feature-importance study reports training accuracy only; test-set curves would better support claims about distributed encoding.
  - The impact of hyperparameters (window size, negative sampling, vector size), alternative embedding models, and context order is not explored.
  - Ambiguous lemmas and OOV handling are only partially addressed; the omission rates and their effect on conclusions should be analyzed.

Questions to authors
- UD evaluation procedure: How were UD lemmas matched to embeddings trained on lemma_POS tokens? Did you append UD gold PoS to form lemma_POS keys at test time? If so, this leaks gold information; please clarify and, if necessary, remove this evaluation or replace it with a clean one.

- Fair baseline: Can you rerun the Stanford tagger and other baselines (e.g., a simple dictionary majority-tag baseline, a modern neural tagger) on full sentences, then compare type-level confusions to your outlier list?

- Manual validation: For the top 100–200 outliers, can you provide a manual audit with inter-annotator agreement to quantify the proportions of (a) true boundary cases, (b) genuine corpus errors, and (c) model errors due to sparsity?

- Context-free classifier: Please report per-class precision/recall, OOV statistics, and performance as a function of frequency and ambiguity. Can you compare to a baseline that tags each lemma by its majority PoS in training (no embeddings)?

- Feature analysis: Can you provide test-set accuracy vs k curves, and assess stability of top-k dimensions across different random seeds/corpora/hyperparameters?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the qualitative analysis is informative. Addressing the leakage concern, strengthening the baselines, and adding validation would significantly improve technical soundness and the weight of the conclusions.
