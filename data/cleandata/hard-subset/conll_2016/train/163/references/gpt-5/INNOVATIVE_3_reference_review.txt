Summary and main contributions
- Conceptual reframing: The paper treats parts of speech as emergent clusters in distributional space and uses a simple probe (logistic regression over embeddings) to test and visualize how strongly POS is encoded and how boundaries blur in usage.
- Corpus auditing via “distributional outliers”: By inspecting systematic misclassifications, the paper surfaces boundary cases and likely annotation inconsistencies (e.g., years/decades as NUM vs NOUN, currency strings, participles, adverb/adjective confusions), arguing for data-driven reconsideration of tagset boundaries and corpus corrections.
- Context-free POS predictor: A minimalist POS tagger that uses only static embeddings trained on raw text and a small set of prototypical lemmas achieves reasonable accuracy, suggesting a practical bootstrapping path for low-resource settings; analysis of feature importances indicates POS information is distributed across many dimensions.

Strengths
- Bold, useful framing: Positioning POS as emergent distributional clusters and turning probe errors into a principled auditing tool is a compelling, high-level contribution. The paper goes beyond reporting probe accuracy to show how embedding-space behavior can guide tagset design and corpus cleanup—precisely the kind of conceptual push that can influence practice.
- Concrete, actionable insights: The outlier analysis is rich and specific (years/decades behaving nominally; currency compounds; participles with adjectival behavior; pre-/pro-/cross used adjectivally; adverbs like “ultra/kinda” skewing nominal). The discovery of systematic BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs) is particularly persuasive.
- Cross-resource robustness: Results on BNC and UD English Treebank, including a very high type-level score on UD and a reasonable token-level accuracy (0.84) with no sentential context, indicate that the signal is not corpus-specific. The OOV accounting and the differences between BNC vs UD policies (e.g., treatment of currency) are discussed thoughtfully.
- Simplicity and reproducibility: The approach (Skip-gram, small window, logistic regression, frequency weighting) is simple and transparent, making it easy to replicate and to adapt for resource-poor scenarios. The “prototypical PoS vectors” and component-importance analysis provide interpretable artifacts.
- Feature-distribution insight: The finding that POS information is spread across many dimensions (with smooth gains up to ~100 components) adds nuance to prior claims about specific dimensions encoding specific linguistic features and helps temper over-interpretation of single axes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior probing/induction work: That embeddings encode POS and cluster by syntactic class is well established. The classification probe per se is not novel; the main new angle is the corpus-auditing/byproduct analysis. The paper should sharpen positioning versus POS induction and probing literature and explicitly claim the auditing methodology as the contribution.
- Baselines and comparisons: The “context-free” tagger should be compared to a strong unigram/lexicon baseline (most-frequent-tag-per-lemma from UD training). Without this, the 0.84 accuracy is hard to interpret—it may or may not beat a trivial baseline on ambiguous lemmas. Similarly, comparisons to standard POS induction baselines (e.g., clustering or HMM variants with embeddings) would clarify what is gained by the proposed probe.
- Evaluation design concerns: 
  - Section 3 learns embeddings on lemma+POS tokens; while not leaking labels into contexts, this setup might inflate separability compared to lemma-only embeddings. An ablation with lemma-only embeddings would strengthen claims.
  - Frequency weighting of types is reasonable, but results reported as weighted F might obscure type-level behavior; include type-level metrics and per-class coverage in the main results.
  - The UD type-level F=0.99 seems unusually high; more detail is needed (class distribution, number of types, confusion matrix).
- Stanford tagger baseline is misused: Running a contextual tagger on isolated tokens is not an informative comparator and may confound claims about “different” errors. Either compare on sentences (and then analyze different error profiles) or omit this baseline in favor of more appropriate context-free baselines.
- Generality and validation: Only English is considered. While the conceptual claim is language-agnostic, even a small-scale experiment on a typologically different language would strengthen the case. Some manual validation of the flagged outliers (beyond anecdotal examples) would make the auditing claim more convincing.

Questions to authors
- Please report a unigram baseline: most-frequent-tag-per-lemma learned from UD training, evaluated on the same UD test tokens (with identical OOV handling). How does your 0.84 compare overall and on ambiguous lemmas?
- Clarify the UD type-level 0.99 result: How many types were evaluated? What was the class distribution and confusion matrix? Which types were omitted as OOV?
- Does the core Section 3 result still hold if you train embeddings on lemmas only (no POS concatenation) and probe POS with the same classifier? Please report.
- How sensitive are your findings to window size, negative samples, and subsampling? A brief ablation (especially window size and subsampling) would help.
- Can you release the full list of flagged outliers with predicted tags and confidence, and a small manual assessment to quantify true annotation errors vs genuine boundary cases?

Overall: I find the central idea—using embedding-space probes not just to measure POS information but to audit tagsets and surface boundary phenomena—novel enough and potentially impactful. Despite methodological gaps, the paper opens a promising direction and provides concrete insights; with stronger baselines and a bit more care in evaluation, it would make a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
