Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether such signals can be used to probe the boundaries of PoS categories and detect annotation inconsistencies. The authors (i) train skip-gram embeddings on BNC with tokens represented as lemma+UPOS, (ii) train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings and analyze “outliers” where predictions disagree with BNC, and (iii) build a more realistic PoS predictor by training embeddings without tags and classifying lemmas into PoS using a small set of “prototypical” UD lemmas. They report high accuracy for the first setup, a qualitative analysis of misclassifications (e.g., participles, currency/percent expressions, years/decades), and moderate accuracy (~84%) for lemma-only tagging without context.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Using distributional embeddings to audit PoS annotation and surface boundary phenomena between PoS categories, with concrete, linguistically plausible case studies (e.g., passive participles, numeral–noun confusions, adverb–adjective confusions).
2) Evidence that PoS information is broadly distributed across many embedding dimensions (feature selection curve), rather than localized in a few.
3) A simple, low-supervision lemma-level PoS predictor for languages with limited resources, trained on a small set of prototypical lemmas.

Strengths
1) Insightful qualitative error analysis that connects model confusions to real linguistic phenomena. The paper convincingly highlights well-known gray areas (proper vs common nouns, adjectival nouns, passive participles) and concrete annotation issues (e.g., “£70”, “33%”, years/decades tagged as NUM vs NOUN; “immediately” tagged as SCONJ in BNC). The cross-check with UD strengthens the claim that some BNC inconsistencies are systematic.
2) Simple methodology with good signal-to-noise. Even very simple linear classifiers over embeddings recover much of PoS structure. The frequency-weighting choice for the classifier addresses closed-class underrepresentation and yields robust performance.
3) Useful proposal of embeddings for corpus auditing. The idea of using model-driven outlier detection to prioritize human review of annotation errors or boundary cases is practical and potentially impactful for corpus maintenance and harmonization.
4) Empirical observation that PoS information is distributed. The feature-accumulation curve suggests that PoS cues are not confined to a few dimensions, aligning with broader understanding of distributed representations.

Weaknesses
1) Label leakage in the main experiment. Training embeddings on tokens concatenated with their gold UPOS (lemma_POS) injects the target label into the identity of the vector. The subsequent classifier predicting POS from these vectors risks being tautological and inflates performance, weakening the central claim that “unsupervised embeddings” encode PoS. While the authors acknowledge this setup served outlier discovery, its use to support broader claims about embeddings is problematic. A stronger test would train embeddings on lemmas without tags and then analyze outliers.
2) Missing baselines for the lemma-only predictor. The 0.84 token accuracy without context is not compared to simple and strong baselines such as majority-tag-per-lemma (or per word form), a lexicon produced from training data, or a distributional classifier using non-embedding context features (e.g., co-occurrence counts or SVD). Without these, it is unclear whether the reported performance improves on a trivial lexical baseline.
3) Unfair comparison to a supervised tagger. The Stanford tagger is applied to isolated tokens, outside its intended sentence-context setting. The resulting confusion patterns are therefore not informative for an apples-to-apples comparison and may misrepresent what a standard tagger would reveal about boundary cases. Either use sentence context or omit this comparison.
4) Limited novelty relative to prior work. Prior literature has shown that embeddings cluster by PoS, that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties, and that embeddings can help PoS tagging. The distinctive contribution here is the corpus-auditing angle, but its quantitative validation is limited (see next point).
5) Insufficient validation of “outliers.” The paper treats classifier disagreements as linguistically interesting, but does not provide a systematic human evaluation quantifying how many disagreements correspond to true annotation errors vs frequency sparsity or noise. Frequency sensitivity is suggested (e.g., rare proper nouns), but not controlled for. The scope is also limited to English/BNC; generalization to other languages is only suggested as future work.

Questions to Authors
- Can you repeat the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on lemmas without appended PoS tags? Does the set of “interesting” disagreements persist, and how does it change?
- What is the accuracy of a majority-tag-per-lemma baseline and a lexicon lookup built from the same training split? How does your 0.84 compare?
- Did you quantify with human annotators how many disagreements are genuine annotation errors vs legitimate ambiguities vs modeling artifacts (e.g., low frequency)? Please provide an adjudication sample and inter-annotator agreement.
- How sensitive are the results to embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, dimensionality) and to using word forms vs lemmas?
- Can you report results for at least one morphologically richer language, where PoS boundaries and ambiguity patterns are different?

Additional comments
- The title overpromises; consider reframing to emphasize corpus auditing of PoS labels with distributional models rather than “redefining” PoS classes.
- The references are incomplete/inconsistent; please fix citations and provide links/code for reproducibility (you mention released mappings).
- Figures 1–2: clarify what is plotted and add scales/legends; consider quantifying the “component 94” effect across runs.
- Consider evaluating on tokens like “£70” using a consistent tokenization (NUM + SYM) to align BNC with UD, then reassessing disagreements.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The paper contains valuable qualitative insights and a promising auditing use-case, but the main experimental setup suffers from label leakage, comparisons are not well controlled, and novelty over prior work is limited. With a revised methodology (embeddings trained without PoS tags for the outlier analysis), stronger baselines, and a small-scale human validation of detected issues, this work could make a solid contribution.
