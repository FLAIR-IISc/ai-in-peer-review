Summary
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether such representations can surface PoS boundary phenomena and annotation inconsistencies. The authors train skip-gram embeddings (BNC) over lemma_POS tokens and use logistic regression to predict PoS, achieving high F-scores. They analyze misclassifications (“outliers”) to identify borderline lexical subclasses (e.g., passive participles), systematic tagging inconsistencies (e.g., certain adjectives tagged as adverbs in BNC), and problematic categories (proper vs common nouns, numerals). They then train embeddings without PoS tags and show a lemma-only PoS predictor (trained on UD “unambiguous” lemmas) achieves 0.84 token-level accuracy without sentence context. Finally, they study which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS, finding information is distributed across many components.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical evidence that standard unsupervised embeddings robustly encode PoS signals, and that a simple classifier can leverage them for context-free PoS prediction with reasonable accuracy (0.84) using only lemma embeddings.
- A systematic misclassification analysis that surfaces linguistically meaningful PoS boundary cases and points to corpus annotation inconsistencies (e.g., BNC adverb/adjective confusions, numerals vs nouns).
- Feature analysis indicating PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (not concentrated in a few), with some interpretable exceptions.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven outlier analysis yielding linguistic insights. The qualitative examination of frequent confusions (e.g., nouns vs adjectives, verbs vs adjectives for passive participles, numerals vs nouns for dates/currency) is convincing and grounded in examples. The finding about BNC’s systematic adverb/adjective inconsistencies (e.g., “plain,” “clear,” “sharp”) is particularly concrete and useful for corpus curation.
- Robust experimental setup spanning multiple datasets. The study evaluates on BNC (train/test splits with frequency-based generalization) and UD (type- and token-level), including cross-corpus testing that yields strong scores (e.g., 0.99 F on UD lemmas present in the embedding vocab), supporting generality of the observations.
- Simple, interpretable methods with strong baselines for the task at hand. Using logistic regression on embeddings and frequency-weighted instances is transparent and effective, making the analyses easier to reproduce and to interpret.
- Useful negative/contrastive comparison. The Stanford tagger “token-only” comparison highlights that the distributional predictor reveals different confusion patterns than a standard supervised tagger deprived of context, underscoring complementary diagnostics.
- Insight on feature distribution. The ANOVA-based ranking and k-feature experiments suggest PoS cues are distributed across many embedding dimensions (rough plateau after ~100), aligning with broader expectations in distributional semantics and informing future feature selection/interpretability work.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in the main outlier analysis. The primary experiment trains embeddings over lemma_POS tokens, so the contexts are themselves PoS-marked. This bakes gold PoS information into the co-occurrence structure and makes the high F-scores and some “outlier” signals partially circular. Consequently, the linguistic conclusions from misclassifications may reflect properties of the tagged training signal as much as properties of natural distribution. A stronger design would repeat the outlier study with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (or tokens) without PoS and show similar patterns.
- Baseline comparison is weakened by an artificial constraint. The Stanford tagger is run on isolated tokens, which removes its main advantage (context), making the contrast less informative. A fairer comparison for confusion analyses would either (a) use the tagger with full sentence context (and then decouple contextual vs lexical effects), or (b) compare against established unsupervised PoS induction/cluster baselines (e.g., Brown clustering, HMM/CRF induction), which target the same “context-free” lexical category signal.
- Limited quantification of annotation error detection. While the paper provides compelling anecdotes (e.g., ~600 mis-tagged adverbial “plain/clear/sharp” cases), it lacks a systematic protocol for validating predicted errors (e.g., human audit on a stratified sample with precision estimates, or cross-corpus verification against UD). As written, claims about “detecting and fixing” errors are plausible but not rigorously supported.
- Incomplete reporting and replication concerns. Key implementation details are missing (regularization for logistic regression, feature scaling, vocab sizes after cutoffs, random seeds), figures/tables are partially placeholders, and references are incomplete. The PoS mapping is said to be “released online” but is anonymized, limiting reproducibility for now.
- Feature analysis uses only training accuracy curves. The k-feature plot reports training accuracy vs number of dimensions; without held-out curves, it’s hard to separate genuine signal from overfitting and to conclude where the plateau lies. Reporting cross-validated or test accuracy per k would strengthen the claim.

Questions to Authors
- Can you replicate the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS in the training text) and show that the same major confusion clusters and annotation issues emerge?
- How robust are the findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, dimension, subsampling) and to different corpora (e.g., news vs fiction)? Any sensitivity analyses?
- For the lemma-only classifier (0.84 accuracy), what is its confusion matrix across UD coarse tags? Which classes are hardest, and how does this compare to a frequency/lexicon baseline?
- How was the logistic regression regularized and features scaled? Did you tune C or apply L1/L2 penalties? Are results stable across seeds?
- Could you provide a small-scale human validation of the proposed BNC error classes (e.g., precision of predicted adverb/adjective fixes), and quantify gains if such fixes are applied?

Overall, the paper offers a valuable and readable exploration of how embeddings encode PoS information and how misclassifications can reveal boundary phenomena and annotation noise. Addressing the leakage concern and strengthening baselines/validation would substantially improve its rigor and impact.
