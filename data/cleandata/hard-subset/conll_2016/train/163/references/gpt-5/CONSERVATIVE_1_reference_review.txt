Summary and main contributions
- The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS). It trains a skip-gram model on the BNC and uses a multinomial logistic regression classifier over word vectors to predict PoS, with the aim of surfacing “outliers” whose distributional behavior does not match their annotated PoS and to identify potential annotation inconsistencies.
- It further attempts a “proper” context-free PoS predictor trained on embeddings learned without tag-concatenation, using unambiguous UD lemmas as prototypes, reporting 0.84 token-level accuracy on the UD test set. It also analyzes which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.
- The paper provides a qualitative analysis of common misclassifications, arguing they correspond to fuzzy PoS boundaries (e.g., nouns vs adjectives, participles vs adjectives) and to annotation issues in BNC (e.g., ADJ/ADV confusions), and claims that embeddings can help flag such cases.

Strengths
- Empirical probing of embeddings for PoS signal: The work adds another data point showing that distributional embeddings encode substantial PoS information, including when trained without explicit context order (small window, no ordering). While not novel, the evidence (including a context-free predictor) is clear.
- Qualitative outlier analysis: The discussion connects specific error types (e.g., currency and percentages, years/decades, participles) to known gray areas in PoS annotation and provides plausible explanations. The observation about ADJ/ADV inconsistencies in BNC is useful and practically relevant for corpus curation.
- Simple, interpretable methodology: Using logistic regression and frequency weighting makes the probing setup transparent and easy to replicate. The “coverage” perspective on errors (fraction of types within a PoS) is a reasonable complement to absolute counts.
- Cross-resource check: Applying the trained type-level classifier to UD vocabulary (even if problematic; see below) hints at generality beyond a single corpus. The dimension-importance analysis (ANOVA F-values) also supports the view that PoS signal is distributed across many components.

Weaknesses
- Fundamental leakage in the main probing setup: The central experiment trains embeddings on tokens that are lemma+PoS (e.g., love_VERB vs love_NOUN) and then trains a classifier to predict PoS from those vectors. This is perilously close to predicting a label from representations that already encode that label by construction. Even if the hypothesis is about distributional similarity, the presence of PoS in every token’s identity changes all contexts and makes the probing result hard to interpret. A rigorous test should use embeddings trained on lemmas only (no tag concatenation), then predict PoS at the type level.
- Questionable external evaluation: The reported 0.99 F-score on UD vocabulary appears to require feeding vectors for lemma+PoS pairs (since the embedding model vocabulary is lemma+PoS). If so, the evaluation is not meaningful (the input encodes the gold tag). This needs clarification; otherwise the claim of robustness across corpora is unsubstantiated.
- Weak baselines and unfair comparisons: The comparison to the Stanford tagger is made by depriving the tagger of sentence context (predicting on isolated tokens), which is not a fair or informative baseline. For the context-free predictor (0.84 accuracy), there is no comparison to simple, strong type-level baselines (e.g., suffix features, capitalization, Brown clusters, character n-grams, bag-of-context counts) or to a classifier trained on count-based context features. Without these, the contribution of embeddings is unclear.
- Limited scope and rigor of analysis: The outlier analysis is mostly anecdotal. There is no systematic manual audit with precision/recall of detected annotation errors, no confidence calibration, and no stability analysis across runs/hyperparameters. Claims about specific dimensions (e.g., dim 94 for conjunctions) are not tested for robustness across random seeds, corpora, or embedding algorithms. The choice of a very small window (2) is not ablated; window size is known to affect syntax vs semantics.
- Overstated novelty: Prior work has repeatedly shown that embeddings encode PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic features. The paper largely reaffirms these findings on English and adds limited qualitative commentary. The title (“Redefining PoS classes”) and some claims about discovering boundaries are not matched by methodological innovation or extensive, cross-lingual validation.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues: Important details are missing (random seeds, preprocessing specifics, extent of released resources), some figures/tables are referenced but not shown here, and references are inconsistently formatted. The mapping choices between BNC and UD tags and their potential impact are not thoroughly examined.

Questions to authors
- Main probing setup: Why train embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens when the target is to infer PoS from embeddings? Can you rerun the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on lemmas only, then classify PoS at the type level and compare?
- UD evaluation at 0.99 F-score: Precisely what was fed to the classifier? If the embedding vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens, how did you obtain vectors for UD lemmas without using their tags? If you did use lemma+PoS vectors, this evaluation is not valid.
- Baselines: For the context-free predictor (0.84 accuracy), can you compare against type-level baselines using (i) simple morphological features (suffixes, capitalization), (ii) bag-of-context counts, (iii) Brown clusters, (iv) character n-gram CNNs, trained on the same supervision?
- Stability: How stable are the dimension-importance findings across different random seeds, window sizes, vector dimensions, and algorithms (CBOW, GloVe, fastText)? Is the “conjunction” dimension reproducible?
- Error auditing: For claimed BNC inconsistencies (e.g., ADJ/ADV), can you provide a manual evaluation on a stratified sample, with precision/recall of your detector? How many true annotation errors vs true gray-area cases are there?
- Cross-lingual generality: Do your findings hold in languages with richer morphology and freer word order? If not yet tested, can you at least report preliminary experiments or a plan with realistic expectations?

Overall, while the qualitative insights are interesting and the setup is simple, the core methodology suffers from label leakage and weak baselines, and several claims are not rigorously validated. I would encourage a substantial revision focusing on (i) removing leakage (lemma-only embeddings for the main analysis), (ii) fair and strong baselines, (iii) robust, quantified error auditing, and (iv) at least preliminary cross-lingual or cross-corpus validation.
