Summary and contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS), and uses this to (i) predict PoS from embeddings and (ii) analyze misclassifications as evidence of PoS boundary phenomena and corpus annotation issues. The workflow has two variants: (A) train Skip-gram embeddings on BNC tokens concatenating lemma and PoS tag, then train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from the vectors and analyze “outliers”; (B) train embeddings on unlabeled lemmas, learn a simple PoS classifier from a small set of “prototypical” UD lemmas, and evaluate as a context-free tagger. They also compare error patterns against a Stanford tagger run on isolated tokens.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that distributional embeddings encode substantial PoS information, and that a simple linear probe can recover it; plus an exploratory study of which dimensions are most PoS-informative.
2) A qualitative error analysis suggesting that embedding-based misclassifications surface PoS boundary cases (e.g., adjectives vs. nouns, participles vs. adjectives) and inconsistencies/possible errors in BNC annotations (e.g., “clear/plain/sharp” tagged as adverbs in certain constructions).
3) A proof-of-concept context-free PoS predictor trained from a small set of unambiguous lemmas achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD tokens, suggesting feasibility for low-resource bootstrap tagging.

Strengths
- Clear, linguistically motivated analysis: The paper ties misclassification patterns to well-known grey zones in PoS (e.g., participles, nominal modifiers, years as nouns). Several cases are convincing and insightful, and the connection to annotation policy choices is well argued. This is the strongest aspect and is likely to interest CoNLL’s audience.
- Simple, transparent methodology: Using a linear classifier as a probe over embeddings is an established, interpretable approach. The feature ranking analysis and “prototypical vectors” are easy to understand and reproduce in principle, supporting the claim that PoS information is distributed across dimensions.
- Useful diagnostic for corpus quality: The work illustrates how distributional signals can flag systematic corpus tagging issues (e.g., specific adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs). This is practical and, if validated, could help improve resources.
- Low-resource angle: The small-supervision tagger result (0.84 without context) is not SOTA but is a reasonable baseline showing that corpus-wide distributional information suffices for a decent tag prior. The framing as a bootstrapping tool is plausible.

Weaknesses
- Methodological circularity in the main outlier analysis: The primary embeddings are trained on tokens that include the PoS tag (lemma_POS). This injects PoS information into every context token, making separability by PoS almost tautological. The classifier may exploit contexts defined by other POS-labeled tokens, not just syntactic distribution. This undermines claims about “unsupervised” emergence of PoS from raw distributional patterns and weakens conclusions about boundary cases. A stronger design would learn embeddings on unlabeled lemmas and then probe for PoS; use the “with-tags” model only for a controlled auxiliary analysis.
- Unclear and potentially inflated cross-corpus result (F1=0.99 on UD): It is not clear how UD lemmas were mapped to vectors in the BNC lemma+PoS space. If the model requires lemma+PoS tokens, applying it to UD “lemmas” implies label leakage or mapping ambiguities. Given the near-perfect score, more detail is needed; otherwise, this result is not credible evidence of robustness.
- Unfair baseline comparison: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens (no sentence context), which cripples a context-dependent tagger while the embedding model exploits corpus-wide co-occurrence. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison for error profiling; conclusions that the distributional predictor “reveals different error cases” may be artifacts of the evaluation setup.
- Missing key baselines for the context-free tagger: The 0.84 accuracy on UD should be compared to strong, simple baselines such as majority-PoS-per-lemma and frequency/morphology heuristics. On UD, the majority-lemma baseline can be very strong; without this, it is unclear whether the embedding-based classifier is truly competitive or even useful.
- Limited validation of error claims: While examples are compelling, there is no quantitative audit (e.g., sample manual re-annotation) to confirm that flagged cases are genuine annotation errors vs. edge cases under the schema. This limits the strength of the “corpus quality” contribution.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps: Some details are missing or opaque (UD version, mapping procedures, exact class distributions, per-class metrics), and the bibliography has multiple formatting errors. Code/models are not clearly promised for release, which would matter for a diagnostic tool.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the UD “0.99 F-score” result: What exactly was the input to the classifier (lemma or lemma+PoS)? How were UD items mapped into the BNC lemma+PoS embedding space? What portion of UD types/tokens were covered? Provide a confusion matrix and per-class scores.
- For the main outlier analysis, how much do results change if embeddings are trained on unlabeled lemmas (no PoS in tokens) and the same linear probe is used? This would address the circularity concern.
- What is the majority-PoS-per-lemma baseline accuracy on the UD test set under your evaluation protocol (same token filtering)? How does your 0.84 compare per class?
- How robust are the “conjunction dimension 94” and other top features across random seeds, window sizes, and subsampling? Do you see consistent components or just rotated equivalents?
- Did you conduct any manual verification (even small-scale) of the suspected BNC errors (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as adverbs)? If so, report precision; if not, can you provide counts and contexts to support the claim?

Overall evaluation
The paper presents interesting, linguistically grounded analyses and a simple methodology that could be a useful diagnostic. However, the primary experimental setup with PoS-tagged tokens in the embedding training introduces a serious confound that weakens key claims, the cross-corpus result is unclear, and baseline comparisons for the context-free tagger are missing. With clarifications and a revised experimental design that avoids label leakage and adds proper baselines, this could be a solid contribution. As it stands, the paper partially meets CoNLL’s criteria (interesting analysis, moderate novelty) but needs methodological tightening to warrant acceptance.
