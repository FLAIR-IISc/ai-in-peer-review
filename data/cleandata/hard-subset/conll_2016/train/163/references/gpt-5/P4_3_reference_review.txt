Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and how this can be used to (i) predict PoS from word vectors, (ii) surface “outlier” words near PoS boundaries, and (iii) detect annotation inconsistencies. The authors train skip-gram models on the BNC with tokens replaced by lemma+PoS, fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors, and analyze misclassifications. They also train an embedding model on lemmas only and fit a small PoS predictor using unambiguous UD lemmas, achieving token-level PoS tagging without sentence context. They further examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that distributional embeddings (even with small windows) encode robust PoS information, enabling a simple classifier to identify typical and boundary cases; coupled with a qualitative error analysis that highlights PoS boundary phenomena and potential corpus annotation issues.

2) A lightweight, zero-context PoS predictor trained from a small seed of unambiguous lemmas that attains reasonable accuracy, suggesting a practical path for bootstrapping PoS tagging in low-resource settings.

3) An analysis of embedding dimensions’ relation to PoS, showing that PoS cues are distributed across many components rather than concentrated in a few.

Strengths
- Clear empirical signal that embeddings encode PoS: The classifier trained on embeddings achieves high performance on unseen BNC word types (F1≈0.91) and reasonable token accuracy on UD (≈0.84) without using sentence context. This supports the central claim that distributional information correlates strongly with morphosyntactic classes.

- Useful outlier/error analysis with linguistic insights: Misclassifications (e.g., nouns vs adjectives, participles as adjectives, numerals vs nouns for years/currencies) align with known boundary phenomena and highlight inconsistencies (e.g., clear/plain/sharp tagged as adverbs in BNC). This shows the method’s utility for corpus auditing and for discussing category boundaries.

- Simple, reproducible pipeline with minimal supervision: The approach relies on standard skip-gram embeddings and logistic regression, plus a small seed set of unambiguous lemmas. The method is easy to replicate/adapt and could be valuable for preliminary tagging or corpus diagnostics.

- Evidence that PoS information is distributed across dimensions: The ANOVA-based ranking and k-feature ablation indicate that robust PoS prediction requires many components, aligning with expectations for dense embeddings and offering a caution against overinterpreting single dimensions.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage and inflated cross-validation: In Section 3, training instances are repeated proportional to frequency and 10-fold cross-validation is done over instances, not clearly grouped by word type. If the same word vector appears in both train and validation folds, the reported cross-validated F1=0.979 is not meaningful. A type-level split is required. Similarly, in Section 5, it is unclear whether frequency “weighting” duplicates instances, risking the same issue.

- Circularity in the main outlier analysis: The core analysis uses embeddings trained on lemma+PoS tokens in a corpus already PoS-annotated. Context tokens themselves carry PoS labels, so the embedding space encodes supervised PoS information. While the analysis remains informative for corpus consistency and boundary cases, claims about discovering PoS structure “from unsupervised models” are weakened. An ablation using embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no tags) for the outlier analysis would address this.

- Clarity gaps in cross-corpus evaluation: The paper applies the BNC-trained classifier to UD lemmas and reports F1≈0.99, but it is unclear how lemma-only forms are embedded if the original model’s vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens. The preprocessing and matching strategy across corpora need clarification. The omission rate (27% of types) may bias results; coverage and error breakdown should be reported.

- Comparison baseline is nonstandard: The Stanford tagger is evaluated by feeding individual tokens (no context), which is not the intended usage. This comparison does not isolate what the proposed method does better; a fairer comparison would use clustering/induction baselines or an embedding-based classifier trained/evaluated under comparable conditions.

- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Prior literature has shown PoS information in embeddings, clustering by PoS, and correlations of embedding dimensions with linguistic features. The novelty here lies mainly in the corpus-auditing/outlier framing and the zero-context predictor from a small seed. The paper would benefit from stronger differentiation and quantitative validation of the corpus-auditing utility (e.g., human validation of flagged errors).

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did you ensure type-level splits so that all occurrences of a given word vector appear in a single fold? If not, please re-run CV with type-level partitioning. In Section 5, was frequency weighting done via sample weights or instance duplication?

- UD evaluation with BNC-trained model: When applying the classifier trained on lemma+PoS embeddings to UD “lemmas,” how were vectors obtained for those lemmas? Were UD tokens also concatenated with tags, or was there a mapping from lemma-only to lemma+PoS space?

- Outlier validation: For the proposed annotation inconsistencies (e.g., plain/clear/sharp as ADV), can you quantify precision via manual checks on a random sample? How many of the flagged cases are true errors vs acceptable analyses?

- Robustness: How sensitive are results to window size, negative samples, downsampling, and dimensionality? Do similar outlier patterns emerge when training embeddings on raw lemmas (no PoS tags) for the Section 3 analysis?

- Reproducibility details: Please specify logistic regression settings (regularizer, C, multiclass strategy, solver), the UD English treebank version/split, and make the BNC-to-Universal mapping and code available (post-review).

Overall, the paper is clear in motivation and presents a practically useful perspective on using embeddings for PoS boundary analysis and corpus auditing, but it needs methodological clarifications, fairer baselines, and stronger evidence to support claims about generality and unsupervised discovery.
