Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this can be exploited to (i) recover canonical PoS categories, (ii) surface boundary cases/outliers that challenge discrete PoS inventories, and (iii) build a simple PoS predictor from embeddings alone. Using skip-gram vectors trained on the BNC (with and without PoS-appended tokens) and multinomial logistic regression, the authors show high type-level PoS predictability, analyze misclassifications to reveal annotation inconsistencies (notably in the BNC), and present a lemma-only classifier achieving ~84% token accuracy on UD. They also examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Systematic use of embedding-driven PoS prediction errors to identify boundary phenomena and corpus annotation issues, with concrete linguistic analysis.
2) A simple, context-free PoS prediction baseline from embeddings alone and evidence that PoS information is broadly distributed across dimensions.
3) Prototypical PoS vectors and preliminary feature analysis (ANOVA-based) illustrating how embeddings reflect coarse syntactic classes.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis that surfaces real annotation problems and gray areas between PoS classes. The discussion of adjectives vs adverbs (plain/clear/sharp), passive participles behaving adjectivally, numerals vs years/currency tokens, and SCONJ/ADP/ADV confusions is thoughtful and well grounded. The discovery of systematic BNC annotation issues (hundreds of instances) is a concrete, useful outcome.
- Clear, reproducible high-level pipeline and reasonable hyperparameter transparency. Training details (window size, negatives, iterations, min-count, lemmatization, UPOS mapping) are provided, and frequency weighting addresses class imbalance.
- The “prototypical vectors” and dimension-wise analysis are intuitive diagnostic tools. The observation that PoS signals are distributed (plateau near ~100 top-ranked features) aligns with the general understanding of distributed semantics and may motivate probing-style diagnostics for shallow syntax in embeddings.
- Cross-corpus exploration (BNC to UD) and the distinction between open and closed classes provide useful qualitative insights, e.g., proper nouns vs common nouns and function-word coverage.
- The proposed use case for low-resource settings (seeding with a small set of unambiguous prototypes) is reasonable and potentially impactful if validated more broadly.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation leakage and questionable experimental design in the first part. The main embedding model is trained on lemma+PoS tokens, and the classifier takes the corresponding vectors as input. The UD evaluation appears to require gold PoS to retrieve a lemma+PoS vector; otherwise, there is no vector to input. If true, this renders the UD evaluation tautological. The paper must clarify how UD words were embedded when the model vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens. If gold UD PoS was used to fetch vectors, the reported UD F1 is invalid.
- Cross-validation methodology likely overestimates performance. Training instances are repeated per frequency, and 10-fold CV appears to split over instances, not word types. This allows the same word vector to appear in both train and test folds, inflating reported F-scores (0.979). Proper type-level splits (entire word types held out) are necessary.
- Weak and unfair comparisons. The Stanford tagger baseline is handicapped by being given only token strings (no sentence context), whereas the proposed method leverages corpus-wide distributional context; the resulting confusion matrices are not comparable and do not credibly support the claim that the proposed approach reveals different/more useful “boundary” cases. The paper should compare to stronger type-level baselines (e.g., majority tag per lemma, Brown/Clark word-class clusters, simple orthographic/morphological features, capitalization) and to established unsupervised/weakly supervised PoS induction literature.
- Second experiment lacks competitive baselines and controls. The 0.84 token accuracy for a lemma-only predictor may be below a trivial majority-per-lemma baseline trained on UD (which is often very strong given low ambiguity of many lemmas). No breakdown by lemma ambiguity, frequency, or domain is provided; no OOV analysis beyond counts; and no per-class confusion analysis. The training set restriction to “unambiguous” words biases the prototype set and does not probe how the model handles ambiguous lemmas at test time.
- Overinterpretation of dimensions without robust controls. The ANOVA ranking is computed on the training set; there is no held-out probing evaluation, no control tasks/randomized labels, and no assessment of stability across random seeds, corpora, or embedding hyperparameters. The specific “dimension 94” observation for conjunctions is anecdotal and could be idiosyncratic to the training setup (including the PoS-suffixed vocabulary).

Questions to authors
- UD evaluation in Section 3: How were UD word vectors obtained given that the embedding model’s vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens? Did you use UD gold tags to construct the lookup key (lemma_UPOS)? If so, this evaluation is circular. Please clarify and, if necessary, remove or replace with a valid evaluation.
- Cross-validation splits: Were folds created by word types or by repeated instances? If by instances, please re-run with type-level folds and report results.
- Majority-per-lemma and other baselines: For Section 5, what is the accuracy of assigning each lemma its majority UD tag (computed on the UD training set)? Also, how does your method compare to simple morphology/orthography baselines (e.g., suffixes, capitalization), and to clustering-based word class induction?
- Frequency and ambiguity: Can you report performance as a function of word frequency and lemma ambiguity, and quantify how much of the outlier list is explained by data sparsity?
- Probing robustness: Are the ANOVA feature rankings and the “dimension 94” effect stable across different seeds, window sizes, models (CBOW/GloVe/fastText), and corpora?

Additional comments
- Related work is incomplete and dated. Please discuss unsupervised/weakly supervised PoS induction and word-class induction (e.g., Brown clusters; Clark 2003; Christodoulopoulos et al. 2010), early neural taggers (Collobert et al. 2011), and probing literature. References are also inconsistently formatted.
- The comparison to the Stanford tagger should be replaced by fairer baselines and/or normal tagging with full context, followed by a careful type-level error analysis.
- Consider manual validation: randomly sample predicted “outliers” and have annotators judge whether the BNC tag is wrong or boundary phenomena are at play; report inter-annotator agreement and proportions.

Overall recommendation
The idea of using embedding-based PoS probing to surface boundary cases and annotation errors is interesting and the qualitative analysis is promising. However, the current experimental design has major issues (likely leakage, inflated CV, weak baselines) that undermine the quantitative claims. I encourage a substantial revision with clarified UD evaluation, type-level CV, competitive baselines, and more rigorous probing controls. Pending those, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
