Summary
This paper investigates whether unsupervised distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether this signal can be used (a) to surface words near PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies, and (b) to build a simple PoS predictor with no sentence context. The authors train Skip-gram embeddings on the BNC (first with lemma+PoS tokens, then with plain lemmas), fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS, and analyze systematic misclassifications. They find that many “errors” correspond to genuine boundary phenomena (e.g., participles vs adjectives; noun–adjective modifiers; numerals vs years/currencies) and to apparent BNC tagging mistakes. A second experiment trains a lemma-only embedding model and a small classifier on “prototypical” UD lemmas, achieving 0.84 token accuracy without using context. They also show PoS signal is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical evidence and analysis that off-the-shelf distributional embeddings encode robust PoS signals, and that mining misclassifications yields linguistically meaningful boundary cases and can uncover corpus annotation errors.
- A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained on embeddings of unambiguous lemmas that attains reasonable accuracy, demonstrating the strength and generality of the PoS signal in embeddings.
- An initial exploration of which embedding dimensions contribute to PoS, showing the signal is distributed rather than concentrated.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis: The paper compellingly shows that misclassifications highlight known grey areas (participles as adjectives; noun–adjective modifiers; numerals vs years/currencies) and surfaces likely systematic BNC errors (e.g., clear/plain/sharp tagged as adverbs). This is valuable for corpus QA and for refining PoS inventories.
- Simple, reproducible pipeline with clear intuition: Training standard Skip-gram embeddings and a logistic regression makes the results easy to reproduce and interpret, and the methodology is accessible to the community.
- Cross-corpus generalization: The classifier trained on BNC embeddings generalizes well to UD vocabulary and the lemma-only model trained on BNC transfers to UD tagging (0.84 accuracy without context), supporting robustness beyond a single corpus.
- Quantitative and qualitative alignment with linguistic intuition: Similarities among prototypical PoS vectors (e.g., determiners–pronouns; ADP–SCONJ) and the distributed nature of the PoS signal across many dimensions match expectations, adding credibility.
- Practical potential: The approach could be used to triage annotation errors and to bootstrap preliminary PoS resources in low-resource settings using a small set of unambiguous seeds.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in the first experiment: Embeddings are trained on lemma+PoS tokens (e.g., love_VERB), and the classifier predicts the PoS of that same token. While the target PoS is not explicitly a feature, the contexts themselves are also lemma+PoS tokens, which may make PoS separability artificially easy. This limits how much we can attribute to “semantics” vs explicit PoS-marked contexts. A stronger test would train embeddings on plain lemmas or word forms and then predict BNC PoS.
- Cross-validation protocol and weighting: CV is done over repeated instances of identical word vectors, weighted by frequency. Unless folds are split by word type, the same vector likely appears in both train and validation folds, inflating the 0.979 CV score. The authors partially address this with a held-out set (0.91), but a clean type-level split should be used throughout.
- Unfair comparison to a supervised tagger: Feeding single tokens to the Stanford tagger (a sequence model) strips it of its main advantage and yields a non-standard confusion matrix. This comparison does not convincingly show that the proposed approach reveals qualitatively different errors; an apples-to-apples baseline (e.g., a type-level classifier using simple distributional features or character n-grams trained on the same supervision) would be fairer.
- Limited scope and ablations: Only English is considered; no analysis of how embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, dimension, corpus size) or alternative representations (SVD, PPMI, fastText subword) affect the PoS signal or the error analyses. The dimension-importance analysis is promising but remains shallow (e.g., no probing beyond ANOVA ranking, no regularization path).
- Clarity and presentation issues: Some figures/tables are referenced but not clearly shown; references are incomplete/corrupted; details needed for reproducibility (e.g., logistic regression regularization, seeds, tokenization/lemmatization specifics, OOV handling) are missing. The mapping between BNC and Universal tags is said to be released but is anonymized here.

Questions to authors
- Can you re-run the first experiment with embeddings trained on plain lemmas (no PoS tags in the token identities) and report the same analyses? This would address the potential leakage and strengthen the main claim.
- How were folds constructed for cross-validation? Were identical word types kept in the same fold? Please report type-level CV results.
- For the lemma-only predictor, what is the class distribution and the majority/lexicon baseline? How does your model compare to a simple type lexicon baseline that tags each lemma by its dominant UD tag?
- How sensitive are results to window size and subsampling? Given PoS is largely distributional, one might expect strong dependence on local context windows.
- Could you quantify the BNC “error” findings (precision-recall of suspected errors) and provide a list to enable external verification?

Additional comments
- Consider stronger baselines: (i) bag-of-context POS co-occurrence features without embedding; (ii) character n-gram or morphology-only classifiers; (iii) clustering baselines with subsequent label mapping. These help isolate what the embedding adds.
- Report per-class performance and confusion matrices for the context-free predictor; ambiguous vs unambiguous lemmas; OOV handling.
- The intuition about distributed PoS signal could be probed with modern probing methods (lasso-regularized linear probes, CKA/CCA), and controlling for frequency.
- Clean up references and ensure figures/tables are included and readable; release code, models, and the list of flagged annotation issues to maximize impact.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The paper’s core insight—using embedding-based PoS predictions to surface boundary phenomena and annotation inconsistencies—is interesting and practically useful, despite methodological weaknesses that should be addressed in revision.
