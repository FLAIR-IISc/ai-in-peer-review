Summary
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether this signal can be used to (i) identify “outlier” words that blur PoS boundaries and (ii) spot potential annotation inconsistencies. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on a PoS-tagged BNC (tokens replaced by lemma_POS), fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from the embeddings, and analyze misclassifications. They also train embeddings on untagged BNC and build a “prototype-based” PoS predictor using unambiguous lemmas from UD, achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens without context. They further probe which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that type-level word embeddings carry strong PoS information, and that misclassifications systematically surface boundary phenomena (e.g., participles, noun–adjective interchangeability) and corpus inconsistencies (e.g., adverbs vs adjectives) in BNC.
2) A contextless, prototype-based PoS predictor trained on untagged embeddings that achieves reasonable accuracy on UD, suggesting potential for low-resource bootstrapping and corpus QA.
3) An exploratory analysis of embedding dimensions correlated with PoS and similarity between prototypical PoS centroids.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven error analysis that surfaces linguistically meaningful boundary cases. The analysis of nouns/adjectives, participles-as-adjectives, numerals vs years/currencies, and time expressions illustrates that embedding-driven misclassifications often align with genuine categorial ambiguity or known annotation pitfalls. The discovery of systematic BNC mislabeling (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as adverbs in specific constructions) is a concrete corpus QA win.
- Cross-corpus testing enhances credibility. Training on BNC and evaluating on UD lemmas (and vice versa for the prototype predictor) shows the phenomenon is not corpus-idiosyncratic. The high type-level F-scores on UD lemmas (0.99 with seen vocabulary) indicate the learned representation generalizes.
- Simple, reproducible methodology that isolates the signal. Using a basic multinomial logistic regression on static embeddings (with frequency reweighting) and reporting performance both in-domain and on held-out types makes the core point without heavy modeling confounds.
- Potential practical utility. Using embedding-based misclassification to triage annotation errors and to flag boundary cases is a compelling workflow for corpus maintenance and tagset discussion, particularly for resource development.
- Quantitative feature probing. Ranking embedding dimensions by ANOVA F-scores and showing that PoS information is distributed across many components is informative for interpretability and aligns with prior findings.

Weaknesses
- Risk of circularity and label-induced structure in the main experiment. Training embeddings on lemma_POS strings means all contexts are also tagged; skip-gram will encode PoS transition regularities directly into the space. While the classifier does not see the PoS label of the target word, the embedding geometry is strongly shaped by PoS-tagged contexts, making PoS discrimination unsurprising. This weakens claims about discovering PoS from “semantics.” The second experiment on untagged embeddings partly addresses this but is not used for the main outlier analysis.
- Evaluation and baselines are insufficient. 
  • The reported 10-fold cross-validation on the 10k-word training set appears to split by instances, not by type. Because identical vectors are repeated proportional to frequency, the same word type likely appears in train and test folds, inflating the 0.979 F-score. A type-level split is needed. 
  • For the UD token-level predictor, there is no comparison to a strong lexicon baseline (assign each lemma its majority PoS from the UD training set), which is a natural and likely strong contextless baseline, nor to a simple nearest-centroid classifier using PoS prototypes (which the paper informally motivates). 
  • The Stanford tagger comparison constrains the tagger to token-level inputs, which is not how it is intended to operate, so the confusion matrix is not an informative baseline.
- Claims about component-level interpretability are fragile. Embedding dimensions are not identifiable up to rotation; singling out “component 94” for conjunctions is not robust unless you demonstrate stability across random seeds, rotations, or apply post-hoc alignment (e.g., varimax) and replicate across runs. The ANOVA analysis is univariate; multivariate diagnostics (e.g., logistic coefficients, mutual information, CCA with PoS indicators) would strengthen interpretability claims.
- Lack of quantitative validation of “annotation errors.” The paper provides illustrative cases, but does not quantify precision/recall of flagged errors via human validation. Without a controlled audit, it is hard to assess how often the method raises true issues versus idiosyncratic distributional quirks or sparsity effects (especially for rare proper names).
- Limited engagement with prior PoS induction and distributional baselines. There is a substantial literature on unsupervised PoS induction (e.g., Brown clustering, HMM variants, Bayesian models) and type-level PoS assignment; the paper reports that naive clustering “failed” but provides no quantitative comparison or mapping-based evaluation. Including at least a Brown-cluster-based baseline or a bag-of-contexts classifier would strengthen the positioning.

Questions to Authors
1) How was cross-validation performed for the 10k-type classifier? Did folds ensure disjoint word types (no identical vectors in train/test), or were folds created over repeated instances, allowing the same type in both train and test?
2) Can you repeat the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on untagged BNC (lemma only), to eliminate label-induced structure, and report whether the same boundary/error patterns emerge?
3) For the UD prototype predictor, how does performance compare to (a) lemma-majority PoS from UD training, and (b) nearest-centroid classification to PoS prototypes? Please report accuracy and per-class scores.
4) How stable are the identified “PoS-correlated” dimensions across random seeds or after applying an arbitrary orthogonal rotation? Do your conclusions hold if you retrain embeddings multiple times?
5) For the claimed BNC errors (e.g., adverb vs adjective), can you provide a small, blinded human validation with counts of true vs false positives?
6) Why window size = 2 and no subsampling? Did you explore hyperparameters (window, subsampling, negative samples) and their effect on PoS separability?
7) Could you provide per-class precision/recall for the 17k held-out type evaluation and for the UD token-level test to assess which classes drive overall performance?

Additional comments
- Consider stronger related-work coverage on PoS induction and distributional clustering, and a fair quantitative baseline. 
- Reporting standard errors over multiple runs would help assess robustness. 
- The idea of using embedding-based outlier detection for corpus QA is promising; a more systematic validation would make this a solid contribution. Overall, the paper is insightful but would benefit from stronger evaluation design and baselines.
