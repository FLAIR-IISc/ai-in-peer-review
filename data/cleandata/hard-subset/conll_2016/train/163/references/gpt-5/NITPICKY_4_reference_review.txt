Summary and contributions
This paper explores whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether such information can be leveraged to (a) detect “outlier” words that straddle PoS boundaries or expose annotation inconsistencies, and (b) build a context-free PoS predictor. The work uses BNC (with author-constructed mappings to Universal PoS) and UD English Treebank.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that embedding spaces contain robust PoS signals distributed across many dimensions, plus an ANOVA-based analysis of component importance.
2) An error-mining approach: training a simple classifier over embeddings to surface “boundary cases” and suspected annotation errors in BNC (e.g., adjective/adverb confusions, numerals vs nouns, proper/common nouns).
3) A proof-of-concept context-free PoS classifier trained on embeddings without PoS-marked tokens, achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens.

Strengths
- Using embedding-based misclassification to audit corpora is a neat idea. The qualitative analyses (e.g., adjectival nouns, participles, numerals/years) are plausible and could be useful for corpus curation and discussions of PoS fluidity.
- The paper shows that PoS information is spread across many dimensions, not a single feature. The component-ranking experiment (accuracy vs top-k features) is informative and aligns with the distributed nature of embeddings.
- Cross-corpus exploration (BNC→UD) and qualitative contrasts with a mainstream tagger’s confusions suggest the proposed tool surfaces different kinds of issues than standard taggers.
- Methodological transparency on several training choices (skip-gram, vector size, negatives, window), and an attempt to mitigate class imbalance via frequency weighting.

Weaknesses
1) Methodological flaws in evaluation/design likely inflate results.
   - In the main experiment, you train embeddings on BNC using lemma+POS tokens, then train a classifier to predict PoS from those vectors. This setup bakes the gold PoS into the token identity used to build the vector space. The 0.99 F-score on UD “lemmas” is especially unclear: if vectors are keyed by lemma+POS, how did you obtain a vector for a bare UD lemma without knowing its PoS? If you used UD lemma+UD-PoS to look up the vector, this leaks gold labels into the input and invalidates the evaluation. This needs immediate clarification.
   - Cross-validation on the 10k BNC set uses frequency-weighted duplicated instances of identical word vectors. If folds are made over instances, the exact same word vector can appear in train and test folds, inflating scores. CV must be by word type, not by instance, and duplicates should be handled by sample weights or class weights rather than creating repeated identical examples.
   - The Stanford tagger comparison is unfair: feeding it isolated tokens (not sentences) makes it operate explicitly out-of-design, so the “confusion matrix” it yields is not a meaningful baseline against your method, which relies on distributional contexts from a large corpus.

2) Weak baselines and missing controls for the context-free classifier.
   - On UD, a strong baseline is “majority tag per lemma” (or a lexicon lookup from the training portion). For English UD, this baseline is often >0.9 accuracy. Your 0.84 accuracy without comparing to this baseline makes the contribution hard to assess. Similarly, suffix/character-n-gram or simple morphological baselines should be reported.
   - No per-tag results or confusion matrix for the context-free classifier are provided. Without them, it’s hard to assess which classes benefit and which suffer.

3) Novelty is limited relative to prior work.
   - That embeddings encode PoS and morphosyntactic cues is well established (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; numerous follow-ups). The truly novel angle would be a rigorous annotation-audit pipeline with human validation. However, you provide no systematic manual evaluation (e.g., sampling misclassifications, double-blind annotation, IAA) to substantiate claims of “systematic errors” beyond anecdotal examples.

4) Mapping and preprocessing choices introduce confounds not controlled for.
   - You manually map BNC tags to Universal tags (released but anonymized). Errors in the mapping could drive some misclassifications. No audit or agreement on the mapping is given.
   - Tokenization/lemmatization details for numerals/ currencies/ punct/ casing are not fully specified; e.g., how “£70” is lemmatized and whether lowercasing was applied for proper nouns. These choices materially affect your analyses.

5) Presentation and formatting problems.
   - Figures and tables are missing or incomplete (Tables 1–4 and Figures 1–3 are referenced but not present/legible). Some numbers appear only anecdotally.
   - References are heavily malformed and often incomplete (authors/venues/years inconsistent).
   - Numerous typos and terminological slips (e.g., “nominative parts of speech” likely “nominal”; “concatination”; “is is possible”), which hinder readability and raise concerns about overall rigor.

Questions to authors
- UD 0.99 result with the lemma+POS embedding model: Precisely how did you obtain vectors for UD items? If embeddings are keyed by lemma+POS, did you use UD PoS to fetch the embedding before predicting PoS? If not, what vector did you use for a lemma?
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds formed by word type or by instance? Please re-run CV by holding out word types entirely to eliminate leakage from duplicate vectors.
- Context-free classifier baselines: What is the majority-tag-per-lemma accuracy on the same UD test set? Also report a simple character n-gram/suffix classifier and a lexicon lookup baseline.
- Error mining validation: Did you conduct a manual evaluation (with IAA) of a sample of “misclassified” items to quantify real annotation errors vs true PoS ambiguity vs model errors? Please provide counts and agreement.
- Preprocessing specifics: casing, digit/number normalization, tokenization of currencies/percentages, treatment of hyphenation, and whether subsampling was tried (no subsampling can overweight function words).

Recommendation
While the idea of using embedding-based predictors for corpus auditing is interesting, the paper in its current form suffers from serious methodological issues (potential label leakage, improper CV, weak baselines), limited novelty, and significant presentation problems. I recommend rejection. With corrected evaluations, stronger baselines, and a rigorous human validation of detected “errors,” this line of work could become a valuable resource.
