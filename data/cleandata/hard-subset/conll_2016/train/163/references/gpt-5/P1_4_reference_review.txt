Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether such signal can be used to (a) surface “boundary” cases between PoS classes and annotation inconsistencies, and (b) build a simple context-free PoS predictor. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on the BNC in two settings: (1) tokens are lemma+PoS (supervised/tag-aware); (2) tokens are plain lemmas (unsupervised). A multinomial logistic regression on embeddings predicts PoS with high accuracy; misclassifications are analyzed to identify outliers and potential corpus tagging errors. A small prototype classifier trained on a thousand unambiguous lemmas from UD achieves 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens without using sentence context. They also probe which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS and argue that information is distributed across many components.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical analysis showing that word embeddings contain robust, broadly distributed PoS signal, and that a very small prototype lexicon suffices to build a reasonable context-free PoS predictor (0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens).
2) A systematic, linguistically grounded error analysis demonstrating how embedding-based PoS predictions surface PoS boundary phenomena and reveal potential annotation inconsistencies/errors (e.g., adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs in BNC; numerals vs years/currencies).
3) Observations about the distribution of PoS information across embedding dimensions (no single dimension dominates; >100 features contribute materially).

Strengths
- Linguistically insightful error analysis: The paper goes beyond aggregate scores to analyze frequent/confusing PoS pairs (PROPN–NOUN; ADJ–NOUN; VERB passive participles vs ADJ; NUM vs NOUN for years/currencies; SCONJ vs ADV). Several cases plausibly reflect annotation choices or inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs), which is valuable for corpus curation and for refining tagsets.
- Demonstration of strong PoS signal in embeddings: The context-free UD experiment trained on ~1.5K unambiguous lemmas is compelling: 0.84 accuracy without sentence context shows that simple distributional neighborhoods encode PoS fairly well, with potential utility for bootstrapping in low-resource settings.
- Distributed nature of the signal: The probing study (ANOVA ranking, accuracy vs number of dimensions) supports the claim that PoS information is not localized to a few dimensions. This informs interpretability and the design of lightweight probes.
- Practical methodology for finding “outliers”: Using a PoS predictor on embeddings to flag boundary cases/errors is simple, scalable, and complementary to sentence-level tagger analyses; it finds different confusions than a tagger-based confusion matrix.
- Cross-corpus check: Applying the trained (tag-aware) classifier to UD lemmas (F≈0.99 on covered items) and the replication of qualitative patterns on UD mitigates overfitting-to-BNC concerns to some extent.

Weaknesses
- Use of tag-aware embeddings in the main analysis introduces supervision/circularity: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens means the contexts are themselves PoS-labeled. This bakes PoS information into both targets and contexts, inflating separability and undermining the “unsupervised” claim for the main outlier analysis. The interesting qualitative findings would be stronger if replicated with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no tags).
- Cross-validation leakage via instance weighting: The 10-fold CV for the 10K-word experiment samples repeated instances of the same word vector proportional to frequency. If the split is at the instance level (not by word type), identical feature vectors can appear in both train and test folds, inflating CV F-scores. A type-level split should be used; the authors do provide a separate unseen-type test (0.91 F), but the CV number is not reliable as reported.
- Baselines and comparisons are weak: Beyond majority and one-feature baselines, there is no comparison to simple type-based baselines (e.g., majority PoS per lemma), count-based distributional vectors, Brown clusters, or character/subword features. The Stanford tagger comparison is not apples-to-apples (feeding isolated tokens strips its core advantage: sentence context), making resulting confusion matrices less informative for comparative claims.
- Limited validation of “annotation error” claims: While examples are persuasive, there is no systematic manual validation to estimate precision/recall of detected errors or quantify how many cases reflect genuine errors vs legitimate boundary phenomena or domain-specific usage (e.g., “immediately” as SCONJ vs ADV).
- Scope and generality: All quantitative results are English-only, BNC/UD-specific, with a single embedding configuration (skip-gram, window=2, 300d). No ablations on window size, subword modeling, frequency control, or other corpora/languages are presented, which limits generalization of the claims.

Questions to Authors
- Can you replicate the outlier discovery (Section 4) using embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS in tokens)? How much of the qualitative signal persists without tag-aware contexts?
- How was cross-validation split constructed when weighting by frequency? Was the split at the word type level to avoid identical-vector leakage across folds?
- What is the performance of a simple lemma-majority baseline on UD (i.e., always assign the most frequent PoS per lemma from training), and how does your 0.84 compare? Similarly, how does a count-based PPMI/SVD embedding or Brown clustering feature perform?
- For the UD prototype classifier: how many lemmas per class were used, and how sensitive is accuracy to the size/selection of the “unambiguous” lexicon? Any ablation on window size or vector dimensionality?
- For the BNC error claims (e.g., ADJ tagged as ADV): did you perform any manual validation on a random sample to estimate the precision of detected errors?
- The UD evaluation dropped 27% of types (10% tokens) due to OOV in the tag-aware model. Do the covered items differ systematically (e.g., higher frequency, fewer rare/proper names), and could that partly explain the near-perfect F-score?

Additional comments
- The idea is promising and the analysis is interesting, but the reliance on tag-aware embeddings for the main discovery analysis and the evaluation methodology gaps limit the strength of the claims. Strengthening with tag-free embeddings, type-level splits, stronger baselines, and modest manual validation would substantially improve the paper.
- Several references are malformed; figures/tables should be fully legible, and released resources (mappings, code) would aid reproducibility.
