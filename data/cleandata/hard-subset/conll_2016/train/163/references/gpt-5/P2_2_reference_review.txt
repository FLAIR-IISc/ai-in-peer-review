Summary and contributions
- The paper examines how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and proposes using a simple classifier over embeddings to (a) surface “boundary” lexical items whose usage patterns diverge from their annotated PoS and (b) detect potential annotation inconsistencies. It also trains a lemma-only PoS predictor using embeddings learned without PoS tags.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) An empirical, example-rich analysis showing that misclassifications from an embedding-based PoS predictor align with known fuzzy category boundaries (e.g., participles, noun–adjective alternations, numerals vs nouns) and uncover systematic annotation issues (e.g., certain adjectives tagged as adverbs in BNC, treatment of years/currency).
  2) A simple probing setup demonstrating that PoS information is robustly present in embeddings and is distributed across many dimensions (feature ablation with ANOVA-based ranking).
  3) A lemma-only PoS predictor (no sentence context) achieving reasonable accuracy, illustrating a potential low-resource utility.

Strengths
- Insightful, linguistically grounded error analysis. The paper doesn’t stop at aggregate scores; it drills into concrete misclassification clusters with convincing examples (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagging issues; years and currency as NUM vs NOUN; proper vs common nouns; passive-participle verbs as ADJ-like). This delivers genuine value for corpus QA and for thinking about category gradience.
- Clear evidence that PoS information is distributed in embeddings. The ranking/ablation suggests performance grows steadily with the number of top features and that no single dimension suffices (aside from a conjunction-specific spike). This supports the claim that PoS signals are broadly encoded, aligning with the distributional hypothesis.
- Methodological simplicity and transparency. Using skip-gram with stated hyperparameters and multinomial logistic regression makes the approach easy to reproduce and extend. Frequency-weighted training to balance closed vs open classes is sensible and explained.
- Some cross-corpus robustness. Applying the predictor to UD English (with stated OOV rate) and obtaining high type-level performance indicates the signal is not tightly overfit to BNC idiosyncrasies.
- Practical utility. As a diagnostic tool for annotation validation and for identifying borderline PoS cases, the approach is lightweight and likely to generalize. The lemma-only predictor shows promise for bootstrap tagging in low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty vis-à-vis prior work. That embeddings encode PoS is well established (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; many probing studies). The primary new element is the particular outlier-driven analysis for annotation errors; useful, but incremental. The title/positioning (“Redefining PoS classes”) overstates what is delivered (no formal redefinition or new taxonomy).
- Methodological circularity in the main experiment. The core analysis trains embeddings on tokens of the form lemma_POS (thus conditioning representations on corpus-assigned tags), then trains a classifier to predict the (same) PoS from these vectors. High F-scores are thus expected; the probe may conflate learning distributional behavior with the injected supervision from tokenization by tag. While the later “proper predictor” (untagged embeddings) mitigates this concern, the central error analysis—and its conclusions about boundary items—would be stronger if replicated with embeddings trained on untagged text.
- Evaluation choices and reporting. 
  - Cross-validation on frequency-weighted duplicates can inflate scores and blur type vs token effects; clearer separation of type-level vs token-level metrics and per-class F1 would help.
  - The UD evaluation omits 27% of types (10% of tokens), which may bias results; reporting coverage-adjusted metrics and OOV analyses per class would strengthen claims.
  - The ablation reports training accuracy only; test-set curves would better support the “distributed across many dimensions” claim.
- Unfair comparator. The “comparison” to the Stanford tagger feeds tokens without sentence context, which handicaps a contextual tagger by design. Consequently, conclusions drawn from the confusion matrix differences are weak; a fairer comparison would either use sentence context for the tagger or compare against a lexicon-only baseline.
- Scope and robustness. Experiments are English-only, with a single algorithm (skip-gram), one window size, and no downsampling. Given claims about the generality of PoS signals and planned multilingual extensions, at least a small sensitivity study (window size, CBOW vs SG, downsampling) or a second language would improve credibility. 
- Presentation quality. Some references are malformed; figures/tables are referenced but not fully shown; footnotes/links are anonymized. This affects clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Can you replicate the outlier/error analyses using embeddings trained on untagged BNC text (no lemma_POS concatenation), to rule out supervision leakage? How do the key findings (e.g., adjectives mis-tagged as adverbs, NUM vs NOUN for years/currency) change?
- How sensitive are your results (both classifier performance and the set of surfaced outliers) to embedding hyperparameters (window size, negative sampling, downsampling, vector size) and to model choice (CBOW, GloVe, fastText)?
- Please report per-class precision/recall/F1 for the UD test, with and without OOVs, and provide test-set ablation curves for the feature-importance study.
- For the UD evaluation, how many tokens/lemmas are ambiguous by your criterion, and how does ambiguity affect performance? Could you provide a breakdown for closed vs open classes?
- Could you release the mapping between BNC and Universal tags and, if possible, code to reproduce the analyses?

Overall assessment
- The paper is clear, the analyses are thoughtful, and the diagnostic use of embedding-based probes to surface PoS boundary phenomena and annotation issues is valuable. However, the main technical claims are incremental, and the core experiment risks circularity because embeddings are trained on lemma_POS tokens. Strengthening the unsupervised embedding condition, improving evaluation rigor, and expanding beyond English would materially improve the paper’s significance for CoNLL.
