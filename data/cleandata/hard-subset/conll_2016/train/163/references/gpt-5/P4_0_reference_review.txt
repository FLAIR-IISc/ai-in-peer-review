Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and how that information can be used to (i) infer PoS categories, (ii) surface “boundary” phenomena between PoS, and (iii) detect corpus tagging inconsistencies. The authors first train Skip-gram embeddings on the BNC with tokens of the form lemma_POS, then train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from word vectors and analyze misclassifications as outliers that illuminate PoS boundary cases or annotation errors. They then train embeddings on lemmas without PoS and build a small, context-free PoS predictor using “prototypical” unambiguous UD lemmas. They also study which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS and compare their “distributional predictor” with a standard tagger’s confusion patterns.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that standard word embeddings encode strong, linearly recoverable PoS signals distributed across many dimensions, with a simple classifier achieving high accuracy and revealing interpretable PoS similarities/differences.
2) A systematic outlier-based analysis that identifies PoS boundary phenomena and likely tagging inconsistencies in BNC (e.g., adjectives vs adverbs; numerals vs nouns/propn; participles as adjective-like verbs), with concrete examples and cross-corpus comparison to UD.
3) A lightweight, context-free lemma-based PoS predictor trained on a small set of “prototypical” words, demonstrating reasonable accuracy and suggesting potential utility for low-resource bootstrapping.

Strengths
- Clear empirical demonstration that embeddings encode PoS signals: With Skip-gram (300d, window 2), a linear classifier achieves high weighted F on BNC (0.979 CV; 0.91 on a disjoint frequency band), and accuracy 0.84 on UD lemmas using lemma-only embeddings (no contextual features at test time). The feature ablation (ANOVA F ranking) shows performance increases smoothly with k up to ~100 features, supporting the claim that PoS information is distributed rather than localized.
- Outlier analysis surfaces linguistically meaningful patterns and annotation issues: The analysis highlights known PoS boundary areas (e.g., noun–adjective modification overlap; passive participles behaving adjectivally; years/decades functioning as nouns) and pinpoints likely systemic BNC tagging errors (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” mis-tagged as adverbs with high frequency). The comparison with UD supports that some observed confusions reflect schema differences rather than model errors (e.g., NUM vs SYM splits).
- Methodological care to reduce class imbalance and leakage across closed vs open classes: Weighting training instances by token frequency allows learning closed-class predictors effectively despite fewer types, aligning training emphasis with token distribution in corpora.
- Insight into embedding dimensions: The finding that many dimensions contribute to PoS discrimination (rather than a single “PoS dimension”) is informative, and the observation of an idiosyncratic feature for conjunctions provides an interpretable case study.
- Potential practical utility: The lemma-only predictor trained on ~1.5k prototypical UD lemmas achieving 0.84 accuracy without context indicates a simple, low-resource strategy for preliminary tagging or lexicon induction.

Weaknesses
- Potential leakage in UD evaluation for the lemma_POS embedding model: In Section 3, embeddings are trained on tokens with lemma+PoS. The paper then reports 0.99 F on UD “lemmas,” but the embedding vocabulary does not contain raw lemmas, implying the test must have used UD gold PoS to select the corresponding lemma_POS vector. If so, the gold label is used to construct the input feature, invalidating that result. This needs clarification and, if confirmed, that number should be withdrawn. The later lemma-only experiment (Section 5) avoids this issue and is the appropriate evaluation to report.
- Non-standard and potentially unfair comparison to a mainstream tagger: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens (no sentence context) and after tagset conversions and collapsing, which does not reflect intended usage. The derived confusion patterns are therefore not directly comparable and may undercut the conclusion that the distributional predictor reveals qualitatively different errors. A better baseline would be a context-free classifier on handcrafted lexical features, or contextual taggers evaluated in their standard setting for a separate comparison.
- Cross-validation splitting at the instance level risks optimistic estimates: Since the same word vector is repeated proportional to frequency, random 10-fold CV may place identical vectors in train and test folds, inflating CV performance. A type-level split (by word type) should be used for CV. While the separate frequency-band test set partially mitigates this, the CV number should be recalculated with type-level folds.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Prior studies have shown that embeddings encode morphological/PoS information, that classes cluster in embedding space, and that dimensions correlate with linguistic features. The methodological contribution is incremental (linear classifier on embeddings), with the main added value being the error/outlier analysis. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning versus Tsuboi (2014), Tsvetkov et al. (2015), and related work, and from deeper, more systematic evaluation.
- Incomplete rigor and clarity in several places: Figures/tables are partially referenced but not fully presented; reference formatting is inconsistent; some results (e.g., component “94”) are anecdotal without broader validation; OOV handling (27% types omitted on UD) is substantial and may bias reported performance; hyperparameter sensitivity (window size, CBOW vs SG, subword) is not explored though claimed as future work.

Questions to Authors
1) UD evaluation in Section 3: How did you obtain vectors for UD items given the lemma_POS vocabulary? Did you use the UD gold tag to select the lemma_POS vector? If yes, please remove/qualify the 0.99 F and rely on the lemma-only predictor in Section 5.
2) Cross-validation protocol: Were folds created at the instance or type level? Please report CV results with type-level folds.
3) Error validation: For the BNC adjective/adverb mis-tags (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp”), how many instances did you manually verify? Can you provide a list of top suspected systematic errors with counts and example contexts?
4) Sensitivity analyses: How do results vary with window size (e.g., 2 vs 5), CBOW vs SG, and the use of subword information? Does frequency weighting materially change confusion patterns?
5) Reproducibility: Will code, tag mappings (BNC-to-UD), and trained models be released to enable replication of the analyses?

Additional comments
- The comparison with UD is valuable; consider adding a quantitative breakdown of confusions by schema differences (e.g., UD splitting NUM/SYM) versus suspected tagger errors.
- Clarify whether the feature-importance analysis (top-k) is assessed on held-out data, not only training accuracy.
