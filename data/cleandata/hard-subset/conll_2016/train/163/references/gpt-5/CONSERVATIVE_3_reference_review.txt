Summary and contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS), and whether such information can be used to (a) identify “outlier” lexical items that blur PoS boundaries or expose annotation inconsistencies and (b) build a rudimentary PoS predictor without contextual cues. The authors train SGNS embeddings on the BNC with lemma+PoS tokens, fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings, analyze misclassifications as potential boundary cases/errors, and then train a second model on embeddings trained without PoS tags to predict PoS for UD lemmas.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Diagnostic use of embedding-based PoS prediction errors to surface PoS boundary phenomena and likely annotation inconsistencies in BNC/UD, supported by qualitative analysis.
2) Empirical confirmation (on English) that PoS is largely linearly decodable from SGNS embeddings, with information distributed across many dimensions.
3) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained from a small lexicon of unambiguous lemmas, with modest accuracy.

Strengths
- Clear diagnostic angle with concrete qualitative insights: The analysis of misclassifications (e.g., proper vs common nouns, adjectives vs nouns, participles, numerals/years, time expressions) is informative and generally aligns with well-known borderline phenomena and known corpus idiosyncrasies. Several case studies (e.g., clear/plain/sharp tagged as adverbs in BNC) plausibly reveal annotation issues; this is a useful practical contribution.

- Simple, reproducible methodology (in principle): The approach—SGNS embeddings + logistic regression—keeps the setup transparent, and most hyperparameters are specified. The frequency reweighting rationale for training is intuitive.

- Cross-corpus check: Applying the classifier to UD lemmas (with appropriate caveats) reduces concerns that results are entirely corpus-specific and shows the potential for diagnostic transfer.

- Feature-distribution observation: The experiment ranking dimensions by ANOVA F-values indicates that PoS information is distributed across many components rather than concentrated in one or two. While not surprising, the empirical curve is useful.

- Discussion of failures: The paper acknowledges where naive clustering fails and where predictions degrade (e.g., rare proper nouns), and gives concrete interpretations rather than overclaiming.

Weaknesses
- Methodological circularity and leakage in the main experiment: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens and then predicting PoS from those embeddings makes the task nearly tautological: the representation space is explicitly shaped by gold PoS contexts for all tokens. Moreover, the reported 10-fold cross-validation on “instances” duplicates the same type vector across folds (due to frequency weighting), leading to leakage; CV should be done at the type level with proper disjoint word sets. The later held-out type-band experiment partially mitigates this but still uses the same underlying tagged corpus to construct the embedding space.

- Weak baselines and unfair comparisons: The paper compares to majority and a “one-dimension” baseline but omits critical baselines. For the “proper predictor,” the natural, strong baseline is a lexicon: assign each lemma its majority PoS from training. Without showing how 0.84 token accuracy compares to a majority-lemma baseline on the same splits, it is impossible to assess value. Likewise, comparing error profiles to the Stanford Tagger run on isolated tokens is not a fair baseline for tagger behavior and offers limited insight.

- Limited novelty relative to prior work: That PoS is linearly decodable from embeddings and that dimensions correlate with linguistic features has been extensively studied (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; numerous follow-ups). The paper’s main new element is the qualitative error analysis as a diagnostic tool, which is interesting but incremental. Claims about individual “idiosyncratic” dimensions (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions) are also questionable given basis indeterminacy in SGNS.

- Scope restricted to English/BNC, overgeneralized claims: All core experiments and insights are English-specific. The paper suggests utility for low-resource languages but provides no cross-lingual evidence. Even within English, the findings partly reflect BNC-specific annotation and preprocessing choices (manual UD mapping, treatment of numerals/symbols), limiting generalizability.

- Evaluation/reporting gaps: Only weighted F-scores are emphasized; per-class (macro) results and confusion matrices for the main models are not fully reported. There is no statistical significance analysis, no ablation on SGNS hyperparameters (e.g., window size, subsampling), and no control baselines using simple orthographic/morphological features (suffixes, capitalization) that are known to be strong PoS signals. For the “component analysis,” training accuracy is shown, but generalization vs k and robustness across random seeds/rotations is not.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Did your 10-fold CV ensure that the same word type vector never appears in both train and validation folds (i.e., true type-level CV)? If not, please recompute with type-disjoint folds and report results.

- Majority-lemma baseline: For the UD “proper predictor,” what is the token accuracy of a lexicon that assigns each lemma its majority PoS from the UD training split? How does your 0.84 compare on exactly the same test set (including OOV handling)?

- Basis sensitivity: How stable are the “interpretable” high-F dimensions (e.g., component 94) across different random seeds and equivalent embeddings up to orthogonal transforms? Have you tried rotating the embedding space (e.g., PCA) to test whether component-level claims persist?

- Hyperparameter sensitivity and context window: How do results vary with window size, subsampling, negative samples, and dimensionality? In particular, does a larger window blur PoS signals as expected?

- Generalization beyond English: Can you provide at least one additional language (preferably morphologically rich) to substantiate claims about broader utility?

- Diagnostic utility vs frequency: Many “outliers” are rare types. How much of the misclassification signal remains if you control for frequency (e.g., downsample majority classes, analyze equally frequent slices)?

Additional comments
- For the fairness of the comparison to a supervised tagger, please either provide sentence-level tagging results or clearly frame the “token-only” setup as a non-comparable stress test.

- Consider adding a simple affix/case/capitalization baseline and a static lexical baseline; both are strong for PoS and would contextualize the embedding-only gains.

- Please report macro-averaged metrics and per-class results for both main experiments.
