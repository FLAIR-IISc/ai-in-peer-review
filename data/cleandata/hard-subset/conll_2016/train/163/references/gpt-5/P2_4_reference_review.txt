Summary and contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and proposes using a simple classifier over embeddings to (a) identify “outlier” words that sit near PoS boundaries and (b) surface annotation inconsistencies in tagged corpora. The authors first train skip-gram embeddings on the BNC where each token is replaced by lemma+UD-PoS (so embeddings are learned for lemma–PoS types), then train a logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors; misclassifications are analyzed as boundary cases and annotation issues. They then train embeddings on lemmas only and build a “context-free” PoS predictor from a small seed of unambiguous UD lemmas, achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens. They also examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A practical diagnostic methodology that uses embedding-based PoS prediction errors to surface linguistically meaningful boundary cases and likely annotation errors, with compelling qualitative analyses (e.g., numerals vs nouns, participles vs adjectives, time expressions vs adverbs, systematic BNC adverbial mislabels).
2) Empirical evidence and analysis that PoS information is broadly distributed across many embedding dimensions (rather than isolated), including “prototypical” PoS vectors and feature-count ablations.
3) A preliminary, context-free PoS predictor trained from unambiguous seeds, showing moderate accuracy and suggesting a low-resource bootstrapping direction.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis with concrete linguistic value: The paper convincingly shows how misclassifications reveal PoS boundary phenomena (e.g., adjectival noun modifiers, passive participles behaving like adjectives, numerals vs currency/years/decades) and detect systematic corpus annotation issues (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs in BNC). The analyses are detailed, plausible, and actionable for corpus cleaning or guidelines refinement.
- Simple, transparent method with strong signal: A multinomial logistic regression over type-level embeddings (no sentence context) attains high F on BNC-held-out types and near-perfect F on UD lemmas in-vocabulary, underlining that distributional statistics encode PoS tendencies robustly. The frequency-weighting to address closed-class sparsity is reasonable and well-justified.
- Cross-corpus test suggests robustness: Applying the classifier trained on BNC-derived vectors to UD lemmas (achieving 0.99 F on tokens present in the model) indicates portability of the signal across annotation projects, at least for the high-frequency core.
- Distributedness of PoS cues: The ANOVA-based feature ranking and accuracy-vs-k curve make a clear case that many components carry PoS information, which aligns with intuitions about embeddings and is a useful empirical observation.
- Resource value: The BNC-to-UD mapping and the general workflow, if released, would be a useful diagnostic pipeline for corpus builders and tagset designers.

Weaknesses
- Supervision leakage in the main experiment: The principal outlier analysis is built on embeddings trained on tokens of the form lemma_POS, and the contexts also consist of lemma_POS neighbors. This injects gold PoS labels into the distribution, making it much easier to learn PoS-correlated structure than standard “unsupervised” embeddings trained on raw text. As a result, the strength of the signal and some findings could be artifacts of this supervision. The second experiment (embeddings on lemmas only) partly addresses this, but the outlier discovery—the core contribution—was not repeated in that truly unsupervised setting.
- Missing baselines and ablations: There is no comparison to simpler distributional representations (e.g., raw co-occurrence counts/PCA, Brown clusters) or to alternative embeddings (dependency-based) for the core analysis. For the context-free PoS predictor, a strong lemma-majority baseline is missing (often very competitive on UD), as are lexical-lookup baselines or simple morphological features. Without these, the 0.84 accuracy is hard to interpret.
- Questionable comparison to a standard tagger: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens (without context), a regime for which it was not designed. The resulting confusion matrix is not an informative or fair yardstick against the embedding-based approach and may mislead readers about relative diagnostic value.
- Limited scope and generalization: All core analyses are on English (BNC, UD-EN). Claims about typological generality are not substantiated. The BNC is relatively small and dated, and UD coverage/OOVs are nontrivial; it would help to replicate on a larger modern corpus and at least one typologically different language.
- Clarity about data preprocessing and availability: Some crucial details (e.g., whether casing/digits were normalized, handling of multiword tokens, treatment of punctuation beyond exclusion, and the exact released mappings/code) are either anonymized or insufficiently specified for full reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you replicate the outlier/annotation-error analysis using embeddings trained on untagged lemmas (no PoS in the vocabulary) to rule out supervision leakage? How many of the highlighted cases persist?
- What is the lemma-majority baseline accuracy on the UD test set (and for the unambiguous-lemma subset)? How does your 0.84 compare to that and to Brown clusters or simple co-occurrence-based classifiers?
- In the first experiment, were all context tokens also lemma+PoS? If yes, please clarify how you separate the contribution of PoS-labeled neighbors from purely lexical distributional cues.
- How sensitive are findings to window size, negative sampling, subsampling, and dependency-based contexts?
- Will you release the BNC-to-UD mapping, code, and trained models to enable replication?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a useful diagnostic perspective with thoughtful linguistic analyses and a lightweight methodology. However, the primary experiment’s reliance on PoS-labeled tokens weakens claims about unsupervised embeddings and leaves open whether the core insights hold without label leakage. Strengthening with truly unsupervised embeddings, stronger baselines, and at least one additional language would substantially improve the paper’s impact and rigor.
