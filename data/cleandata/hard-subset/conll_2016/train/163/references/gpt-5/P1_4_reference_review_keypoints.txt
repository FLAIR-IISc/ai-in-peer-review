- Paper asks whether distributional embeddings encode PoS information and whether this reveals PoS boundary cases and enables a context-free PoS predictor.
- Authors train skip-gram embeddings on BNC in two settings: lemma+PoS (tag-aware) and plain lemmas (unsupervised).
- A multinomial logistic regression on embeddings predicts PoS with high accuracy; misclassifications are analyzed to find outliers and possible tagging errors.
- A prototype classifier trained on ~1.5K unambiguous UD lemmas achieves 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens without context.
- Probing shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (>100 features contribute), not localized to a single dimension.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that embeddings contain robust, distributed PoS signal and a small lexicon can yield a decent context-free PoS predictor.
- Contribution: linguistically grounded error analysis that surfaces PoS boundary phenomena and potential annotation inconsistencies (e.g., ADJ vs ADV, NUM vs NOUN).
- Contribution: observations on distribution of PoS information across embedding dimensions, informing probe interpretability and design.
- Strength: insightful qualitative error analysis beyond aggregate metrics, identifying plausible annotation issues useful for corpus curation.
- Strength: compelling context-free UD experiment (0.84 accuracy) showing distributional neighborhoods encode PoS and potential for low-resource bootstrapping.
- Strength: evidence that PoS signal is distributed across dimensions, supported by ANOVA ranking and accuracy vs number-of-dimensions analysis.
- Strength: practical, scalable methodology for flagging outliers/errors complementary to sentence-level tagger analyses.
- Strength: cross-corpus checks (applying BNC-trained classifier to UD) partially mitigate overfitting concerns.
- Weakness: main analysis relies on tag-aware embeddings, introducing supervision/circularity that inflates separability and weakens unsupervised claims.
- Weakness: potential cross-validation leakage—instance-weighted 10-fold CV may allow identical word vectors in train and test folds; type-level split needed.
- Weakness: weak baselines and comparisons—no lemma-majority baseline, count-based vectors, Brown clusters, or subword features; Stanford tagger comparison is not directly comparable.
- Weakness: limited validation of claimed annotation errors—no systematic manual validation to estimate precision/recall of detected errors.
- Weakness: limited scope and generality—English-only, BNC/UD-specific, single embedding config (skip-gram, window=2, 300d); no ablations or other corpora/languages.
- Question: Can outlier discovery be replicated with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS tags) and how much signal persists?
- Question: Was cross-validation split done at type level to avoid identical-vector leakage across folds?
- Question: What is performance of a simple lemma-majority baseline and of alternatives like PPMI/SVD or Brown clusters?
- Question: For the UD prototype classifier, how many lemmas per class were used and how sensitive is accuracy to lexicon size/selection and embedding hyperparameters?
- Question: Was any manual validation performed on BNC error claims to estimate precision of detected errors?
- Question: Do the UD-covered items differ systematically (frequency, type) given that 27% of types / 10% tokens were OOV in the tag-aware model?
- Additional: Paper is promising but needs tag-free replications, type-level splits, stronger baselines, modest manual validation, fixed references/figures, and released resources for reproducibility.
