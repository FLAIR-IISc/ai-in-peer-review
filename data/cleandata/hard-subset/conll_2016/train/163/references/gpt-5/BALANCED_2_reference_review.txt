Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings trained on large corpora encode information about parts of speech (PoS), and whether such information can be used to (i) detect boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies across PoS classes, and (ii) build a simple, context-free PoS predictor. Using Skip-gram embeddings trained on the BNC, the authors train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from word vectors, analyze misclassifications to surface “outliers,” compare with confusions from a standard PoS tagger, and explore which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS. They also train embeddings without PoS labels and build a lightweight PoS predictor using a small set of “prototypical” lemmas from UD, achieving ~0.84 accuracy on UD tokens without using context.

Claimed and actual contributions (ordered by strength)
1) Empirical evidence and analysis showing that standard word embeddings strongly encode PoS information, and that misclassifications can reveal PoS boundary phenomena and corpus annotation inconsistencies. The qualitative case studies (e.g., participles, numerals, time expressions, ADJ/NOUN border cases) are the most interesting and valuable part.
2) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained on embeddings of lemmas and a small set of prototypical UD lemmas, illustrating that PoS affiliation is recoverable from type-level distributional information with reasonable accuracy.
3) Feature-level observations that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (via ANOVA F ranking), with occasional idiosyncratic dimensions for particular classes.

Strengths
- Insightful qualitative error analysis: The outlier analysis surfaces linguistically meaningful boundary cases (e.g., passive participles behaving like adjectives, numerals vs. nouns for years/currency, pre/post modifiers) and highlights plausible BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs). This demonstrates the utility of embedding-based diagnostics for PoS scheme auditing.
- Robust PoS signal in embeddings: The paper demonstrates high type-level separability of PoS using simple linear models, with strong weighted F-scores on BNC-held-out types and near-perfect performance on UD lemmas seen in the embedding vocabulary. The analysis that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions aligns with prior intuitions and is supported by experiments.
- Practical, low-resource angle: The context-free predictor trained on a small number of prototypical lemmas (∼1.5k) achieving ~0.84 accuracy suggests a pragmatic route for bootstrapping PoS in low-resource settings where contextual taggers are hard to train.
- Clear presentation of methodology: Data preparation (BNC to UD tags; frequency-based instance weighting), training details (Skip-gram hyperparameters, logistic regression), and evaluation sets are described sufficiently to broadly understand the pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: That embeddings encode PoS information and that PoS can be predicted from embeddings has been shown (e.g., Brown clusters and earlier distributional work; Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The main novelty here is the specific diagnostic use of misclassifications for PoS boundary analysis, but the methodological core is incremental.
- Methodological confound in Section 3: Training embeddings on tokens concatenated with their PoS tags means the contexts themselves are PoS-labeled (e.g., the_DET), likely amplifying PoS separability. This design could overstate how much PoS signal standard embeddings (trained on raw text) naturally contain. Section 5 partially addresses this by using raw lemma embeddings, but the main outlier analysis relies on the PoS-augmented setting.
- Baselines and comparisons: For the context-free predictor, a strong lexicon baseline (assign each lemma its dominant PoS from training) is missing and may be competitive or superior for UD. Similarly, k-NN over embeddings or a character n-gram model (fastText) would be informative. In Section 3, comparisons beyond majority and “one-feature” baselines are limited.
- Unfair comparison to a standard tagger: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens (no sentential context), which is not what it is designed for; its confusion patterns in that setup are not a fair point of comparison. This weakens the claim that the distributional predictor reveals distinctive errors relative to a mainstream tagger.
- Validation of “annotation errors” is anecdotal: While many observations are plausible, the paper lacks a systematic manual validation (e.g., a sample adjudicated by annotators) quantifying how often flagged cases are indeed annotation errors versus legitimate boundary phenomena or embedding artifacts.

Questions to Authors
- How much do results in Section 3 change if embeddings are trained on raw lemmas (no PoS-suffixed tokens) while keeping the same classifier/outlier analysis? This would isolate the effect of PoS-augmented contexts.
- For the context-free predictor, how does performance compare to a lemma-dominant-tag baseline derived from UD training data, and to k-NN over embeddings? Please include these baselines.
- Did you L2-normalize embeddings or control for frequency-related effects (e.g., vector norms correlate with frequency) before training the classifier?
- Can you report a small-scale manual study validating a sample of detected “errors” in BNC and UD, with inter-annotator agreement?
- How sensitive are findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, dimension) and to corpus domain/size? Any evidence from another language?
- In Section 4, coverage numbers and absolute counts sometimes seem inconsistent across datasets (BNC vs. UD). Could you clarify OOV rates and the exact splits used?

Overall assessment
The paper offers a useful and well-argued qualitative perspective on how distributional embeddings reflect PoS and how misclassifications can be leveraged to probe PoS boundaries and annotation issues. However, the methodological novelty is limited, and some experimental choices (PoS-augmented training, unfair tagger comparison, absence of strong baselines, lack of systematic validation) reduce the strength of the empirical claims. I recommend rejection in its current form, but the work could become publishable with clearer methodological controls, stronger baselines, and a validated error-auditing study (ideally across multiple languages) to substantiate its most interesting claims.
