Summary and contributions
- The paper empirically investigates the extent to which distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and proposes to leverage this geometry to (i) surface PoS “boundary” cases and corpus annotation inconsistencies; and (ii) build a simple lexeme-level PoS predictor with minimal supervision.
- Contributions (as I see them):
  1) Conceptual and empirical framing of using embedding-space misclassifications to audit PoS tagsets and corpora, revealing boundary phenomena (e.g., participles, noun–adjective alternations, years and numerals) and potential systematic tagging issues (e.g., BNC adverb/adjective confusions).
  2) A minimal-supervision PoS predictor (lemma-only, no context) trained on ~1.5k “prototypical” lemmas achieving ~84% accuracy on UD, suggesting a lightweight route for bootstrapping tagging in low-resource scenarios.
  3) Analysis indicating that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (not localized), with some idiosyncratic features (e.g., conjunctions), plus practical tweaks (frequency weighting) to better handle closed classes.

Strengths
- Novel auditing angle: Using embedding-based PoS predictions to probe category boundaries and annotation quality is a compelling and underexplored direction. The qualitative analyses (e.g., NUM↔NOUN for years/currencies, SCONJ↔ADV for items like “immediately,” PRE/PRO as ADJ-like) are linguistically meaningful and actionable for corpus maintenance.
- Minimal supervision predictor: The lemma-only, no-context classifier trained on a very small prototypical lexicon is a strong signal that embeddings capture robust PoS cues. While not SOTA, 0.84 accuracy from ~1.5k seeds is promising and practically useful in low-resource bootstrapping or as a prior.
- Practical insight for closed classes: The frequency-weighted training (to counter type imbalance) is simple and effective in improving predictions for closed classes, a real pain point for distributional approaches.
- Distributed encoding analysis: The feature ablation shows PoS cues are spread across many dimensions (plateau near ~100 features), which both corroborates prior findings and cautions against overinterpreting any single dimension—yet uncovers interesting outliers (e.g., “component 94” for conjunctions).
- Cross-resource triangulation: Comparing “distributional confusion” with a mainstream tagger’s confusion matrix (even if imperfectly) illustrates that the proposed approach surfaces a different set of boundary phenomena than standard tagger errors—supporting the utility of the method as a complementary diagnostic.

Weaknesses
- Methodological confound in Section 3: The main experiment trains embeddings on tokens that concatenate lemma and PoS (lemma_POS) and then predicts PoS from these vectors. This artificially separates contexts by gold PoS and bakes label information into the token identity, making subsequent PoS classification much easier and potentially inflating “outlier detection.” The conclusions about boundary cases would be far stronger if the embeddings were trained on lemmas (no PoS) throughout.
- Cross-validation leakage risk: The classifier is trained on repeated instances of the same word vector weighted by frequency, and 10-fold CV is performed on instances rather than word types. Identical feature vectors likely appear in both train and test folds, inflating CV scores. Type-level splits are needed.
- Inconsistency in UD evaluation (Section 3): It is unclear how the classifier trained on lemma_POS embeddings was applied to UD “lemmas” (without tags). If the embedding vocabulary contains only lemma_POS tokens, many UD lemmas should be OOV. The text notes omissions but does not resolve the mismatch; the reported 0.99 F-score seems implausibly high under this setup and requires clarification.
- Unfair tagger comparison: Running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens is not how it is designed; comparing its confusion matrix in that setting to the distributional predictor is not informative. A better comparison would be: (i) a context-free baseline (e.g., lexicon majority class, Brown clusters), and (ii) a context-aware tagger used as intended (sentence-level), with care taken in tagset mapping.
- Limited validation of “annotation errors”: The claim of systematic errors (e.g., BNC JJ/RB confusions) is plausible and interesting, but needs stronger evidence: counts with clear criteria, alignment to corpus guidelines, and a small human validation study. As written, several cases rest on anecdotal inspection.
- Scope and polish: Only English is considered; figures/tables and references contain glitches; the framing (“redefining PoS classes”) is bolder than what’s delivered (diagnostics rather than redefinition).

Questions to authors
- Section 3 setup: Please clarify precisely how embeddings and classifiers were trained and evaluated:
  - Were embeddings trained on lemma_POS tokens only, or also on plain lemmas? If only lemma_POS, how did you obtain vectors for UD “lemmas” in Section 3?
  - For the 10-fold CV, were folds created at the type level (one vector per word type) or at the instance level (duplicated vectors by frequency)? If the latter, can you redo CV at the type level?
  - Can you replicate the outlier analysis using embeddings trained on lemmas without PoS tags? Do you recover the same boundary cases?
- Tagger comparison: Could you include baselines that are context-free (e.g., lexicon majority tag, Brown clusters, clustering + mapping) and a context-aware tagger in its intended mode? This would better position your approach.
- “Annotation errors”: For the JJ/RB and NUM/NOUN cases, can you quantify prevalence, connect to tagging guidelines (BNC/UD), and provide a small human study to verify corrections?
- Resource release: Will you release the PoS mapping, the outlier lists, and the prototype lexicon used in Section 5?

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. I find the conceptual direction—using embedding geometry to interrogate PoS boundaries and audit corpora—promising and aligned with bold, impactful research. However, core methodological issues (label leakage via lemma_POS embeddings, likely CV leakage, and unclear UD evaluation) substantially undermine the main empirical claims. If these are addressed (train embeddings without PoS tags for the outlier analysis, ensure type-level splits, clarify UD setup, add fair baselines, and strengthen validation of proposed annotation errors), I would be inclined toward acceptance.
