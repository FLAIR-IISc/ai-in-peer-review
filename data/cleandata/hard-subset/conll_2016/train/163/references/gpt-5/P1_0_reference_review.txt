Summary
The paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this can be leveraged to (i) probe PoS boundaries and uncover annotation inconsistencies, and (ii) build a simple PoS predictor without contextual information. The authors train Skip-gram embeddings on the BNC with tokens concatenated with PoS (lemma_POS) and fit a multinomial logistic regression classifier to predict PoS from vectors. High accuracy is reported and misclassifications are analyzed to surface linguistically interesting “boundary” cases (e.g., adjectives vs. nouns, passive participles) and likely corpus tagging errors. A second experiment trains embeddings on lemmas only and uses a small lexicon of “prototypical” UD lemmas to train a type-level PoS classifier that reaches ~0.84 token accuracy on UD without using sentence context. An analysis of feature importance suggests PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical and qualitative evidence that static word embeddings robustly encode PoS category information and that probing errors can reveal genuine boundary phenomena and annotation inconsistencies.
2) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained from a small lexicon of unambiguous words that attains competitive accuracy given the lack of context, suggesting utility for bootstrapping or auditing in low-resource settings.
3) A feature-level analysis indicating PoS signals are distributed over many dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven error analysis that surfaces real linguistic phenomena. The discussion of proper vs. common nouns, nominal modifiers, passive participles, currency/percent numerals, and time expressions is insightful and grounded in concrete examples, including identification of probable BNC tagging errors (e.g., adjectives tagged as adverbs such as “plain,” “clear,” “sharp”).
- Practical utility as an auditing tool. Using embedding-based misclassifications to flag systematic inconsistencies or errors is compelling, complementary to standard tagger confusion analyses, and could aid corpus curation.
- Simple, transparent methodology. The use of multinomial logistic regression over pre-trained embeddings makes the approach easy to reproduce and interpret; the ANOVA-based ranking of embedding dimensions is a reasonable probe for where PoS signal resides.
- Cross-resource check. Applying the trained classifier to UD (and the lemma-only predictor trained on UD) provides some evidence that observations are not limited to a single dataset, and the discussion highlights differences in annotation guidelines (e.g., NUM vs. SYM segmentation), which is informative.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in the main setup. Training embeddings on lemma_POS tokens and then predicting PoS from those vectors risks circularity: the contexts used to learn vectors include tags for all words, making PoS an overt signal in the training data. While the authors acknowledge this setup is “artificial” for outlier detection, it weakens claims about discovering PoS structure “from semantics.” A stronger control would use lemma-only embeddings and show similar outlier patterns.
- Inflated cross-validation estimates due to instance duplication. The 10-fold CV is performed over 177k “instances” but only 10k unique vectors, duplicated by frequency. This likely places the same word vector in both train and test folds, overestimating performance. Type-level splits (no word vector seen in both train/test) should be reported. The held-out test (F1=0.91) is more credible; emphasize that and downplay the CV figure.
- UD evaluation with 0.99 F-score is unclear and potentially problematic. It is not clear how lemma vectors were obtained for UD words given the embedding space contains lemma_POS tokens; what representation is fed to the classifier at test time? If UD lemmas are mapped to BNC lemma_POS vectors, which tag is chosen? Please clarify; as written, this result seems surprisingly high and may reflect a mapping artifact.
- Baselines and comparisons are limited or mismatched. The “Stanford tagger” comparison degrades a contextual tagger by supplying only isolated tokens, which is not a fair or particularly informative baseline. More relevant probes include: (i) type-level majority tag baselines, (ii) linear probes on lemma-only embeddings for the main outlier analysis, (iii) simple character/subword features (suffixes) to compare against embeddings for the context-free predictor.
- Scope and generality not fully demonstrated. The work is English-only, with a single embedding configuration (window=2, no subsampling), and no robustness checks (e.g., different windows, CBOW vs. SG, subword models). Given the paper’s claims about PoS information and future applications, at least one additional language or a hyperparameter sensitivity study would strengthen the case.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues. Figures/tables are referenced but not always present; references are incomplete/garbled; links are anonymized; some details are missing (e.g., how OOV handling affects UD test; class priors in training; regularization settings for logistic regression). This impedes verification.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was the UD F=0.99 evaluation performed with the classifier trained on lemma_POS embeddings? What vector was used for a UD lemma at test time? If a lemma maps to multiple lemma_POS vectors in BNC, how was the choice made?
- For the 10-fold CV with duplicated instances: did you ensure that the same word type never appears in both train and test folds? If not, can you report type-level CV?
- Can you replicate the outlier analysis with lemma-only embeddings and a linear probe trained to predict PoS, to rule out reliance on explicit tag signals in contexts?
- How sensitive are your findings to window size, subsampling, and negative sampling? Does the strong conjunction component (dim 94) persist across seeds/configurations?
- For the lemma-only PoS predictor, how does accuracy vary with the size/selection criteria of the “prototypical” lexicon? What happens if dominance threshold is relaxed/tightened?

Additional comments
- Consider releasing code, mappings, and outlier lists for reproducibility. Clarify evaluation splits, OOV rates, and regularization.
- The qualitative findings are valuable; framing the main claim more modestly (as an auditing/analysis tool rather than a new tagging method) would align better with the evidence.
- Clean up references and ensure figures/tables are included and legible.
