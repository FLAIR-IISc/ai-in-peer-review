Summary and main contributions
- The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether this can be used to (i) detect “outlier” lexical items that straddle category boundaries and (ii) spot inconsistencies/errors in PoS annotations. It trains skip-gram models on BNC (lemmas with and without PoS suffixes), learns a multinomial logistic classifier from embeddings to PoS, analyzes misclassifications, and builds a lemma-only PoS predictor trained on “unambiguous” lemmas from UD.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical error analysis showing that distributional vectors can surface annotation inconsistencies and category-boundary phenomena (e.g., participles as ADJ-like, years as NOUN-like, currency/percent tokens as NUM-like), with qualitative comparisons to UD vs. BNC.
  2) A lemma-only, context-free PoS predictor trained on unambiguous lemmas that attains ~0.84 token accuracy on UD test data, suggesting strong PoS signals in embeddings even without sentence context.
  3) Exploratory analysis of embedding dimensions vis-à-vis PoS (e.g., conjunctions’ salient component; F-value feature ranking), arguing PoS information is distributed across many dimensions.

Strengths
- Clear, data-driven qualitative insights: The outlier analysis is thoughtful and linguistically plausible. It highlights well-known gray areas (participles, noun–adjective modification, years/decades, currency/percent expressions) and identifies likely corpus-specific inconsistencies (e.g., clear/plain tagged as adverbs in BNC). The cross-check against UD reinforces the annotation point for numerals/time expressions.
- Simple methodology with strong signals: Despite a small window and no order information, a basic classifier trained on embeddings achieves very high type-level F-scores when embeddings are PoS-suffixed, and decent token-level accuracy (0.84) when trained on lemma-only embeddings. This convincingly supports the hypothesis that PoS information is encoded in distributional representations.
- Practical angle for corpus diagnostics: Using classifier disagreement to flag suspect annotations is a useful idea. This work could be a practical tool to triage large corpora for targeted human review, especially for closed-class items and systematic boundary cases.
- Attempt to quantify feature distribution: The ANOVA feature ranking and learning curve over top-k dimensions support the claim that PoS information is distributed (not isolated to a single dimension), tempering over-interpretation of individual components.

Weaknesses
- Circularity in the main experiment and external validation: The primary classifier is trained on embeddings built from lemma+PoS tokens; the input to the classifier is the embedding of lemma+PoS, and the target is the PoS. This is intrinsically tautological for evaluation: to obtain the embedding for a UD item, you need its PoS tag to construct the key (lemma_POS) in the embedding vocabulary, thus “using” gold PoS at inference time. The authors acknowledge this is “artificial,” but then report 0.99 F1 on UD, which is not a meaningful validation. The outlier analysis remains interesting, but quantitative performance claims from this setup should be down-weighted.
- Unfair baseline/comparison: The Stanford tagger is run on isolated tokens without context, which is not how discriminative PoS taggers are intended to be used. The confusion patterns will be dominated by this misconfiguration, making the comparison uninformative. A fairer comparison is to a context-free baseline, e.g., lemma→majority PoS lexicon baseline, Brown clusters/PPMI context features with a simple classifier, or a lexicon derived from UD training statistics.
- Missing critical baselines for the lemma-only predictor: The 0.84 token accuracy should be compared to (i) a type-level majority tag per lemma baseline (computed from UD training), (ii) a simple frequency/morphology heuristic (e.g., suffixes), and (iii) count-based distributional features. Since training uses only “unambiguous” lemmas, coverage and difficulty are reduced; the benefit over a lexicon baseline is unclear. Per-tag performance and coverage (oov, ambiguous lemmas) are also not reported.
- Reliability of component-level interpretations: Claims about specific dimensions (e.g., component 94 for conjunctions) should account for the rotational invariance of embeddings. Without aligning spaces across random seeds or using supervised linear probes (e.g., logistic weights, PCA on class-centroid differences), component-wise interpretations may be idiosyncratic. The later result that ~100 top features are needed is more convincing; the one-dimension finding risks overinterpretation.
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps: Some referenced tables/figures are missing or lightly described; references are partially corrupted; details about the UD/BNC tag mapping, random seeds, and whether subword handling was used are sparse. The frequency-weighting scheme duplicates identical vectors multiple times rather than using class/sample weights; potential overfitting or bias introduced by this choice is not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a lemma→majority-PoS baseline (from UD training) for the lemma-only predictor, and compare token accuracy and per-tag metrics? Also report performance on ambiguous lemmas separately from unambiguous ones.
- For the “artificial” classifier using lemma+PoS embeddings, can you reframe the analysis to avoid quantitative performance claims and instead focus solely on outlier discovery? Alternatively, can you train embeddings on lemmas only and perform the same outlier analysis to ensure conclusions are not an artifact of injecting PoS into the token IDs?
- Can you provide a fair context-free baseline using simple count-based distributional features (e.g., PPMI vectors with window 2) and/or Brown clusters, to show that embeddings specifically (not just distributional statistics) drive the observed effects?
- How stable are the component-level findings across random seeds/hyperparameters? If you train multiple embedding models, do the top-k features (by F-value) align after a Procrustes rotation, and do the qualitative outlier patterns persist?
- For the annotation error claims (e.g., clear/plain as adverbs), can you include a small human validation (annotation of a sample of flagged tokens) to quantify precision of the error-detection signal?
- Have you tried larger windows, position-sensitive contexts, or subword modeling? Do these change the error profile (e.g., proper nouns vs common nouns) or improve the lemma-only predictor?

Additional comments
- The idea of using distributional disagreement to triage annotation problems is promising. To strengthen the paper for CoNLL, I recommend (i) removing potentially misleading quantitative claims from the lemma+PoS experiment, (ii) adding strong, fair baselines for the lemma-only predictor, (iii) reporting per-tag metrics and coverage, and (iv) validating the error-detection utility with a small-scale manual study.
