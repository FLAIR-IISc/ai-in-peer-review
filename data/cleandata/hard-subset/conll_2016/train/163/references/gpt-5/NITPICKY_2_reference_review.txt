Summary and contributions
This paper explores whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether such information can expose fuzzy PoS boundaries and corpus annotation inconsistencies. The authors (i) train skip-gram embeddings on BNC with tokens as lemma_POS and fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors; (ii) analyze misclassifications as “outliers” near PoS boundaries and as potential tagging errors; (iii) attempt a “proper” PoS predictor trained on embeddings learned without tags, using UD data for supervision; (iv) probe which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS via ANOVA feature ranking.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical error analysis suggesting that embedding-based PoS predictions surface boundary phenomena and some annotation inconsistencies in BNC. 
2) Evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions, and a simple type-based PoS predictor trained on ~1.5k “prototypical” lemmas achieves ~0.84 token accuracy without sentence context.
3) Cross-corpus sanity checks (BNC → UD) suggesting some robustness (though see major caveats below).

Strengths
- Focus on boundary cases: The error analysis identifies plausible gray areas (e.g., nouns vs adjectives in modifiers; participles vs adjectives; numerals functioning as nouns), which is linguistically interesting and potentially helpful for corpus QA.
- Simple, interpretable methodology: Using logistic regression over pretrained embeddings allows clear inspection (prototype averaging, dimensional relevance) without confounds from complex architectures.
- Distributed encoding of PoS: The feature-ablation experiment (ANOVA ranking) supports the claim that PoS signals are widely distributed, not tied to a few axes, aligning with recent findings on embedding interpretability.
- Low-supervision PoS predictor: A context-free, lemma-only classifier hitting ~0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens is a useful baseline for low-resource bootstrapping scenarios and shows the strength of global distributional information.
- Cross-resource tests: Attempting both BNC-based and UD-based evaluations, and contrasting with a mainstream tagger (even if flawed; see below), shows intent to test robustness and situate the approach.

Weaknesses
- Serious methodological ambiguity and potential leakage in the BNC→UD evaluation: The primary embedding model is trained on lemma_POS tokens (e.g., love_VERB). The paper then claims to “apply the trained classifier to all lemmas from UD.” How are vectors obtained for bare lemmas if the model’s vocabulary consists of lemma_POS? If UD gold PoS is appended to retrieve vectors (e.g., love_VERB), this leaks the label into the input. If not, then where do lemma vectors come from? This needs precise clarification; as written, the 0.99 F-score on UD is not interpretable and likely invalid.
- Inflated cross-validation due to instance duplication: The classifier is trained and evaluated (10-fold CV) on repeated instances of the same word vector, weighted by frequency. Random splitting at the instance level means the identical vector for a word type can appear in both train and test folds, inflating the 0.979 F-score. Proper CV should split by word type, not by repeated instances of the same vector.
- Unfair comparison to Stanford tagger: Feeding isolated tokens to a contextual tagger is inappropriate. The resulting confusion matrix reflects an adversarial setting and cannot support conclusions about what “insights” a mainstream tagger can or cannot reveal versus a type-based embedding predictor. At minimum, compare under standard sentence-level tagging and then conduct post-hoc analyses.
- Confounding training setup (lemma_POS tokens) for “outlier” discovery: By splitting tokens into lemma_POS during embedding training, contexts for different uses of the same lemma are artificially segregated by gold tags, which may exaggerate PoS separability and directly encode the corpus’s tagging decisions. This circularity undermines the claim that misclassifications reveal “true” boundary phenomena rather than artifacts of the tag-conditioned embeddings.
- Weaknesses in analysis rigor: Many findings are anecdotal (e.g., “600” instances of adjectives mislabeled as adverbs) without systematic corpus-level validation, inter-annotator checks, or error-rate estimates beyond a few words. No per-class precision/recall, no significance tests, and limited control for frequency, polysemy, capitalization, or named-entity subtypes. Clustering “failed,” but no details (algorithm, k-selection, evaluation) are given.
- Reproducibility and formatting issues: Links “released online” are anonymized; mapping rules BNC→Universal are not fully specified; many references are malformed/mismatched; figures/tables are referenced but not included or are incomplete (e.g., Table 1 missing; Table 2 truncated/unclear); numerous typos and style inconsistencies. The title oversells (“Redefining” PoS) relative to the descriptive, exploratory scope.

Questions to authors
1) UD evaluation with BNC lemma_POS embeddings: How exactly were vectors obtained for UD items? Did you append UD gold tags to lemmas to retrieve lemma_POS vectors? If yes, this leaks gold labels. If not, was there a separate lemma-only embedding model? Please specify models used for each evaluation and provide vocabulary overlap statistics per model.
2) Cross-validation protocol: Did you ensure that no word type appears in both training and validation folds? If not, please redo CV by splitting on word types, not on repeated instances. Report revised scores.
3) Stanford tagger comparison: Please rerun with full sentence context and standard PTB tags. Then compare confusion matrices and derive insights fairly. Alternatively, compare to a type-based baseline that, like your method, has no access to context.
4) Clustering attempts: What clustering methods and settings were tried (k-means, GMMs, spectral, k selection, normalization)? How was “failure” assessed (e.g., NMI/ARI vs gold tags)? This is important given the paper’s premise about natural clustering by PoS.
5) Outlier validation: Can you provide a systematic evaluation (manual annotation sample) confirming that your highest-coverage error types correspond to boundary cases or true annotation errors rather than frequency/OOV effects? For the claimed BNC adverb/adjective issue, please quantify corpus-wide counts and contexts, and verify against original guidelines.
6) Feature analysis: The ANOVA-based ranking and training-set accuracy curves may overfit. Can you report test-set performance as a function of k, and per-class performance changes? Also, report regularization details (C, penalty) for logistic regression.
7) Data and mappings: Please provide the exact BNC→Universal mapping, preprocessing (lemmatization, punctuation handling, case), and hyperparameters, along with code and the anonymized artifacts necessary to reproduce all results.

Overall, the idea that embeddings encode PoS and can surface boundary phenomena is worthwhile, but the current submission has significant methodological ambiguities, unfair comparisons, and presentation issues that need to be addressed before the claims are convincing.
