Summary and claimed contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. The authors (i) train SGNS embeddings on BNC tokens concatenated with gold PoS (lemma_POS), then fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors and analyze misclassifications as “outliers” that illuminate PoS boundaries and annotation inconsistencies; (ii) examine “prototypical” PoS vectors and feature importance, suggesting the PoS signal is distributed across many dimensions; (iii) present a lemma-only PoS predictor trained on embeddings without PoS tags, using UD English as supervision from unambiguous lemmas, achieving 0.84 accuracy without sentence context. They also compare their confusion analysis to one obtained by running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical corpus-diagnostics methodology: using embedding-based PoS prediction errors to surface PoS boundary phenomena and systemic corpus tagging issues (e.g., currencies/years, participles, “make it plain” type).
2) Empirical evidence that PoS affiliation is strongly present yet distributed across many embedding dimensions; simple proto-vector analysis and ANOVA feature ranking illustrate this.
3) Proof-of-concept lemma-only PoS prediction from global distributional contexts, suggesting a potential tool for preliminary tagging or lexicon induction.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis with concrete, linguistically meaningful findings. The paper surfaces plausible boundary cases (NOUN–ADJ, VBN–ADJ, NUM–NOUN/PROPN) and likely annotation errors in BNC (e.g., plain/clear tagged as ADV), showing real utility for corpus quality control.
- Held-out evaluation by word type (100–500 frequency slice) supports that PoS information in embeddings generalizes beyond the top-10k vocabulary; the outlier analysis is based on non-trivial errors rather than isolated anecdotes.
- The lemma-only predictor achieves 0.84 accuracy without using sentence context, indicating substantial PoS signal in global co-occurrence patterns and suggesting applications when sentence-level tagging is not feasible.
- The paper argues (with feature ranking) that the PoS signal is not localized to a few axes, countering simplistic interpretations of individual embedding dimensions while also highlighting interesting idiosyncrasies (e.g., a conjunction-sensitive component).
- The proposed approach is simple, reproducible in spirit, and potentially valuable to annotation projects seeking to audit or refine PoS schemes.

Weaknesses
- Supervision leakage and interpretability of the main experiment. Training embeddings over lemma_POS tokens injects gold PoS into the token identity; the classifier then “predicts” PoS from representations learned with explicit access to those labels. This makes high F-scores unsurprising and undermines claims that unsupervised embeddings capture PoS. The UD evaluation in Sec. 3 is unclear: if the embedding vocab is lemma_POS, how are vectors obtained for UD lemmas without using their gold tags? If gold PoS is used to pick lemma_POS vectors at test time, the evaluation is invalid.
- Cross-validation protocol likely inflated. CV is performed over frequency-weighted repeated instances of the same word vector; unless folds are split by word type, identical vectors appear in train and test, inflating scores. Type-level CV should be reported.
- Unfair baseline comparison. Running the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens removes its principal information source (sentence context). Drawing conclusions from that confusion matrix is not meaningful; a fair comparison should use full sentences and standard settings.
- Limited breadth and ablations. Results are English-only; claims about low-resource utility are speculative without multilingual or low-data experiments. No sensitivity analyses for window size, model family (CBOW, GloVe, PPMI-SVD), dimensionality, or subword/casing effects. The feature analysis reports training accuracy vs. k features; test curves and statistical tests are needed.
- Reproducibility and preprocessing details. Important choices (lemmatizer, casing, tokenization of numerics/symbols, OOV handling) are not fully specified. The mapping to UPOS is “released” but anonymized; figures are referenced but not visible; classifier hyperparameters beyond “logistic regression” are not detailed.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was the UD evaluation in Sec. 3 conducted? If embeddings were trained on lemma_POS tokens, what vectors were fed for UD items? Was gold PoS used to select lemma_POS vectors at test time?
- For the 10-fold CV on the 10k most frequent words, did you split by word type (ensuring no identical vectors cross folds)? Can you report type-level CV results?
- Can you re-run the Stanford tagger comparison on full sentences and discuss the resulting confusion patterns?
- In Sec. 5, what proportion of test tokens correspond to ambiguous lemmas? How does lemma-only accuracy vary by PoS and frequency? Would using surface wordforms (rather than lemmas) help?
- How sensitive are your findings to embedding hyperparameters and model families (CBOW/GloVe/PPMI-SVD) and to window sizes? Does the distributed nature of PoS signal persist?

Additional comments/suggestions
- To avoid supervision leakage in outlier detection, prefer embeddings trained on raw lemmas (or wordforms), then learn PoS centroids from a small seed (as in Sec. 5) and flag items by distance/conformity; compare the resulting outliers to your current list.
- Provide a ranked list of high-confidence outliers with examples and release code/data to facilitate independent auditing of corpora.
- Replicate on at least one typologically different language and a smaller corpus to substantiate generality and low-resource claims.
- Report held-out accuracy vs. number of features (k) to support the “distributed signal” claim with generalization, not training curves.

Overall, the paper offers a useful and timely diagnostic perspective, but key methodological clarifications and stronger, leakage-free evaluations are needed to support the central claims.
