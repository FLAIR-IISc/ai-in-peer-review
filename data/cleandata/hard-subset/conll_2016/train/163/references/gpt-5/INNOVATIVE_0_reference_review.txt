Summary
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and proposes to use embedding-space behavior to (i) reveal “outlier” words that sit near PoS boundaries and (ii) detect inconsistencies and errors in corpus PoS annotation. The authors train skip-gram embeddings over BNC where tokens are lemma+PoS, learn a multinomial classifier over the top 10k types, and analyze systematic misclassifications as evidence of boundary phenomena and annotation issues. They also train a minimalist, zero-context PoS predictor using embeddings trained on raw lemmas and a small lexicon of “prototypical” UD words, achieving 0.84 token accuracy. Finally, they probe which embedding dimensions carry PoS signal.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An operational and data-driven framework that uses embedding-space classification to surface PoS boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies, with concrete case studies (e.g., participles vs adjectives, years as nouns, currency/percent tokens, SCONJ/ADV confusions, and systematic BNC errors like “plain/clear/sharp”).
2) A simple, low-supervision type-level PoS predictor that achieves reasonable accuracy (0.84) without any sentence context, highlighting how strongly PoS is encoded in standard embeddings and suggesting a practical bootstrapping path for low-resource settings.
3) Empirical evidence that PoS signal is distributed across many embedding dimensions (feature ranking and ablation), plus identification of interpretable dimensions for specific classes.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and potential impact: Recasting embeddings as a diagnostic lens on PoS categories is a bold and appealing idea. The paper goes beyond showing that embeddings contain PoS information; it leverages misclassifications to question category boundaries and reveal annotation issues. This is a useful and under-explored application with real utility for corpus curation and linguistic analysis.
- Concrete insights backed by examples: The analysis yields several linguistically plausible and actionable findings (e.g., currency/percent tokens behaving like numerals; years functioning as nouns; participles clustering with adjectives; pre/pro/cross behaving adjectivally/adverbially; SCONJ/ADV boundary for “immediately”), including evidence of systematic BNC errors. This demonstrates the method’s value beyond raw accuracy numbers.
- Complementary to standard taggers: The comparison with a mainstream tagger’s confusion profiles shows that the proposed approach exposes different classes of issues than typical tagging errors, supporting the claim that embedding-based diagnostics add value.
- Minimal supervision PoS predictor: The “prototypical words” approach is refreshingly simple yet effective, reinforcing the core thesis (PoS is strongly encoded in type embeddings) and suggesting a practical bootstrapping strategy for low-resource languages.
- Feature-level probing: Ranking dimensions by ANOVA F-values and showing gradual accuracy gains with more features is a clean way to argue that PoS signal is distributed, not localized, aligning with the distributed semantics hypothesis.

Weaknesses
- Potential circularity in the main outlier analysis: The primary outlier-detection experiment trains embeddings over lemma+PoS tokens. This design can bake tagger decisions into the token identities and may exaggerate separability across classes. While the misclassification analysis still uncovers interesting inconsistencies, the risk of label leakage should be addressed more directly. A parallel outlier analysis using embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS tokenization) would strengthen the core claim.
- Limited quantitative validation of “annotation error detection”: The paper provides convincing anecdotes but lacks a systematic manual evaluation quantifying how many flagged items are true annotation errors versus legitimate boundary cases. Even a small blinded study (precision of error detection across top-K flagged types) would substantially increase the credibility of the diagnostic claim.
- Baselines and comparisons for the zero-context tagger are thin: The 0.84 accuracy is promising, but there is no comparison to simple frequency/lexicon baselines, character- or morphology-based heuristics, or prior type-level PoS predictors. It is also unclear whether performance is reported on tokens or types in all settings, and how ambiguity/OOVs are handled—details that matter for interpreting the result.
- Sensitivity to modeling choices: Many choices are ad hoc (window=2, no subsampling, negative sampling=10, instance weighting by frequency). No ablation explores how these affect both classification accuracy and the qualitative outlier profile, which is important for claiming robustness of the diagnostic tool.
- Scope and related work: Experiments are English-only; assessing even one additional typologically different language would materially strengthen the generality claim. Related work on probing/diagnostic classifiers and type-level PoS prediction is not fully covered, and citations are messy/incomplete in places.

Questions to Authors
- Can you replicate the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS concatenation) and the same classifier? Does the set/ranking of boundary cases and annotation issues persist?
- Have you conducted any manual evaluation to estimate the precision of “true annotation errors” among the top-N flagged items? If not, can you include a small study in the camera-ready?
- How sensitive are your findings to hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, CBOW vs SG, negative samples), training corpus (BNC vs another English corpus), and frequency weighting? Do the same outliers show up?
- For the UD experiments: is the 0.99 reported on types or tokens? For the 0.84 result, what is the class distribution after OOV removal? How are ambiguous lemmas treated in evaluation?
- Could you replicate the core analyses on at least one non-English language (e.g., a morphologically richer or word-order flexible language) to support the “redefining PoS classes” narrative?

Overall, I find the central idea bold and valuable, with promising evidence. The methodology is simple yet yields interesting linguistic and practical insights. The main reservations concern potential circularity and limited quantitative validation; addressing these would make this a strong and influential contribution.
