Summary
This paper investigates whether unsupervised distributional word embeddings encode useful signals about parts of speech (PoS). The authors train Skip-gram embeddings on the BNC, sometimes with lemma+PoS concatenated as tokens, and fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings. They then analyze misclassifications to surface “boundary” cases, possible annotation inconsistencies, and potential improvements to tagsets. Finally, they build a context-free PoS predictor trained on “prototypical” (mostly unambiguous) lemmas from UD, achieving 0.84 token-level accuracy on UD test data, and examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Qualitative, data-driven analysis showing how embedding-based PoS prediction errors reveal category boundaries and corpus annotation issues (e.g., adjective/adverb confusion, numerals vs. nouns, years, participles, pre-/pro-/cross). This is the most substantive and interesting contribution.
2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is robustly encoded across many embedding dimensions (not a single axis), supported by ANOVA ranking and “prototypical” PoS vectors.
3) A very simple, context-free PoS predictor based on proximity to prototypical words that attains 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens, suggesting a possible low-resource bootstrapping route.

Strengths
- Insightful error analysis with concrete linguistic takeaways: The paper surfaces several plausible and useful cases—e.g., participles behaving adjectivally, nominal–adjectival boundary cases, numbers/years vs. nouns, and currency strings—many aligning with known grey areas. The “plain/clear/sharp” adverbial tagging issue in BNC is a persuasive example of discovering systematic annotation problems.
- Clear evidence that embeddings encode PoS signals: High performance on held-out BNC word types (F≈0.91) using word-level embeddings, and the finding that PoS-related signal spreads across many dimensions (plateau near ~100 features) rather than a single feature are valuable observations in line with prior work but presented with an accessible analysis.
- Minimalist predictor shows nontrivial accuracy: A context-free, lemma-level classifier trained on unambiguous words reaches 0.84 token accuracy on UD test data, which is notable given it lacks sentence context and uses a tiny supervised lexicon (n≈1.5k). This underscores the strength of corpus-wide distributional cues.
- Potential practical utility for corpus curation: The approach could help flag annotation inconsistencies and guide tagset refinement, which is genuinely useful to treebank maintainers.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to prior work: Prior studies have shown clustering by PoS, leveraging embeddings for PoS, and correlating dimensions with linguistic features. The methodological core (logistic regression on embeddings) is standard; the distinctive aspect is the qualitative error analysis, which, while interesting, may be too incremental for a venue like CoNLL without stronger quantitative validation.
- Methodological concerns about data leakage and evaluation clarity: The main classifier is trained and tested on embeddings built from lemma+PoS tokens; this makes sense for outlier discovery but conflates the representation with the gold tag. More critically, the UD “generalization” test (F≈0.99) is unclear: if UD lemmas were mapped to lemma+PoS tokens to retrieve embeddings, the gold PoS would have been used to pick the vector, invalidating the test. This needs clarification and likely revision.
- Cross-validation inflation due to duplicated instances: The classifier is trained on many repeated copies of the same word vector (frequency-weighted) and 10-fold CV appears to be at the instance level, so identical vectors may appear in both train and validation folds, inflating the 0.979 result. CV should be at the word-type level; class weighting in the loss would avoid this issue.
- Baselines are weak/unfair or missing: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on single tokens without sentence context, which is not its intended regime and is not a meaningful comparator. For the context-free predictor, a simple “most frequent tag per lemma” baseline built from the UD training set is the critical baseline; without it, it is unclear whether embeddings beat a lexicon-based majority baseline. Similarly, a character/subword morphological baseline could be informative.
- Limited scope and quantitative rigor: Only English is studied; hyperparameter sensitivity is not probed; the dimensional analysis uses training accuracy rather than held-out performance; figures/tables are referenced but not fully shown; references and footnotes are messy. Stronger quantitative validation of “annotation error detection” (e.g., human re-annotation or agreement) would elevate the contribution.

Questions to authors
1) UD generalization test: How exactly were embeddings retrieved for UD “lemmas” given your BNC model was trained on lemma+UPOS tokens? Did you use the UD gold PoS to construct lemma+PoS keys? If so, this evaluation is invalid. Please clarify and, if needed, replace with a fair test.
2) Cross-validation: Was 10-fold CV performed at the word-type level (ensuring a word’s vector appears in only one fold), or at the instance level after frequency expansion? If the latter, can you report type-level CV results?
3) Context-free predictor baseline: What is the performance of a “most frequent tag per lemma” baseline trained on the same UD training set? Does your embedding-based classifier outperform it on UD test tokens?
4) Stanford tagger comparison: Do you have results with the tagger in its standard, sentence-context setting? If the intent is to compare “no context” methods, can you provide a token-based morphological/character baseline instead?
5) Reproducibility: Can you release the BNC→UPOS mapping, training code, and word lists of flagged outliers to support verification and follow-up studies? Also, please fix broken/placeholder references and figures.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The paper addresses an interesting question and the qualitative findings are valuable, but methodological issues (potential leakage, inadequate baselines, inflated CV) and limited novelty reduce confidence. With clearer, leakage-free evaluations, stronger baselines (especially MFT-by-lemma), and broader quantitative validation (possibly across languages), this work could become a solid contribution.
