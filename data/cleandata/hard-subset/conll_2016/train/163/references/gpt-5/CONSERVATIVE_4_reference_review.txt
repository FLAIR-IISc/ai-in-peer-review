Summary and main contributions
- The paper investigates whether static distributional word embeddings encode sufficient information to recover coarse part-of-speech (PoS) classes and to surface boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies. Using BNC-based Skipgram embeddings, a multinomial logistic regression predicts PoS for word types with high F-scores; errors are qualitatively analyzed to identify “outliers.” A second experiment trains embeddings without PoS-tagged tokens and builds a simple lemma-level PoS predictor on UD, yielding 0.84 accuracy without using sentential context. An ANOVA-based feature ranking suggests PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.
- As I see it, the contributions are:
  1) A qualitative, corpus-driven analysis showing that errors of a simple embedding-based PoS predictor align with known PoS boundary phenomena and can flag annotation issues (e.g., BNC adverb/adjective confusions, numerals vs nouns/years).
  2) A straightforward probing-style result that static embeddings contain strong PoS signals distributed across many dimensions.
  3) A simple type-level PoS predictor trained on unambiguous lemmas, suggesting a possible utility for preliminary tagging and corpus curation.

Strengths
- Clear qualitative analysis with concrete examples. The examination of misclassifications provides linguistically interpretable cases (participles, noun–adj alternations, years as nouns, currency tokens) and plausibly surfaces real inconsistencies in BNC and differences with UD conventions.
- Simple, transparent methodology. The use of logistic regression and frequency-weighted instances is easy to reproduce and interpret, which helps isolate what embeddings capture without complex architectures.
- Cross-corpus checks. Applying the classifier to UD lemmas and discussing differences in tokenization/tagging conventions strengthens the claim that the observations are not entirely corpus-specific.
- Useful perspective for corpus maintenance. Using embedding-based “outlier” detection as a tool to audit PoS annotations is a practical idea with potential impact for resource curation, especially where manual QA is costly.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work. That static embeddings encode PoS is well established (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The methodological core—probing with a linear classifier—adds modestly incremental evidence. Claims about “redefining PoS classes” are not supported by new theory or broad cross-linguistic validation.
- Methodological concerns and potential leakage:
  - In Sec. 3 the embeddings are trained on tokens concatenated with PoS tags (“lemma_POS”), then a classifier predicts the PoS of those same lemma_POS tokens. This setup risks circularity: the contexts for lemma_POS are already disambiguated by tag, making the task easier and weakening any claim about discovering PoS from purely distributional semantics.
  - The 10-fold cross-validation with frequency-weighted repeated instances likely splits identical word vectors across train/test folds unless grouped by type, inflating the 0.979 F-score. The unseen-word test (0.91) helps, but the protocol needs a strict type-level split throughout.
- Missing and unfair baselines:
  - The “no context” PoS predictor (0.84 accuracy on UD) is compared only to trivial baselines. A strong and appropriate baseline is a unigram dictionary assigning each lemma its majority PoS from UD training; such a baseline typically performs very strongly on lemma-level tagging and may exceed 0.84. Without this comparison, it is unclear that the embedding-based predictor is competitive or “impressive.”
  - Other natural baselines (suffix/prefix and orthographic features, Brown clusters, fastText subword features, character n-gram linear classifiers) are not included.
  - The comparison to the Stanford tagger is unfair: evaluating it on isolated tokens strips away its core advantage (context), so the confusion matrix comparison does not meaningfully support the claimed advantages of the distributional approach.
- Confounds and controls:
  - Frequency and orthography (capitalization, digits) can heavily influence embeddings and PoS separability; no controls are reported (e.g., regressing out log-frequency, lowercasing, masking digits).
  - The outlier claims (e.g., “systematic errors” in BNC) would be much stronger with a small-scale manual verification study or citations to prior errata.
  - Stability across random seeds and embedding hyperparameters is largely deferred to future work; current results may be brittle.
- Scope and claims:
  - The title and framing (“Redefining PoS classes”) overreach relative to evidence limited to English and static embeddings. No quantitative demonstration that these insights improve a downstream tagger or annotation workflow is provided; the current study is exploratory.

Questions to authors
- Type-level CV: Did you ensure that all copies of a given word vector (after frequency weighting) reside in the same fold? If not, please re-run with strict type-level splits and report results.
- PoS-tagged embeddings: For Sec. 3, can you replicate the outlier findings when training embeddings on raw lemmas only (no PoS in the vocabulary), to remove potential circularity?
- Baselines: What is the accuracy of a unigram lemma-to-majority-PoS baseline on your UD setup? How do suffix/orthographic or fastText-based linear baselines perform?
- Controls: Were embeddings lowercased? How did you handle digits? Did you control for frequency effects (e.g., partialling out frequency) in the probing analysis?
- Validation: For the BNC “adverb vs adjective” issues you cite, can you provide a small manual audit to quantify precision of detected annotation errors?
- Generality: How stable are the outlier lists and probing results across random seeds, embedding algorithms (CBOW, GloVe, fastText), and hyperparameters (window size, dim, negatives)?

Overall recommendation
- While the qualitative insights are interesting and the tool could be useful for corpus QA, the paper’s novelty is limited and the methodology has important shortcomings (potential leakage, missing strong baselines, unfair comparisons, lack of controls). Claims about redefining PoS boundaries are not adequately substantiated. I recommend rejection in the current form; a revised version should include rigorous baselines (especially lemma-majority), strict type-level evaluation, removal of circularity, proper controls for confounds, and at least a small-scale human validation of detected annotation issues.
