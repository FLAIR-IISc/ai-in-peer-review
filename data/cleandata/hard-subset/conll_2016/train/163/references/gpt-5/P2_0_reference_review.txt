Summary
The paper investigates how well static distributional word embeddings (Skip-gram) encode coarse parts-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this signal can be used to (i) surface boundary cases between PoS classes and potential corpus annotation errors, and (ii) build a simple PoS predictor without sentence context. The main experiment trains embeddings on BNC tokens concatenated with their PoS tags (lemma_TAG), then fits a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors. Misclassifications are analyzed qualitatively, revealing many intuitive boundary cases (e.g., NOUN↔ADJ, VERB participles↔ADJ), currency/percent tokens as NUM, years/decades as NOUN/PROPN, and alleged systematic BNC tagging inconsistencies (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as ADV). A second experiment trains embeddings on lemmas without tags and trains a classifier on “prototypical” UD words to predict PoS for lemmas without context, achieving 0.84 accuracy. A feature analysis suggests PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence and qualitative analysis showing static embeddings encode robust PoS signals and that embedding-based misclassification can surface PoS boundary phenomena and corpus annotation issues.
2) A simple, context-free PoS predictor from embeddings trained on raw lemmas, and a probing-style analysis indicating PoS information is distributed across many dimensions.
3) A practical methodology for corpus auditing (outlier discovery) using distributional vectors and a lightweight classifier.

Strengths
- Clear, useful qualitative insights into PoS boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies: The paper provides concrete, interpretable examples (e.g., participles, currency/percent tokens, years/decades, SCONJ vs ADV), many of which align with linguistic intuition and known tagging pain points. This supports the claim that distributional representations can help audit corpora and rethink PoS boundaries.
- Simple, reproducible methodology with strong signal: A linear classifier on static embeddings yields very high F1 for PoS, reinforcing that distributions largely determine coarse PoS. The “prototypical PoS vectors” and feature-importance curve convincingly show the signal is robust and distributed rather than hinging on a single component.
- Cross-corpus probe: Testing on a different resource (UD) and discussing differences in tokenization/tagging (e.g., NUM+SYM vs single token for currency) strengthens the generality of some findings and grounds the annotation discussion.
- Practical angle: The approach is lightweight, needs no sentence context, and could be valuable for pre-annotation or error mining in low-resource settings, where a small seed list of unambiguous words can bootstrap a lexicon-level tagger.

Weaknesses
- Circularity/leakage in the main setup: Training embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens bakes PoS-discriminative contexts into the representation (neighbors are also PoS-annotated), making the classification task easier and potentially biasing the error analysis. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 5, the central outlier analysis hinges on these embeddings; repeating the analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no tags) would be more convincing.
- Questionable baseline comparison: Feeding the Stanford tagger isolated tokens (without sentence context) is not a fair comparator to an approach that leverages massive corpus-wide distributional context. The resulting confusion patterns are unsurprisingly different and the claim that the distributional predictor “reveals different cases” is hard to interpret without a fair baseline (e.g., a lexicon-based PoS prior, or a tagger with its intended context).
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: That embeddings encode PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties is well established (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; numerous probing studies). The new element is mainly the specific error-mining workflow and the qualitative case studies. The paper would benefit from clearer positioning versus probing literature and stronger quantitative validation of the error-mining utility.
- Incomplete and partly inconsistent evaluation: The report of 0.99 F1 on UD is unclear given vocabulary mismatch (embeddings trained on lemma+tag cannot straightforwardly index plain lemmas). Details on how UD items were matched to vectors are missing. For error detection, there is no quantitative validation (e.g., sample adjudication with precision/recall of true annotation errors vs genuine ambiguity). The second experiment’s 0.84 accuracy (no context) is interesting but of limited practical value; its contribution would be stronger with ablations (window size, subword features) and comparisons to simpler baselines (e.g., frequency-based priors, capitalization/shape features).
- Presentation issues: References are messy/incomplete, some figures/tables are referenced but not fully described, and resource availability (e.g., tag mapping, outlier lists) is unclear. This harms reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- UD evaluation: How, precisely, were UD lemmas mapped to embedding vectors in the lemma+PoS model? Did you construct lemma+gold-tag tokens to look up vectors (which would entangle gold information) or use plain lemmas (which would not exist in that model’s vocabulary)?
- Outlier validation: What fraction of the surfaced “errors” are true annotation mistakes vs acceptable ambiguity according to the BNC guidelines? Can you provide a small, double-annotated adjudication to quantify precision?
- Tag leakage: To what extent do you think the lemma+PoS embeddings inflate separability because contexts also carry PoS tags? Can you replicate the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas to rule this out?
- Baselines: How does the context-free classifier compare to (i) a frequency-based prior per lemma, (ii) a simple shape/capitalization model, and (iii) fastText subword embeddings? How sensitive are results to window size and negative sampling?
- Resources: Will you release the BNC-to-UD tag mapping, the list of outliers with predicted labels and confidence, and code to reproduce the analyses?

Overall assessment
The paper is clear, and the qualitative insights are interesting and potentially useful for corpus auditing. However, methodological circularity in the main experiment, limited novelty beyond established probing results, and gaps in evaluation (especially for UD and error validation) weaken the contribution. With clarifications and a replication of the outlier analysis on tag-free embeddings plus a small-scale adjudication study, this work would be significantly stronger for CoNLL.
