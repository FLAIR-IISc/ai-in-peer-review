Summary and contributions
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether this signal can be used both for PoS prediction and to probe PoS category boundaries and corpus annotation inconsistencies. Using skip-gram embeddings trained on the BNC and simple multinomial logistic regression, the authors (i) show high type-level PoS predictability, (ii) analyze misclassifications to surface boundary cases and likely PoS annotation errors (notably in BNC, with some cross-checking on UD), and (iii) build a context-free PoS predictor from embeddings trained without PoS tags, evaluating on UD.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence and analysis that standard distributional embeddings encode strong PoS signals that are distributed across many dimensions, not localized.
2) A systematic error analysis showing that misclassifications correlate with linguistically plausible boundary cases (e.g., adjectival nouns, participles) and with annotation inconsistencies (e.g., years as NUM vs NOUN; adverbs vs adjectives), suggesting a practical method to audit PoS-annotated corpora.
3) A simple, low-supervision PoS predictor that assigns PoS to lemmas without context using embeddings and prototypical training sets, with preliminary results on UD.

Strengths
- Linguistically meaningful error analysis: The misclassification study surfaces well-known grey areas (e.g., nouns vs adjectives, passive participles vs adjectives) and concrete annotation issues (currency/percentages tagged as NOUN, years/decades tagged as NUM vs NOUN, BNC’s “plain/clear/sharp” adverbs). This demonstrates the utility of embedding-based diagnostics for auditing PoS inventories and annotations.
- Cross-corpus checks: Beyond BNC internal evaluation, the classifier is applied to UD English lemmas, attaining high F1 (0.99 on retained items) and corroborating several error patterns (with differences that reflect UD’s tokenization/tagging choices). This supports some robustness of the observations beyond a single corpus.
- Low-supervision PoS prediction: The context-free predictor trained on “prototypical” lemmas from UD achieves 0.84 token-level accuracy without any sentence context, which is competitive as a purely lexical/embedding baseline and suggests usefulness for bootstrapping in low-resource settings.
- Feature distribution finding: The ANOVA-based ranking and learning curves indicate that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (with a rough plateau around 100 top features), aligning with prior intuitions and providing a quantitative view.
- Clarity of experimental setup: The embedding training configurations, tagset mapping decisions, and frequency weighting rationale are described with sufficient detail to support replication of the core experiments.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation leakage in cross-validation: The 0.979 F-score on the 10k most frequent words is computed with 10-fold CV over frequency-weighted instances, meaning identical word-type vectors appear in both train and test folds. This inflates performance. Type-level splits (no shared word types across folds) are necessary to assess generalization.
- Circularity and dependence on gold tags: The primary outlier analysis uses embeddings trained on tokens concatenated with gold PoS tags. This introduces dependence on the very annotation being audited and may bias the embedding geometry. While misclassifications can still highlight mismatches, the approach is not strictly independent of the gold tags it critiques.
- Unfair baseline comparison: The comparison to the Stanford PoS tagger feeds isolated tokens (no sentence context), which the tagger is not designed for. Resulting confusion matrices are not directly comparable to those from the embedding-based predictor and weaken claims that the latter reveals “different” insights. A fairer baseline would be a lexicon-based majority tag per lemma and/or a tagger evaluated in standard sentence context.
- Missing key baselines for the context-free predictor: For UD evaluation, a lemma-majority baseline (most frequent PoS per lemma from training) is not reported; such a baseline often performs strongly at token level and is necessary to assess added value over simple lexicon heuristics. Similarly, a frequency-matched random or prior-based baseline would contextualize the 0.84 accuracy.
- Feature analysis assessed on training data: The feature ablation (top-k ANOVA dimensions) reports training accuracy curves. Without validation/test curves, this may overstate the number of features needed and risks overfitting. Reporting test accuracy vs k would be more informative.
- Novelty is incremental: Prior work has shown that embeddings encode morphological/PoS information and that embedding dimensions correlate with linguistic properties. The paper’s methodological advance primarily lies in using misclassification-driven auditing of PoS boundaries and annotations, which is useful but not a substantial algorithmic novelty.
- Reporting and formatting issues: Some tables/figures are referenced but not fully presented; references are inconsistently formatted; minor typos persist. While not central to scientific merit, they impede clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you re-run the PoS classification (Section 3) with type-level cross-validation (no shared word types across folds) and report the revised performance? This would remove leakage introduced by frequency-weight weighting across folds.
- For the UD context-free predictor, what is the lemma-majority baseline accuracy on the same test set (with the same token filtering)? How does your 0.84 compare to that baseline?
- How sensitive are your findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, downsampling, negative samples) and model types (e.g., CBOW, fastText)? Do the outlier categories and the “distributed PoS signal” persist across configurations?
- In the auditing results for BNC (e.g., adverbs mis-tagged as adjectives), can you provide quantitative counts and precision of detected errors after spot-checking? This would substantiate the claim that the method surfaces systematic annotation errors.
- For the “prototypical lemma” training set, how does performance vary when including increasingly ambiguous lemmas (e.g., allowing alternates up to 0.75 of dominant frequency)? Is there a trade-off between coverage and accuracy?

Overall, the paper offers a clear and useful empirical analysis showing that standard embeddings carry strong PoS signals and that misclassification-based probing can highlight PoS boundary phenomena and annotation inconsistencies. However, methodological issues (type-level CV, fair baselines, fairness of tagger comparison) limit the strength of the evidence. Addressing these concerns would substantially improve the technical soundness and the impact of the contribution.
