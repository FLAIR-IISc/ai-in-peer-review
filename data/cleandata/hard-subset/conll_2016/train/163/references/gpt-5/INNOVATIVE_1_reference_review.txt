Summary and claimed contributions
This paper investigates how distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and proposes using these representations to (i) surface “boundary” cases across PoS classes and (ii) flag annotation inconsistencies in treebanks. The authors first train a Skip-gram model on the BNC with tokens of the form lemma_POS, then train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings and analyze outliers (words whose distributional patterns suggest a different PoS than annotated). They then build a context-free PoS predictor trained on embeddings built from lemma-only text and show moderate accuracy on UD. Finally, they examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A corpus-auditing method that leverages embedding-based PoS prediction to identify boundary phenomena and likely annotation inconsistencies; accompanied by a thoughtful qualitative analysis.
2) Empirical confirmation (and quantification) that PoS information is strongly distributed across many embedding dimensions, along with a simple, context-free PoS predictor using only a small set of prototypical words.
3) A conceptual argument and set of tools toward rethinking rigid PoS boundaries using unsupervised distributional structure.

Strengths
- Novel direction: using embeddings explicitly as a lens on PoS boundaries and annotation quality. While prior work has shown that embeddings capture PoS, this paper’s focus on outlier discovery for linguistic insight and corpus auditing is fresh and potentially impactful. The analyses (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, participles vs. adjectives, time expressions, currency) convincingly illustrate the value of the approach.
- Practical utility for resource creation/cleanup. The method surfaces plausible systematic errors in BNC (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs), points to inconsistent treatment of years/currencies across tagsets, and suggests adjustments for borderline items (e.g., “pre/pro” behaving adjectivally). This is a useful tool for treebank maintenance and harmonization.
- Cross-corpus sanity checks and qualitative depth. The authors don’t stop at a single metric: they compare outlier patterns on BNC and UD, and contrast their confusion analysis to one derived from the Stanford tagger (even if that comparison has caveats). The qualitative case studies are concrete and linguistically well-motivated.
- Minimal supervision and strong signal. The context-free predictor trained on ~1.5k prototypical lemmas reaches 0.84 accuracy without any sentence context, underscoring how robustly embeddings encode PoS tendencies. The feature-ranking study shows PoS signal is distributed across many dimensions (not a few “magic” axes).
- Reproducible design choices and clear framing of limitations. Hyperparameters and preprocessing are stated; the paper is upfront that the contextless predictor is not a state-of-the-art tagger and positions it as complementary.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art on embeddings and PoS. Earlier work has documented that PoS correlates with embedding structure (clustering, diagnostic classifiers, dimension-linguistic correlations). The paper’s main new technical element is the outlier auditing workflow. The conceptual pitch (“redefining PoS classes”) is bolder than what is ultimately delivered; the results read more as systematic auditing and qualitative analysis than a redefinition.
- Potential circularity and overstated generality in Section 3. The main classifier is trained on embeddings built from lemma_POS tokens in a corpus where every context token also carries a POS tag. This setup likely amplifies POS-related regularities in the embedding space, so the strong PoS predictability there is unsurprising. The paper partially addresses this with the lemma-only model in Section 5, but the core outlier analysis relies on the tagged-token training regime.
- Evaluation issues and unclear details:
  - Cross-validation appears to be over repeated instances of the same word vector (frequency-weighted), not by word type, which can inflate CV scores. A type-level split is needed.
  - The UD “F=0.99” evaluation in Section 3 is unclear: were UD lemmas mapped to lemma_POS to retrieve vectors from the lemma_POS model? If so, gold tags are used to fetch the vector, undermining the claim. If not, how do lemma-only entries exist in the lemma_POS model?
  - The Stanford tagger comparison is not a fair baseline (feeding isolated tokens to a sequence tagger). As used, it mainly shows that different tools produce different confusion patterns, not that the proposed method is superior for surfacing boundary cases.
- Scope and stability. All experiments are English-only. Given claims about rethinking PoS boundaries, testing typologically diverse languages would strengthen the paper. Also, no analysis is provided of stability across random seeds/training runs (important when interpreting specific “dimension 94” effects).
- Presentation and reference quality. The paper contains numerous formatting issues, incomplete references, and missing figures/tables in the text. For a conceptual contribution, clarity and polish matter.

Questions to authors
- UD evaluation clarity: In Section 3, how exactly were UD lemmas vectorized when the embedding model’s vocabulary consists of lemma_POS tokens? Did you construct lemma_POS from UD gold tags to look up vectors? If yes, the evaluation does not demonstrate generalization without gold tags.
- Cross-validation splits: Did 10-fold CV in Section 3 split by word type (ensuring a word’s vector never appears in both train and test) or by instances (allowing the same vector in both)? Please report type-level splits.
- Stability: Are the PoS-discriminative dimensions (e.g., “component 94 for conjunctions”) stable across random seeds, corpora, and hyperparameters (window size, negative sampling, downsampling)?
- Human validation: For a random sample of flagged “outliers,” what proportion do human annotators judge as (i) true annotation errors, (ii) genuine boundary/gradient cases, versus (iii) model errors due to sparse or noisy contexts?
- Beyond English: Do the same auditing patterns emerge on a morphologically richer and/or freer-word-order language? Even a small pilot would reinforce the generality of the claim.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). Despite methodological caveats and limited novelty, the paper advances a useful, conceptually provocative direction: using embeddings to interrogate PoS boundaries and audit annotations. With clarifications on evaluation and a strengthened empirical section (type-level splits, stability checks), this could be a valuable contribution to CoNLL’s agenda on representation learning and linguistic structure.
