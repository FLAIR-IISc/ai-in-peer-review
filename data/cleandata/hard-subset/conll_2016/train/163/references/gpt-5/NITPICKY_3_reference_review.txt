Summary
The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS) and whether this can be used (i) to surface “boundary” cases and annotation inconsistencies and (ii) to build a simple PoS predictor without contextual features. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on BNC with lemma+PoS tokens, fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from the vectors, and analyze misclassifications as outliers. They also train embeddings on lemmas only, learn a linear classifier from a small set of “prototypical” UD lemmas, and obtain ~0.84 accuracy on UD tokens (lemma only). They argue PoS information is spread across many dimensions and present qualitative insights about nouns vs adjectives, participles, numerals, etc.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence (via a simple linear probe) that distributional embeddings carry strong PoS signal and can yield reasonable PoS predictions without local context.
2) Qualitative error analysis suggesting embeddings can surface PoS boundary cases and likely annotation inconsistencies (e.g., currency amounts, years, participles, some adverb/adjective confusions).
3) A claim (with a preliminary analysis) that PoS information is not localized in a single axis but is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Strengths
- The idea of using misclassifications from a simple probe to surface likely boundary cases/annotation inconsistencies is appealing, and several examples (currencies as NUM, years as NOUN/PROPN, passive participles behaving adjectivally) are plausible and linguistically interesting.
- The “lemma-only” predictor with a small window shows that even very local distributional context encodes a substantial amount of PoS signal; the linear probe and small seed set make the point simply.
- Cross-corpus testing (BNC→UD) and qualitative comparison with a mainstream tagger’s confusions, albeit flawed (see below), point toward generalizability of the qualitative insights.
- The focus on closed-class vs open-class behavior and coverage rates is useful for understanding where distributional signals are strong or weak.

Weaknesses
1) Potential evaluation leakage and invalid comparison in Section 3/UD 0.99 result. The embeddings used there are trained on lemma+PoS tokens. Applying that model to UD “lemmas” requires knowing the PoS tag to select the correct lemma+PoS vector. The reported F=0.99 suggests the gold PoS was used to retrieve the vector, which invalidates the evaluation. This must be clarified and, if confirmed, re-evaluated without using gold tags at inference.

2) Cross-validation leakage due to frequency-weighted duplication. The 10-fold CV on the 10k BNC words uses repeated identical vectors proportional to frequency. Unless folds were stratified by word type (all duplicates of a type kept in the same fold), the same vector appears in both train and test, inflating the 0.979 F-score. Please rerun type-level CV and report per-type performance.

3) Unfair baseline/comparison. The Stanford tagger was evaluated on isolated tokens, which is not how it is designed to be used; this handicaps it and renders the confusion matrix comparison largely uninterpretable. A fair comparison should feed full sentences with sentence-level decoding and appropriate feature configuration.

4) Axis-level interpretability claims are not justified. Word2vec embeddings are identifiable only up to an orthogonal rotation; statements about “component 94” being specific to conjunctions and ANOVA on raw axes are not stable under rotation. If the goal is interpretability, use rotation-invariant analyses or report linear probe weights/subspaces (e.g., CCA/SVD on probe coefficients) across multiple random seeds/runs.

5) Main analysis relies on embeddings trained with PoS-infused tokens. Many conclusions about PoS boundaries and component behavior are drawn from a model where PoS tags are part of the token identity, which biases the model toward separating PoS. While you acknowledge this was for outlier detection, the linguistic conclusions should be tempered and validated on the lemma-only model. At minimum, replicate the outlier analysis with the tag-free embeddings.

6) Insufficient controls and quantification. Frequency is a major confound (you partly note this for rare proper names), but there is no systematic analysis of error rates vs frequency or polysemy. Provide per-class precision/recall, frequency-binned performance, and robustness across multiple random seeds. The feature-count curve (Figure 3) is reported on the training set, not held-out data, so it does not support generalization claims.

7) Reproducibility and presentation issues. Many implementation details are missing (library, random seeds, LR solver/regularization, learning rate schedule). Footnotes/links are “anonymized”; figures/tables are incomplete or inconsistent; references are severely malformed; the text contains numerous typos and formatting glitches. As submitted, the paper does not meet CoNLL presentation standards.

Questions to Authors
- Section 3 UD evaluation (F=0.99): How did you obtain vectors for UD items given your BNC model uses lemma+PoS tokens? Did you use gold PoS to choose the vector? If not, please detail the procedure and reconcile the very high score.
- In the 10-fold CV with frequency-weighted instances, did you enforce that all instances of a word type reside in the same fold? If not, please rerun with type-level folds and report the result.
- For Figure 3, can you report the k-vs-accuracy curve on a held-out test set (not training accuracy)? Also, given rotation invariance, how do you justify component-wise claims (e.g., #94) across different random initializations?
- Can you provide per-class P/R for the lemma-only predictor, and performance as a function of word frequency and ambiguity?
- Can you redo the Stanford comparison on full sentences with a standard configuration, and compare confusion patterns fairly?
- Please provide precise implementation details (tooling, hyperparameters, seeds), clean references, and working links to mappings/resources.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains interesting qualitative observations and a simple, serviceable probing setup, but there are critical methodological flaws (likely evaluation leakage; CV leakage; unfair baseline), overinterpretation of embedding axes, and substantial presentation/reproducibility issues that preclude acceptance in its current form. Addressing the leakage, rerunning fair evaluations, and tightening the analysis would make this a much stronger submission.
