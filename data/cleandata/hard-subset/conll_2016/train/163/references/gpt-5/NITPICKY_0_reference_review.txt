Summary and key contributions
- The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS), and leverages a simple multinomial logistic regression classifier over embeddings to (i) predict PoS and (ii) surface “outlier” words whose distributional behavior appears atypical for their annotated PoS. A subsequent experiment trains embeddings without PoS tags and uses a small set of “unambiguous” UD lemmas to build a PoS predictor.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical evidence that distributional embeddings carry substantial PoS information, with an analysis of which classes are most confusable and what that reveals about PoS boundaries and corpus annotation inconsistencies.
  2) An outlier-driven error analysis that identifies plausible annotation errors and boundary phenomena (e.g., adjectives vs adverbs; numerals vs nouns; participles vs adjectives) in BNC and to some extent UD.
  3) A preliminary “lemma-only” PoS predictor trained on a small set of prototypical UD lemmas, showing decent performance without access to sentence context.

Strengths
- Insightful qualitative analysis: The error breakdowns (e.g., SCONJ vs ADV; NUM vs NOUN/PROPN; participles; adjectival noun modifiers) are linguistically meaningful and align with known gray areas. The case study on “plain/clear/sharp” being mistagged as adverbs in BNC is a nice demonstration of how distributional evidence can flag systematic annotation issues.
- Demonstrates robustness of PoS signal in embeddings: The “lemma-only” experiment (no PoS in the training corpus, no sentence context at test time) still achieves ~0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens, suggesting the signal is strong and widely distributed across dimensions.
- Practical utility for corpus diagnostics: The approach could be used to triage likely annotation errors or to audit tagset/mapping inconsistencies, and to reconsider tag boundaries (e.g., numerals denoting years and time-of-day tokens).
- Component-level observations: The finding that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions (≥100) rather than a single axis is useful for interpretability and model design, and tempers oversimplified narratives about “PoS dimensions.”

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in the main experiment: The core analysis trains embeddings on tokens of the form lemma_POS and then predicts the POS label from those vectors. This injects annotation directly into the token identities and their contexts (neighbors are also POS-suffixed), so the embeddings are not “unsupervised” with respect to PoS. As a result, the classifier essentially recovers a tag distribution over tag-enriched contexts. This makes outlier detection results difficult to interpret independently of the original annotation and risks circularity.
- Cross-validation and data splitting concerns: The 0.979 weighted F-score is reported from 10-fold CV on 177,343 duplicated instances derived from 10,000 word types. It appears folds may contain repeated identical vectors for the same type across train/test, inflating scores. Type-level splits (grouped by word type) should be used. Similarly, frequency-weighting by duplicating instances is a crude substitute for class-weighting in the loss.
- Inconsistencies in the UD evaluation for the main classifier: The paper claims to “apply the trained classifier to all lemmas from UD” and obtains 0.99 F-score, but the classifier was trained on lemma_POS embeddings; UD lemmas would not exist in that vocabulary. This requires clarification: were UD lemmas also POS-suffixed and embedded? If so, that again bakes in PoS supervision; if not, how are vectors obtained? As written, this result seems inconsistent.
- Unfair comparison to a standard tagger: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens without sentential context, which negates the primary strength of sequence taggers and yields uninformative confusion matrices. This is not an appropriate baseline. A fairer baseline would be: (a) a context-free tagger trained on type-level features; (b) a simple classifier over bag-of-contexts; or (c) evaluate the distributional method on tokens with context to compare like for like.
- Missing figures/tables and incomplete reporting: Figures 1–3 and Tables 1–4 are referenced but not present or are truncated. Core quantitative results (full confusion matrices, per-class precision/recall, macro vs weighted F1, train/test splits by type) are missing. Many claims (e.g., 600 mis-tagged cases) are not substantiated with precise counts and lists. Hyperparameter sensitivity is not examined. No statistical tests or error bars.
- Reference and formatting issues: Numerous citation errors (author lists garbled; year markers with “? \Q2013\E”; incorrect attributions; inconsistent 2013a/2013b; presence of anonymized links in main text), multiple typos (“concatination”, “is is”), misuse of “nominative” vs “nominal,” and various copy-editing issues detract from clarity and polish.

Questions to authors
- How exactly was the 10-fold cross-validation performed on the 177,343 instances? Were folds stratified by word type to avoid the same vector appearing in both training and validation? If not, please re-run with grouped CV and report results.
- For the UD 0.99 F-score: What embeddings and vocabulary were used? If the model trained on lemma_POS tokens, how were vectors for bare lemmas obtained? If the UD tokens were also POS-suffixed, isn’t this supervised with respect to PoS?
- Can you report per-class P/R/F1 (macro and weighted), and the full confusion matrices for the key experiments? Also include the list of top-100 outliers per error type.
- How sensitive are the outlier findings to (i) window size, (ii) subsampling, (iii) negative samples, and (iv) using raw tokens vs lemmas?
- In the component-importance study (Figure 3), training accuracy increases toward near-perfect. What is the corresponding test accuracy as k grows? Please report curves for both train and test (or cross-validated).

Recommendation
While the qualitative insights are interesting, the main technical setup relies on embeddings trained on POS-suffixed tokens, leading to supervision leakage; the evaluation contains splitting and baseline issues; and the paper has substantial presentation and citation problems. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. A revised version should (1) ground the outlier analysis on embeddings trained without POS in the corpus, (2) adopt type-level splits and fair baselines, (3) resolve the UD evaluation inconsistency, and (4) fix missing figures/tables and references.
