Summary
The paper investigates to what extent distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this can be used to (i) identify “boundary” cases between PoS classes and annotation inconsistencies, and (ii) build a simple, context-free PoS predictor. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on BNC with tokens concatenated with their PoS tags, then train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings; they analyze misclassifications to surface interesting borderline phenomena and potential BNC tagging errors. They also train embeddings without PoS tags and show that a small set of “prototypical” lemmas can train a lightweight classifier that reaches 0.84 accuracy on UD English without using sentence context. Finally, they analyze which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Using classifier errors over distributional embeddings to surface systematic PoS boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies (e.g., numerals vs. nouns, participles vs. adjectives, currencies/percentages, years/decades), with concrete qualitative examples.  
2) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained on ~1.5k “prototypical” lemmas achieving 0.84 accuracy on UD English, suggesting robust PoS signals in embeddings and a potential low-resource bootstrapping path.  
3) Evidence that PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions (feature ranking via ANOVA), not concentrated in a few.

Strengths
- Clear empirical evidence that embeddings carry strong PoS signals: The logistic regression over embeddings (even with relatively small windows) performs very well on BNC and holds up in a separate test split. The feature-importance study also supports distributed encoding of PoS information, aligning with prior observations and strengthening reproducibility.
- Insightful error analysis that reveals linguistically meaningful boundary cases: The paper provides concrete, interpretable examples (e.g., participles behaving like adjectives, currency tokens, years/decades, pre/pro as adjectives) that would be useful for corpus QA and for refining tagset guidelines. The identification of specific systematic BNC issues (e.g., plain/clear/sharp tagged as adverbs) is practically valuable.
- Lightweight, low-supervision PoS predictor: Training with a small set of unambiguous lemmas to achieve 0.84 accuracy without any sentence context is promising as a bootstrap for resource-poor settings and complements standard taggers.
- Broad accessibility: Methods are simple, well-described, and use public corpora; hyperparameters are stated; mapping to UD is provided (though anonymized here), aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited relative to prior work: That embeddings encode PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic features is well-established (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The main novelty is the specific outlier-driven analysis and the annotation-QA angle, which could be strengthened with more systematic validation.
- Potential leakage/confound in cross-corpus evaluation (Sec. 3): You train embeddings on lemma+PoS tokens. When “applying the classifier to UD lemmas,” it is unclear whether you use lemma or lemma+PoS to retrieve vectors. If the latter, the gold PoS is effectively used to select the input vector, which invalidates the 0.99 F-score claim as a generalization result. This needs clarification and likely correction.
- Unfair comparison with the Stanford tagger: Running the tagger on isolated tokens (not sentences) dramatically handicaps it, making the confusion matrix comparison uninformative. Conclusions about the unique insights offered by the distributional predictor should be supported by a fair comparison (tagger run on sentences).
- Missing strong baselines for the context-free predictor: A lemma-majority baseline (predict the dominant PoS per lemma from the training set) is a natural baseline that could be very strong, possibly rivaling or exceeding 0.84. Without it, it’s difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed approach. Similarly, a simple count-based distributional model or Brown clusters could be informative baselines.
- Limited quantification of annotation errors: While examples are compelling, there is little systematic evaluation (e.g., manual verification of a random sample with inter-annotator agreement) to estimate precision/recall of the error detector. The proportion of errors attributable to frequency sparsity vs. true boundary/annotation issues is also not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- In Section 3, when evaluating on UD English, did you use lemma or lemma+PoS to look up vectors in the embedding model trained on BNC? If lemma+PoS, how did you avoid using the gold label to construct the input?
- For the context-free predictor (Sec. 5), what is the lemma-majority baseline accuracy on the UD test set? How does your approach compare on ambiguous lemmas only?
- How robust are your findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, negative sampling rate) and to training corpus choice (e.g., web corpora vs. BNC)? Any sensitivity analysis?
- Can you quantify the proportion of outliers that correspond to true annotation errors vs. genuine boundary phenomena vs. data sparsity? Did you conduct a small manual study to validate?
- Why choose window size 2? Given PoS is strongly syntactic, did you try positional contexts or dependency-based contexts?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide a fair Stanford (or other) tagger confusion analysis using full sentences; then contrast the types of confusions each method surfaces.  
- Add baselines: lemma-majority, Brown clusters, and simple count-based distributions; also report results on ambiguous lemmas separately.  
- Clarify the UD cross-corpus evaluation to avoid label leakage; if necessary, remove or revise the 0.99 claim.  
- Consider reporting coverage and performance across frequency buckets to disentangle sparsity effects.  
- A small annotation study (e.g., 200 randomly sampled outliers) with expert judgment would notably strengthen the corpus-QA contribution.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The qualitative insights and the QA angle are valuable, but novelty over prior work is limited, the cross-corpus evaluation appears confounded, the comparison to a standard tagger is unfair, and important baselines are missing. With clarified evaluation, stronger baselines, and a more systematic validation of the annotation-error findings, this would be much stronger.
