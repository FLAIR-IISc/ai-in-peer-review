Summary
The paper investigates how static distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this can be used to (i) surface boundary cases between PoS categories and annotation inconsistencies, and (ii) build a simple context-free PoS predictor. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on the BNC, first with lemma+PoS tokens to create separate vectors per PoS, and then train a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings. They analyze misclassifications to highlight outliers (e.g., participles, numerals vs. years, proper vs. common nouns) and apparent BNC tagging issues. They then train embeddings on lemmas without tags and use a small set of unambiguous UD lemmas as prototypes to train a logistic regression that predicts PoS without context, achieving ~0.84 token accuracy on UD. They also probe which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, corpus-driven procedure for discovering PoS boundary cases and corpus annotation inconsistencies by classifying embedding vectors and analyzing misclassifications, with qualitative evidence on BNC and cross-checks on UD.  
2) Evidence that PoS information is strongly present in static embeddings and distributed across many dimensions; a small prototype-based, context-free classifier yields reasonable PoS accuracy (0.84) without sentence context.  
3) Observations on specific PoS confusions (e.g., proper vs. common nouns, numerals vs. years/currencies, participles vs. adjectives) that suggest possible refinements to annotation schemes.

Strengths
- Insightful qualitative analysis of boundary cases. The error analysis surfaces linguistically plausible gray areas (participles behaving like adjectives; numerals vs. years/percentages; adverbs vs. adjectives), and highlights likely BNC tagging inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as adverbs). Cross-checking with UD where relevant strengthens these observations.
- Simple, reproducible methodology. Training skip-gram embeddings and a logistic regression probe is transparent. The frequency weighting heuristic addresses closed-class underrepresentation and yields strong PoS separability (F≈0.98 CV; ≈0.91 on held-out BNC words).
- Demonstration of PoS information in embeddings and its distributed nature. The ANOVA-based feature ranking and accuracy vs. number-of-features curve indicate that PoS affiliation is not tied to a single dimension but spread across many (>100) dimensions, consistent with prior probing results.
- Low-resource relevance. The prototype-based, context-free PoS predictor trained from ~1.5k unambiguous lemmas attains 0.84 token accuracy on UD, suggesting a practical bootstrapping path for preliminary tagging in resource-scarce settings.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and somewhat overstated claims. Prior work has shown that embeddings encode PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The framing “redefining PoS classes” overpromises relative to the primarily exploratory analysis presented.
- Potential circularity in the main outlier analysis. The primary experiment learns embeddings over tokens that already include PoS tags (lemma_POS), and then predicts the same PoS from those vectors. This injects tag-level distributional regularities into the representations (e.g., DET preceding NOUN) and can make the probe’s task easier. While the goal is to find atypical members within a tag, the setup risks reinforcing the original tagging scheme. A stronger test would repeat the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on untagged tokens and compare the lists.
- Lack of quantitative validation of “detected errors.” The paper provides convincing anecdotes but no systematic human evaluation to estimate precision/recall of error detection, nor inter-annotator agreement on whether flagged cases are true errors vs. genuine category fuzziness. The comparison to the Stanford tagger uses token-level inputs (no context), which is an unusual and arguably weak baseline for this purpose.
- Methodological details and ablations are thin. Frequency “weighting” by repeating vectors is unconventional; class-weighted loss would be preferable. No analysis of sensitivity to embedding hyperparameters (window size, negative samples, subsampling), or classifier choices (regularization, calibration). The remarkably high 0.99 F on UD lemmas (types/tokens subset) needs clearer reporting (coverage, confusion matrix) to rule out dataset bias.
- Presentation issues. References are incomplete/garbled; figures and tables are referenced but not visible here; some claims (e.g., component 94 as “idiosyncratic” to conjunctions) are not robustly substantiated beyond a single observation.

Questions to authors
- Can you replicate the outlier discovery using embeddings trained on untagged lemmas (no PoS in the token) and report overlap/differences with the current outlier list? This would address leakage concerns.
- How many of the flagged cases are true annotation errors vs. legitimate boundary phenomena? Please conduct a small blinded human study (e.g., 100 items, two annotators) and report agreement and precision.
- How exactly was frequency weighting implemented in training the classifier (instance replication vs. class weights)? Did you try class-weighted loss, and does it change the results?
- Please report confusion matrices and per-class scores for the UD experiments, including coverage (types/tokens dropped as OOV) and a majority-by-lemma baseline.
- Sensitivity to embedding hyperparameters: how do window size, subsampling, and dimension affect PoS separability and the feature-count curve? Any statistical tests to support the observed trends?
- Can you release code, mappings (BNC↔UD↔PTB), and the outlier lists to aid reproducibility?

Additional suggestions
- Strengthen novelty by (i) applying the pipeline to multiple typologically diverse languages, (ii) repeating the analysis with contextualized embeddings (e.g., ELMo/BERT, pooled to types) to see if boundary cases differ, and (iii) providing an extrinsic evaluation (e.g., post-editing BNC tags based on your detector and measuring impact on a downstream PoS tagger).
- Tighten claims and scope: emphasize “using embedding probes to audit PoS annotations and boundary cases” rather than “redefining PoS classes.”

Overall recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper offers interesting observations and a simple, useful auditing procedure, but the methodological circularity, limited novelty, and lack of quantitative validation weaken the case for acceptance at this venue. With the above clarifications, stronger validation, and broader scope, this could become a solid contribution.
