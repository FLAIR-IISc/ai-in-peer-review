Summary and main contributions
- This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode enough information to recover parts of speech (PoS), and uses this to analyze PoS category boundaries and annotation inconsistencies. The authors train Skip-gram embeddings on the British National Corpus (BNC) with tokens as lemma+PoS, fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from vectors, and examine misclassifications (“outliers”) as evidence of boundary phenomena and errors. They also train embeddings on lemmas only and build a simple PoS predictor using “prototypical” unambiguous UD lemmas, obtaining reasonable accuracy without sentence context. An analysis of vector components suggests PoS information is distributed across many dimensions.
- Contributions (as assessed):
  1) Empirical evidence that standard word embeddings encode robust PoS signals which can be exploited to identify “boundary” items and systematic tagging inconsistencies in PoS-annotated corpora (notably in BNC).
  2) A simple, context-free PoS predictor trained on embeddings of unambiguous lemmas that achieves competitive accuracy given zero contextual features, suggesting a practical bootstrapping route for low-resource settings.
  3) Feature analysis showing PoS affiliation is distributed across many embedding dimensions, with occasional highly diagnostic components.

Strengths
- Clear empirical demonstration that embeddings capture PoS patterns. The classifier trained on lemma+PoS embeddings achieves high F-scores on BNC and generalizes (within the same paradigm) to held-out word types, indicating the signal is not confined to a few special cases. The analysis of similarity between “prototypical” PoS vectors (e.g., determiners vs pronouns; prepositions vs subordinating conjunctions) aligns with linguistic intuition.
- Insightful outlier analysis that surfaces genuine boundary phenomena and probable annotation issues. The findings on participles (VERB→ADJ), adjectival modifier nouns (NOUN↔ADJ), numerals vs years/currencies (NUM↔NOUN/PROPN), and items like “pre/pro/cross” (ADP→ADJ) are compelling and well motivated, with concrete examples. The identification of systematic BNC mistaggings (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as adverbs) illustrates practical utility for corpus error detection.
- Complementarity to standard taggers. The comparison to the Stanford tagger (run on isolated tokens) shows the embedding-based approach yields different confusion patterns, surfacing cases a conventional tagger’s confusion matrix would not highlight. This supports the claim that the proposed method adds diagnostic value for corpus auditing and PoS boundary exploration.
- Simple, reproducible modeling with sensible controls. The use of logistic regression makes the analysis interpretable and avoids conflating results with complex architectures. The feature ranking (ANOVA F) and k-best experiments substantiate the claim that PoS information is distributed across many dimensions rather than concentrated in a few.
- Practical potential for low-resource scenarios. The lemma-only embeddings plus unambiguous-lemma prototypes achieve approximately 0.84 token-level accuracy without sentence context, suggesting a feasible warm-start lexicon-level tagger that could reduce annotation costs.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in cross-validation on the BNC training set. The approach “weights” by duplicating word vectors proportional to corpus frequency and performs 10-fold cross-validation over instances rather than word types. This likely places identical vectors in both training and validation folds, inflating the reported 0.979 F-score. A type-level split (no word type overlap across folds) is needed for a rigorous estimate.
- Ambiguity and potential circularity in UD evaluation for the lemma+PoS embedding model. Applying the classifier to UD “lemmas” when the embedding vocabulary consists of lemma+PoS tokens implies either (a) the gold UD PoS tag was used to form the input token string (undermining the independence of evaluation), or (b) a mapping strategy that is not described. The reported 0.99 F-score is not interpretable without clarifying how embeddings were retrieved and whether gold labels were used to index vectors.
- Use of lemma+PoS tokens during embedding training may bias the representation. Splitting a lemma into separate lemma+PoS tokens changes its distribution and may make PoS clustering easier than in a standard corpus where PoS is not exposed to the embedding learner. A control experiment training embeddings over lemmas (or surface forms) with no PoS suffix, followed by PoS classification, would better quantify how much PoS signal is learned from natural contexts versus injected through token splitting.
- Outlier analysis is mostly qualitative and lacks systematic validation. While the examples are plausible, there is limited quantification (e.g., proportion of suspected BNC errors verified by manual inspection) or inter-annotator validation. A small manual audit or leveraging UD as an alternate gold standard to confirm error categories would strengthen claims about annotation inconsistencies.
- Limited novelty relative to prior art. Prior work has shown embeddings encode PoS/morphosyntactic information and that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties. The novel angle here is primarily the corpus-auditing/outlier use case and a simple context-free predictor. The methodological contributions are incremental, and some comparisons (e.g., against stronger baselines or recent contextual models) are missing.
- Presentation issues. References contain formatting errors, and some experimental details are under-specified (e.g., precise train/test splits, hyperparameter sensitivity, how unknowns were handled in UD tests). Figures are referenced but not fully interpretable in the text alone.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation: Did the 10-fold CV ensure no word type appeared in both training and validation folds? If not, can you report type-level CV results without instance duplication? How do results change without frequency-based duplication (using class weights instead)?
- UD evaluation (Section 3): When applying the lemma+PoS embedding classifier to UD lemmas, how were vectors retrieved? Were gold PoS tags appended to lemmas to index embeddings? If not, what mapping was used?
- Control embeddings: What is performance when embeddings are trained on lemmas (or surface forms) without PoS tags, but the same classifier is used? This would isolate how much PoS signal is learned from natural contexts versus injected by the lemma+PoS tokenization.
- Outlier validation: Can you provide a quantified analysis (e.g., sample of N cases, percent confirmed as annotation errors by manual review) for the claimed BNC inconsistencies (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as ADV)?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to window size, negative sampling, down-sampling, and vector dimensionality? Does the “distributed across ≥100 components” conclusion hold across hyperparameters?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, the BNC–Universal tag mapping, trained models, and the outlier lists to facilitate verification and reuse?
