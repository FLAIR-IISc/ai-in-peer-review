Summary
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode information about parts of speech (PoS). The authors train skip-gram embeddings on a lemmatized, PoS-tag-augmented BNC (tokens as lemma_POS), and fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings. They analyze “outliers” where predicted PoS disagrees with the BNC tag, arguing these reveal fuzzy PoS boundaries or annotation inconsistencies. They also train a “proper predictor” using embeddings trained without PoS tags and a small set of “unambiguous” UD lemmas, achieving 84% token-level accuracy (lemma-only features). Finally, they offer a brief component-level analysis suggesting PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Qualitative analysis showing that misclassifications from a distributional PoS predictor highlight boundary phenomena and some likely annotation inconsistencies (e.g., years as NUM vs NOUN, adjectival nouns, passive participles).
2) Demonstration that word embeddings contain substantial PoS signal even without context, via a simple classifier trained on a small set of “prototypical” lemmas.
3) Observations about the dispersion of PoS information across embedding dimensions (limited, largely anecdotal, evidence).

Strengths
- Empirical evidence that embeddings encode PoS-relevant information. Both the “tag-augmented” and “tag-free” setups indicate strong separability of major coarse PoS classes, reinforcing prior findings and providing additional supporting experiments on English corpora (BNC, UD).
- Useful qualitative error analysis. The discussion of proper vs common nouns, adjectival use of nouns, passive participles, currency/percentages, and time expressions is insightful and plausibly highlights true boundary cases and inconsistencies. Some examples (e.g., adverbs tagged as adjectives) indeed look like systematic tagging issues.
- Simple, transparent methodology. The approach (skip-gram + multinomial LR) is straightforward and easy to replicate conceptually, aiding interpretability of the error patterns.
- Potential diagnostic utility. The idea of using distributional misclassifications as a tool to audit PoS annotations is promising and could be practically valuable for corpus maintenance.

Weaknesses
- Circularity and data leakage in the main setup. Training embeddings on tokens augmented with gold PoS (lemma_POS) makes PoS trivially recoverable from distributional context, since all neighboring tokens also carry PoS suffixes. The reported high F-scores are therefore unsurprising. Moreover, the cross-validation on 177k “instances” created by repeating identical word vectors proportional to frequency likely leaks the same word types across folds (if splitting at instance level), inflating CV performance. The authors should confirm fold construction by word type and report type-level metrics without repetition.
- Unclear and potentially flawed UD evaluation in Section 3. It is not stated how vectors are obtained for UD lemmas when embeddings were trained on lemma_POS tokens. If the correct PoS is needed to retrieve the lemma_POS vector, this constitutes label leakage. The 0.99 F-score is suspiciously high and must be clarified.
- Weak and unfair baselines. For the “proper predictor” (84% token accuracy with lemma-only embeddings), there is no comparison to a straightforward lexicon baseline: assign the most frequent PoS per lemma from training (or a simple suffix/morphology baseline). Such baselines are strong for coarse PoS and often exceed 84% without context; without them, the claimed utility for resource-poor settings is unconvincing. Similarly, comparing to the Stanford tagger run on isolated tokens is not a meaningful baseline; that model is designed to use sentence context.
- Limited novelty relative to established literature. That embeddings encode PoS/morphosyntactic information and that misclassifications can expose linguistic regularities has been shown in prior work (e.g., PoS induction, Brown clusters, Clark 2003; Tsvetkov et al.; Tsuboi; others). The paper’s methodological contribution is modest and the analysis remains anecdotal without a systematic validation of discovered “errors” (e.g., human reannotation study with precision/recall of detected issues).
- Incomplete experimental rigor and reporting. Key details are missing or informal: quantitative results of failed clustering attempts; robustness to embedding hyperparameters; sensitivity to window size; effect of lemmatization quality; frequency effects beyond the single 100–500 bucket; how “unambiguous” lemmas are selected and how ambiguous lemmas affect performance; how many misclassifications are truly boundary cases vs sparsity/OOV; and reproducibility (data/code links are anonymized, but crucial clarifications are absent).

Questions to Authors
1) How were the folds constructed for the 10-fold CV on the 177,343 instances? Were splits done by word type to avoid the same vector appearing in train and test? Please report type-level CV without repetition.
2) In the UD evaluation with the classifier trained on lemma_POS embeddings: how did you obtain vectors for UD lemmas? Did you need the gold PoS to retrieve lemma_POS vectors? If not, what was the mapping? Please detail and reconcile the 0.99 F-score with potential leakage.
3) For the “proper predictor” (tag-free embeddings), what is the performance of a majority-PoS-per-lemma baseline on the same UD split (and perhaps a suffix-based baseline)? Also report per-class scores and performance restricted to ambiguous lemmas.
4) Can you provide a small, blinded manual evaluation estimating precision of “detected annotation errors” (e.g., sample 100 misclassifications and have annotators judge whether the gold tag is wrong or boundary-ambiguous)?
5) How sensitive are your findings (both classification accuracies and misclassification patterns) to hyperparameters (window, dimension, negative samples), corpus choice/size, and whether input is lemma vs surface form?

Overall, while the qualitative observations are interesting, the core experimental design leans heavily on PoS-augmented training that risks circularity, and the quantitative claims lack strong baselines and rigorous controls. I recommend rejection in the current form; strengthening the methodology, clarifying leakage issues, and adding fair baselines and validation would substantially improve the work.
