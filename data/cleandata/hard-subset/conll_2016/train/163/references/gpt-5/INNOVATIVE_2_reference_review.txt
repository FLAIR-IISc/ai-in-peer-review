Summary and contributions
This paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode robust information about parts of speech (PoS), and exploits this signal to (i) surface boundary phenomena between PoS classes and (ii) detect annotation inconsistencies. The authors first train embeddings on a PoS-annotated BNC variant (lemma+tag tokens) and a simple multinomial logistic classifier to predict PoS from word vectors; misclassifications are analyzed as “outliers.” They then train embeddings on lemmas without tags and build a small-seed classifier to predict PoS without using sentence context. They also explore which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A diagnostic methodology: using embedding-based PoS prediction errors to reveal systematic boundary cases and annotation issues (e.g., participles behaving adjectivally, numerals vs years/currencies, SCONJ/ADP proximities, and concrete corpus tagging errors like “plain/clear/sharp”).
2) Empirical evidence that PoS information is strong and distributed across many embedding dimensions, with interpretable “prototypical” vectors (e.g., a distinctive conjunction dimension) yet requiring many components for robust classification.
3) A context-free, low-supervision PoS predictor trained from ~1.5k “unambiguous” lemmas reaching 84% token accuracy on UD, suggesting utility for bootstrapping in low-resource settings and for corpus auditing.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and utility: Reframing embeddings as a lens on PoS boundaries and corpus quality is a bold, useful direction. The qualitative analyses are insightful and concrete (currency amounts and percentages behaving like NUM; years/decades acting as NOUN; participles with adjectival distribution; SCONJ/ADV confusion), and the discovery of systematic BNC tagging errors (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” tagged as ADV) is compelling.
- Simple methods, strong signal: With minimal modeling (skip-gram + logistic regression), the approach achieves high type-level F on unseen words (0.91) and produces interpretable clusters and prototypes. This supports the core claim that distributional context alone encodes substantial PoS information.
- Cross-corpus patterns: Applying the classifier to UD vocabulary and building a lemma-only predictor that attains 84% token accuracy without any sentence context bolsters generality, and points to practical applications in pre-tagging and tagset auditing.
- Interpretability of embeddings: The analysis of “prototypical PoS vectors” and feature ranking (ANOVA F) provides a human-readable bridge between neural representations and linguistic categories, and the finding that PoS signals are distributed (not localized to a few dimensions) is informative for the community.

Weaknesses
- Leakage and circularity in the main setup: Training embeddings on “lemma+PoS” tokens bakes PoS information into every context token, making PoS prediction almost tautological. While the authors acknowledge using this setup to find outliers, it still confounds the claim that embeddings learned “semantics” carry PoS signals. A stronger design would repeat the outlier analysis with lemma-only embeddings to ensure boundary phenomena are not artifacts of tag-augmented contexts.
- Cross-validation leakage and reporting: The 10-fold CV on 177k “instances” repeats identical word vectors proportional to frequency. Unless folds were made by type (not by instance), identical vectors likely appear in both train and test, inflating CV scores. The unseen-type test is more informative; please report type-level CV (grouped by lemma) to avoid leakage.
- UD evaluation clarity: The reported 0.99 F-score “applying the trained classifier to UD lemmas” is unclear. If the classifier and embeddings were trained on lemma+tag tokens, how were UD lemmas embedded and classified? Were UD tokens mapped to BNC lemma+tag forms (risking leakage), or were they bare lemmas? Please clarify preprocessing and whether the evaluation is type- or token-level.
- Baselines and related work: The comparison to the Stanford tagger on isolated tokens is not compelling (it is designed to use context). More relevant are prior unsupervised/weakly supervised PoS induction and distributional clustering (e.g., Schütze 1995; Clark 2003; Goldwater/Griffiths; Christodoulopoulos et al. 2011), as well as recent embedding-based diagnostics. The paper would benefit from citations and positioning relative to that literature, and from baseline comparisons (e.g., Brown clusters, HMM-based induction) for the lemma-only predictor.
- Validation of corpus fixes: The claims about annotation errors (beyond a few illustrative cases) would be stronger with a small-scale human adjudication of a random sample of flagged items, plus quantitative precision/recall of error detection. As written, the conclusions on “corpus correction” are promising but preliminary.
- Overclaiming in title/scope: The work “redefines” PoS classes mainly by surfacing boundary cases and inconsistencies; it does not propose a new taxonomy nor a principled data-driven re-partitioning. Consider tempering the claim or adding a concrete proposal.

Questions to authors
1) Tag leakage: For the outlier analysis (Sections 3–4), can you replicate the experiments with embeddings trained on lemmas only (no tags) and demonstrate that the same major boundary patterns emerge?
2) CV protocol: Did you perform fold splits by type (one vector per lemma(+tag)) or by instance? If by instance, can you re-run type-level CV to avoid leakage?
3) UD 0.99 F-score: Precisely how were UD items mapped to the BNC-trained model? Were inputs lemmas, lemma+tag tokens, or something else? Was the evaluation type-level or token-level?
4) Corpus error validation: Can you provide a small manual study (e.g., 100 randomly sampled flagged errors) with agreement statistics to substantiate the corpus-correction claim?
5) Sensitivity analysis: How sensitive are the findings to window size, vector dimension, and subword modeling? Do the conjunction “component 94” and the “distributed signal across ~100 dims” persist across hyperparameters?
6) Low-resource claim: Can you demonstrate the lemma-only predictor on a genuinely low-resource setting (e.g., smaller corpus, different language), perhaps with a few hundred seed types?

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite methodological caveats, the paper advances a compelling, conceptually novel diagnostic use of embeddings to probe PoS boundaries and corpus quality, with clear, interpretable findings and a simple yet effective lemma-only predictor. Addressing leakage, clarifying UD evaluation, and adding a small human validation would significantly strengthen the contribution.
