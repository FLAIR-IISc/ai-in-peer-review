Summary
This paper investigates how much part-of-speech (PoS) information is encoded in distributional word embeddings. Using the BNC (lemmatized and appended with Universal PoS tags), the authors train word2vec embeddings and a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from type embeddings, analyze misclassifications as “outliers” to surface boundary phenomena and corpus inconsistencies, and then build a “proper” predictor by training embeddings on raw lemmas (no PoS), using a small set of prototypical lemmas from UD to train a type-level PoS classifier. They further examine which embedding dimensions correlate with PoS classes. Qualitative analyses highlight well-known PoS boundary cases (participles, nominal modifiers, numerals/years) and suggest the method can reveal tagging inconsistencies in BNC.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A practical diagnostic: using embedding-based PoS prediction errors to surface PoS boundary cases and potential corpus annotation inconsistencies, with concrete case studies (e.g., participles as adjectives, numerals vs years, DET/PRON/ADP/SCONJ confusions).
2) A simple, type-level, context-free PoS predictor trained from unsupervised embeddings on raw text that achieves moderate accuracy (0.84) and shows PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.
3) Empirical evidence (via ANOVA ranking) that PoS affiliation is not localized to a few embedding dimensions but spread broadly, plus a “prototype” analysis of PoS vectors.

Strengths
- Insightful qualitative error analysis: The error breakdowns and examples (proper vs common nouns; participles; numerals vs currency/years; DET/PRON/ADP/SCONJ) are linguistically plausible, align with prior observations, and illustrate how the method can flag corpus inconsistencies (e.g., BNC adverb/adjective issues). This is the paper’s most convincing contribution.
- Clear, simple methodology and transparent hyperparameters: Standard models (Skip-gram, logistic regression) and well-known corpora (BNC, UD) make the work easy to replicate in principle. The authors also use a disjoint frequency range for the “unseen” evaluation, which partially mitigates leakage concerns from frequency weighting.
- Cross-corpus perspective: Applying the approach to both BNC and UD surfaces annotation policy differences (e.g., tokenization of currency/percent), supporting the claim that the tool can aid corpus QA.
- Distributed nature of PoS signals: The feature-ranking experiment (accuracy vs top-k dimensions) supports the claim that PoS information is distributed and not confined to a few axes, which is consistent with prior findings and worth documenting.

Weaknesses
- Label leakage in Section 3: The main analysis trains embeddings on lemma_POS tokens, so PoS labels pervade the training signal (every context token is also marked with its PoS). Predicting PoS from such embeddings is partially tautological and overstates the conclusion that “unsupervised embeddings contain PoS.” The resulting separability and outliers may be artifacts of injecting PoS throughout the contexts. The outlier analyses are interesting, but the design confounds the central claim.
- Potential circularity in UD evaluation: It is unclear how vectors for UD lemmas were obtained with a model whose vocabulary consists of lemma_POS tokens. If UD gold PoS were appended to retrieve vectors, then the reported 0.99 F-score is not meaningful. This needs clarification; if circular, the claim should be removed or re-evaluated with raw-lemma embeddings.
- Unfair baseline comparison: The Stanford tagger is evaluated on isolated tokens without sentence context “to mimic our workflow,” which severely handicaps a contextual tagger and skews its confusion matrix. This comparison does not support the claim that the distributional approach “reveals different errors”; it more likely reflects the crippled setup.
- Limited novelty: Prior work has already shown that embeddings encode PoS and that dimensions correlate with linguistic properties (e.g., Tsuboi 2014; Tsvetkov et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2016). The paper’s main novelty lies in the qualitative corpus-diagnostics angle, which is valuable but would benefit from stronger validation.
- Methodological and reporting gaps: 
  - Frequency “weighting” via duplicated, identical vectors combined with random 10-fold CV risks leakage (same word vector in train and test folds), inflating CV scores. Prefer type-level splits and class-weighted loss.
  - Lack of stronger baselines (e.g., Brown clusters, PPMI+SVD, bag-of-context words) and per-class metrics.
  - No human validation of “detected errors” to quantify precision/recall as a corpus QA tool.
  - Only English; no analysis of robustness to hyperparameters; incomplete, messy references; missing figures/tables referenced.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
1) How did you obtain UD word vectors for the model trained on lemma_POS tokens? Did you append UD gold PoS to lemmas to look up vectors? If yes, please remove/redo that evaluation with raw-lemma embeddings.
2) Can you repeat the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS in tokens) to rule out label leakage? Do the same boundary cases emerge, and with what precision?
3) How does your approach compare to non-neural distributional baselines (Brown clusters, PPMI-SVD, bag-of-context) and to subword-aware embeddings for OOVs? Please provide per-class metrics and ablations (window size, corpus size, downsampling).
4) For the 0.84-accuracy type-level predictor, what are per-class scores and the effect of frequency/ambiguity? How much is driven by orthographic cues (digits, capitalization)?
5) Can you provide a small human study validating that the flagged BNC “errors” are truly annotation mistakes or legitimate boundary cases, with quantified rates?

Overall assessment
The paper raises a useful diagnostic use-case for embeddings and contains thoughtful qualitative analyses. However, the core methodology in Section 3 suffers from label leakage, the UD evaluation may be circular, and the baseline comparison is unfair. Novelty beyond prior literature is moderate. I would lean toward reject unless the authors can (i) redo the key analyses with embeddings trained on raw text (no PoS in tokens), (ii) clarify/remove circular evaluations, and (iii) strengthen baselines and validation.
