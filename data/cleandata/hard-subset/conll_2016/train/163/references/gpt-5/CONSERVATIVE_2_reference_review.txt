Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper investigates whether distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information. It trains a Skip-gram model on BNC where tokens are lemma_POS, fits a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings, and analyzes misclassifications as “boundary” cases and potential annotation errors.
- It further trains a “proper” predictor using embeddings learned from untagged lemmas and a small set of “prototypical” UD lemmas to predict PoS without context, and probes which embedding dimensions are most informative, arguing PoS information is distributed across many components.
Main contributions (as I see them):
1) An error analysis showing how distributional representations can surface PoS boundary phenomena and likely annotation inconsistencies.
2) A simple probe indicating that PoS affinity is encoded across many embedding dimensions.
3) A preliminary contextless PoS predictor trained on prototypical lemmas.

Strengths
- Insightful qualitative error analysis: The discussion of proper noun vs noun, noun–adjective alternations, participles behaving like adjectives, numerals vs years/amounts, and specific adverb/adjective confusions (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp”) is concrete and linguistically plausible. The paper identifies some likely systematic inconsistencies in BNC and contrasts with UD design choices.
- Simple, interpretable methodology: The use of linear classifiers and frequency weighting exposes class-structure in embeddings without complex models, making findings easier to interpret.
- Practical diagnostic value: The approach has potential as a corpus audit tool to flag annotation problems or boundary cases for adjudication; this is a useful, low-cost application.
- Cross-corpus perspective: The comparison with UD and with a mainstream tagger (Stanford) suggests the distributional predictor emphasizes different confusion patterns, complementing standard tagger analyses.
- Observation about distributed PoS signals: The k-best feature curve (ANOVA ranking) suggests PoS information is not localized to a few axes, aligning with the general understanding of embeddings.

Weaknesses
- Methodological leakage in the main experiment: Training embeddings on tokens of the form lemma_POS means all contexts are also tagged (e.g., the_DET). The representation for love_VERB is learned from contexts that explicitly encode neighbors’ PoS, which is tantamount to using gold PoS context as features to predict PoS. This makes the task much easier and undermines claims about discovering PoS information “unsupervised from raw text.” An ablation with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS in the corpus) is essential for the main analysis; otherwise, the high F-scores are not informative about what standard embeddings encode.
- Limited novelty and missing comparisons: It is well-established that distributional representations encode PoS/morphosyntax and can be used for PoS induction/probing (e.g., Schütze 1995; Clark 2003; Brown clustering; more recent probing work). The paper does not position itself rigorously relative to that literature nor compare against simple/strong baselines (e.g., Brown clusters + logistic regression; nearest-neighbor in embedding space; majority-tag lexicon; character/morphological baselines).
- Questionable evaluation design and baselines: 
  - Frequency weighting via repetition in cross-validation conflates type- vs token-level evaluation and can inflate performance on frequent items; better to use class weights or report both type- and token-weighted metrics.
  - The “majority class” and “one-dimension” baselines are not competitive. Missing: per-lemma majority (most frequent tag), 1-NN, linear probe on lemma-only embeddings, and a standard tagger without sentence context.
  - The 0.99 F-score on UD lemmas is surprisingly high given OOV omissions; the setup is insufficiently described and may reflect overlap or leakage.
- Inadequate validation of error claims: While examples are suggestive, there is no systematic human evaluation to confirm that flagged cases are genuine annotation errors vs model artifacts. Counts for specific phenomena (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp”) are presented, but no adjudication protocol, IAA, or corrections are provided.
- Ambiguity and selection biases: The “prototypical” lemma selection based on a >2x dominant tag threshold introduces selection bias and label noise. OOV handling (27% types/10% tokens omitted; 860 tokens omitted in UD test) further biases evaluation. Clustering is dismissed as “failed” without methodological detail, which misses an opportunity to connect to PoS induction literature.

Questions to authors
- Main experiment leakage: Can you retrain the primary Skip-gram model on the same BNC but using lemmas only (no PoS tags anywhere in the corpus) and repeat the misclassification/error analysis? How do results and F-scores change?
- UD 0.99 F-score: Precisely how was this computed? What was the mapping between UD lemmas and your BNC-trained representations in the PoS-tagged-embedding experiment? Were any UD PoS labels used during embedding training? Please report coverage, OOV rates, and per-class scores.
- Baselines: Can you add per-lemma majority, 1-NN in embedding space, Brown/Clark clusters + linear classifier, and a linear probe trained on lemma-only embeddings? For the “proper predictor,” compare to a lexicon baseline that assigns the dominant UD tag per lemma.
- Validation: Will you provide a manual evaluation of a sample of flagged “errors,” with two annotators and adjudication, to estimate precision of the audit signal? Can you release the candidate lists?
- Clustering: What clustering methods and settings were attempted (k-means with multiple k, GMMs, spectral)? How do induced clusters align with PoS if trained on lemma-only embeddings?

Overall recommendation
Interesting analysis direction with potential practical utility as a diagnostic tool, but the core experimental setup suffers from label leakage and insufficient baselines, and the novelty relative to established work is limited. I would encourage a substantial revision addressing the ablations, comparisons, and validation before acceptance.
