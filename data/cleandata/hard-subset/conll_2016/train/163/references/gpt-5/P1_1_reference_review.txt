Summary
This paper investigates whether unsupervised distributional word embeddings encode part-of-speech (PoS) information and whether this signal can be used both to (i) surface boundary cases and annotation inconsistencies across PoS classes and (ii) build a simple, context-free PoS predictor. The authors train skip-gram embeddings on the BNC, first with lemma+gold-PoS tokens, then fit a multinomial logistic regression to predict PoS from embeddings, analyze misclassifications as “outliers,” and compare to a confusion-matrix analysis obtained from the Stanford tagger. They also train embeddings on raw lemmas and a small logistic classifier on “prototypical” UD lemmas to predict PoS without context, reporting 0.84 accuracy. A simple feature analysis suggests PoS information is distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A practical methodology to audit PoS annotations and identify borderline cases by training a simple classifier on (lemma+PoS)-based embeddings and inspecting systematic misclassifications; demonstrated with linguistically interpretable cases in BNC and UD.
- Evidence that static embeddings carry a strong PoS signal that supports a lightweight, context-free PoS classifier trained from a small set of prototypical lemmas (∼1.5k), suggesting utility for low-resource bootstrapping.
- An initial analysis of feature importance indicating that PoS information is broadly distributed across many embedding dimensions.

Strengths
- Clear, simple pipeline with strong empirical signal: A multinomial logistic regression over skip-gram vectors yields high F-scores (0.979 CV on frequent BNC types; 0.91 on held-out types), and the “context-free” variant achieves 0.84 accuracy on UD test tokens using only lemma embeddings. Given no sentence context, this is a solid baseline.
- Insightful error analysis with concrete, linguistically plausible findings: The paper surfaces several meaningful boundary or annotation issues (e.g., passive participles behaving adjectivally; numerals vs nouns for currencies and years; adverbs tagged for adjectives like “plain/clear/sharp” in BNC). These examples convincingly show that distributional patterns can spotlight corpus inconsistencies and gray areas in PoS definitions.
- Method complements standard tagger-based audits: The confusion analysis from the distributional approach yields different high-coverage misclassification types than those obtained from running a tagger in isolation (e.g., SCONJ→ADV, ADP→ADJ), showing complementary diagnostic value.
- Low data requirement and potential practical impact: The “prototypical” approach (training on ~1.5k unambiguous UD lemmas) is promising for low-resource settings where a small seed lexicon could be curated to bootstrap tagging.
- Reproducibility-friendly choices: Standard embeddings, simple classifier, and explicit pre-processing (e.g., frequency weighting) make the approach accessible.

Weaknesses
- Major methodological confound in the main analysis: Training embeddings on lemma+gold-PoS tokens introduces supervision into the representation learning. The classifier then “predicts” the PoS associated with the very token used to build the embedding. While the authors acknowledge this is “artificial” and use it to find outliers, it weakens claims about what unsupervised embeddings encode. A stronger demonstration would repeat the outlier analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas and show similar boundary cases emerge.
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: Prior studies have shown that embeddings reflect PoS (clustering, linear regularities, correlations with linguistic features). The paper’s unique angle is the corpus-audit via misclassifications, but the overarching framing (“redefining PoS classes”) seems over-claimed relative to the mostly confirmatory evidence and case studies on English.
- Evaluation gaps and baselines: No comparison to simpler distributional baselines (e.g., PPMI+SVD, Brown clusters) or to morphology-aware/static embeddings (fastText) and contextualized models (ELMo/BERT) for the context-free task. For the 0.84-accuracy “proper” predictor, there is no comparison to trivial heuristics (suffix/shape or character n-gram classifiers), nor per-tag confusion matrices. The reported 0.99 F-score on UD lemmas (with 27% OOV types excluded) looks unusually high and warrants more detail on coverage and per-tag performance.
- Generalization and scope: All experiments are English-only, with embeddings trained on a single corpus (BNC). Claims about low-resource applicability and typological generality are speculative without multilingual evidence. The feature analysis is shallow (ANOVA ranking); no robustness tests over hyperparameters (window size, subsampling), and the notable “dimension 94” case remains anecdotal.
- Questionable comparison setting with the Stanford tagger: Running a sentence-level tagger on isolated tokens is a strawman; unsurprisingly, its confusion profile differs. A fairer comparison would analyze the tagger’s confusion on full sentences and then discuss complementarities.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Can you replicate the outlier-detection analysis with embeddings trained on raw lemmas (no PoS in tokenization)? Do the same boundary cases surface with comparable coverage?
- For the context-free classifier on UD, please report per-tag precision/recall and a confusion matrix. How does it compare to (i) a character n-gram/suffix baseline and (ii) fastText embeddings? How many prototypes per tag are needed to approach your reported accuracy?
- How sensitive are your findings to embedding hyperparameters (window size, subsampling, vector size)? Why choose window=2 and no subsampling?
- For the BNC annotation issues you highlight (e.g., “plain/clear/sharp” as ADV), what proportion did you manually verify as clear errors vs debatable boundary cases?
- Will you release code, embeddings, and the BNC-to-UD mapping to enable replication? Under what license?

Additional comments
- Improve references (many entries are incomplete/garbled) and clarify dataset splits and OOV handling. Tone down claims about “redefining” PoS; the work is better framed as corpus auditing and exploratory analysis of PoS signals in embeddings. Consider adding multilingual experiments and stronger baselines to bolster impact.
