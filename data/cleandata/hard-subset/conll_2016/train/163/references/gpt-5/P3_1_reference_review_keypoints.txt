- Paper investigates how distributional embeddings encode PoS information and uses them to recover PoS categories, find boundary/outlier cases, and build a lemma-only PoS predictor.

- Contributions: (1) using embedding-driven PoS prediction errors to detect boundary phenomena and annotation issues, (2) a context-free PoS prediction baseline from embeddings and evidence that PoS is distributed across dimensions, (3) prototypical PoS vectors and ANOVA-based feature diagnostics.

- Strength: Insightful, linguistically grounded error analysis that uncovers real annotation problems and gray areas between PoS classes (e.g., adjectives vs adverbs, participles, numerals).

- Strength: Clear, reproducible pipeline with reasonable hyperparameter transparency and frequency weighting to address class imbalance.

- Strength: Prototypical vectors and dimension-wise analysis are intuitive diagnostics showing PoS signals are broadly distributed.

- Strength: Cross-corpus exploration (BNC to UD) and discussion of open vs closed classes yield useful qualitative insights.

- Strength: Proposed low-resource use case (seeding with unambiguous prototypes) is reasonable and potentially impactful.

- Weakness: Possible evaluation leakage—main embeddings trained on lemma+PoS tokens; unclear how UD vectors were retrieved, risking circular (tautological) UD evaluation.

- Weakness: Cross-validation likely overestimates performance because folds appear to be by instances (with repeated frequency-weighted entries) rather than by word types, allowing same vectors in train and test.

- Weakness: Comparisons are weak/unfair—Stanford tagger baseline had no context while the method used corpus-wide context; lacks comparisons to majority-per-lemma, clustering (Brown/Clark), morphological/orthographic baselines, and PoS induction literature.

- Weakness: Second experiment (lemma-only predictor, ~84% token accuracy) lacks competitive baselines or breakdowns and may be beaten by trivial majority-per-lemma; no analysis by lemma ambiguity, frequency, or OOV handling.

- Weakness: Overinterpretation of embedding dimensions without robust controls—ANOVA done on training set with no held-out probing, random-label controls, seed stability, or cross-corpus/hyperparameter checks; specific dimension anecdotes may be idiosyncratic.

- Weakness: Training prototype selection biased to “unambiguous” words, which may not test handling of ambiguous lemmas at test time.

- Question: How were UD word vectors obtained given a lemma+PoS embedding vocabulary—did you use gold UD tags as lookup keys (lemma_UPOS), making the evaluation circular?

- Question: Were cross-validation folds created by word types or by repeated instances; please rerun with type-level splits if needed.

- Question: What is the accuracy of a majority-per-lemma baseline on UD, and how does your method compare to simple morphology/orthography and clustering baselines?

- Question: Can you report performance by word frequency and lemma ambiguity, and quantify how much outliers are due to data sparsity?

- Question: Are ANOVA rankings and the “dimension 94” effect stable across seeds, window sizes, other embedding models (CBOW/GloVe/fastText), and different corpora?

- Additional comment: Related work coverage is incomplete—discuss unsupervised/weakly supervised PoS induction, Brown/Clark clusters, early neural taggers, and probing literature; fix reference formatting.

- Additional comment: Replace or supplement the Stanford tagger comparison with fairer baselines or full-context tagging and a careful type-level error analysis.

- Additional comment: Consider manual validation of predicted outliers with annotator judgments and report inter-annotator agreement.

- Overall recommendation: Idea and qualitative analysis are interesting but major experimental issues (possible leakage, inflated CV, weak baselines, lack of probing controls) require substantial revision before acceptance.
